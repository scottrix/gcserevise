GC1: The Design Process

Foundation Higher All Boards

Understanding briefs; research and mind-mapping; generating ideas; developing and refining concepts; client requirements and target audiences; presenting design solutions; iteration and feedback.

Graphic Communication: The Design Process

Understanding briefs; research and mind-mapping; generating ideas; developing and refining concepts; client requirements and target audiences; presenting design solutions; iteration and feedback.

Key Fact: A design brief defines the problem — who is the audience, what is the message, what are the constraints.
Key Fact: The design process is iterative: research, ideas, development, refinement, feedback, revision.
Key Fact: Mind-mapping and mood boards help generate and organise ideas visually at the start of a project.
Key Fact: Presenting to a client requires clear rationale — explain design decisions, not just show finished work.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For The Design Process, you must know:

  • Topic-specific techniques, materials and processes
  • How to experiment with and refine approaches
  • How to record ideas and observations visually and through annotation
  • How to develop personal responses informed by contextual research
  • Relevant artists, designers or craftspeople and their contexts

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Create a series of experimental studies exploring the design process. Annotate your process.

Q2: Research an artist or designer who works with the design process. Write a critical analysis of their approach and explain how it has informed your own work.

Q3: Evaluate the effectiveness of your experiments with the design process. Which approaches were most successful and why?

✅ Answers

  1. A range of experimental studies showing different approaches to the design process, each annotated with media, technique and purpose. Show development from initial experiments to refined outcomes.
  2. A critical analysis naming a specific artist/designer, using specialist vocabulary, explaining their context and approach, and making clear connections to personal work.
  3. A reflective evaluation identifying successful and less successful experiments, explaining WHY using technical and conceptual language, and showing how evaluation led to further refinement.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Address ALL four Assessment Objectives — AO1 (develop), AO2 (refine), AO3 (record), AO4 (present)
  • Show the FULL creative journey — from initial research to final outcome
  • Annotate your work using specialist vocabulary (not just "I like it")
  • Draw from primary sources wherever possible — real objects, not photos
  • Experiment with a RANGE of media and techniques before selecting
  • Make clear connections between artist research and your own developing work

📝 Exam Technique

Graphic Communication Exam Tips:
Show the FULL JOURNEY — research, thumbnails, roughs, development, final. Annotate at every stage. Explain design DECISIONS — why this typeface, why this colour, why this layout? Show ITERATION.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes with this topic. Wrong: Start designing immediately — research wastes time. Correct: AQA AO1 requires you to DEVELOP ideas through INVESTIGATIONS informed by sources. Without research, your work lacks the contextual understanding that earns marks. Research into existing designs, target audience and context is ESSENTIAL.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Respond to a design brief for a music festival poster. Show your complete design process from initial research and mood boards through idea generation to final design. Annotate each stage.

A grade 9 response will: demonstrate thorough research into the brief, audience and context; generate multiple ideas before selecting; show systematic development and refinement; annotate every decision with clear rationale; reference graphic designers and existing designs.

Mark scheme: AO1 (develop through investigation) + AO2 (refine through experimentation) + AO3 (record ideas and observations) + AO4 (present personal response) = total marks

Detailed Notes

The Design Process: Step by Step

The design process follows a structured sequence: brief analysis, research, specification, ideation, development, modelling, testing, and final outcome. Each stage builds on the previous one. Professional designers iterate between stages rather than following a rigid linear path. The double diamond model (Discover, Define, Develop, Deliver) is widely used in UK design studios.

Example

Dyson engineers went through 5,127 prototypes over five years when developing their first vacuum cleaner. Each iteration refined the cyclone technology — demonstrating that the design process is cyclical, not linear.

Writing a Design Brief and Specification

A design brief outlines the problem to solve, the target user, and key constraints (budget, materials, time). A specification translates the brief into measurable criteria: dimensions, materials, ergonomics, environmental requirements, and regulatory standards. The specification becomes the benchmark against which the final design is evaluated.

Example

A GCSE student designing a point-of-sale display for a fairtrade chocolate brand would write a specification including: must be A3 size, use recycled card, display 6 products, cost under £5 to produce, and use the brand colours.

Mind Mapping and Ideation Techniques

Effective ideation uses techniques like mind mapping (radiant thinking from a central concept), SCAMPER (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other use, Eliminate, Reverse), morphological analysis (combining design features in a grid), and thumbnail sketching. Quantity of ideas matters more than quality at the initial stage — avoid self-censoring.

Example

When designing a sustainable packaging solution, a student might use SCAMPER: Substitute plastic with biodegradable film, Combine the box with a carrying handle, Adapt Japanese origami folds to create structure without glue, Eliminate the plastic window entirely.

Extended Practice

Q: Explain why iteration is important in the design process, using a specific example.

Q: Evaluate the importance of writing a detailed specification before beginning to design.

Extended Answers

  1. Iteration allows designers to test and refine ideas through repeated cycles rather than getting the design right first time. For example, when James Dyson developed his vacuum cleaner, he built over 5,000 prototypes because each test revealed issues — suction loss, filter clogging, poor ergonomics — that required design modifications. Without iteration, these problems would remain unsolved. In GCSE projects, iteration means testing a prototype, identifying weaknesses (e.g. a joint that is too weak), redesigning that aspect, and retesting. This cycle continues until the design meets the specification. Iteration improves functionality, aesthetics, and user experience.
  2. A specification provides clear, measurable criteria that guide design decisions and provide a framework for evaluation. Without one, designers may drift from the original purpose or forget key requirements. For instance, if a specification does not mention that a product must be water-resistant, the designer might choose an absorbent material, leading to failure. However, an overly rigid specification can stifle creativity — if criteria are too specific early on, designers may miss innovative solutions. The best approach is a specification that defines essential requirements (must be under 200g, must use recycled materials) while leaving room for creative interpretation in other areas (aesthetics, form, surface finish).
