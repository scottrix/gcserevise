Graphic Communication: Typography and Lettering

Anatomy of type (baseline, x-height, ascenders, descenders); serif, sans-serif, script, display fonts; hierarchy and readability; hand lettering; logo type; combining type and image.

Key Fact: Type anatomy: baseline, x-height, ascenders, descenders, cap height — understanding these helps you choose and use type effectively.
Key Fact: Serif fonts have decorative strokes; sans-serif are cleaner and more modern; script fonts suggest elegance or informality.
Key Fact: Typographic hierarchy uses size, weight, colour and position to guide the reader through information in order of importance.
Key Fact: Kerning (space between letters), tracking (overall letter spacing) and leading (line spacing) dramatically affect readability.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Typography and Lettering, you must know:

  • Topic-specific techniques, materials and processes
  • How to experiment with and refine approaches
  • How to record ideas and observations visually and through annotation
  • How to develop personal responses informed by contextual research

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Create a series of experimental studies exploring typography and lettering. Annotate your process.

Q2: Research an artist or designer who works with typography and lettering. Write a critical analysis of their approach and explain how it has informed your own work.

Q3: Evaluate the effectiveness of your experiments with typography and lettering. Which approaches were most successful and why?

✅ Answers

  1. A range of experimental studies showing different approaches to typography and lettering, each annotated with media, technique and purpose. Show development from initial experiments to refined outcomes.
  2. A critical analysis naming a specific artist/designer, using specialist vocabulary, explaining their context and approach, and making clear connections to personal work.
  3. A reflective evaluation identifying successful and less successful experiments, explaining WHY using technical and conceptual language, and showing how evaluation led to further refinement.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Address ALL four Assessment Objectives — AO1 (develop), AO2 (refine), AO3 (record), AO4 (present)
  • Show the FULL creative journey — from initial research to final outcome
  • Annotate your work using specialist vocabulary (not just "I like it")
  • Draw from primary sources wherever possible — real objects, not photos
  • Experiment with a RANGE of media and techniques before selecting
  • Make clear connections between artist research and your own developing work

📝 Exam Technique

Graphic Communication Exam Tips:
Show FONT EXPERIMENTS — compare different typefaces for the same brief. Demonstrate understanding of hierarchy through varied weight, size and position. Annotate with type vocabulary.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes with this topic. Wrong: Using lots of different fonts makes a design more interesting and creative. Correct: Good typography uses a LIMITED palette of 1-2 font families. Too many fonts create visual chaos and look unprofessional. Create variety through WEIGHT, SIZE and CASE within one family instead.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Design a typographic poster using only letterforms (no images). Use typography to communicate a specific message, mood or theme. Annotate explaining your font choices, hierarchy and layout decisions.

A grade 9 response will: demonstrate understanding of type anatomy and classification; use typographic hierarchy effectively; show experimentation with type as image; annotate with specialist vocabulary (kerning, leading, tracking, x-height).

Mark scheme: AO1 (develop through investigation) + AO2 (refine through experimentation) + AO3 (record ideas and observations) + AO4 (present personal response) = total marks

Detailed Notes

Typeface Classifications

Typefaces are classified into broad families: Serif (Times New Roman, Garamond) have decorative strokes at letter ends; Sans-serif (Helvetica, Arial, Calibri) are clean without serifs; Display/Decorative (Impact, Lobster) are designed for headlines; Script (Brush Script) mimic handwriting; Monospace (Courier) have uniform character widths. Each classification communicates a different tone — serif suggests tradition and authority, sans-serif suggests modernity and clarity.

Example

The BBC uses Gill Sans (a humanist sans-serif) for its logo and branding, conveying trustworthiness and British heritage. Tech startups like Spotify use circular sans-serif typefaces to feel modern and approachable.

Typography Hierarchy and Readability

Typographic hierarchy uses size, weight, colour and position to guide the reader through content in order of importance. A typical hierarchy uses: H1 (large, bold, 24-36pt for titles), H2 (medium, semi-bold, 18-24pt for subtitles), body text (regular, 10-12pt for reading). Readability is affected by line length (45-75 characters optimal), line height (1.2-1.5x font size), letter spacing, and contrast with background.

Example

A concert poster might use a 72pt bold display font for the band name, 24pt regular for the venue and date, and 14pt light for supporting acts. This hierarchy means viewers first see the most important information from a distance.

Kerning, Tracking and Leading

Kerning adjusts space between specific character pairs (e.g. reducing space between A and V which naturally appear too far apart). Tracking adjusts spacing uniformly across all characters in a text block. Leading (line spacing) is the vertical distance between baselines of consecutive lines. All three affect readability and aesthetic quality — tight tracking can look modern but becomes hard to read at small sizes.

Example

The word "WAVE" has natural gaps between W-A and A-V. Reducing kerning between these pairs creates visual cohesion. Apple uses tight tracking in their marketing to create a sleek, compact look, while a children's book would use generous leading (1.5x or more) for easy reading.

Extended Practice

Q: Explain how typographic hierarchy helps a reader navigate a page of information.

Q: A designer is creating a poster for a heavy metal music festival. Justify their choice of typeface, kerning and colour.

Extended Answers

  1. Typographic hierarchy uses visual cues — size, weight, colour, and position — to signal which information is most important and should be read first. A large bold heading immediately draws the eye, while smaller lighter text is read afterwards. This prevents readers from having to scan an entire page to find key information. For example, on a magazine cover, the main headline is the largest text, secondary stories are medium-sized, and the barcode area is smallest. Without hierarchy, all text appears equally important, making the page confusing and difficult to navigate. Good hierarchy improves readability and user experience.
  2. A heavy metal festival poster requires aggressive, impactful typography. A display or distorted typeface (e.g. a grunge or gothic font) communicates the genre's intensity and raw energy. Tight kerning and tracking create a dense, powerful look — letters pressed together suggest volume and force. Colour choices would include black, red, and silver — colours associated with danger, power, and metal. High contrast (white or red text on black) ensures readability at a distance. Sans-serif or distorted sans-serif typefaces work better than serif, which would look too formal and classical for this genre. The overall effect should feel bold and intimidating, matching the music style.
