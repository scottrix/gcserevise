GC5: Packaging and Print Design

Graphic Communication: Packaging and Print Design

Net design and die-cutting; materials and sustainability; point-of-sale display; magazine and book layout; poster design; information graphics; print processes (offset, digital, screen).

Key Fact: Packaging nets are 2D templates that fold into 3D containers — understanding tabs, glue flaps and die-cuts is essential.
Key Fact: Sustainable packaging considers material choice, minimal waste, recyclability and environmental impact.
Key Fact: Print processes differ: offset litho for high-volume, digital for short runs and variable data, screen for speciality finishes.
Key Fact: Information graphics use visual design to communicate data clearly — charts, diagrams, maps and infographics.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Create a series of experimental studies exploring packaging and print design. Annotate your process.

Q2: Research an artist or designer who works with packaging and print design. Write a critical analysis of their approach and explain how it has informed your own work.

Q3: Evaluate the effectiveness of your experiments with packaging and print design. Which approaches were most successful and why?

✅ Answers

  1. A range of experimental studies showing different approaches to packaging and print design, each annotated with media, technique and purpose. Show development from initial experiments to refined outcomes.
  2. A critical analysis naming a specific artist/designer, using specialist vocabulary, explaining their context and approach, and making clear connections to personal work.
  3. A reflective evaluation identifying successful and less successful experiments, explaining WHY using technical and conceptual language, and showing how evaluation led to further refinement.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Address ALL four Assessment Objectives — AO1 (develop), AO2 (refine), AO3 (record), AO4 (present)
  • Show the FULL creative journey — from initial research to final outcome
  • Annotate your work using specialist vocabulary (not just "I like it")
  • Draw from primary sources wherever possible — real objects, not photos
  • Experiment with a RANGE of media and techniques before selecting
  • Make clear connections between artist research and your own developing work

📝 Exam Technique

Graphic Communication Exam Tips:
Show the FLAT NET and the ASSEMBLED package. Test that your net actually folds — make a prototype. Consider sustainability — annotate material choices. Include all required information (barcode, contents, warnings).

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes with this topic. Wrong: Packaging design is just about making the box look attractive. Correct: Packaging design must consider: PROTECTION (does it keep the product safe?), FUNCTION (does it open/close properly?), SUSTAINABILITY (materials, waste, recyclability), LEGISLATION (barcodes, ingredients, safety warnings), and aesthetics.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Design packaging for a product. Create the net, consider materials and sustainability, and design the surface graphics. Build a prototype and annotate your design decisions.

A grade 9 response will: design a functional net that folds correctly; choose appropriate materials with sustainability considered; create effective surface graphics with clear information hierarchy; build a working prototype.

Mark scheme: AO1 (develop through investigation) + AO2 (refine through experimentation) + AO3 (record ideas and observations) + AO4 (present personal response) = total marks

Detailed Notes

Functions of Packaging

Packaging serves four key functions: Protection (keeping the product safe during transport and storage), Containment (holding the product securely), Information (displaying legal requirements, ingredients, barcodes, and instructions), and Promotion (attracting customers at point of sale). Good packaging balances all four — a beautiful box that fails to protect the product is useless, and strong protection that hides the brand wastes a marketing opportunity.

Example

Toblerone's triangular packaging is iconic and functional: the shape protects the chocolate segments, the distinctive form stands out on shelves (promotion), the box contains the product neatly, and the packaging displays brand information and ingredients. The shape is so distinctive it has been trademarked.

Print Processes and Finishes

Lithography (litho) prints high-quality, consistent colour in large quantities — ideal for magazines and packaging. Digital printing is cost-effective for short runs and personalisation. Screen printing suits thick inks on textiles and posters. Foil blocking applies metallic foil using heat and pressure for premium finishes. Embossing/debossing creates raised or recessed designs. Spot UV varnish highlights specific areas with gloss against a matt background. Each finish adds cost but increases perceived value.

Example

A luxury chocolate box might use litho printing for the main design, foil blocking for the brand name in gold, embossing for a raised logo, and spot UV on the product image to make it shine against the matt background. These finishes together create a premium feel that justifies a higher price point.

Sustainable Packaging Design

Sustainable packaging minimises environmental impact through: using recycled or recyclable materials (card, paper, glass, aluminium), reducing material quantity (lightweighting), eliminating unnecessary layers (minimal packaging), using vegetable-based inks instead of petroleum-based, and designing for disassembly (easy separation of material types for recycling). The UK Packaging Regulations require businesses to meet recycling targets.

Example

Lush Cosmetics uses "naked" packaging (solid shampoo bars with no packaging at all), recycled black pots for creams, and paper bags made from recycled paper. When packaging is necessary, they use biodegradable materials like popcorn instead of polystyrene packing peanuts for shipping.

Extended Practice

Q: Explain why a designer must consider all four functions of packaging when developing a new product container.

Q: Evaluate the environmental trade-offs between using glass and plastic for a drinks bottle packaging.

Extended Answers

  1. All four functions — protection, containment, information, promotion — must work together. If a designer focuses only on promotion (attractive graphics) but ignores protection, the product may arrive damaged, leading to returns and lost trust. If protection is prioritised without promotion, the product may not attract buyers on the shelf. Information must meet legal requirements (ingredients, weight, barcode, recycling symbols) — missing these can result in the product being refused by retailers. Containment ensures the product does not leak, spill or fall out. For example, a perfume bottle needs an attractive box (promotion), secure internal moulding (protection and containment), and regulatory text including volume, ingredients, and recycling symbols (information). Neglecting any function results in a failed product launch.
  2. Glass is infinitely recyclable without quality loss, inert (no chemical leaching), and perceived as premium — supporting higher pricing. However, glass is heavy (increasing transport emissions and costs), fragile (breakage risk), and energy-intensive to manufacture and recycle (requiring very high temperatures). Plastic (PET) is lightweight (lower transport emissions), shatterproof, and cheaper to produce. However, plastic degrades with each recycling cycle, can leach chemicals, and much of it ends up in landfill or ocean pollution. Even "recyclable" PET is only recycled around 20-30% of the time in the UK. A balanced solution might use recycled glass for premium products (where weight is less critical and price premium covers costs) and recycled PET with clear recycling instructions for mass-market drinks, while investing in closed-loop systems that ensure bottles are actually collected and reprocessed.
