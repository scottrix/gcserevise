Textile Design: Knitting Weaving and Crochet

Casting on/off; knit and purl stitches; colour work (Fair Isle, intarsia); tension and gauge; basic weaving (tabby, twill); tapestry weaving; crochet stitches; combining techniques for textural effects.

Key Fact: Knitting and crochet create fabric from yarn through interlocking loops — knit/purl for knitting; chain, double crochet, treble for crochet.
Key Fact: Tension/gauge determines fabric density — always make a tension square before starting a project to ensure correct sizing.
Key Fact: Weaving interlaces warp (lengthwise) and weft (crosswise) threads — tabby (plain) weave and twill weave are fundamental structures.
Key Fact: Colour work techniques (Fair Isle, intarsia) create patterns within knitted fabric through colour changes.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Create a series of experimental studies exploring knitting weaving and crochet. Annotate your process.

Q2: Research an artist or designer who works with knitting weaving and crochet. Write a critical analysis of their approach and explain how it has informed your own work.

Q3: Evaluate the effectiveness of your experiments with knitting weaving and crochet. Which approaches were most successful and why?

✅ Answers

  1. A range of experimental studies showing different approaches to knitting weaving and crochet, each annotated with media, technique and purpose. Show development from initial experiments to refined outcomes.
  2. A critical analysis naming a specific artist/designer, using specialist vocabulary, explaining their context and approach, and making clear connections to personal work.
  3. A reflective evaluation identifying successful and less successful experiments, explaining WHY using technical and conceptual language, and showing how evaluation led to further refinement.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Address ALL four Assessment Objectives — AO1 (develop), AO2 (refine), AO3 (record), AO4 (present)
  • Show the FULL creative journey — from initial research to final outcome
  • Annotate your work using specialist vocabulary (not just "I like it")
  • Draw from primary sources wherever possible — real objects, not photos
  • Experiment with a RANGE of media and techniques before selecting
  • Make clear connections between artist research and your own developing work

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes with this topic. Wrong: Knitting and crochet are the same thing — just different names for looping yarn. Correct: Knitting uses TWO needles to create fabric from multiple live stitches; crochet uses ONE hook to complete each stitch before moving on. Knitted fabric stretches and drapes; crocheted fabric is thicker and more structured. They produce distinctly different textile qualities.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Create samples using at least two construction techniques (knitting, weaving, crochet). Annotate each sample explaining the technique, its properties and how it could be developed in your project.

A grade 9 response will: demonstrate competent use of at least two construction techniques; show understanding of each technique's unique properties; annotate with precise specialist vocabulary (warp, weft, gauge, intarsia); evaluate which technique best serves their intention.

Mark scheme: AO1 (develop through investigation) + AO2 (refine through experimentation) + AO3 (record ideas and observations) + AO4 (present personal response) = total marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Art and Design has four equally-weighted Assessment Objectives: AO1 Develop ideas through investigations informed by contextual and other sources, demonstrating analytical and cultural understanding (25%); AO2 Refine ideas through experimenting and selecting appropriate resources, media, materials, techniques and processes (25%); AO3 Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions in visual and other forms (25%); AO4 Present a personal, informed and meaningful response demonstrating critical understanding, realising intentions and making connections (25%). For grade 9, you must show exceptional independence, sustained investigation, sophisticated understanding of sources, confident and purposeful experimentation, perceptive recording, and a highly personal, coherent response that makes illuminating connections.
