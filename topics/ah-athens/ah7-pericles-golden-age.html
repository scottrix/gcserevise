Pericles & the Golden Age of Athens

Pericles' leadership, the Athenian building programme, cultural achievements, and the tensions that led to the Peloponnesian War.

Key Fact: Pericles dominated Athenian politics for over 30 years (c. 461-429 BC), elected strategos repeatedly
Key Fact: The Athenian building programme: Parthenon, Propylaea, Erechtheion on the Acropolis, funded by Delian League tribute
Key Fact: Pericles' Funeral Oration (431 BC, recorded by Thucydides) celebrated Athenian democracy, freedom and cultural superiority
Key Fact: The Peloponnesian War (431-404 BC) between Athens and Sparta devastated Greece and ended the Athenian Golden Age
Key Fact: The plague of Athens (430 BC) killed a third of the population, including Pericles himself in 429 BC
Key Fact: Thucydides is our main source: he wrote a contemporary history of the Peloponnesian War, valued for its accuracy and analysis

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Pericles & the Golden Age of Athens, you must know:

  • Strategos: A general in Athens; one of ten elected annually; the only office that could be held repeatedly, enabling Pericles' long dominance
  • Funeral Oration: Pericles' famous speech honouring Athenian war dead in 431 BC, recorded by Thucydides, celebrating Athenian democracy and values
  • Parthenon: The great temple to Athena on the Acropolis, built 447-432 BC under Pericles; symbol of Athenian power and culture
  • Peloponnesian War: The war between Athens (and its empire) and Sparta (and its allies) from 431-404 BC, ending in Athenian defeat
  • Thucydides: Athenian historian who wrote a contemporary account of the Peloponnesian War, considered one of the first scientific historians

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How did Pericles maintain his political influence for so long?

Q: Why was the building programme controversial?

Q: What did Pericles' Funeral Oration celebrate?

Q: What caused the Peloponnesian War?

Q: How did the plague affect Athens?

✅ Answers

  1. Pericles was repeatedly elected strategos (general), one of the few offices that could be held multiple times. He used his authority and oratory to persuade the assembly, introduced state pay for office-holders (attracting popular support), and used Delian League tribute to fund public building that provided jobs and beautified Athens.
  2. Pericles used Delian League tribute (intended for defence against Persia) to fund magnificent buildings on the Acropolis, including the Parthenon. Allies resented their money being spent on Athenian glorification rather than collective security. Plutarch records that opponents accused Pericles of adorning Athens like a vain woman with jewels bought with other people's money.
  3. In 431 BC, Pericles honoured fallen soldiers by celebrating Athens itself: its democracy (power in the hands of the many), freedom, openness, cultural achievements and the willingness of citizens to die for their city. Thucydides presents it as an idealised vision of Athenian values.
  4. Growing tension between Athens' expanding empire and Sparta's fear of Athenian power. Athens' imperialism threatened Spartan allies, and trade disputes with Corinth (Sparta's ally) escalated. The immediate cause was Athenian interference in a Corinthian colony (Corcyra) and the Megarian decree restricting trade. Sparta declared war to 'free the Greeks' from Athenian tyranny.
  5. The plague struck Athens in 430 BC when the rural population was crowded within the city walls. It killed about a third of the population, caused social breakdown (lawlessness, despair) and undermined Pericles' strategy. Pericles himself died of the plague in 429 BC.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Pericles = strategos repeatedly elected, not a tyrant or king
  • Building programme = funded by tribute, controversial with allies
  • Funeral Oration = Thucydides' version, idealised, not a verbatim transcript
  • Peloponnesian War: Spartan fear of Athens + Athenian imperialism

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Pericles was a king or tyrant ✓ Pericles was an elected strategos who dominated Athenian politics through persuasion in the assembly. He had no constitutional authority beyond any other general; his power came from his oratory, reputation and repeated election

✗ The Funeral Oration is Pericles' exact words ✓ Thucydides wrote the speech decades later and admitted he composed speeches that expressed what was appropriate for the occasion. It represents Thucydides' interpretation of Pericles' views and Athenian ideals, not a verbatim record

✗ The plague caused Athens to lose the Peloponnesian War ✓ The plague was devastating but Athens continued fighting for another 25 years after it. The war was ultimately lost due to strategic mistakes (the Sicilian Expedition) and Persian support for Sparta

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe Pericles' leadership of Athens and explain why the Peloponnesian War began. [6 marks]

Pericles dominated Athenian politics for over 30 years (c. 461-429 BC) through repeated election as strategos. He extended democracy by introducing pay for public office and jury service, enabling poorer citizens to participate. He used Delian League tribute to fund an ambitious building programme on the Acropolis, including the Parthenon (447-432 BC), which employed citizens, beautified Athens and demonstrated its power. His Funeral Oration (431 BC) celebrated Athenian democracy, openness and civic values. The Peloponnesian War (431-404 BC) resulted from escalating tensions between Athens and Sparta. Athens' growing empire alarmed Sparta and its allies, particularly Corinth, whose commercial interests were threatened by Athenian expansion. The immediate causes included Athenian intervention in a dispute between Corinth and its colony Corcyra (433 BC), and the Megarian decree that excluded Megara (a Spartan ally) from Athenian markets and ports. Corinth pressured Sparta to act, arguing that Athenian power would only grow if unchecked. Sparta declared war in 431 BC, claiming to 'free the Greeks' from Athenian imperialism. Thucydides identified the truest cause as Spartan fear of growing Athenian power. Pericles' strategy was to abandon the countryside and shelter behind Athens' walls, relying on naval supremacy and tribute to outlast Sparta. However, the plague (430 BC) devastated the crowded city and killed Pericles himself in 429 BC.
