Greek Philosophy & Thought

The development of Greek philosophy, the Sophists, Socrates, and the intellectual life of Classical Athens.

Key Fact: Greek philosophy began with questioning the nature of the universe; early philosophers sought natural rather than divine explanations
Key Fact: The Sophists were professional teachers who travelled offering instruction in rhetoric and persuasion for pay; they claimed to make the weaker argument appear stronger
Key Fact: Socrates (469-399 BC) challenged assumptions through questioning (the Socratic method/dialectic); he sought truth rather than persuasion
Key Fact: Socrates was tried and executed (399 BC) for impiety and corrupting the youth; his trial illustrates the tension between free thought and Athenian democracy
Key Fact: Plato (Socrates' student) founded the Academy and wrote dialogues preserving Socratic ideas while developing his own philosophy
Key Fact: Philosophy and intellectual debate were central to Athenian cultural life and attracted thinkers from across the Greek world

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Greek Philosophy & Thought, you must know:

  • Sophists: Professional teachers of rhetoric and persuasion in 5th-century Athens who charged fees; criticised by Socrates and Plato for teaching persuasion over truth
  • Socratic method: A form of philosophical inquiry using questions and answers to expose contradictions in someone's beliefs and lead them toward truth
  • Dialectic: The art of investigating or discussing the truth of opinions through logical argument; central to Socratic philosophy
  • Academy: The school of philosophy founded by Plato in Athens (c. 387 BC); the first institution of higher learning in the Western world
  • Apology: Plato's account of Socrates' defence speech at his trial; the name means 'defence' not an expression of regret

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How did Socrates differ from the Sophists?

Q: What was the Socratic method?

Q: Why was Socrates put on trial?

Q: What was Plato's contribution to philosophy?

Q: Why was Athens a centre for philosophy and intellectual life?

✅ Answers

  1. Socrates sought truth through questioning (dialectic) and did not charge fees. The Sophists taught rhetoric and persuasion for pay, claiming to make the weaker argument appear stronger. Socrates criticised the Sophists for teaching persuasion without concern for truth or morality.
  2. Socrates asked a series of probing questions that exposed contradictions in his interlocutor's beliefs. By showing that confident claims were actually ill-founded, he aimed to lead people toward genuine knowledge. He claimed to know only that he knew nothing (Socratic ignorance).
  3. In 399 BC, Socrates was charged with impiety (not recognising the city's gods) and corrupting the youth. His questioning had embarrassed powerful Athenians, and he was associated (unfairly) with Critias, a leader of the brutal oligarchy of the Thirty Tyrants. His trial reflected anxiety about threats to democratic values after the trauma of the Peloponnesian War.
  4. Plato preserved Socratic ideas through his dialogues and developed original philosophy including the Theory of Forms (that perfect, eternal ideas exist beyond the physical world). He founded the Academy, the first institution of higher learning in the Western world, and wrote influential works including the Republic on justice and ideal government.
  5. Athens' democracy encouraged open debate and free speech (parrhesia). Its wealth, cosmopolitan population and cultural confidence attracted thinkers from across the Greek world. The agora and symposia provided venues for discussion. Athenian imperialism brought wealth that supported leisure for philosophical activity.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Socrates vs Sophists: truth vs persuasion, free vs paid
  • Socratic method = questions that expose contradictions
  • Trial of Socrates: impiety + corrupting youth; really about politics
  • Plato's dialogues are our main source for Socrates

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Socrates wrote philosophical books ✓ Socrates wrote nothing himself. Our knowledge of him comes entirely from the writings of his students, especially Plato and Xenophon. This raises the 'Socratic problem' — we cannot be certain which ideas are genuinely Socratic and which are Plato's

✗ Socrates was executed for being a philosopher ✓ Socrates was formally charged with impiety and corrupting the youth. The real reasons were political: his questioning undermined traditional authority, and he was associated with critics of democracy after the Peloponnesian War

✗ The Sophists were all dishonest frauds ✓ While Plato portrays the Sophists negatively, they were genuine intellectuals who made important contributions to rhetoric, grammar and ethics. Protagoras, Gorgias and others were respected thinkers; Plato's criticism reflects a philosophical disagreement, not objective truth

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe the development of Greek philosophy in Athens and explain why Socrates was executed. [6 marks]

Greek philosophy emerged from questioning traditional explanations of the world. Early philosophers sought natural rather than divine causes for phenomena. In 5th-century Athens, the Sophists were professional teachers who offered instruction in rhetoric and persuasion for fees, claiming they could make the weaker argument appear stronger. They were popular with ambitious young Athenians but criticised for prioritising persuasion over truth. Socrates (469-399 BC) fundamentally challenged both the Sophists and conventional thinking. Through his dialectical method of persistent questioning, he exposed contradictions in people's beliefs and aimed to lead them toward genuine knowledge. Famously claiming to know only that he knew nothing, he argued that the unexamined life was not worth living. He did not charge fees and attracted a devoted following of young men. Socrates was executed in 399 BC, charged with impiety (not recognising the city's gods and introducing new deities) and corrupting the youth. The formal charges masked deeper political tensions: his relentless questioning had embarrassed powerful Athenians, his associations with Alcibiades and Critias (who had betrayed democracy) made him suspect, and Athens was still traumatised by its defeat in the Peloponnesian War and the rule of the Thirty Tyrants. Many Athenians saw his criticism of democracy and traditional values as dangerous. Plato, his most famous student, preserved Socratic ideas in dialogues and founded the Academy, ensuring Socrates' influence on Western philosophy despite his execution.
