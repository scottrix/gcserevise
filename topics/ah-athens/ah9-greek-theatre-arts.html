Greek Theatre & the Arts

Greek drama, architecture, sculpture and pottery as expressions of Athenian cultural achievement and civic identity.

Key Fact: Greek drama developed from religious festivals honouring Dionysus; tragedies and comedies were performed at the City Dionysia festival in Athens
Key Fact: Three great tragedians: Aeschylus (introduced second actor), Sophocles (introduced third actor, painted scenery), Euripides (questioned traditional values)
Key Fact: Aristophanes wrote Old Comedy that satirised contemporary politicians and issues (e.g. The Wasps, The Clouds)
Key Fact: Theatre was a civic institution: the state funded productions, citizens attended as a duty, and wealthy choregoi (producers) sponsored choruses as a liturgy
Key Fact: Greek architecture developed the Doric, Ionic and Corinthian orders; the Parthenon combined Doric and Ionic elements
Key Fact: Red-figure pottery (c. 530 BC onwards) allowed detailed painted scenes showing mythology, daily life and athletic contests

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Greek Theatre & the Arts, you must know:

  • City Dionysia: Athens' major annual festival honouring Dionysus, featuring dramatic competitions for tragedies and comedies
  • Choregos: A wealthy Athenian citizen who financed a chorus for a dramatic production as a civic duty (liturgy)
  • Skene: The building behind the orchestra in a Greek theatre, used as a backdrop and for costume changes
  • Orchestra: The circular dancing area in front of the skene where the chorus performed in a Greek theatre
  • Doric order: The simplest and sturdiest Greek architectural style, with plain capitals and no base on the columns

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How was Greek drama connected to religion and civic life?

Q: What were the contributions of the three great tragedians?

Q: How did Aristophanes use comedy for political commentary?

Q: What made the Parthenon architecturally significant?

Q: What was the role of the choregos in Greek theatre?

✅ Answers

  1. Drama developed from choral hymns to Dionysus at the City Dionysia festival. The state organised dramatic competitions, citizens were expected to attend, and wealthy choregoi funded productions as a civic liturgy. Plays explored moral and political themes relevant to the polis, making theatre a form of public debate.
  2. Aeschylus introduced the second actor, enabling dialogue and dramatic conflict (Persae, Oresteia). Sophocles added the third actor, painted scenery and larger choruses (Oedipus Rex, Antigone). Euripides questioned traditional values and portrayed complex, realistic characters (Medea, The Bacchae).
  3. Aristophanes wrote Old Comedy that directly satirised living politicians, generals and intellectuals. The Clouds mocked Socrates, The Wasps satirised jury-mad Athenians, and Lysistrata showed women ending war through a sex strike. Comedy was politically bold because of Athenian free speech (parrhesia).
  4. Built 447-432 BC under Pericles, the Parthenon combined Doric and Ionic elements, used optical refinements (slight curves to counteract visual distortion), housed a massive gold-and-ivory statue of Athena, and displayed sculptural friezes celebrating Athenian mythology and civic pride. It symbolised Athenian power and cultural supremacy.
  5. A choregos was a wealthy citizen assigned to finance a play's chorus as a liturgy (civic duty). This was expensive but brought prestige. The choregos paid for costumes, training and maintenance of the chorus. The best-funded production won a prize, creating competition among the elite.

  • Three tragedians: Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides — know their innovations
  • Aristophanes = Old Comedy, satirised living people
  • Parthenon = symbol of Athenian power and cultural achievement

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Greek theatre was purely entertainment ✓ Theatre was a religious and civic institution. Plays were performed at a religious festival, funded by the state and wealthy citizens, attended by citizens as a civic duty, and explored moral and political themes relevant to the polis

✗ Women acted in Greek plays ✓ All actors in Greek drama were male. Men played female roles wearing masks. Women may have been in the audience but this is debated by scholars

✗ Greek plays had realistic sets and scenery ✓ Greek theatre used minimal scenery: the skene provided a fixed backdrop, and actors wore masks and exaggerated costumes. The focus was on language, chorus and dramatic action rather than visual realism

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe the main features of Greek theatre and explain its importance in Athenian society. [6 marks]

Greek theatre developed from religious festivals honouring Dionysus. The City Dionysia was Athens' major dramatic festival, where tragedians and comic playwrights competed for prizes before an audience of thousands in the Theatre of Dionysus on the Acropolis's south slope. Three great tragedians defined the genre: Aeschylus introduced the second actor (enabling dialogue), Sophocles added the third actor and painted scenery, and Euripides questioned traditional values with psychologically complex characters. Aristophanes wrote Old Comedy satirising living politicians and contemporary issues. Theatre was fundamentally a civic institution. The state organised competitions, wealthy choregoi funded choruses as a liturgy (civic duty), and citizens attended as a public obligation. Admission was subsidised by the state (theoric fund). All actors were male and wore masks. The chorus commented on the action and represented collective opinion. Plays explored themes central to Athenian identity: justice, democracy, the relationship between individual and state, and the power of the gods. Tragedies like Sophocles' Antigone raised questions about law, conscience and authority directly relevant to democratic debate. Comedy's freedom to mock living leaders reflected Athenian parrhesia (free speech). Theatre thus served as a space for communal reflection on the values and challenges of democratic life, making it far more than mere entertainment.
