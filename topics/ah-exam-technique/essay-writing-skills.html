Essay Writing Skills

How to structure and write high-scoring essays for OCR J198 Ancient History

Key Fact: A good essay has a clear structure: introduction, 3-4 developed paragraphs, and a conclusion
Key Fact: The introduction should define key terms, show understanding of the question, and indicate your line of argument
Key Fact: Use the PEEL structure for paragraphs: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link back to the question
Key Fact: Each paragraph should focus on one main point that directly answers the question
Key Fact: Evidence must be specific — named people, events, dates, and sources score highest marks
Key Fact: Explanation means showing how your evidence supports your argument, not just listing facts
Key Fact: The conclusion must directly answer the question with a clear, justified judgement
Key Fact: For 'how far' essays, present both sides in your main body before reaching a judgement in the conclusion
Key Fact: Signposting language ('Furthermore', 'However', 'On the other hand') helps the examiner follow your argument
Key Fact: Always plan before writing — even 2 minutes of planning produces a more coherent essay
Key Fact: A Level 4 answer (top band) shows sustained analysis, precise evidence, and a convincing judgement
Key Fact: Time management: spend roughly 1 minute per mark as a guide

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Essay Writing Skills, you must know:

  • PEEL: Paragraph structure: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link — ensures each paragraph is focused and developed
  • Line of argument: The overall position or thesis that runs through your essay, connecting all your paragraphs
  • Judgement: A clear, reasoned decision about the question, supported by evidence, usually in the conclusion
  • Signposting: Using connecting words and phrases to guide the reader through your argument
  • Sustained analysis: Consistent and thorough examination of evidence throughout the essay, not just in one paragraph
  • Counter-argument: An opposing viewpoint that you acknowledge before explaining why your position is stronger

✅ Answers

  1. Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link. It ensures each paragraph has a clear focus, is supported by specific evidence, is developed through explanation, and connects back to the question.
  2. Definition of key terms, understanding of the question, and an indication of your line of argument.
  3. Because the conclusion is where you deliver your direct, justified judgement — without it, you cannot access the top mark band.
  4. A 'how far' essay requires presenting both sides of the argument before reaching a judgement, whereas an 'explain' essay focuses on giving reasons for something.
  5. Sustained analysis throughout, precise and specific evidence, and a convincing, well-justified judgement.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Spend 2-3 minutes planning your essay structure before writing — it saves time overall
  • Start each paragraph with a clear topic sentence that directly relates to the question
  • Use specific named evidence: 'In 490 BCE at Marathon...' rather than 'the Greeks won a battle'
  • In your conclusion, don't just repeat what you said — make a clear, definitive judgement
  • If you're running out of time, write a brief conclusion — it's better than none at all

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Ancient History Exam Tips — Essay Writing Skills:
1. For Essay Writing Skills, include specific factual detail: dates, names, events and statistics where relevant
2. For source questions, analyse provenance (who, when, why, audience) before using the content
3. In evaluation questions, discuss multiple factors and weigh their relative significance
4. Always link back to the question focus — don't just narrate what happened with Essay Writing Skills
5. Use phrases like 'the most significant factor was... because...' to show analytical judgement

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Writing a conclusion that just repeats the introduction ✓ The conclusion should deliver your final judgement, synthesising the arguments from your paragraphs, not merely restating the introduction

✗ Listing facts without explaining how they answer the question ✓ Every piece of evidence needs explanation — show how it supports your argument and links to the question

✗ Writing a one-sided argument for a 'how far' question ✓ Always present evidence for and against before reaching your judgement — balance is essential for 'how far' questions

✗ Starting to write immediately without planning ✓ Even a brief plan ensures your essay has a clear structure, line of argument and covers all required points

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How far was Spartan military strength the main reason for its dominance in the Peloponnese? (15 marks)

Spartan military strength was a significant reason for its dominance in the Peloponnese, but it was not the only factor — its political system and diplomatic strategies were also crucial. The Spartan agoge produced the most feared hoplite army in Greece. Their discipline at battles like Plataea in 479 BCE demonstrated overwhelming military capability, and this reputation alone deterred many rivals from challenging Spartan authority. The Peloponnesian League, however, was equally important. By binding neighbouring states into a network of alliances, Sparta secured loyalty without constant military action. States like Corinth and Megara contributed forces and paid tribute, extending Spartan influence far beyond its own resources. Furthermore, the Spartan political system provided remarkable stability. The dual kingship, ephors and gerousia created a balanced constitution that prevented the kind of internal strife seen in other Greek cities. This stability allowed consistent long-term foreign policy. Nevertheless, military strength underpinned both the League and the political system — without the threat of Spartan force, allies might have defected. Additionally, Sparta's treatment of the helots was a critical weakness: the constant fear of helot revolt meant Sparta could rarely send its full army abroad, limiting expansion. In conclusion, Spartan military strength was the foundation of its dominance but operated alongside political stability and diplomatic skill. Military power was necessary but not sufficient — it was the combination of all three factors that made Sparta the dominant Peloponnesian power.
