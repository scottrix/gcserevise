Period Study Technique

Specific techniques for the OCR J198 Period Study paper

Key Fact: The Period Study (Paper 1) is worth 50% of the GCSE and lasts 1 hour 30 minutes
Key Fact: It focuses on narrative, causation, and change and continuity across a broad chronological period
Key Fact: You must know the key events in chronological order — timeline knowledge is essential
Key Fact: Narrative questions ask you to tell the story of events — use chronological structure
Key Fact: Causation questions require you to explain why something happened, identifying multiple causes
Key Fact: Change and continuity questions ask you to identify what changed and what stayed the same over a period
Key Fact: For causation, distinguish between long-term and short-term causes, and identify triggers
Key Fact: The Period Study rewards breadth of knowledge — you need to cover the full period, not just one event
Key Fact: When writing a narrative, link events together with causal connectives: 'this led to', 'as a result', 'consequently'
Key Fact: Always support your points with specific dates, names and events
Key Fact: Practice writing timed essays — you have approximately 45 minutes for the extended question
Key Fact: The most common Period Study topics are: Persian Wars (Greece) or founding of Rome and growth of the Republic (Rome)

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Period Study Technique, you must know:

  • Period Study: Paper 1 of OCR J198, focusing on a broad chronological period — worth 50% of the GCSE
  • Causation: The reasons why an event happened — may include long-term, short-term and trigger causes
  • Change and continuity: Identifying what changed and what remained the same over a period of time
  • Narrative: A chronological account of events, linking causes and consequences together
  • Long-term cause: A cause that builds up over a long period, creating the conditions for an event
  • Trigger cause: The specific event that directly sets off the thing being studied — the spark

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What percentage of the GCSE is the Period Study worth?

Q: What is the difference between a long-term cause and a trigger cause?

Q: How should you structure a narrative answer?

Q: What does a 'change and continuity' question require you to do?

Q: Approximately how long should you spend on the extended question in the Period Study?

✅ Answers

  1. 50% — it is Paper 1 and is equally weighted with the Depth Study.
  2. A long-term cause builds up over time, creating the conditions for an event; a trigger cause is the specific event that directly sets it off.
  3. Chronologically, linking events together with causal connectives like 'this led to', 'as a result', and 'consequently'.
  4. Identify what changed and what stayed the same over the period being studied, supporting both with specific evidence.
  5. About 45 minutes, as it is worth the most marks and requires a sustained, developed response.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • For narrative questions, use dates as structural markers: 'In 490 BCE...', 'By 480 BCE...'
  • In causation answers, group causes into long-term and short-term, then identify which was most significant and why
  • For change and continuity, create a before-and-after comparison in your plan
  • Don't just write about one event in isolation — show how events connect across the period
  • Use the wording of the question to frame every paragraph — keep linking back to it

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Ancient History Exam Tips:
Use precise historical terminology in all answers. When analysing sources, always consider provenance (who wrote it, when, and why). For narrative questions, use chronological structure with specific dates and named individuals. For explanation questions, focus on causation and significance. For 'how far' questions, present a balanced argument with a clear judgement. OCR J198 rewards answers that demonstrate understanding of the ancient world through use of specific evidence from both ancient sources and modern interpretations.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Writing about events in random order in a narrative question ✓ Always use chronological order — the examiner needs to see you understand the sequence of events and how one led to another

✗ Only identifying one cause for a causation question ✓ Identify multiple causes (long-term and short-term) and explain how they combined to produce the outcome

✗ Only discussing change without continuity (or vice versa) ✓ A change and continuity question requires both — explicitly state what changed AND what stayed the same

✗ Focusing on one small part of the period instead of the whole period ✓ The Period Study tests breadth of knowledge — make sure your answer covers the full chronological range of the question

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Write a narrative account of the key events of the Persian invasion of 480-479 BCE. (10 marks)

In 480 BCE, King Xerxes launched a massive invasion of Greece, determined to avenge his father Darius's defeat at Marathon a decade earlier. Xerxes assembled the largest army and navy the ancient world had seen, crossing the Hellespont on a bridge of boats. The Greeks, led by Sparta, initially attempted to hold the Persians at the pass of Thermopylae. King Leonidas and 300 Spartans, along with several thousand other Greeks, held the narrow pass for three days in August 480 BCE, inflicting heavy casualties. However, a local Greek named Ephialtes betrayed a mountain path, allowing the Persians to outflank the defenders. Leonidas and his Spartans fought to the death. With Thermopylae lost, the Greek army retreated and the Persians sacked Athens, which had been evacuated. The decisive turning point came at the naval Battle of Salamis in September 480 BCE. The Athenian general Themistocles lured the Persian fleet into the narrow straits near Salamis, where the larger Persian ships could not manoeuvre effectively. The Greek triremes won a crushing victory, destroying much of the Persian fleet. Xerxes withdrew to Asia, leaving his general Mardonius with a land army in Greece. The final blow came at the Battle of Plataea in 479 BCE, where a combined Greek army under the Spartan Pausanias defeated Mardonius's forces decisively. Mardonius was killed and the Persian threat to Greece was effectively ended.
