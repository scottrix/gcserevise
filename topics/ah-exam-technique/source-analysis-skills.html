Source Analysis Skills

How to analyse ancient and modern sources effectively for OCR J198

Key Fact: Source analysis is a core skill in OCR J198, especially in the Depth Study paper
Key Fact: Always consider three elements: content (what it says), provenance (who wrote it, when, why), and context (what else was happening)
Key Fact: Ancient sources include writers like Herodotus, Thucydides, Livy, Polybius and Plutarch
Key Fact: Ancient sources may be biased, incomplete, or written long after events — always note their limitations
Key Fact: Modern historians provide interpretation and analysis but also have their own perspectives and biases
Key Fact: Utility means how useful a source is for answering a specific question — not whether it is true or reliable
Key Fact: A source can be useful even if biased — bias itself reveals attitudes and values
Key Fact: Cross-referencing sources strengthens arguments by showing where sources agree or disagree
Key Fact: When asked about a source's usefulness, consider both what it tells you and what it leaves out
Key Fact: Archaeological evidence (inscriptions, coins, pottery, buildings) complements literary sources
Key Fact: The Depth Study paper requires you to use sources as evidence to support arguments
Key Fact: Never accept a source at face value — always question its purpose and perspective

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Source Analysis Skills, you must know:

  • Provenance: The origin of a source — who created it, when, where, and for what purpose
  • Utility: How useful a source is for answering a particular historical question
  • Bias: A tendency to present information in a way that favours one perspective over others
  • Cross-referencing: Comparing different sources to see where they agree or disagree about an event
  • Primary source: A source created at or near the time of the events being studied
  • Secondary source: A source created later by someone who was not present at the events, often analysing primary evidence

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What three elements should you always consider when analysing a source?

Q: Why can a biased source still be useful?

Q: What is the difference between reliability and utility?

Q: Name two ancient historians relevant to OCR J198 Greek topics.

Q: Why should you never accept a source at face value?

✅ Answers

  1. Content (what it says), provenance (who wrote it, when, why), and context (what else was happening at the time).
  2. Because bias itself reveals attitudes, values and perspectives of the author or their society, which can be valuable evidence.
  3. Reliability concerns whether a source is accurate and trustworthy; utility concerns how useful it is for answering a specific question — a source can be useful even if unreliable.
  4. Herodotus (called the 'Father of History') and Thucydides (author of the History of the Peloponnesian War).
  5. Because every source has a purpose, perspective and potential bias that shapes what it includes, omits or emphasises.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Start source answers by identifying the source's provenance — who, when, why
  • Use the formula: 'This source is useful because... However, it is limited because...' for utility questions
  • Always support your points about a source with specific evidence from the source itself
  • For highest marks, cross-reference the source with your own knowledge
  • Remember that asking 'what does this source NOT tell me?' is just as important as what it does

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Ancient History Exam Tips — Source Analysis Skills:
1. For Source Analysis Skills, include specific factual detail: dates, names, events and statistics where relevant
2. For source questions, analyse provenance (who, when, why, audience) before using the content
3. In evaluation questions, discuss multiple factors and weigh their relative significance
4. Always link back to the question focus — don't just narrate what happened with Source Analysis Skills
5. Use phrases like 'the most significant factor was... because...' to show analytical judgement

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Saying a source is 'reliable' or 'unreliable' without explaining why ✓ Always justify your judgement with specific evidence from the provenance and content

✗ Confusing reliability with utility ✓ A source can be unreliable but still useful — utility depends on the question being asked, not just accuracy

✗ Only summarising what a source says without analysing it ✓ Description of content alone earns few marks — you must evaluate the source's provenance, purpose and usefulness

✗ Ignoring the provenance of ancient sources ✓ Always consider who wrote the source, their background, their purpose, and how long after the events they were writing

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How useful is Herodotus as a source for the Persian Wars? (12 marks)

Herodotus is useful as a source for the Persian Wars in several ways, but also has significant limitations. In terms of utility, Herodotus provides the most detailed surviving narrative of the Persian Wars — his Histories cover the Ionian Revolt, Marathon, Thermopylae, Salamis and Plataea in detail. He travelled widely and collected accounts from different peoples, giving both Greek and Persian perspectives. His work is the foundation of almost all modern knowledge of the wars. However, his provenance creates limitations. He wrote around 430 BCE, roughly 50-60 years after the events, meaning he relied on oral traditions that may have been exaggerated. He was Greek and clearly pro-Greek, describing the Persians in stereotypical terms. He sometimes includes clearly mythical elements alongside factual accounts. Furthermore, he aimed to entertain as well as inform, which may have led him to dramatise events. Despite these limitations, Herodotus remains invaluable because there are simply no comparable surviving accounts. A skilled historian can use his work critically, cross-referencing with archaeological evidence such as the Serpent Column and with later historians like Diodorus Siculus. Overall, Herodotus is essential but must be used with awareness of his biases and the gap between his writing and the events.
