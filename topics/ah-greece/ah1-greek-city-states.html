Greek City-States (Poleis)

The structure and organisation of Greek city-states, citizenship, and the role of the polis in Archaic Greek society.

Key Fact: A polis (city-state) was the fundamental political unit of ancient Greece, typically consisting of a city and its surrounding countryside
Key Fact: Each polis was self-governing with its own laws, customs, constitution and patron deity
Key Fact: Citizens (free adult males) had political rights and duties; women, slaves and metics (foreign residents) were excluded from citizenship
Key Fact: The acropolis (high city) was the religious and defensive centre; the agora was the marketplace and civic meeting place
Key Fact: Greek colonisation from the 8th century BC spread poleis across the Mediterranean and Black Sea
Key Fact: The two most powerful poleis were Athens (democratic) and Sparta (oligarchic/militaristic)

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Greek City-States (Poleis), you must know:

  • Polis: A Greek city-state; an independent self-governing community of citizens
  • Metic: A foreign resident in a Greek city-state who paid taxes but had no political rights
  • Hoplite: A heavily armed Greek infantry soldier who fought in a phalanx formation
  • Acropolis: The fortified hilltop area of a Greek city, containing temples and public buildings
  • Agora: The central public gathering place in a Greek city; used as a marketplace and for political assemblies

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What was a polis and why was it important in ancient Greece?

Q: Who was excluded from citizenship in a Greek polis?

Q: What were the acropolis and agora, and what were their functions?

Q: Why did the Greeks establish colonies across the Mediterranean?

Q: How did Athens and Sparta differ as poleis?

✅ Answers

  1. A polis was an independent city-state consisting of a city and its surrounding countryside. It was the fundamental political, social and religious unit of Greek life. Each polis had its own government, laws, army and patron deity. The concept of citizenship and civic duty centred on the polis.
  2. Women, slaves and metics (foreign residents) were excluded. Only free adult male citizens could participate in politics and hold office. Metics paid taxes and served in the military but had no political rights.
  3. The acropolis was the fortified hilltop, serving as a religious sanctuary (temples to patron gods) and defensive refuge. The agora was the central public space used as a marketplace, meeting place for political assemblies, and venue for social interaction.
  4. Overpopulation, land shortage and political conflict at home drove colonisation from the 8th century BC. Colonies provided agricultural land, trade opportunities and resources. Each colony remained culturally Greek but was politically independent from its mother city (metropolis).
  5. Athens developed democracy, valued education, arts and naval power, and had a large population including many metics. Sparta was an oligarchy focused on military training, had a small citizen body supported by helot labour, and valued discipline and conformity above individual freedom.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Polis is the key concept — always define it precisely
  • Citizenship = rights AND duties (military service, political participation)
  • Athens vs Sparta is a classic comparison question
  • Colonisation: driven by land shortage and overpopulation, not just trade

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ All residents of a polis were citizens ✓ Only free adult males whose parents were both citizens held full citizenship; women, slaves and metics were excluded from political life

✗ Greek city-states were united under one government ✓ Each polis was completely independent with its own laws, government and foreign policy; there was no unified Greek state

✗ The agora was only a marketplace ✓ The agora was also the political, social and religious heart of the polis, used for assemblies, law courts, festivals and public announcements

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe the key features of a Greek polis and explain how citizenship worked in the Archaic period. [6 marks]

The polis (city-state) was the fundamental unit of Greek political and social life. Each polis consisted of an urban centre (often on a defensible hill) and the surrounding agricultural countryside (chora). Key physical features included the acropolis (fortified hilltop with temples) and the agora (central public space for markets and assemblies). Each polis was self-governing with its own constitution, laws, military and patron deity. Citizenship was the defining political status, but it was highly exclusive: only free adult males whose parents were both citizens could be citizens. Citizens enjoyed political rights (voting in the assembly, holding office, serving on juries) but also had duties (military service as hoplites, paying taxes, participating in civic life). Women, slaves and metics (foreign residents) were excluded from citizenship, though metics paid taxes and could be required to serve in the military. The concept of the polis was so central to Greek identity that colonists across the Mediterranean replicated the polis structure, maintaining Greek culture while establishing politically independent new cities. The tension between individual poleis and pan-Hellenic identity would shape Greek history throughout the Archaic and Classical periods.
