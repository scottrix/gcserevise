Greek Warfare & the Hoplite Phalanx

The hoplite phalanx, Greek military organisation, naval warfare and the social implications of warfare in the Greek world.

Key Fact: The hoplite phalanx was the dominant Greek fighting formation: a dense line of heavily armed infantry fighting shoulder to shoulder
Key Fact: Each hoplite carried an aspis (large round shield), dory (long spear), Corinthian helmet, bronze cuirass and greaves
Key Fact: The phalanx's strength lay in solidarity: each man's shield protected his neighbour's right side, creating an interdependent wall
Key Fact: Naval warfare became crucial after the Persian Wars; the trireme (three-banked oared warship) was the standard warship
Key Fact: Athens built a large navy funded by silver mines at Laurium; the thetes (poorest citizens) served as rowers, increasing their political importance
Key Fact: Warfare was seasonal (summer campaigning), and war was a normal state of affairs between Greek poleis

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Greek Warfare & the Hoplite Phalanx, you must know:

  • Phalanx: A close-ranked formation of hoplites fighting shoulder to shoulder with overlapping shields and projecting spears
  • Aspis: The large round hoplite shield (about 1m diameter); also called a hoplon, held on the left arm
  • Dory: The long spear carried by hoplites (2-3 metres); used for thrusting over the shield wall
  • Trireme: A Greek warship with three banks of oars and a bronze ram for attacking enemy ships; required about 170 rowers
  • Strategos: A general in Athens; ten were elected annually and could be re-elected, making it the most important elected office

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How did the hoplite phalanx work as a fighting formation?

Q: Why did the rise of the Athenian navy increase democracy?

Q: What was a trireme and how was it used in battle?

Q: What equipment did a hoplite carry and who provided it?

Q: Why was the right wing of a phalanx so important?

✅ Answers

  1. Hoplites stood in close ranks, typically 8 men deep, with shields overlapping on the left. Each man's shield protected his neighbour's exposed right side. Spears projected forward over the shield wall. The formation advanced as a solid mass, pushing against the enemy phalanx (othismos). Breaking the enemy line usually decided the battle.
  2. Athens' navy required thousands of rowers (thetes, the poorest citizens). Since thetes were essential for Athens' military power, their political influence grew. Pericles introduced pay for public service partly because thetes who rowed in the fleet demanded political rights commensurate with their military contribution.
  3. A trireme was a fast, manoeuvrable warship with three banks of oars (about 170 rowers) and a bronze ram at the prow. In battle, triremes tried to ram enemy ships, rowing at speed to hole their hulls, or to shear off their oars by rowing past closely.
  4. Each hoplite provided his own equipment: aspis (large round shield), dory (spear), xiphos (short sword), Corinthian helmet, bronze cuirass (breastplate) and greaves (shin guards). This meant only citizens wealthy enough to afford the equipment could serve as hoplites.
  5. Each hoplite's shield covered his left side and his neighbour's right side, so the right side of each soldier was exposed. Phalanxes therefore drifted rightward as soldiers sought the protection of the man to their right. The right wing was the position of honour and typically held the best troops.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Phalanx = solidarity and interdependence, not individual heroism
  • Naval power = thetes = democracy (key connection for Athens)
  • Equipment was self-provided: wealth = military role = political status
  • Right wing = position of honour and greatest vulnerability

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Hoplites fought as individual warriors ✓ The phalanx was a collective formation where each man depended on his neighbour. Individual heroics were dangerous — breaking ranks could collapse the entire formation

✗ Only the rich served in the Athenian military ✓ The richest served as cavalry, the middle class as hoplites, and the poorest (thetes) as naval rowers. Athens' military power actually depended most on its thetes who manned the fleet

✗ Greek warfare was continuous year-round ✓ Greek warfare was seasonal: campaigns took place in summer when weather permitted and farmers could be spared from their land. Winters were generally a truce period

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe the hoplite phalanx and explain how changes in military organisation affected Athenian politics. [6 marks]

The hoplite phalanx was the dominant Greek military formation. Heavily armed infantry (hoplites) stood in close ranks about 8 deep, each carrying an aspis (large round shield) on the left arm and a dory (long spear) in the right hand. Shields overlapped so each man's shield protected his neighbour's exposed right side, creating an interdependent wall. Battles involved two phalanxes pushing against each other (othismos) until one broke formation and fled. Equipment was self-provided, so only citizens wealthy enough to afford arms could serve as hoplites, linking wealth, military service and political rights. This changed dramatically with the rise of naval power after the Persian Wars. Athens built a large fleet of triremes requiring about 170 rowers each, drawn from the thetes (poorest citizens). Since the navy became Athens' primary military force, thetes gained political influence proportional to their military importance. Pericles introduced state pay for public office and jury service in the 450s BC, enabling thetes to participate in democracy. The connection between military service and political rights meant that as the thetes became militarily essential, democracy became more radical. The Athenian empire, funded by tribute from allied states, paid for the navy that protected it — and the thetes who rowed the ships demanded and received greater political power.
