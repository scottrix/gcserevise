The Ionian Revolt (499-494 BC)

The Ionian Revolt against Persian rule that triggered the Persian Wars, including the role of Aristagoras and Athenian involvement.

Key Fact: The Ionian Revolt (499-494 BC) was a rebellion of Greek city-states in Asia Minor against Persian rule
Key Fact: Aristagoras, tyrant of Miletus, initiated the revolt after a failed attack on Naxos left him fearing Persian punishment
Key Fact: Athens sent 20 ships and Eretria sent 5 to support the Ionians — this enraged King Darius
Key Fact: The burning of Sardis (498 BC) was the revolt's greatest success but provoked a devastating Persian response
Key Fact: The revolt was crushed at the Battle of Lade (494 BC); Miletus was sacked and its people enslaved
Key Fact: Herodotus is our main source; he presents the revolt as the beginning of the chain of events leading to the Persian Wars

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For The Ionian Revolt (499-494 BC), you must know:

  • Aristagoras: The tyrant of Miletus who initiated the Ionian Revolt after a failed expedition against Naxos; he resigned his tyranny and declared democracy to gain support
  • Sardis: The capital of the Persian satrapy of Lydia, burned by Ionian rebels and their Athenian allies in 498 BC; enrage Darius
  • Battle of Lade: The naval battle (494 BC) where the Persian fleet defeated the Ionians, effectively ending the revolt
  • Satrapy: A province of the Persian Empire governed by a satrap (governor)
  • Herodotus: The 'Father of History' who wrote The Histories, the main source for the Persian Wars; he interviewed witnesses and travelled extensively

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Why did Aristagoras start the Ionian Revolt?

Q: Why did Athens and Eretria support the revolt?

Q: Why did the burning of Sardis provoke such a strong Persian response?

Q: How was the Ionian Revolt finally crushed?

Q: What was Herodotus' view of the Ionian Revolt?

✅ Answers

  1. Aristagoras persuaded the Persian satrap Artaphernes to support an attack on Naxos, but the expedition failed. Aristagoras now owed money to Persia and feared punishment. Rather than face this, he resigned as tyrant, declared democracy in Miletus and launched a revolt against Persian rule.
  2. Athens supported the Ionians partly because Miletus was a fellow Ionian city (shared ethnic and cultural ties) and partly because of the oracle advising them to free Miletus. Eretria supported them because Miletus had previously helped Eretria. Athens sent 20 ships and Eretria sent 5.
  3. Sardis was the capital of the satrapy and a symbol of Persian authority. Its destruction (including a temple of Cybele) was a profound insult to Persian imperial prestige. Herodotus says Darius asked who the Athenians were and demanded a servant remind him to punish them at every meal.
  4. The Persians systematically recaptured rebel cities. The decisive Battle of Lade (494 BC) saw the Persian fleet defeat the Ionian navy after the Samian and Lesbian contingents defected. Miletus was then besieged, sacked and its population enslaved or deported.
  5. Herodotus saw the revolt as the origin of the Persian Wars and a critical turning point. He presents it as a chain of cause and effect: the failed Naxos expedition led Aristagoras to revolt, Athenian support enraged Darius, and this led directly to the Persian invasions of mainland Greece.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ The Ionian Revolt was a spontaneous popular uprising ✓ The revolt was initiated by Aristagoras for personal reasons (fear of punishment after the Naxos failure). He cynically resigned as tyrant and declared democracy to gain popular support, but the revolt's origins were self-serving

✗ Athens supported the revolt out of pure idealism ✓ Athens had practical motives: shared Ionian identity, commercial interests and the desire to weaken Persian influence in the Aegean. Their 20 ships represented a modest commitment, not a full military effort

✗ The Persians were easily defeated in the revolt ✓ The Persians ultimately crushed the revolt decisively. The early success at Sardis was the exception; the revolt's end saw Miletus destroyed and its people enslaved

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain the causes and consequences of the Ionian Revolt. [6 marks]

The Ionian Revolt (499-494 BC) was triggered by Aristagoras, tyrant of Miletus, after a failed joint expedition with the Persian satrap Artaphernes against Naxos. When the attack failed, Aristagoras feared Persian punishment and financial ruin. Rather than face this, he resigned as tyrant, declared democracy in Miletus to gain popular support, and launched a rebellion against Persian rule across Ionia. He sought Greek allies: Sparta refused, but Athens sent 20 ships and Eretria sent 5, motivated by shared Ionian identity and practical interests. The rebels achieved a dramatic early success by marching on and burning Sardis, the satrapal capital, though they were then defeated by Persian forces at Ephesus. The revolt spread but was gradually crushed as Persia recaptured cities and rebuilt its fleet. The decisive Battle of Lade (494 BC) saw the Persian navy defeat the Ionian fleet after Samian and Lesbian contingents defected. Miletus was then sacked, its men killed and its women and children enslaved. The consequences were far-reaching: Darius was determined to punish Athens and Eretria for supporting the revolt, leading directly to the Persian invasions of mainland Greece. Herodotus presents the revolt as the origin of the entire Persian Wars, making it one of the most consequential events in Greek history.
