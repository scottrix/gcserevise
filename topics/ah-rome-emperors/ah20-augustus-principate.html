OCR J198

Augustus & the Principate

How Augustus transformed Rome from a Republic to an empire while maintaining the illusion of Republican government.

Key Fact: Octavian (later Augustus) was Caesar's adopted heir who defeated Mark Antony at Actium (31 BC) to become Rome's sole ruler
Key Fact: The Second Triumvirate (43 BC): Octavian, Antony and Lepidus divided the Roman world; it ended in civil war between Octavian and Antony
Key Fact: Augustus established the Principate: he held supreme power but maintained Republican forms and titles (Princeps = First Citizen)
Key Fact: The constitutional settlement (27 BC): Augustus 'restored the Republic' but kept consulship, tribunician power and control of key provinces
Key Fact: The Pax Romana (Roman Peace) brought stability, prosperity and cultural flourishing across the empire
Key Fact: Augustus reformed the army, morality, religion and building programme; he boasted he 'found Rome brick and left it marble'

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Augustus & the Principate, you must know:

  • Principate: The system of government established by Augustus where he held supreme power under Republican forms; Princeps = First Citizen
  • Battle of Actium: The naval battle (31 BC) where Octavian's fleet defeated the combined forces of Mark Antony and Cleopatra, making Octavian Rome's sole ruler
  • Res Gestae: Augustus' autobiography inscribed after his death, listing his achievements; 'the achievements of the deified Augustus'
  • Pax Romana: The period of relative peace and stability across the Roman Empire lasting approximately 200 years from Augustus' reign
  • Constitutional settlement: The arrangement of 27 BC where Augustus returned power to the Senate and people but retained consulship, tribunician power and control of key provinces

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How did Octavian become Augustus?

Q: What was the constitutional settlement of 27 BC?

Q: How did Augustus maintain the illusion of the Republic?

Q: What were Augustus' main reforms?

Q: What was the Pax Romana and why was it significant?

✅ Answers

  1. After Caesar's assassination (44 BC), Octavian joined the Second Triumvirate with Antony and Lepidus. After defeating Caesar's assassins, the Triumvirate collapsed. Octavian defeated Antony and Cleopatra at Actium (31 BC). In 27 BC the Senate granted him the title Augustus (revered one), and he established the Principate, maintaining Republican forms while holding supreme power.
  2. Augustus formally 'returned' power to the Senate and people, who then granted him consulship, tribunician power and control of provinces with armies (Gaul, Spain, Syria, Egypt). This gave him command of the army while appearing to restore the Republic. The arrangement was renewed in 23 BC with tribunician power as his primary authority.
  3. He refused the title of king or dictator, used the title Princeps (First Citizen), held traditional Republican offices (consul, tribune), consulted the Senate, held elections, and allowed the Senate to govern 'senatorial' provinces. The reality was that he controlled the army, the treasury and the key provinces, making him effectively a monarch.
  4. Military: standing army of 28 legions with fixed terms of service and a praetorian guard. Moral: laws promoting marriage, discouraging adultery and encouraging childbearing. Religious: revived traditional cults and temples. Building: massive construction programme (forums, temples, theatres). Administrative: reformed taxation and provincial government.
  5. The Pax Romana was a period of relative peace and stability across the Roman Empire lasting about 200 years from Augustus. It enabled trade, cultural exchange, road building and economic prosperity throughout the Mediterranean world. It represented Augustus' greatest legacy: the end of civil wars and the beginning of stable imperial government.

  • Principate = monarchy disguised as Republic (Princeps = First Citizen)

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Augustus abolished the Republic ✓ Augustus carefully preserved Republican institutions (Senate, consulships, elections, tribunes). He held power through these offices rather than creating new ones. The Republic existed in form, but Augustus dominated it in practice

✗ Augustus called himself emperor ✓ Augustus never used the title 'emperor' (imperator was a military acclamation). He preferred Princeps (First Citizen), suggesting primacy among equals rather than rule over subjects

✗ The Pax Romana meant no wars at all ✓ The Pax Romana meant no major civil wars between Romans. Rome still fought external wars to expand and secure borders. It was 'peace' defined as internal stability, not the complete absence of military conflict

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how Augustus established the Principate and describe how he maintained the illusion of the Republic while holding supreme power. [6 marks]

After defeating Antony at Actium (31 BC), Octavian faced the challenge of establishing stable government without repeating Caesar's fate. In 27 BC, he enacted the constitutional settlement: he formally 'returned' all powers to the Senate and people, who promptly granted him consulship, tribunician power and control of key provinces with armies (Gaul, Spain, Syria, Egypt). This gave him command of most of the army while appearing to restore the Republic. The Senate awarded him the title Augustus (revered one). Augustus maintained the Republic's illusion through several strategies. He refused titles like king or dictator, preferring Princeps (First Citizen), implying primacy among equals. He held traditional Republican offices (consul until 23 BC, then tribunician power as his primary authority). He consulted the Senate regularly, held elections and allowed the Senate to govern 'senatorial' provinces without armies. The Res Gestae, his autobiography, presented his rule as service to the Republic. However, the reality was that Augustus held supreme power: he controlled the army, the treasury and the key provinces; he could veto any act; and his auctoritas (authority) meant the Senate followed his wishes. The Principate was thus a disguised monarchy — the Republic existed in form but not in substance. This careful balance allowed Augustus to rule for 41 years and establish the Pax Romana, ending decades of civil war while avoiding the fate of Julius Caesar.
