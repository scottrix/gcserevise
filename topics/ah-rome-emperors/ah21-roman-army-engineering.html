The Roman Army & Engineering

The Roman army's organisation, tactics and engineering achievements including roads, aqueducts and Hadrian's Wall.

Key Fact: The Roman legion was the basic military unit of about 5,000 men, divided into 10 cohorts of 6 centuries of 80 men each
Key Fact: Legionaries carried a pilum (javelin) and gladius (short sword); the pilum's soft iron shank bent on impact, making shields unusable
Key Fact: Roman engineering: roads (over 400,000 km of roads connecting the empire), aqueducts (gravity-fed water supply), and fortifications like Hadrian's Wall
Key Fact: Hadrian's Wall (122 AD) was a 117 km defensive wall across northern Britain, marking the northwest frontier of the empire
Key Fact: Roman camps (castra) were built to a standard plan every night on campaign, with gates, roads and defences
Key Fact: The army was also an engineering force: building roads, bridges, siege works and infrastructure wherever they went

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For The Roman Army & Engineering, you must know:

  • Legion: The basic unit of the Roman army, about 5,000 heavy infantry citizens, plus auxiliaries (non-citizen troops)
  • Centurion: A professional officer commanding a century (80 men); the backbone of the legion's command structure
  • Pilum: A Roman javelin with a soft iron shank that bent on impact, making it impossible to throw back and disabling enemy shields
  • Testudo: The 'tortoise' formation where legionaries locked shields above and in front for protection during sieges
  • Aqueduct: An engineered channel using gravity to transport water from distant sources to Roman cities

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How was a Roman legion organised?

Q: Why was the pilum an effective weapon?

Q: How did Roman roads contribute to imperial control?

Q: Why was Hadrian's Wall built?

Q: How did the Roman army serve as an engineering force?

✅ Answers

  1. A legion had about 5,200 men, divided into 10 cohorts. Each cohort had 6 centuries of 80 men commanded by a centurion. The first cohort was double-strength. Auxiliaries (non-citizen soldiers) provided cavalry, archers and specialist troops. A legate (senior senator) commanded the legion with tribunes and a senior centurion (primus pilus) as deputies.
  2. The pilum had a soft iron shank connecting the heavy pyramidal point to the wooden shaft. On impact, the iron shank bent, preventing the enemy from throwing it back and making the embedded javelin drag down their shield, forcing them to discard it and fight unprotected.
  3. Roman roads (over 400,000 km total) enabled rapid troop movement across the empire, allowing quick response to rebellions or invasions. They facilitated trade, official communication (using relay stations) and cultural integration. The standard design (straight, well-drained, stone-paved) made them durable and efficient.
  4. Hadrian's Wall (begun 122 AD) stretched 117 km across northern Britain from the Tyne to the Solway. It marked the northwest frontier of the Roman Empire, controlling movement of people and goods, demonstrating Roman power, and separating the Romanised south from unconquered Pictish territory to the north.
  5. Legionaries built roads, bridges, forts and aqueducts as part of their regular duties. They constructed siege works (ramps, towers, battering rams) during campaigns. Standard overnight camps were built to a precise grid plan with ditches, ramparts and gates. The army's engineering capabilities were central to Rome's ability to conquer and control territory.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Legion = 5,000 citizen infantry; auxiliaries = non-citizen specialists
  • Pilum = bending javelin that disabled shields
  • Roads = military control + trade + communication
  • Army = soldiers AND engineers; they built the empire's infrastructure

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Roman legions were the entire army ✓ Legionaries (citizen heavy infantry) were only part of the army. Auxiliaries (non-citizens) provided cavalry, archers and specialist troops, often equalling or exceeding legionary numbers. The navy was also separate

✗ Hadrian's Wall successfully kept all barbarians out ✓ The Wall controlled and monitored movement rather than being an impenetrable barrier. Gates (milecastles) allowed controlled trade and passage. Its primary function was border management and demonstrating Roman power, not total exclusion

✗ Roman soldiers only fought ✓ Legionaries spent much of their time on engineering and construction: building roads, forts, aqueducts and infrastructure. On campaign, they built a fortified camp every night. The army was as much an engineering corps as a fighting force

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe the organisation of the Roman army and explain how Roman engineering supported imperial control. [6 marks]

The Roman army was organised around the legion, a unit of about 5,200 citizen heavy infantry. Each legion was divided into 10 cohorts, each containing 6 centuries of 80 men led by a centurion. The first cohort was double-strength with about 800 men. A senior senator (legate) commanded the legion, assisted by tribunes and the primus pilus (chief centurion). Auxiliary units of non-citizen soldiers provided cavalry, archers and specialist troops, often equalling or exceeding legionary numbers in total. Legionaries carried a pilum (bending javelin that disabled enemy shields) and gladius (short thrusting sword). The testudo (tortoise) formation protected soldiers during sieges by locking shields overhead. Roman engineering was central to imperial control. The army built over 400,000 km of roads on a standard design — straight, well-drained and stone-paved — enabling rapid troop movement to suppress rebellions, facilitating trade and official communication through relay stations. Aqueducts used gravity to transport water to cities, supporting urban populations of hundreds of thousands. Hadrian's Wall (122 AD) stretched 117 km across northern Britain, controlling movement across the frontier with milecastles and observation towers. Legionaries constructed a fortified camp (castra) to a standard grid plan every night on campaign, with ditches, ramparts and four gates. This engineering capability meant the army not only conquered territory but built the infrastructure to hold it: roads for movement, aqueducts for cities, and fortifications for borders. Military power and engineering went hand in hand.
