The Punic Wars & Hannibal

Rome's wars with Carthage, Hannibal's invasion of Italy, and the transformation of Rome into a Mediterranean superpower.

Key Fact: The three Punic Wars (264-146 BC) were fought between Rome and Carthage for control of the Western Mediterranean
Key Fact: First Punic War (264-241 BC): Rome built its first navy and won Sicily as its first province
Key Fact: Second Punic War (218-201 BC): Hannibal crossed the Alps with elephants, won at Cannae (216 BC) but was eventually defeated by Scipio at Zama (202 BC)
Key Fact: The Battle of Cannae was Rome's worst military defeat: perhaps 50,000-70,000 Romans died in a single day
Key Fact: Third Punic War (149-146 BC): Carthage was destroyed; the city was burned and its territory became a Roman province
Key Fact: Cato the Elder ended every speech with 'Carthago delenda est' (Carthage must be destroyed)

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For The Punic Wars & Hannibal, you must know:

  • Carthage: A powerful North African city-state and maritime empire that was Rome's chief rival in the Western Mediterranean
  • Hannibal: The Carthaginian general who crossed the Alps in 218 BC and campaigned in Italy for 16 years, winning stunning victories including Cannae
  • Battle of Cannae: Hannibal's greatest victory (216 BC) where his smaller force enveloped and destroyed a Roman army of perhaps 80,000, killing 50,000-70,000
  • Scipio Africanus: The Roman general who defeated Hannibal at the Battle of Zama (202 BC), ending the Second Punic War
  • Carthago delenda est: 'Carthage must be destroyed' — the phrase Cato the Elder appended to every speech in the Senate to argue for the destruction of Carthage

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What caused the First Punic War?

Q: How did Hannibal achieve his victory at Cannae?

Q: Why did Hannibal ultimately fail despite his brilliant victories?

Q: Why was Carthage destroyed in the Third Punic War?

Q: How did the Punic Wars transform Rome?

✅ Answers

  1. Rome and Carthage both wanted control of Sicily, a strategically and economically valuable island. The conflict began when the Mamertines (Italian mercenaries in Messana) asked Rome for protection against Carthage. Rome, seeing an opportunity, intervened and war followed (264-241 BC).
  2. Hannibal deliberately made his centre thin and weak while strengthening his flanks. When the Romans pushed forward against the centre, the Carthaginian flanks wrapped around the Roman army (double envelopment). The Romans were surrounded and slaughtered — perhaps 50,000-70,000 died in a single day.
  3. Hannibal won battles but could not take Rome itself. He lacked siege equipment and reinforcements from Carthage were limited. Rome's allies largely remained loyal, and Rome raised new armies after each defeat. Eventually Scipio attacked Carthage directly in Africa, forcing Hannibal to leave Italy and fight at Zama (202 BC), where he was defeated.
  4. Cato the Elder argued that Carthage, even after military defeat, remained a potential threat to Rome. Carthage had recovered economically, which frightened Rome. When Carthage violated a treaty by defending itself against Numidian attacks, Rome used this as a pretext for war. After a three-year siege, Carthage was destroyed in 146 BC.
  5. Rome gained its first overseas provinces (Sicily, Sardinia, Spain), built a powerful navy, and became the dominant power in the Western Mediterranean. The wars also brought wealth, slavery and Greek cultural influence to Rome, while creating social tensions as the rich bought up land worked by war-displaced peasants.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Three Punic Wars: 264-241, 218-201, 149-146 BC
  • Cannae = Rome's worst defeat; Zama = Scipio defeats Hannibal
  • Hannibal's failure: couldn't take Rome, allies stayed loyal, Carthage didn't reinforce
  • Aftermath: Rome = Mediterranean superpower, but social tensions grew

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Hannibal conquered Rome ✓ Hannibal never captured Rome. He campaigned in Italy for 16 years, winning battles but never taking the city itself. Rome's walls, resilient allies and ability to raise new armies prevented his ultimate success

✗ The Third Punic War was a fair fight ✓ By 149 BC, Carthage had been disarmed and was no real threat. The war was essentially an unprovoked Roman aggression driven by fear and greed, as Cato's obsessive 'Carthago delenda est' rhetoric demonstrates

✗ Rome won the Punic Wars easily ✓ Rome suffered catastrophic defeats: at Cannae alone, more Romans died in one day than in any other battle in Western history. Rome's ultimate victory came from resilience, manpower and the strategic brilliance of Scipio, not from easy superiority

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe the Second Punic War and explain why Rome ultimately defeated Hannibal. [6 marks]

The Second Punic War (218-201 BC) began when Hannibal attacked Saguntum, a Roman ally in Spain. Rather than waiting for a Roman invasion of Africa, Hannibal made his famous crossing of the Alps with an army including war elephants, entering Italy from the north. He won a series of stunning victories: at the River Trebia (218 BC), Lake Trasimene (217 BC) where he ambushed and destroyed a Roman army, and most devastatingly at Cannae (216 BC), where his double envelopment killed perhaps 50,000-70,000 Romans in a single day — Rome's worst ever military defeat. Despite these victories, Hannibal could not capture Rome itself. He lacked siege equipment and sufficient reinforcements from Carthage. Crucially, most of Rome's Italian allies remained loyal, denying Hannibal the support he needed. Rome's greatest strength was its resilience: after each defeat, it raised new armies from its large citizen population and allied manpower. The turning point came when the Roman general Scipio adopted a new strategy. Rather than continuing to fight Hannibal in Italy, he attacked Carthage's interests in Spain and then invaded North Africa itself (204 BC). This forced Carthage to recall Hannibal from Italy. At the Battle of Zama (202 BC), Scipio defeated Hannibal in a pitched battle, ending the war. Rome's victory came from its manpower, the loyalty of its allies, strategic flexibility and Scipio's brilliance — not from individual battlefield superiority over Hannibal.
