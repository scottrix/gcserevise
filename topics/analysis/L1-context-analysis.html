L1: Context Analysis

Foundation Higher All Boards AO3

Understanding historical, social and cultural context of texts - when and why they were written.

📋 What is Context?

Definition: Context refers to the circumstances in which a text was written - the historical period, social conditions, author's life, and cultural influences that shaped the work.

In English Literature, context analysis (AO3) is worth 15-25% of marks. You need to show how the time and place a text was written influences its meaning.

Key Types of Context:
• Historical context - when it was written
• Social context - society at the time
• Cultural context - beliefs, values, attitudes
• Author's context - writer's life and experiences
• Literary context - the writing traditions of the period

🏛️ Historical Context

Historical context covers the major events and time period when a text was written.

Shakespearean Era (1564-1616)

  • Elizabeth I and James I reigned
  • The Renaissance - revival of classical learning
  • Religious turmoil - Protestant vs Catholic tensions
  • Belief in the Great Chain of Being - strict social hierarchy
  • Belief in witches and the supernatural
  • Theatre was popular entertainment for all classes

Victorian Era (1837-1901)

  • Industrial Revolution transformed society
  • Rapid urbanisation and poverty
  • Strict class divisions
  • Gender roles - women had few rights
  • Science vs religion debate (Darwin)
  • British Empire at its height

Modern Era (1914 onwards)

  • World Wars shattered old certainties
  • Class system began to break down
  • Women's rights movements
  • Post-war social change
  • Immigration and multiculturalism
  • Decline of traditional authority
Example: Historical Context in "Macbeth"

Written in 1606, after the Gunpowder Plot (1605). Shakespeare's portrayal of the dangers of regicide (killing a king) would have resonated with an audience still shaken by the attempt to blow up Parliament. James I was obsessed with witchcraft - Shakespeare included witches to appeal to him.

👥 Social Context

Social context examines the society in which a text was written and how it influenced the writer.

Key Social Factors

  • Class structure - Social hierarchies and divisions
  • Gender roles - Expectations of men and women
  • Wealth and poverty - Economic conditions
  • Education - Who could read and write
  • Family structures - Marriage, children, inheritance
  • Work and industry - How people earned a living
Example: Social Context in "An Inspector Calls"

Written in 1945 but set in 1912. Priestley uses dramatic irony to show how the Edwardian class system and capitalist attitudes led to suffering. Post-war audiences would have supported his message of social responsibility - the NHS and welfare state were being created when the play premiered.

📖 Author's Context

An author's personal experiences often influence their writing.

Key Biographical Factors

  • Family background - Class, wealth, education
  • Life experiences - Major events that shaped them
  • Beliefs and values - Political, religious, social views
  • Other works - Recurring themes across their writing
  • Purpose - Why they wrote this text
Example: Author Context for "A Christmas Carol"

Charles Dickens experienced poverty as a child when his father was sent to debtors' prison. He worked in a blacking factory at age 12. This personal experience of hardship and social injustice strongly influenced his writing and his desire to expose the suffering of the poor.

✍️ How to Use Context in Essays

Golden Rule: Context should be INTEGRATED, not bolted on. Link it to specific points about the text - don't just dump historical facts.

What NOT to Do

Bad Example (Context Bolted On)

"Macbeth kills Duncan to become king. This shows he is ambitious. Queen Elizabeth I died in 1603 and James I became king. He was from Scotland and wrote a book about witches."

Problem: Context is irrelevant to the point about Macbeth's ambition.

What TO Do

Good Example (Context Integrated)

"Macbeth's decision to kill Duncan would have shocked Shakespeare's Jacobean audience, who believed in the Divine Right of Kings. Regicide was not just murder but a violation of God's order - the Great Chain of Being. This makes Macbeth's ambition appear even more transgressive and disturbing to contemporary viewers."

Effective Context Strategies

  • Link context to specific quotations
  • Explain how context affects meaning
  • Use phrases like "This reflects...", "In the context of...", "Shakespeare's audience would have..."
  • Show how context shapes the writer's choices
  • Consider how different audiences might respond

📊 Context by Text Type

Shakespeare Plays

  • Elizabethan/Jacobean theatre conditions
  • Divine Right of Kings
  • Great Chain of Being
  • Beliefs about gender, fate, and the supernatural
  • Theatre as propaganda for the monarchy

19th Century Novels

  • Victorian class system
  • Industrial Revolution and urbanisation
  • Science vs religion (Darwin, evolution)
  • Gothic literary tradition
  • Serialised publication (cliffhangers)

Modern Texts

  • Post-war social change
  • Class and equality movements
  • Changing gender roles
  • Rise of democracy and socialism
  • Reaction against Victorian values

Poetry

  • Poet's biography
  • Historical events (wars, social change)
  • Literary movement (Romantic, Victorian, Modern)
  • Anthology theme and its significance

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Dumping context without linking it Shows you memorised facts but can't apply them Link every context point to a quotation or idea
Using context instead of analysis Context supports analysis, doesn't replace it Always analyse language, form, structure FIRST
Too much biographical detail Not all author facts are relevant Only include what affects the text's meaning
Ignoring when the text is SET vs WRITTEN Different contexts may apply Consider both time periods
Forgetting audience reception Context includes how audiences responded Compare contemporary vs modern reactions

💡 Memorising Context

Since exams are closed book, you need to memorise key context points.

Top Memorisation Tips

  • Create flashcards with text on one side, context on the other
  • Link context to specific quotations you've memorised
  • Use mnemonics for key dates and facts
  • Make timelines showing historical events
  • Practice writing context paragraphs for past exam questions
Remember for Each Text:
• WHEN written + WHY written
• WHO wrote it + their background
• WHAT society was like
• HOW audiences would have responded
• WHY this matters for meaning

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Why is the Gunpowder Plot relevant context for "Macbeth"?

Q2: How does Victorian class structure affect "A Christmas Carol"?

Q3: What is the difference between when "An Inspector Calls" was written and when it's set, and why does this matter?

Q4: How do you integrate context into an essay without "bolting it on"?

✅ Answers

  1. "Macbeth" was written in 1606, just after the Gunpowder Plot of 1605. The play's themes of regicide and treason would have resonated with an audience still shaken by this event. Shakespeare's portrayal of the consequences of killing a king reinforces the Divine Right of Kings.
  2. Dickens critiques Victorian social inequality and the treatment of the poor. The Cratchits represent the deserving poor, while Scrooge embodies uncaring capitalism. The story reflects Victorian concerns about charity, social responsibility, and the effects of industrialisation.
  3. Written in 1945, set in 1912. This gap allows Priestley to use dramatic irony - the audience knows about WWI and WWII, but the characters don't. The play warns 1945 audiences that failing to learn from the past leads to disaster, supporting post-war social reforms.
  4. Link context directly to analysis. Use phrases like "This reflects the context of..." or "For Shakespeare's audience, this would have..." Always connect context to specific quotations or moments in the text, explaining how it affects meaning.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Revise key context for each text you're studying
  • Always link context to specific points about the text
  • Use context to deepen analysis, not replace it
  • Consider how contemporary audiences would have responded
  • Don't dump facts - integrate them meaningfully
  • Context is worth 15-25% - make every point count
  • Memorise 3-5 key context facts for each text
