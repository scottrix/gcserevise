L2: Plot Analysis

Foundation Higher All Boards AO1

Understanding and analysing narrative structure and plot development - how stories are constructed.

📋 What is Plot?

Definition: Plot is the sequence of events that make up a story - what happens and in what order. Plot analysis examines how the writer structures these events to create meaning and effect.

Plot analysis is part of AO1 - showing understanding of texts. You need to demonstrate knowledge of key events and explain how they contribute to the whole.

Plot vs Story:
• Story = what happens (the events themselves)
• Plot = how events are arranged (the structure)
The same story can be told in different plot structures.

📐 Narrative Structure Models

Freytag's Pyramid

Most plots follow this five-part structure:

Freytag's Pyramid
  1. Exposition - Characters and setting introduced
  2. Rising Action - Conflict develops, tension builds
  3. Climax - Turning point, most dramatic moment
  4. Falling Action - Consequences of climax unfold
  5. Resolution/Denouement - Loose ends tied up

Applying Freytag to "Macbeth"

Stage Events
Exposition Macbeth wins battle; witches make prophecy
Rising Action Macbeth tells Lady Macbeth; Duncan's murder; Banquo's murder
Climax Macbeth visits witches; believes he's invincible
Falling Action Macduff joins Malcolm; Lady Macbeth's guilt; Birnam Wood moves
Resolution Macduff kills Macbeth; Malcolm becomes king

🔄 Key Plot Concepts

Turning Points

Moments where the direction of the plot changes significantly.

Examples
  • Macbeth: Murder of Duncan (Macbeth commits to evil path)
  • Romeo and Juliet: Tybalt's death (Romeo is banished)
  • An Inspector Calls: Inspector's arrival (exposes all secrets)
  • Jekyll and Hyde: Carew's murder (Hyde's violence revealed)

Dramatic Irony

When the audience knows something characters don't - creates tension and engagement.

Examples
  • Macbeth: Audience knows Macbeth plans to kill Duncan while Duncan praises him
  • Romeo and Juliet: Audience knows Juliet is alive; Romeo doesn't
  • An Inspector Calls: Audience knows about WWI; characters are optimistic about the future

Foreshadowing

Hints or clues about what will happen later - creates suspense and shows careful planning.

Examples
  • Macbeth: Witches' prophecies foreshadow Macbeth's rise and fall
  • Of Mice and Men: Shooting of Candy's dog foreshadows Lennie's death
  • Lord of the Flies: Early references to "beasties" foreshadow the sow's head

📖 Linear vs Non-Linear Structure

Linear Structure

Events unfold chronologically - in time order.

  • Easy to follow
  • Shows cause and effect clearly
  • Builds naturally to climax
  • Most Shakespeare plays use this structure

Non-Linear Structure

Events are arranged out of chronological order.

Technique Definition Example
Flashbacks Scenes from earlier in the story "The Woman in Black" - memories of Arthur's past
Flash-forwards Glimpses of future events "A Christmas Carol" - Scrooge sees future
Cyclical structure Story ends where it began "An Inspector Calls" - ends with another phone call
In media res Starting in the middle of action "The Odyssey" - starts during journey home
Why use non-linear structure?
• Creates mystery and suspense
• Shows psychological truth (memory works this way)
• Highlights contrasts between past and present
• Makes reader piece together the story

🎭 Openings and Endings

Analysing Openings

Consider how the writer sets up the story:

  • How are characters introduced?
  • What mood or atmosphere is established?
  • What questions are raised?
  • Is it slow-paced or dramatic?
"Macbeth" Opening

Opens with witches - establishes supernatural elements, evil, and chaos immediately. "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" sets up the play's themes of appearance vs reality.

Analysing Endings

Consider how the story concludes:

  • Is it resolved or ambiguous?
  • What message does the ending convey?
  • Is it satisfying or shocking?
  • What has changed since the beginning?
"An Inspector Calls" Ending

The play ends where it began - with a phone call about a dead girl. This cyclical structure suggests the family hasn't learned their lesson. The audience is left questioning whether it could happen again.

⏱️ Pacing and Tension

Pacing

How quickly or slowly events unfold - controlled by:

  • Scene length (short scenes = fast pacing)
  • Dialogue vs action
  • Amount of detail
  • Time jumps vs real-time action
Pacing in "Macbeth"
  • Act 1 takes place over several days - slow build
  • Act 2 rushes from murder to discovery - fast pacing
  • Act 5 battles happen in quick succession - climactic

Tension Building

Writers create suspense through:

  • Withholding information
  • Creating deadlines or time pressure
  • Putting characters in danger
  • Using dramatic irony
  • Building to climactic moments

📊 Plot by Text Type

Shakespeare Plays

  • Five-act structure (based on classical drama)
  • Multiple plotlines (main plot + subplots)
  • Comedy/tragedy conventions affect plot
  • Entrances and exits structure scenes

Novels

  • Chapter divisions control pacing
  • Multiple perspectives or narrators
  • Serialised publication (cliffhangers in chapters)
  • Subplots develop themes

Modern Drama

  • Acts and scenes
  • Stage directions indicate action
  • Real-time or compressed time
  • Climactic moments often at act endings

✍️ How to Write About Plot

Golden Rule: Don't just retell what happens. Analyse WHY the writer structured events this way and WHAT EFFECT it creates.

Useful Sentence Starters

  • "The writer positions X early in the text to..."
  • "This turning point marks a shift from..."
  • "The cyclical structure suggests..."
  • "By withholding information until X, the writer creates...""
  • "The contrast between X and Y highlights..."
Good Plot Analysis

Shakespeare positions the murder of Duncan off-stage, focusing instead on Macbeth's psychological turmoil. This structural choice emphasises the internal consequences of regicide over the physical act, aligning with the play's exploration of guilt and moral decay.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Retelling the plot Examiners know the story - they want analysis Focus on HOW and WHY events are structured
Listing events without analysis Doesn't show understanding of effect Explain the purpose and effect of each event
Ignoring structure Plot is about arrangement, not just events Consider order, pace, and dramatic techniques
Forgetting key turning points These are crucial for understanding the whole Identify and memorise 3-5 key moments

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What is the difference between story and plot?

Q2: Why might a writer use a cyclical structure?

Q3: Give an example of dramatic irony from a text you've studied.

Q4: How does foreshadowing affect the reader?

✅ Answers

  1. Story is what happens (the events themselves). Plot is how events are arranged and structured. The same story can be told with different plot structures.
  2. A cyclical structure (ending where it began) can suggest that nothing has changed, that history repeats itself, or that events are inevitable. It can also create a sense of entrapment or reinforce themes.
  3. Answers will vary by text. Examples: Macbeth - audience knows Macbeth plans murder while Duncan praises him; Romeo and Juliet - audience knows Juliet is drugged, Romeo doesn't; An Inspector Calls - audience knows about WWI, characters are optimistic.
  4. Foreshadowing creates suspense and anticipation, shows careful narrative planning, rewards attentive readers, and can make later events feel inevitable or tragic.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Learn key events and their order for each text
  • Focus on HOW and WHY, not just WHAT happens
  • Identify turning points and analyse their significance
  • Consider the effect of structure on meaning
  • Use terminology: turning point, climax, foreshadowing, cyclical
  • Link plot points to themes and character development
  • Never just retell the story - always analyse
