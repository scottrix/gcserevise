L3: Character Analysis

Foundation Higher All Boards AO1 AO2

Analysing characters and their development - understanding who they are, why they act, and how they're presented.

📋 What is Character Analysis?

Definition: Character analysis examines how characters are presented, their role in the text, their development, and how the writer uses them to explore themes.

Characters are central to most exam questions. You need to show understanding of:

  • Who they are - personality, background, relationships
  • What they do - actions, decisions, motivations
  • How they change - development, arc, transformation
  • Why they matter - thematic significance, symbolic role
  • How they're presented - language, dialogue, other characters' views

👤 Character Types

Protagonist

The main character - the story centres on them.

  • Usually undergoes significant change
  • Drives the plot forward
  • Often the "hero" but not always likeable
Examples
  • Macbeth - tragic hero whose ambition leads to downfall
  • Scrooge - protagonist who transforms from miser to benefactor
  • Romeo - tragic hero whose passion leads to tragedy

Antagonist

Opposes or creates conflict for the protagonist.

  • Not always "evil" - may have understandable motives
  • Creates obstacles for the protagonist
  • Often reflects or contrasts with the protagonist
Examples
  • Macduff - opposes Macbeth and ultimately kills him
  • Mr Hyde - dark counterpart to Dr Jekyll
  • Lord Capulet - opposes Juliet's wishes

Minor Characters

Supporting roles that still have important functions:

  • Develop or contrast with main characters
  • Advance the plot
  • Represent themes or ideas
  • Provide exposition or comic relief
Examples
  • Banquo - contrasts Macbeth; shows alternative response to prophecy
  • Bob Cratchit - shows poverty's effects; humanises the poor
  • The Inspector - represents Priestley's moral message

Foil Characters

Characters who contrast with another to highlight their traits.

Examples
  • Banquo vs Macbeth - both receive prophecies; Banquo resists temptation
  • Lady Macbeth vs Macbeth - she's initially stronger, but guilt destroys her
  • Eric vs Gerald - both used Eva; Eric feels guilt, Gerald doesn't

📈 Character Arcs

A character arc is the transformation or journey a character undergoes.

Types of Arcs

Arc Type Definition Example
Positive Arc Character grows, learns, improves Scrooge - transforms from miser to generous man
Negative Arc Character declines, falls, corrupts Macbeth - noble warrior becomes tyrant
Flat Arc Character changes others but stays the same The Inspector - changes the Birlings; remains unchanged
Tragic Arc Rise then catastrophic fall Macbeth - prophecy fulfilled, then destroyed
Analysing Arcs: Focus on the turning point - when does the character start to change? What causes it? What are the consequences?

🔍 Analysing Character Presentation

1. Direct Characterisation

The writer TELLS us about the character directly.

Examples
  • "Scrooge was a squeezing, wrenching, grasping, scraping, clutching, covetous old sinner!" - Dickens directly describes
  • "Macbeth - brave Macbeth - well he deserves that name" - Captain describes Macbeth's reputation

2. Indirect Characterisation

The writer SHOWS us the character through:

  • Actions - what they do reveals who they are
  • Dialogue - what they say and how they say it
  • Thoughts - interior monologue or soliloquy
  • Appearance - physical descriptions
  • Others' reactions - how other characters respond to them
Indirect Characterisation in "Macbeth"
  • Actions: Kills Duncan - shows ambition and moral weakness
  • Dialogue: "Stars, hide your fires" - reveals secret ambition
  • Thoughts: Soliloquies reveal inner turmoil and guilt
  • Appearance: Lady Macbeth says he's "too full o'th'milk of human kindness"
  • Others: Duncan calls him "worthiest cousin" - trusted by king

💬 Analysing Dialogue

What characters say and how they say it reveals their personality.

What to Analyse

Feature What It Shows Example
Vocabulary choice Education, class, personality Scrooge's harsh, business language
Sentence length Emotional state, confidence Macbeth's fragmented speeches when guilty
Tone Attitude to others Lady Macbeth's mocking, controlling tone
Who dominates Power dynamics The Inspector controls conversations
Interruptions Impatience, power Lady Macbeth interrupts Macbeth
Silence Guilt, shock, powerlessness Eric's silence when accused
Analysing Lady Macbeth's Dialogue

"When you durst do it, then you were a man"
She challenges Macbeth's masculinity - manipulates him by attacking his pride. The conditional "when" implies he's failed to be a man. This shows her controlling, ambitious nature.

🎭 Analysing Motivation

Understanding WHY characters act as they do is crucial for analysis.

Types of Motivation

  • Internal - desires, fears, beliefs, ambitions
  • External - pressure from others, society, circumstances
  • Psychological - mental state, past experiences
  • Social - class, gender, social expectations
Macbeth's Motivations
  • Internal: Ambition ("I have no spur to prick the sides of my intent")
  • External: Witches' prophecy, Lady Macbeth's manipulation
  • Psychological: Fear of being caught, growing paranoia
  • Social: Expectations of masculinity, kingship
Key Question: "What drives this character?"
Consider: desires, fears, relationships, context, personality

👥 Character Relationships

How characters interact reveals their personalities and themes.

Analysing Relationships

  • Power dynamics - who holds power? Does it shift?
  • Conflict - what tensions exist? How are they resolved?
  • Love and loyalty - who do characters care about?
  • Contrasts - how do different characters highlight each other?
Macbeth and Lady Macbeth
  • Early: She dominates - "Leave all the rest to me"
  • Middle: Macbeth acts independently - doesn't tell her about Banquo
  • End: They're emotionally separated - he's cold about her death
  • Significance: Shows how guilt and evil destroy their marriage

✍️ How to Write About Characters

Golden Rule: Don't just DESCRIBE characters. ANALYSE how and why the writer presents them this way.

Structure for Character Analysis

  1. Make a point about the character
  2. Use a quotation to support
  3. Analyse language/structure
  4. Link to themes or context
Good Character Analysis

Lady Macbeth is presented as manipulative and calculating. She tells Macbeth "Look like the innocent flower, but be the serpent under't," using imperatives to assert control. The contrasting imagery of "flower" and "serpent" reflects her ability to deceive - she coaches Macbeth to hide his murderous intentions behind a facade of hospitality. This establishes her as the dominant force in their relationship in the early acts.

Useful Verbs for Character Analysis

presents, portrays, depicts, characterises, reveals, suggests, implies, highlights, contrasts, develops, transforms

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Describing instead of analysing Retelling doesn't show understanding Focus on HOW and WHY the character is presented
Ignoring language and structure Character analysis requires AO2 Comment on dialogue, actions, descriptions
Treating characters as real people They're literary constructs Write "Shakespeare presents Macbeth as..." not "Macbeth is..."
Forgetting minor characters They often have important functions Consider what each character contributes
Ignoring development Static analysis misses the arc Track how characters change over the text

📝 Memorising Character Quotations

Since exams are closed book, memorise key quotations for main characters.

Strategy

  • Choose 5-8 key quotations per major character
  • Group by theme or trait
  • Learn quotations that can be used for multiple points
  • Practise writing paragraphs with each quotation
For Each Character, Memorise:
• 1-2 quotations showing key personality traits
• 1-2 showing their relationships
• 1-2 showing their development/turning point
• 1-2 showing their role in themes

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What is a foil character and why are they useful?

Q2: Give an example of a character with a negative arc and explain their decline.

Q3: What's the difference between direct and indirect characterisation?

Q4: How can you tell if one character has power over another in dialogue?

✅ Answers

  1. A foil character contrasts with another character to highlight their traits. By showing how characters differ, the writer emphasises specific qualities. Example: Banquo contrasts Macbeth - both receive prophecies, but Banquo resists temptation, highlighting Macbeth's moral weakness.
  2. Answers vary. Macbeth is the classic example - he begins as "brave Macbeth," a loyal warrior, but declines into a tyrant who orders the murder of women and children. His negative arc is driven by ambition and guilt.
  3. Direct characterisation: the writer tells us about the character directly ("Scrooge was a squeezing, wrenching... old sinner"). Indirect characterisation: the writer shows the character through actions, dialogue, thoughts, and others' reactions.
  4. Look for: who dominates the conversation (word count), who interrupts whom, whose questions are answered, whose language controls the interaction, body language (stage directions), and who has the last word.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Focus on HOW characters are presented, not just what they do
  • Use specific quotations to support analysis
  • Track character development across the text
  • Consider characters' roles in themes
  • Use foil characters to deepen analysis
  • Write about the writer's choices, not just the character's personality
  • Link to context when relevant (social, historical)
