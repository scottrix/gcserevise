L5: Language Analysis

Foundation Higher All Boards AO2

Analysing language choices and their effects - the heart of literary analysis.

📋 What is Language Analysis?

Definition: Language analysis (AO2) examines the specific words and language techniques writers use to create meanings and effects. It's about HOW meaning is created, not just WHAT is said.

AO2 is worth 30-40% of marks - the biggest chunk. You need to:

  • Identify language techniques accurately
  • Use specific quotations
  • Explain the EFFECT of language choices
  • Use appropriate subject terminology
The Language Analysis Formula:
1. QUOTE - Select specific words
2. IDENTIFY - Name the technique
3. ANALYSE - Explain the effect
4. LINK - Connect to themes/context

🔤 Word-Level Analysis

Connotations

What associations do words carry beyond their literal meaning?

Example: "Serpent" in Macbeth

Lady Macbeth: "be the serpent under't"

Literal: A snake
Connotations: Evil, temptation, the Devil, deception, danger, hidden threat
Effect: Associates Macbeth's ambition with original sin and evil, suggesting moral corruption

Semantic Fields

Groups of words related to a particular theme or idea.

Semantic Field of Darkness in "Macbeth"
  • "Stars, hide your fires"
  • "black and deep desires"
  • "thick night"
  • "darkness" and "shadow"

Effect: Creates atmosphere of evil and moral blindness, links to theme of appearance vs reality

Word Classes

Type Definition Example
Dynamic verbs Action words - movement, change "plucked", "tore", "slashed" - violent energy
Stative verbs States of being - stillness "remained", "stood" - permanence
Abstract nouns Ideas, concepts, feelings "ambition", "guilt" - internal states
Concrete nouns Physical things "dagger", "blood" - visceral imagery

🎨 Imagery

Metaphor

One thing IS another - creates a direct comparison.

Examples
  • Macbeth: "Life's but a walking shadow" - life has no substance, is empty and meaningless
  • Jekyll and Hyde: "My devil had been long caged, he came out roaring" - Hyde as beast, Jekyll's repression
  • An Inspector Calls: "We are members of one body" - society as interconnected organism

Simile

Comparison using "like" or "as" - highlights similarities.

Examples
  • Macbeth: "Like a giant's robe / Upon a dwarfish thief" - Macbeth is unfit for kingship
  • A Christmas Carol: "Solitary as an oyster" - Scrooge is closed off, hard shell hiding vulnerability

Personification

Giving human qualities to non-human things - creates empathy or emphasises power.

Examples
  • Macbeth: "Sleep that knits up the raveled sleave of care" - sleep as healer
  • Jekyll and Hyde: The door "was blistered and distained" - building reflects Hyde's corruption
  • Lord of the Flies: "The sun in the west was a drop of burning gold" - nature as powerful, indifferent

Symbolism

An object that represents a larger idea.

Examples
  • Blood in Macbeth: Symbolises guilt that cannot be washed away
  • The conch in Lord of the Flies: Symbolises civilisation, democracy, order
  • Light/dark in Romeo and Juliet: Love vs hate, hope vs despair

🎭 Rhetorical Devices

Repetition

Repeating words or phrases for emphasis.

Examples
  • Macbeth: "Sleep no more! Macbeth does murder sleep" - emphasises eternal guilt
  • An Inspector Calls: "We are responsible for each other" repeated message
  • Romeo and Juliet: "O" repeated - emotional extremity

Triples/Rule of Three

Three items for rhythm and emphasis.

Examples
  • A Christmas Carol: "squeezing, wrenching, grasping" - emphasises Scrooge's cruelty
  • An Inspector Calls: "fire and blood and anguish" - apocalyptic warning

Antithesis

Contrasting ideas in parallel structure.

Examples
  • Macbeth: "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" - establishes theme of appearance vs reality
  • Jekyll and Hyde: "Good and evil" - central duality

Oxymoron

Two contradictory terms together.

Examples
  • Romeo and Juliet: "O brawling love! O loving hate!" - love and hate intertwined
  • Macbeth: "dagger of the mind" - psychological horror, hallucination

Rhetorical Questions

Questions not expecting answers - make readers think.

Examples
  • Macbeth: "Is this a dagger which I see before me?" - uncertainty, psychological torment
  • An Inspector Calls: "But just remember this..." - Inspector challenges audience

🔊 Sound Devices

Alliteration

Repetition of consonant sounds - creates rhythm, emphasises words.

Examples
  • Macbeth: "doubly redoubling strokes upon the foe" - mimics violent action
  • A Christmas Carol: "squeezing, wrenching, grasping, scraping, clutching" - harsh 's' and 'g' sounds reflect Scrooge's harshness

Sibilance

Repetition of 's' sounds - creates sinister, soft, or seductive effects.

Examples
  • Macbeth: "safe in a ditch" - Lady Macbeth's mocking tone
  • Jekyll and Hyde: "slept" and "smiling" - creates sinister calm

Onomatopoeia

Words that sound like what they describe.

Examples
  • Macbeth: "shriek" - sudden, jarring sound of horror
  • Lord of the Flies: "buzz" of flies - decay, corruption

📊 Tone and Mood

Tone

The writer's attitude toward the subject - revealed through word choice.

Tone How It's Created Example
Critical Harsh, negative vocabulary Dickins describing Scrooge's greed
Sympathetic Compassionate, understanding words Narrator describing Tiny Tim
Mocking Sarcastic, belittling language Lady Macbeth to Macbeth
Ominous Dark, foreboding vocabulary Witches' speeches
Triumphant Celebratory, powerful language Malcolm's final speech

Mood

The emotional atmosphere created for the reader.

  • Created through setting, imagery, dialogue
  • Can shift throughout the text
  • Affects how readers respond emotionally
Mood in "Jekyll and Hyde"

Stevenson creates an oppressive, sinister mood through fog, darkness, silent streets, and threatening atmosphere. This reflects Victorian fears about the hidden evil within respectable society.

✍️ How to Write About Language

Golden Rule: Always focus on EFFECT. Don't just identify techniques - explain what they DO to the reader and how they create meaning.

PEEL Paragraph Structure

  1. Point: Make a specific point about language
  2. Evidence: Use a quotation
  3. Explain: Analyse the language technique and its effect
  4. Link: Connect to themes, context, or writer's purpose
Example PEEL Paragraph

Point: Shakespeare uses violent imagery to show Macbeth's psychological torment.

Evidence: Macbeth asks "Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood / Clean from my hand?"

Explain: The hyperbolic reference to "Neptune's ocean" emphasises the scale of Macbeth's guilt - not even an entire ocean can cleanse him. The metaphor of "blood" representing guilt creates a visceral image of moral stain that cannot be removed.

Link: This reflects the play's exploration of guilt as a destructive force that permanently corrupts the human soul, contrasting with Lady Macbeth's earlier dismissal that "a little water clears us of this deed."

Useful Verbs

suggests, implies, emphasises, highlights, conveys, reveals, creates, establishes, reinforces, contrasts

Effect Words

disturbing, unsettling, shocking, poignant, melancholic, threatening, comforting, authoritative, intimate, dramatic

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Feature spotting Identifying techniques without explaining effect Always ask: "What does this DO?"
Using terminology inaccurately Calling similes metaphors, etc. Learn definitions and practise identifying
Generic comments "This makes the reader feel sad" Be specific: "creates a sense of hopeless inevitability"
Over-quoting Copying long sections without analysis Select key words and embed them
Forgetting effect Describing what the quote means, not its effect Focus on HOW language creates meaning
Spelling terminology wrong Shows lack of knowledge Practise: metaphor, personification, oxymoron

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What's the difference between a metaphor and a simile?

Q2: Analyse the effect of the metaphor "solitary as an oyster" from "A Christmas Carol".

Q3: What is a semantic field? Give an example from a text you've studied.

Q4: How does sibilance affect the reader?

✅ Answers

  1. A metaphor says something IS something else ("life's a walking shadow"). A simile compares using "like" or "as" ("solitary as an oyster"). Metaphors create stronger, more direct connections; similes highlight specific similarities.
  2. The simile compares Scrooge to an oyster - hard, closed shell protecting a soft interior. The word "solitary" emphasises his isolation. "Oyster" suggests both his protective emotional barriers and the possibility of something valuable inside (a pearl) that could be revealed. Dickens uses this to hint that Scrooge might be capable of change.
  3. A semantic field is a group of words related to the same theme or topic. Example from "Macbeth": the semantic field of darkness includes "black," "night," "shadow," "dark," "stars hide your fires" - creating atmosphere of evil and moral blindness.
  4. Sibilance (repeated 's' sounds) can create various effects: softness/seduction (whispering), sinister atmosphere (hissing), or emphasis on particular words. In Macbeth, Lady Macbeth's sibilance ("safe in a ditch") creates a mocking, cruel tone that emphasises her contempt.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Focus on EFFECT over identification
  • Use specific, embedded quotations
  • Use terminology accurately but don't overuse it
  • Link language to themes and context
  • Practise PEEL paragraphs with different techniques
  • Learn 5-10 key quotations and the techniques they use
  • Ask: "Why did the writer choose THIS word?"
