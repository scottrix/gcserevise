Astronomy Exam Technique

Strategies for answering GCSE Astronomy exam questions, including mathematical skills, extended writing and common pitfalls.

Key Fact: AO1: Recall knowledge and understanding (e.g. define, state, name)
Key Fact: AO2: Apply knowledge and understanding to familiar and unfamiliar contexts (e.g. explain, describe)
Key Fact: AO3: Analyse, evaluate and make judgements (e.g. discuss, evaluate, compare)
Key Fact: Mathematical skills: using standard form, unit conversions, ratios, percentages and algebra
Key Fact: Always show your working in calculation questions — marks are awarded for method, not just the answer
Key Fact: Extended writing questions require structured, coherent answers using correct scientific terminology

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Astronomy Exam Technique, you must know:

  • AO1: Assessment Objective 1 — recall and demonstrate knowledge of scientific ideas
  • AO2: Assessment Objective 2 — apply knowledge and understanding to familiar and unfamiliar contexts
  • AO3: Assessment Objective 3 — analyse information and ideas, interpret data, evaluate and make judgements
  • Command word: The instruction in a question that tells you what to do (e.g. describe, explain, compare, evaluate)
  • Standard form: A way of writing very large or small numbers using powers of 10 (e.g. 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s)

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the difference between 'describe' and 'explain'?

Q: How should you approach a 6-mark extended writing question?

Q: Why must you show your working in calculations?

Q: Convert 150,000,000 km into metres using standard form.

Q: What does 'evaluate' mean in an exam question?

✅ Answers

  1. 'Describe' means state what happens (the facts). 'Explain' means say why or how it happens (the reasoning/mechanism).
  2. 1) Read the question carefully and identify the command word. 2) Plan your answer — ensure you cover all aspects. 3) Write in structured paragraphs using scientific terminology. 4) Include relevant examples, data or diagrams. 5) Address both sides if asked to evaluate or compare.
  3. Marks are awarded for the method/steps, not just the final answer. If you make an arithmetic error but the method is correct, you can still earn most marks.
  4. 150,000,000 km = 1.5 × 10⁸ km = 1.5 × 10¹¹ m (multiply by 1,000 to convert km to m).
  5. Consider evidence for and against a statement, weigh up the strengths and weaknesses, and reach a supported conclusion.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Command words are critical: 'describe' ≠ 'explain' — know the difference
  • Both AQA and Edexcel use AO1/AO2/AO3 marking — target each in your answers
  • In maths questions: write the formula, substitute values, calculate, include units
  • Extended writing: use connectives (therefore, because, however, as a result) for coherence
  • Check your answer is reasonable — a planet's temperature of 10,000°C would be wrong for most planets

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Astronomy Exam Tips:
Use precise astronomical terminology in all answers. For calculation questions, always show your working and include units. When explaining observations, reference specific evidence or data. For extended questions, use structured paragraphs with clear scientific reasoning. Both AQA and Edexcel reward answers that demonstrate understanding of the nature of astronomy as an observational science.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Just writing the final answer in a calculation gives full marks ✓ You must show your working — method marks can be awarded even if the final answer is wrong

✗ 'Describe' and 'explain' require the same type of answer ✓ 'Describe' = what happens; 'explain' = why/how it happens — many students lose marks by describing when asked to explain

✗ You don't need units in calculations ✓ Omitting units loses marks — always include the correct unit with numerical answers

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how to approach a 6-mark 'evaluate' question in GCSE Astronomy. [6 marks]

An 'evaluate' question requires you to weigh evidence and reach a supported judgement. First, identify exactly what is being evaluated and the command word. Plan your response: you need to present both sides of the argument with evidence. Begin with the strongest points for one position, using specific scientific evidence (e.g. data, examples or named studies). Then present counterarguments or limitations with equal rigour. Use scientific terminology accurately and consistently. Connect your points logically using discourse markers: 'On one hand...', 'However...', 'Furthermore...', 'Therefore...'. After presenting both sides, reach a clear, justified conclusion — do not simply repeat points or sit on the fence. Your conclusion should directly answer the question and be supported by the evidence you have presented. For example, if evaluating the feasibility of a crewed Mars mission, discuss the benefits (scientific return, inspiration, technological spin-offs) AND the challenges (radiation, cost, life support, psychological effects), then conclude with a reasoned judgement. Aim for balanced coverage of at least three developed points to access the full mark range.
