Organisational Structures

How businesses organise their workforce: organisational structures (hierarchical, flat, matrix), spans of control, chains of command, delegation, centralisation and decentralisation, and the impact of structure on communication and motivation.

Key Fact: A hierarchical structure has many layers of management: clear chain of command but slow decision-making and communication can be distorted through layers.
Key Fact: A flat structure has few management layers: faster communication and decision-making, more responsibility for employees, but managers have wider spans of control (more direct reports).
Key Fact: Span of control is the number of subordinates a manager directly supervises: narrow span = closer supervision but more management layers; wide span = less supervision, fewer layers.
Key Fact: Chain of command is the line of authority from top to bottom: in a hierarchy, instructions flow down and reports flow up. Delayering removes middle management layers to speed up communication.
Key Fact: Centralisation means decisions are made at the top; decentralisation means decisions are delegated to lower levels. Centralisation = consistency; decentralisation = faster, more responsive decisions.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two advantages and two disadvantages of a flat organisational structure.

Q2: Describe the difference between centralisation and decentralisation, and give one benefit of each.

Q3: Evaluate whether a large business should delayer its hierarchical structure.

✅ Answers

  1. Advantages of flat structure: faster decision-making (fewer layers to consult), better communication (less distortion), employees have more responsibility and autonomy (improves motivation). Disadvantages: wide span of control means managers may be overstretched, less opportunity for promotion (fewer management levels), may lack clear authority for complex decisions.
  2. Centralisation: decisions made by senior management at headquarters. Benefit: consistency across all branches/departments, senior leaders have the big picture. Decentralisation: decisions made by local managers. Benefit: faster responses to local conditions, local managers understand their market better, develops leadership skills in junior managers.
  3. Arguments for delayering: removes expensive middle management (cost savings), speeds up communication between top and front line, empowers remaining staff, makes the organisation more agile. Arguments against: loss of experienced middle managers, wider spans of control may overwhelm senior managers, demotivation of remaining staff (more responsibility without more pay), possible loss of specialist expertise. Conclusion: delayering can work if combined with empowerment and training, but done hastily it can create chaos and damage morale.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always link structure to communication: more layers = slower and more distorted communication.
  • When evaluating structures, consider the SIZE of the business: what works for a startup may not work for a multinational.
  • Delayering is NOT the same as downsizing: delayering removes management layers, downsizing reduces total staff.

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When evaluating organisational structures, use the FID framework: Flexibility (can the business adapt?), Information flow (how fast and accurate is communication?), Decision-making (are decisions timely and appropriate?). Structure should serve strategy, not the other way around.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: A flat organisational structure is always better than a hierarchical one because it gives employees more freedom. Correct: Flat structures work well for small, creative businesses where employees are skilled and self-motivated. However, large organisations (hospitals, military, manufacturing) often need hierarchy for clear accountability, consistent standards, and managing large workforces. A hospital with a flat structure could lead to confused responsibilities in emergencies. The best structure depends on the business's size, industry, and culture.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether a rapidly growing tech startup should change from a flat structure to a hierarchical one as it expands from 20 to 200 employees.

A grade 9 response will: analyse flat structure benefits at 20 employees (fast decisions, close team, innovation, shared ownership); problems at 200 (founder overwhelmed, inconsistent decisions, unclear accountability, communication breaks down); hierarchical benefits at scale (clear roles, span of control manageable, career progression); drawbacks (bureaucracy, slower innovation, silos); conclude: some structural adaptation is necessary but a full hierarchy may kill the innovative culture - a hybrid with light management layers plus cross-functional teams (matrix elements) could preserve agility while adding needed coordination.
