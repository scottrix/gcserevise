Recruitment and Selection

How businesses recruit and select staff: internal vs external recruitment, job descriptions, person specifications, application forms, CVs, interviews, assessment centres, and the recruitment process.

Key Fact: Internal recruitment fills vacancies from within the business (promotion, transfer): quicker, cheaper, motivating for staff, but limits the talent pool and may cause resentment if not transparent.
Key Fact: External recruitment brings in new people from outside: wider talent pool, fresh ideas, but more expensive and time-consuming (advertising, interviewing) and may demotivate existing staff who wanted the role.
Key Fact: A job description lists the duties and responsibilities of the role; a person specification lists the skills, qualifications, and experience required. Both are essential for fair, effective recruitment.
Key Fact: Selection methods: interviews (most common but subjective), application forms/CVs (screening), assessment centres (observe candidates in tasks), aptitude tests (measure specific skills), and references (verify candidate claims).
Key Fact: The recruitment process: identify vacancy → write job description and person specification → advertise → shortlist → interview/assess → appoint → induct. Each stage has legal requirements (equality, non-discrimination).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Compare internal and external recruitment, giving two advantages of each.

Q2: Explain why a job description and person specification are both needed in the recruitment process.

Q3: Evaluate whether a business should always use external recruitment for senior management positions.

✅ Answers

  1. Internal recruitment advantages: quicker (already knows the business), cheaper (no advertising or onboarding), motivating (shows promotion is possible), known performance. External recruitment advantages: wider talent pool (more candidates), fresh perspectives and ideas, avoids internal resentment/politics, brings new skills the business lacks.
  2. The job description defines WHAT the role involves (duties, responsibilities, reporting lines, salary) so candidates know what they are applying for. The person specification defines WHO is needed (qualifications, skills, experience, qualities) so the business can fairly assess candidates against consistent criteria. Together they ensure: candidates can self-assess suitability, shortlisting is objective, interviews focus on relevant criteria, and discrimination is minimised.
  3. Arguments for external recruitment: brings fresh strategic thinking, wider experience of different industries, avoids 'groupthink' from promoting only insiders, can introduce new networks and contacts. Arguments for internal: person already understands culture and operations, less risky (known performance), faster to start, motivating for other staff. Conclusion: most effective approach is often a mix - consider internal candidates first but advertise externally too, ensuring the best person is appointed regardless of origin.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always distinguish between recruitment (attracting candidates) and selection (choosing the best one).
  • When comparing internal vs external, consider cost, speed, quality of candidate, and impact on existing staff.
  • Legal requirements: the Equality Act 2010 makes it illegal to discriminate in recruitment on grounds of age, gender, race, disability, etc.

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When evaluating recruitment methods, use the CQR framework: Cost (how much does the process cost?), Quality (does it find the best candidate?), Risk (what if the wrong person is hired?). A bad recruitment decision costs far more than a thorough recruitment process.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: External recruitment is always better because it brings in fresh ideas. Correct: External recruitment can bring fresh ideas but also carries risks: the candidate may not fit the culture, takes longer to become productive (learning curve), may leave quickly if the reality doesn't match the interview, and demotivates existing staff who expected promotion. Internal candidates already understand the business, have proven track records, and integrate immediately. Many successful businesses prefer internal promotion where possible, using external recruitment only when specific new skills are needed that don't exist internally.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether a small business should use an expensive recruitment agency to hire a new manager, rather than advertising on its own website and social media.

A grade 9 response will: analyse the agency route (access to pre-screened candidates, saves management time, professional expertise, but costs 15-25% of salary); direct advertising (cheaper, shows independence, but limited reach, time-consuming screening, owner may lack interview skills); consider business context (small business needs the right person urgently, a bad hire is very costly for a small team, but the agency fee could be £5,000-£10,000); conclude: for a critical management role, the agency investment is justified because the cost of a poor hire (lost productivity, re-recruitment, team disruption) far exceeds the agency fee. For less critical roles, direct advertising may suffice.
