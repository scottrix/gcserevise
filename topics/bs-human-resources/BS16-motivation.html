Motivation

How businesses motivate employees: financial motivation (wages, salaries, commission, profit sharing, fringe benefits), non-financial motivation (job rotation, job enrichment, empowerment, teamworking), and motivational theory (Maslow, Herzberg).

Key Fact: Financial motivation methods: wages (hourly pay), salaries (fixed annual pay), commission (percentage of sales), profit sharing (share of profits), bonuses (one-off rewards), fringe benefits (perks like company car, healthcare, gym membership).
Key Fact: Non-financial motivation methods: job rotation (varying tasks), job enrichment (giving more responsibility and challenge), empowerment (giving authority to make decisions), teamworking (collaborative projects), and opportunities for promotion and training.
Key Fact: Maslow's hierarchy of needs: physiological → safety → social → esteem → self-actualisation. Lower needs must be met before higher ones motivate. A wage meets physiological/safety needs; empowerment addresses esteem/self-actualisation.
Key Fact: Herzberg's two-factor theory: hygiene factors (pay, conditions, security) prevent dissatisfaction but don't motivate; motivators (achievement, recognition, responsibility) create true motivation. Improving hygiene factors alone won't increase motivation.
Key Fact: Motivated employees are more productive, provide better customer service, take less sick leave, and are less likely to leave - reducing recruitment costs.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between financial and non-financial motivation, giving two examples of each.

Q2: Describe how Maslow's hierarchy of needs can help a business understand what motivates its employees.

Q3: Evaluate whether a business should focus more on financial rewards or non-financial methods to motivate staff.

✅ Answers

  1. Financial motivation involves monetary rewards: wages (paid per hour worked), salaries (fixed annual amount), commission (percentage of sales achieved), bonuses (one-off performance payments). Non-financial motivation involves non-monetary rewards: job enrichment (more challenging work), empowerment (decision-making authority), job rotation (variety of tasks), fringe benefits like flexible working, praise, and promotion opportunities.
  2. Maslow's hierarchy shows needs in order: physiological (basic pay for food/shelter) → safety (job security, safe conditions) → social (teamworking, belonging) → esteem (recognition, promotion) → self-actualisation (fulfilling potential). A business must ensure lower needs are met first - a pay rise addresses physiological needs, but once met, higher needs like recognition and empowerment become more motivating. A worker earning minimum wage won't be motivated by team-building events.
  3. Financial rewards: attract and retain staff, meet basic needs, easily measured and compared, but can be expensive and their motivational effect fades (people adapt to higher pay). Non-financial methods: lower cost, address higher-level needs, create lasting motivation and job satisfaction, but harder to implement, vary between individuals, and won't work if basic pay is inadequate. Conclusion: an effective approach combines both - financial rewards must be fair and competitive first (hygiene factors), then non-financial methods (motivators) create genuine engagement and commitment.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always apply Maslow and Herzberg to the specific scenario: which level of needs is currently unmet?
  • Financial rewards are hygiene factors in Herzberg's theory - they prevent dissatisfaction but don't create lasting motivation.
  • When evaluating motivation, consider: different employees are motivated by different things (age, career stage, personal circumstances).

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When evaluating motivation strategies, use the HMF framework: Hygiene factors (are pay and conditions adequate?), Motivators (is there challenge, recognition, autonomy?), Fit (does the approach suit the individual and the business?). The best motivation strategies address both hygiene and motivator factors.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Money is the best motivator for all employees. Correct: While money is essential (meeting physiological and safety needs in Maslow's hierarchy), Herzberg showed it is only a hygiene factor - preventing dissatisfaction, not creating motivation. Studies consistently show that once pay is fair, factors like recognition, autonomy, meaningful work, and development opportunities are more powerful motivators. A pay rise motivates temporarily; interesting work motivates continuously. Google, for example, motivates through challenging projects and 20% free time, not just high salaries.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate how a call centre with high staff turnover could use both financial and non-financial methods to improve motivation and retention.

A grade 9 response will: identify problems (repetitive work, low pay, high targets, lack of progression); financial solutions (competitive salary, attendance bonuses, performance commission); non-financial solutions (job rotation between campaigns, team competitions, empowerment to resolve customer issues without escalation, clear promotion path, social events); apply Herzberg (pay rise = hygiene factor, reduces dissatisfaction but doesn't motivate long-term); apply Maslow (once basic pay is adequate, social and esteem needs become more important); conclude: a balanced approach is needed - raise pay to competitive levels first, then implement non-financial methods (empowerment, progression, team belonging) for sustained motivation and retention.
