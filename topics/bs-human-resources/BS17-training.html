Training

How businesses train their workforce: induction training, on-the-job training, off-the-job training, the benefits and drawbacks of each, and how training links to motivation and competitiveness.

Key Fact: Induction training introduces new employees to the business: health and safety, company culture, policies, and basic procedures. It helps new starters settle in quickly and feel valued.
Key Fact: On-the-job training: learning by doing the actual job, guided by an experienced colleague or mentor. It's practical, relevant, and cheap, but the trainer may pass on bad habits and production may slow during training.
Key Fact: Off-the-job training: learning away from the workplace (college courses, workshops, online programmes). It provides broader skills and professional qualifications, but is more expensive and may not be directly relevant to the specific job.
Key Fact: Training benefits: improved skills and productivity, better quality, fewer mistakes and accidents, increased motivation (shows investment in staff), and greater flexibility (multi-skilled workforce can cover different roles).
Key Fact: Training drawbacks: costs (course fees, lost production time, cover staff), trained staff may leave for better jobs elsewhere, some training may not be relevant, and effectiveness can be hard to measure.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between on-the-job and off-the-job training, giving one advantage and one disadvantage of each.

Q2: Analyse how training can improve a business's competitiveness.

Q3: Evaluate whether a business should train its existing staff or recruit new employees who already have the required skills.

✅ Answers

  1. On-the-job training: learning at the workplace while doing the job. Advantage: directly relevant to the role, no travel costs, immediate application. Disadvantage: trainer may pass on bad habits, production may slow while training, no formal qualification. Off-the-job training: learning away from the workplace. Advantage: professional instruction, recognised qualifications, broader knowledge, fresh perspectives. Disadvantage: expensive, time away from work, may not be specific to the business.
  2. Training improves competitiveness by: increasing productivity (skilled workers work faster with fewer errors), improving quality (reducing returns and complaints), enabling innovation (skilled staff can adopt new technologies), enhancing customer service (well-trained staff represent the brand better), and reducing costs (fewer accidents, less waste, lower staff turnover). A business with well-trained staff can outperform competitors who invest less in development.
  3. Training existing staff: cheaper than recruitment, shows commitment to employees (improves retention and motivation), staff already understand the business culture and processes, but takes time and they may lack aptitude for new skills. Recruiting skilled staff: immediately productive, brings fresh expertise, but expensive (recruitment costs, higher salaries), may not fit the culture, demotivates existing staff who feel overlooked. Conclusion: for skills closely related to existing work, training is better; for completely new specialisms the business lacks entirely, external recruitment may be necessary. A blended approach is often most effective.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always link training to specific business outcomes: productivity, quality, motivation, competitiveness.
  • When evaluating training, consider the COST vs the RETURN: training is an investment, not just an expense.
  • Don't forget: trained staff may leave - this is a key drawback to address in evaluation questions.

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When evaluating training decisions, use the RIDE framework: Relevance (is the training directly useful?), Investment (what does it cost vs what does it return?), Development (does it build long-term capability?), Engagement (does it motivate staff?). The best training programmes balance immediate skill needs with long-term workforce development.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Off-the-job training is always better than on-the-job training because it gives employees recognised qualifications. Correct: Off-the-job training provides qualifications but may not be directly relevant to the specific job or business. On-the-job training is practical, immediately applicable, and tailored to the actual work. Many roles (chef, mechanic, electrician) rely heavily on on-the-job learning because the skills are best developed through practice. The most effective training programmes combine both: on-the-job for practical skills and off-the-job for theoretical knowledge and qualifications.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether a small catering business should send its chefs on an expensive off-the-job training course or train them on-the-job through mentoring by the head chef.

A grade 9 response will: analyse off-the-job training (professional qualification, new techniques, networking with other chefs, but expensive, time away, may not cover the specific menu and style); on-the-job mentoring (tailored to the restaurant's cuisine, cheap, immediate application, but limited to head chef's knowledge, may reinforce bad habits, no formal qualification); consider business context (small business with limited budget, head chef is experienced, customer expectations); conclude: a combined approach works best - send one chef on an off-the-job course to learn new techniques and bring knowledge back, while the rest learn on-the-job from the head chef. This balances cost, quality, and innovation.
