Technology in Business

How technology affects business: e-commerce, digital communication, automation, electronic point of sale (EPOS), data security, and the impact of technological change on businesses and stakeholders.

Key Fact: E-commerce allows businesses to sell online 24/7, reaching a global market with lower overhead costs than a physical shop.
Key Fact: Digital communication (email, video conferencing, social media) speeds up decision-making and enables remote/flexible working.
Key Fact: Automation and robotics increase productivity and consistency but reduce unskilled jobs and require high capital investment.
Key Fact: EPOS (Electronic Point of Sale) tracks sales in real time, manages stock levels automatically, and provides data for marketing decisions.
Key Fact: Data security is critical: businesses must comply with GDPR, protect customer data from cyberattacks, and have backup systems.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two ways e-commerce has changed how businesses sell to customers.

Q2: Analyse the impact of automation on a manufacturing business and its workers.

Q3: Evaluate whether a small retail business should invest in an online store.

✅ Answers

  1. E-commerce enables 24/7 trading without shop opening hours, reaches customers worldwide (not just local), reduces costs of rent and staff for physical premises, but requires website maintenance, delivery logistics, and faces online competition.
  2. Positive impacts: higher output, consistent quality, lower long-term labour costs, 24/7 production. Negative impacts: high initial investment, redundancy and morale issues for displaced workers, need for skilled technicians, potential breakdowns halting production.
  3. Arguments for: wider customer reach, lower overheads, trend towards online shopping (25%+ of UK retail), ability to compete with larger firms. Arguments against: website development costs, delivery management, returns handling, cybersecurity risks, existing loyal local customers. Conclusion depends on financial resources and market research - most businesses now need some online presence to remain competitive.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When evaluating technology, always consider both the benefits AND the costs/risks - no technology is purely positive.
  • Use stakeholder analysis: technology may benefit owners (lower costs) but harm workers (job losses).
  • Link technology to competitiveness: businesses that fail to adapt risk losing market share.

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When discussing technology's impact, use the CED framework: Cost (investment vs savings), Efficiency (productivity gains), Disruption (effects on workers, customers, competitors). Always consider short-term costs versus long-term benefits.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Technology always benefits businesses because it reduces costs. Correct: Technology can increase costs initially (equipment, training, maintenance) and may alienate customers who prefer personal service. Some technologies become obsolete quickly, requiring further investment. The key question is whether benefits outweigh costs in the specific business context.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether a traditional bookshop should invest in an e-commerce website.

A grade 9 response will: identify financial factors (website costs £2,000-£10,000, ongoing hosting, potential revenue increase); customer factors (convenience, wider reach, but some prefer browsing); competitive factors (Amazon dominance, differentiation through events/advice); operational factors (delivery logistics, stock management, returns). Conclude with a justified view: a hybrid model may work best - online presence to capture wider market while maintaining the physical shop's unique selling point of personal service and community.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AQA GCSE Business 8132: 2 papers x 90 marks, each 50%. AOs: AO1 Demonstrate knowledge (35%), AO2 Apply knowledge (35%), AO3 Analyse and evaluate (30%). For grade 9, demonstrate perceptive analysis, sophisticated evaluation of trade-offs, and fluent use of business terminology.
