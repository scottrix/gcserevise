Technology in Business

How technology affects business: e-commerce, digital communication, automation, electronic point of sale (EPOS), data security, and the impact of technological change on businesses and stakeholders.

Key Fact: E-commerce allows businesses to sell online 24/7, reaching a global market with lower overhead costs than a physical shop.
Key Fact: Digital communication (email, video conferencing, social media) speeds up decision-making and enables remote/flexible working.
Key Fact: Automation and robotics increase productivity and consistency but reduce unskilled jobs and require high capital investment.
Key Fact: EPOS (Electronic Point of Sale) tracks sales in real time, manages stock levels automatically, and provides data for marketing decisions.
Key Fact: Data security is critical: businesses must comply with GDPR, protect customer data from cyberattacks, and have backup systems.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two ways e-commerce has changed how businesses sell to customers.

Q2: Analyse the impact of automation on a manufacturing business and its workers.

Q3: Evaluate whether a small retail business should invest in an online store.

✅ Answers

  1. E-commerce enables 24/7 trading without shop opening hours, reaches customers worldwide (not just local), reduces costs of rent and staff for physical premises, but requires website maintenance, delivery logistics, and faces online competition.
  2. Positive impacts: higher output, consistent quality, lower long-term labour costs, 24/7 production. Negative impacts: high initial investment, redundancy and morale issues for displaced workers, need for skilled technicians, potential breakdowns halting production.
  3. Arguments for: wider customer reach, lower overheads, trend towards online shopping (25%+ of UK retail), ability to compete with larger firms. Arguments against: website development costs, delivery management, returns handling, cybersecurity risks, existing loyal local customers. Conclusion depends on financial resources and market research - most businesses now need some online presence to remain competitive.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When evaluating technology, always consider both the benefits AND the costs/risks - no technology is purely positive.
  • Use stakeholder analysis: technology may benefit owners (lower costs) but harm workers (job losses).
  • Link technology to competitiveness: businesses that fail to adapt risk losing market share.

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When discussing technology's impact, use the CED framework: Cost (investment vs savings), Efficiency (productivity gains), Disruption (effects on workers, customers, competitors). Always consider short-term costs versus long-term benefits.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Technology always benefits businesses because it reduces costs. Correct: Technology can increase costs initially (equipment, training, maintenance) and may alienate customers who prefer personal service. Some technologies become obsolete quickly, requiring further investment. The key question is whether benefits outweigh costs in the specific business context.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether a traditional bookshop should invest in an e-commerce website.

A grade 9 response will: identify financial factors (website costs £2,000-£10,000, ongoing hosting, potential revenue increase); customer factors (convenience, wider reach, but some prefer browsing); competitive factors (Amazon dominance, differentiation through events/advice); operational factors (delivery logistics, stock management, returns). Conclude with a justified view: a hybrid model may work best - online presence to capture wider market while maintaining the physical shop's unique selling point of personal service and community.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Technology in Business in Business Context

Technology in Business is a key topic in GCSE Business Studies that affects how businesses operate in the UK economy. Understanding this concept requires knowledge of both theoretical principles and real-world application. The AQA specification requires you to explain, analyse and evaluate business concepts using appropriate terminology, and apply them to real business scenarios using UK examples.

When writing about technology in business in GCSE exams, always use precise business terminology, support your points with specific UK business examples, and explain the cause-and-effect relationships clearly. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of how business concepts interact in practice, not just textbook definitions.

Real-world context is essential: technology in business affects sole traders differently from multinational corporations, and startups differently from established firms. Always consider the specific business context when applying your knowledge.

GCSE Example: Understanding Technology in Business in Business Context

A strong GCSE Business answer about technology in business would define the concept precisely, apply it to a real UK business example, analyse the impact on that business, and evaluate the significance considering both advantages and disadvantages.

Applying Technology in Business to UK Business Examples

For technology in business, applying theory to practice means using real UK business examples to illustrate your understanding. Small businesses face different challenges from large corporations when dealing with technology in business. Consider how factors like business size, ownership structure, industry sector and market position affect the impact of technology in business on a business.

UK business examples you could reference: small sole traders like local shops; private limited companies like Virgin Active; public limited companies like Tesco and BP; and social enterprises like The Big Issue. Each type of business responds differently to technology in business based on its resources, objectives and competitive position.

Quantitative skills are important: many business concepts can be expressed numerically. When discussing technology in business, use calculations, percentages and financial data where appropriate to support your analysis. The AQA specification requires you to interpret and use quantitative data.

GCSE Example: Applying Technology in Business to UK Business Examples

When evaluating technology in business, use a structured approach: define the concept, apply it to a specific UK business, analyse the impact (positive and negative), and evaluate the overall significance with a justified conclusion.

Exam Technique for Technology in Business Questions

GCSE Business exam questions about technology in business range from 1-mark definition questions to 12-mark evaluation questions. For definition questions (1-2 marks), give a precise business definition. For explanation questions (3-4 marks), define and explain with a brief example. For analysis questions (6-9 marks), develop a chain of reasoning showing cause and effect. For evaluation questions (12 marks), present both sides, use evidence, and reach a justified conclusion.

For the highest marks on evaluation questions, you must: present balanced arguments (advantages AND disadvantages); support each point with a specific business example; show the connections between points (how one factor affects another); and reach a conclusion that directly answers the question with justification.

Common mistakes: writing everything you know about a topic without answering the specific question; failing to use business terminology; not providing specific examples; and not reaching a justified conclusion for evaluation questions.

GCSE Example: Exam Technique for Technology in Business Questions

A 12-mark evaluation answer structure: Introduction (define technology in business), Argument for (with UK example and analysis), Argument against (with UK example and analysis), Evaluation (which is more significant and why), Conclusion (direct answer with justification).

Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain how technology in business affects a UK business, using a specific example.

A: Technology in Business has a significant impact on UK businesses. For example, a specific UK business experienced measurable effects because of factors related to technology in business. This demonstrates that businesses must respond strategically to remain competitive. A strong GCSE answer would use precise business terminology, reference a real UK business, and explain the cause-and-effect chain clearly.

Q: Evaluate the importance of technology in business for a small business compared to a large business.

A: The importance of technology in business differs between business sizes. Small businesses may face greater challenges because they have fewer resources, less market power, and limited expertise. Large businesses can leverage greater resources and economies of scale. However, small businesses may also have advantages such as flexibility and agility. A balanced evaluation considers both perspectives before reaching a justified conclusion.
