Ethical and Environmental Considerations

How ethical and environmental considerations influence business decisions: corporate social responsibility (CSR), sustainability, fair trade, pressure groups, environmental legislation, and the trade-off between ethics and profit.

Key Fact: Corporate social responsibility (CSR) means businesses consider their impact on society and the environment, not just profit.
Key Fact: Ethical issues include: fair pay and working conditions, avoiding sweatshops, honest advertising, paying fair taxes, and sourcing materials responsibly.
Key Fact: Environmental considerations include: reducing carbon footprint, recycling, sustainable sourcing, minimising waste, and complying with environmental laws.
Key Fact: Pressure groups can influence business behaviour through campaigns, boycotts, and negative publicity - e.g. Greenpeace targeting companies with poor environmental records.
Key Fact: Fair trade ensures producers in developing countries receive a fair price, improving living standards and community investment.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain what is meant by corporate social responsibility and give two examples of how a business might demonstrate it.

Q2: Analyse how pressure groups can influence a business's decisions.

Q3: Evaluate whether a clothing retailer should switch to using only fair trade cotton, even though it will increase costs by 15%.

✅ Answers

  1. CSR means a business takes responsibility for its effects on stakeholders and society beyond legal requirements. Examples: sourcing sustainable materials, paying the living wage, supporting local charities, reducing packaging waste.
  2. Pressure groups use tactics such as: organising boycotts (reducing sales), media campaigns (damaging reputation), lobbying government (leading to stricter regulation), and protests outside premises. Businesses may respond by changing practices to avoid negative publicity and maintain customer loyalty.
  3. Arguments for: ethical reputation attracts conscious consumers (growing market), improves brand loyalty, motivates employees who value ethics, reduces risk of scandals, meets CSR commitments. Arguments against: 15% cost increase may reduce profit margins, prices may rise making products less competitive, fair trade certification has administrative costs, not all customers will pay more. Conclusion: short-term profit decrease may be offset by long-term brand value and customer loyalty, especially as ethical consumerism grows.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always consider the trade-off between ethical behaviour and profit - this is central to AQA questions.
  • Use specific examples when discussing ethics: fair trade, living wage, sustainable sourcing, not just vague statements.
  • Link ethics to competitiveness: ethical businesses may attract customers and staff, improving long-term performance.

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When evaluating ethical decisions, use the SPIR framework: Stakeholders (who is affected?), Profit (financial impact?), Image (reputation effect?), Responsibility (legal/moral obligations?). Conclude by weighing short-term costs against long-term benefits.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Ethical businesses always make less profit because ethical choices cost more. Correct: Ethical choices can increase costs short-term but may boost revenue long-term through: brand loyalty from conscious consumers, reduced staff turnover, avoiding fines/scandals, and attracting investors who value ESG criteria. Some ethical businesses (e.g. The Body Shop, Patagonia) have built highly profitable brands on ethical foundations.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether a coffee shop chain should commit to using only Fairtrade coffee beans.

A grade 9 response will: consider cost implications (Fairtrade premium of ~10%), customer response (some willing to pay more, others price-sensitive), competitive position (differentiation vs cost disadvantage), supplier relationships (more stable, better quality), employee motivation (proud to work for ethical brand). Conclude: the Fairtrade premium is a worthwhile investment for a chain targeting socially conscious consumers, as the brand differentiation and loyalty offset higher costs. For a budget chain competing on price, the cost increase may be harder to absorb.

Understanding Ethical and Environmental Considerations in Business Context

Ethical and Environmental Considerations is a key topic in GCSE Business Studies that affects how businesses operate in the UK economy. Understanding this concept requires knowledge of both theoretical principles and real-world application. The AQA specification requires you to explain, analyse and evaluate business concepts using appropriate terminology, and apply them to real business scenarios using UK examples.

When writing about ethical and environmental considerations in GCSE exams, always use precise business terminology, support your points with specific UK business examples, and explain the cause-and-effect relationships clearly. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of how business concepts interact in practice, not just textbook definitions.

Real-world context is essential: ethical and environmental considerations affects sole traders differently from multinational corporations, and startups differently from established firms. Always consider the specific business context when applying your knowledge.

A strong GCSE Business answer about ethical and environmental considerations would define the concept precisely, apply it to a real UK business example, analyse the impact on that business, and evaluate the significance considering both advantages and disadvantages.

Applying Ethical and Environmental Considerations to UK Business Examples

For ethical and environmental considerations, applying theory to practice means using real UK business examples to illustrate your understanding. Small businesses face different challenges from large corporations when dealing with ethical and environmental considerations. Consider how factors like business size, ownership structure, industry sector and market position affect the impact of ethical and environmental considerations on a business.

UK business examples you could reference: small sole traders like local shops; private limited companies like Virgin Active; public limited companies like Tesco and BP; and social enterprises like The Big Issue. Each type of business responds differently to ethical and environmental considerations based on its resources, objectives and competitive position.

Quantitative skills are important: many business concepts can be expressed numerically. When discussing ethical and environmental considerations, use calculations, percentages and financial data where appropriate to support your analysis. The AQA specification requires you to interpret and use quantitative data.

GCSE Example: Applying Ethical and Environmental Considerations to UK Business Examples

When evaluating ethical and environmental considerations, use a structured approach: define the concept, apply it to a specific UK business, analyse the impact (positive and negative), and evaluate the overall significance with a justified conclusion.

Exam Technique for Ethical and Environmental Considerations Questions

GCSE Business exam questions about ethical and environmental considerations range from 1-mark definition questions to 12-mark evaluation questions. For definition questions (1-2 marks), give a precise business definition. For explanation questions (3-4 marks), define and explain with a brief example. For analysis questions (6-9 marks), develop a chain of reasoning showing cause and effect. For evaluation questions (12 marks), present both sides, use evidence, and reach a justified conclusion.

For the highest marks on evaluation questions, you must: present balanced arguments (advantages AND disadvantages); support each point with a specific business example; show the connections between points (how one factor affects another); and reach a conclusion that directly answers the question with justification.

Common mistakes: writing everything you know about a topic without answering the specific question; failing to use business terminology; not providing specific examples; and not reaching a justified conclusion for evaluation questions.

GCSE Example: Exam Technique for Ethical and Environmental Considerations Questions

A 12-mark evaluation answer structure: Introduction (define ethical and environmental considerations), Argument for (with UK example and analysis), Argument against (with UK example and analysis), Evaluation (which is more significant and why), Conclusion (direct answer with justification).

Q: Evaluate the importance of ethical and environmental considerations for a small business compared to a large business.

A: The importance of ethical and environmental considerations differs between business sizes. Small businesses may face greater challenges because they have fewer resources, less market power, and limited expertise. Large businesses can leverage greater resources and economies of scale. However, small businesses may also have advantages such as flexibility and agility. A balanced evaluation considers both perspectives before reaching a justified conclusion.
