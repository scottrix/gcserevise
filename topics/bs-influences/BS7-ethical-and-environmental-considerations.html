Ethical and Environmental Considerations

How ethical and environmental considerations influence business decisions: corporate social responsibility (CSR), sustainability, fair trade, pressure groups, environmental legislation, and the trade-off between ethics and profit.

Key Fact: Corporate social responsibility (CSR) means businesses consider their impact on society and the environment, not just profit.
Key Fact: Ethical issues include: fair pay and working conditions, avoiding sweatshops, honest advertising, paying fair taxes, and sourcing materials responsibly.
Key Fact: Environmental considerations include: reducing carbon footprint, recycling, sustainable sourcing, minimising waste, and complying with environmental laws.
Key Fact: Pressure groups can influence business behaviour through campaigns, boycotts, and negative publicity - e.g. Greenpeace targeting companies with poor environmental records.
Key Fact: Fair trade ensures producers in developing countries receive a fair price, improving living standards and community investment.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain what is meant by corporate social responsibility and give two examples of how a business might demonstrate it.

Q2: Analyse how pressure groups can influence a business's decisions.

Q3: Evaluate whether a clothing retailer should switch to using only fair trade cotton, even though it will increase costs by 15%.

✅ Answers

  1. CSR means a business takes responsibility for its effects on stakeholders and society beyond legal requirements. Examples: sourcing sustainable materials, paying the living wage, supporting local charities, reducing packaging waste.
  2. Pressure groups use tactics such as: organising boycotts (reducing sales), media campaigns (damaging reputation), lobbying government (leading to stricter regulation), and protests outside premises. Businesses may respond by changing practices to avoid negative publicity and maintain customer loyalty.
  3. Arguments for: ethical reputation attracts conscious consumers (growing market), improves brand loyalty, motivates employees who value ethics, reduces risk of scandals, meets CSR commitments. Arguments against: 15% cost increase may reduce profit margins, prices may rise making products less competitive, fair trade certification has administrative costs, not all customers will pay more. Conclusion: short-term profit decrease may be offset by long-term brand value and customer loyalty, especially as ethical consumerism grows.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always consider the trade-off between ethical behaviour and profit - this is central to AQA questions.
  • Use specific examples when discussing ethics: fair trade, living wage, sustainable sourcing, not just vague statements.
  • Link ethics to competitiveness: ethical businesses may attract customers and staff, improving long-term performance.

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When evaluating ethical decisions, use the SPIR framework: Stakeholders (who is affected?), Profit (financial impact?), Image (reputation effect?), Responsibility (legal/moral obligations?). Conclude by weighing short-term costs against long-term benefits.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Ethical businesses always make less profit because ethical choices cost more. Correct: Ethical choices can increase costs short-term but may boost revenue long-term through: brand loyalty from conscious consumers, reduced staff turnover, avoiding fines/scandals, and attracting investors who value ESG criteria. Some ethical businesses (e.g. The Body Shop, Patagonia) have built highly profitable brands on ethical foundations.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether a coffee shop chain should commit to using only Fairtrade coffee beans.

A grade 9 response will: consider cost implications (Fairtrade premium of ~10%), customer response (some willing to pay more, others price-sensitive), competitive position (differentiation vs cost disadvantage), supplier relationships (more stable, better quality), employee motivation (proud to work for ethical brand). Conclude: the Fairtrade premium is a worthwhile investment for a chain targeting socially conscious consumers, as the brand differentiation and loyalty offset higher costs. For a budget chain competing on price, the cost increase may be harder to absorb.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AQA GCSE Business 8132: 2 papers x 90 marks, each 50%. AOs: AO1 Demonstrate knowledge (35%), AO2 Apply knowledge (35%), AO3 Analyse and evaluate (30%). For grade 9, demonstrate perceptive analysis, sophisticated evaluation of trade-offs, and fluent use of business terminology.
