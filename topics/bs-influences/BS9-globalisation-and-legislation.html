Globalisation and Legislation

How globalisation and legislation affect businesses: international trade, multinational corporations, EU and international law, employment law, consumer protection, and health and safety legislation.

Key Fact: Globalisation is the increasing interconnection of world economies through trade, investment, and technology - it means businesses can operate internationally more easily.
Key Fact: Multinational corporations (MNCs) operate in multiple countries: they benefit from lower labour costs and new markets but face cultural differences and complex regulations.
Key Fact: Employment law protects workers: National Minimum Wage, Working Time Regulations, Health and Safety at Work Act, Equality Act (preventing discrimination).
Key Fact: Consumer protection legislation (Consumer Rights Act 2015) ensures products are as described, fit for purpose, and of satisfactory quality - giving consumers the right to refunds, repairs, or replacements.
Key Fact: Legislation creates compliance costs for businesses (training, monitoring, documentation) but also sets minimum standards that protect stakeholders and create a level playing field.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two benefits and two drawbacks of globalisation for a UK business.

Q2: Describe how the Consumer Rights Act 2015 protects customers and affects businesses.

Q3: Evaluate whether employment legislation does more harm than good to businesses.

✅ Answers

  1. Benefits: access to larger markets (billions of potential customers), lower production costs (manufacturing in countries with cheaper labour), economies of scale. Drawbacks: increased competition from foreign firms, cultural and language barriers, risk of supply chain disruption (e.g. political instability), potential reputational damage from overseas practices.
  2. The Act gives consumers the right to: goods that match their description, are fit for purpose, and are of satisfactory quality. If not, consumers can claim a full refund within 30 days, or a repair/replacement after that. Businesses must: ensure quality control, provide accurate descriptions, train staff on returns policies, and maintain records - increasing costs but also building trust and reducing disputes.
  3. Arguments for: protects vulnerable workers from exploitation, reduces inequality, improves health and safety (fewer accidents), creates a motivated workforce, ensures fair competition (no race to the bottom on wages). Arguments against: increases costs (higher wages, admin, training), reduces flexibility, may make UK businesses less competitive than those in countries with weaker laws, can discourage hiring. Conclusion: while compliance costs exist, the benefits of a protected, motivated workforce and consumer trust outweigh the costs - businesses that treat workers well often see higher productivity and lower staff turnover.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When discussing legislation, always identify WHO it protects and WHAT it costs businesses.
  • Globalisation questions need a balanced answer: consider both UK businesses expanding abroad AND foreign businesses entering UK markets.
  • Use specific legislation names (Consumer Rights Act, Equality Act) to show AO1 knowledge.

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When evaluating legislation, use the PEST framework: Political (government policy direction), Economic (compliance costs vs productivity gains), Social (worker and consumer protection), Technological (how laws adapt to new tech). Always conclude by weighing protection benefits against compliance costs.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Globalisation only benefits large multinational corporations. Correct: Globalisation also benefits small businesses through: access to international markets via e-commerce, cheaper imported raw materials, technology that enables remote work and global collaboration, and cultural diversity that inspires new products. Even a sole trader can sell worldwide through platforms like Etsy or Amazon. However, small businesses may struggle to compete with MNCs on price and brand recognition.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether a UK small business should start exporting to the EU after Brexit.

A grade 9 response will: consider opportunities (access to 450M consumers, potential for growth, diversification of risk); challenges (new customs procedures, tariffs, regulatory divergence, currency risk); resources needed (export documentation, logistics, market research, language skills); and conclude with conditions: the business should export if it has a unique product, financial reserves to manage initial costs, and has researched target markets - but may be better starting with just one EU country rather than the entire bloc.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Globalisation and Legislation in Business Context

Globalisation and Legislation is a key topic in GCSE Business Studies that affects how businesses operate in the UK economy. Understanding this concept requires knowledge of both theoretical principles and real-world application. The AQA specification requires you to explain, analyse and evaluate business concepts using appropriate terminology, and apply them to real business scenarios using UK examples.

When writing about globalisation and legislation in GCSE exams, always use precise business terminology, support your points with specific UK business examples, and explain the cause-and-effect relationships clearly. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of how business concepts interact in practice, not just textbook definitions.

Real-world context is essential: globalisation and legislation affects sole traders differently from multinational corporations, and startups differently from established firms. Always consider the specific business context when applying your knowledge.

GCSE Example: Understanding Globalisation and Legislation in Business Context

A strong GCSE Business answer about globalisation and legislation would define the concept precisely, apply it to a real UK business example, analyse the impact on that business, and evaluate the significance considering both advantages and disadvantages.

Applying Globalisation and Legislation to UK Business Examples

For globalisation and legislation, applying theory to practice means using real UK business examples to illustrate your understanding. Small businesses face different challenges from large corporations when dealing with globalisation and legislation. Consider how factors like business size, ownership structure, industry sector and market position affect the impact of globalisation and legislation on a business.

UK business examples you could reference: small sole traders like local shops; private limited companies like Virgin Active; public limited companies like Tesco and BP; and social enterprises like The Big Issue. Each type of business responds differently to globalisation and legislation based on its resources, objectives and competitive position.

Quantitative skills are important: many business concepts can be expressed numerically. When discussing globalisation and legislation, use calculations, percentages and financial data where appropriate to support your analysis. The AQA specification requires you to interpret and use quantitative data.

GCSE Example: Applying Globalisation and Legislation to UK Business Examples

When evaluating globalisation and legislation, use a structured approach: define the concept, apply it to a specific UK business, analyse the impact (positive and negative), and evaluate the overall significance with a justified conclusion.

Exam Technique for Globalisation and Legislation Questions

GCSE Business exam questions about globalisation and legislation range from 1-mark definition questions to 12-mark evaluation questions. For definition questions (1-2 marks), give a precise business definition. For explanation questions (3-4 marks), define and explain with a brief example. For analysis questions (6-9 marks), develop a chain of reasoning showing cause and effect. For evaluation questions (12 marks), present both sides, use evidence, and reach a justified conclusion.

For the highest marks on evaluation questions, you must: present balanced arguments (advantages AND disadvantages); support each point with a specific business example; show the connections between points (how one factor affects another); and reach a conclusion that directly answers the question with justification.

Common mistakes: writing everything you know about a topic without answering the specific question; failing to use business terminology; not providing specific examples; and not reaching a justified conclusion for evaluation questions.

GCSE Example: Exam Technique for Globalisation and Legislation Questions

A 12-mark evaluation answer structure: Introduction (define globalisation and legislation), Argument for (with UK example and analysis), Argument against (with UK example and analysis), Evaluation (which is more significant and why), Conclusion (direct answer with justification).

Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain how globalisation and legislation affects a UK business, using a specific example.

A: Globalisation and Legislation has a significant impact on UK businesses. For example, a specific UK business experienced measurable effects because of factors related to globalisation and legislation. This demonstrates that businesses must respond strategically to remain competitive. A strong GCSE answer would use precise business terminology, reference a real UK business, and explain the cause-and-effect chain clearly.

Q: Evaluate the importance of globalisation and legislation for a small business compared to a large business.

A: The importance of globalisation and legislation differs between business sizes. Small businesses may face greater challenges because they have fewer resources, less market power, and limited expertise. Large businesses can leverage greater resources and economies of scale. However, small businesses may also have advantages such as flexibility and agility. A balanced evaluation considers both perspectives before reaching a justified conclusion.
