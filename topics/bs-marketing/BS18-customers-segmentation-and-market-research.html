Customers, Segmentation and Market Research

How businesses understand customers: market segmentation (demographic, geographic, psychographic, behavioural), market research methods (primary and secondary, qualitative and quantitative), sampling, and the use of data in decision-making.

Key Fact: Market segmentation divides the market into groups with similar characteristics: demographic (age, gender, income), geographic (location), psychographic (lifestyle, values), and behavioural (purchasing habits, brand loyalty).
Key Fact: Primary research collects new data directly: surveys, focus groups, interviews, observations. It's specific to the business but expensive and time-consuming.
Key Fact: Secondary research uses existing data: government statistics, trade reports, competitor accounts, internet research. It's cheaper and faster but may be outdated or not specific to the business's needs.
Key Fact: Quantitative data is numerical and statistical (percentages, averages, charts); qualitative data is descriptive (opinions, feelings, reasons behind choices). Both are valuable - quantitative shows WHAT, qualitative shows WHY.
Key Fact: Sampling methods: random sampling (everyone has equal chance), stratified (proportional from each subgroup), quota (set number from each group), cluster (from specific locations). Larger samples are more representative but more expensive.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between primary and secondary market research, giving two advantages of each.

Q2: Describe how market segmentation can help a business target its products more effectively.

Q3: Evaluate whether a business should rely more on primary or secondary research when launching a new product.

✅ Answers

  1. Primary research: collected first-hand for a specific purpose. Advantages: up-to-date, directly relevant, owned by the business (competitors can't access it). Secondary research: uses existing data from other sources. Advantages: cheaper, quicker, wider range available (government stats, trade bodies, internet).
  2. Segmentation helps by: identifying which customer groups are most profitable, tailoring products to specific needs (e.g. low-calorie meals for health-conscious segment), targeting marketing spend more efficiently (advertising where the target segment will see it), pricing appropriately for each segment's willingness to pay, and finding gaps in the market that competitors have missed.
  3. Primary research benefits: directly relevant to the new product, current data, can test specific features/pricing, reveals customer preferences. Drawbacks: expensive, time-consuming, small samples may not be representative. Secondary research benefits: cheap, quick, broad context (market size, trends, competitor analysis). Drawbacks: may be outdated, not specific to the product, competitors have same access. Conclusion: secondary research should be used first (to understand the market context cheaply), then primary research to fill gaps and test the specific product concept - a phased approach is most cost-effective.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always specify the TYPE of segmentation: don't just say 'segmentation', say 'demographic segmentation by age'.
  • When evaluating research, link back to DECISION-MAKING: how does the data help the business choose?
  • Remember: primary = new data for a specific purpose; secondary = existing data from other sources.

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When evaluating market research, use the CRED framework: Cost (can the business afford it?), Relevance (how specific is it to the decision?), Efficiency (how quickly can it be gathered?), Depth (does it answer WHY, not just WHAT?). The best research strategies combine primary and secondary data.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Secondary research is always unreliable because it was collected by someone else for a different purpose. Correct: Secondary research from reputable sources (ONS, Mintel, government publications) can be highly reliable and very useful for understanding market size, trends, and competitor activity. It's not always outdated - many sources are updated regularly. The key is to EVALUATE the source: who collected it, when, why, and how? Government census data is extremely reliable. A small business's own survey of 20 customers may actually be less reliable than a professional market report based on thousands of responses.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate which types of market research a new vegan restaurant should conduct before opening.

A grade 9 response will: start with secondary research (market size of vegan dining, local demographics, competitor analysis, food trends); then primary research (local surveys on dining preferences, taste testing with target customers, footfall observations at potential locations); consider segmentation (age: younger adults more likely vegan; income: premium pricing for ethical consumers; psychographic: values-driven diners); sampling (stratified by age group locally); conclude: begin with free secondary data (local authority stats, Google Trends, competitor menus), then commission targeted primary research (local taste tests and preference surveys) - this phased approach manages cost while gathering the specific insights needed for menu and location decisions.

Exam Technique for Customers, Segmentation and Market Research Questions

GCSE Business exam questions about customers, segmentation and market research range from 1-mark definition questions to 12-mark evaluation questions. For definition questions (1-2 marks), give a precise business definition. For explanation questions (3-4 marks), define and explain with a brief example. For analysis questions (6-9 marks), develop a chain of reasoning showing cause and effect. For evaluation questions (12 marks), present both sides, use evidence, and reach a justified conclusion.

For the highest marks on evaluation questions, you must: present balanced arguments (advantages AND disadvantages); support each point with a specific business example; show the connections between points (how one factor affects another); and reach a conclusion that directly answers the question with justification.

Common mistakes: writing everything you know about a topic without answering the specific question; failing to use business terminology; not providing specific examples; and not reaching a justified conclusion for evaluation questions.

GCSE Example: Exam Technique for Customers, Segmentation and Market Research Questions

A 12-mark evaluation answer structure: Introduction (define customers, segmentation and market research), Argument for (with UK example and analysis), Argument against (with UK example and analysis), Evaluation (which is more significant and why), Conclusion (direct answer with justification).
