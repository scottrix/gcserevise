Production Processes

How businesses organise production: job production, batch production, flow production, mass production, productivity, efficiency, lean production, and the impact of technology on production methods.

Key Fact: Job production: one-off, unique products made to customer specification (e.g. custom wedding cake). High quality, high price, but slow and labour-intensive.
Key Fact: Batch production: groups of identical products made together (e.g. bakery making 50 loaves). Allows some variety but requires cleaning/resetting between batches.
Key Fact: Flow (mass) production: continuous, standardised production on an assembly line (e.g. car manufacturing). High volume, low unit cost, but high set-up costs and inflexible.
Key Fact: Productivity measures output per unit of input: labour productivity = output / number of workers. Higher productivity means lower unit costs and greater competitiveness.
Key Fact: Lean production aims to minimise waste while maintaining quality: just-in-time (JIT) inventory, kaizen (continuous improvement), and cutting out non-value-added activities.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Compare job production and flow production, giving one advantage and one disadvantage of each.

Q2: Explain how lean production can reduce a business's costs.

Q3: Evaluate which production method a furniture maker should use if they want to expand from bespoke to selling online nationwide.

✅ Answers

  1. Job production advantages: unique, high-quality products that command premium prices; fully customised to customer needs. Disadvantages: slow, labour-intensive, high unit cost, difficult to scale. Flow production advantages: high output, low unit cost, consistent quality, economies of scale. Disadvantages: inflexible, high set-up costs, standardised products with no customisation, breakdowns halt entire line.
  2. Lean production reduces costs by: eliminating waste (overproduction, waiting time, defects, excess inventory), using JIT to reduce storage costs (stock arrives only when needed), kaizen for continuous small improvements that cumulatively reduce costs, and reducing rework/returns through quality focus. Example: Toyota's lean system reduced inventory costs by 60% compared to traditional manufacturing.
  3. Currently using job production (bespoke). Expanding nationwide online requires: higher volume, consistent quality, lower unit costs. Batch production is the best intermediate step: can produce in batches for online orders while still offering some customisation. Flow production may be too inflexible and requires too much investment initially. A hybrid approach: batch for standard ranges, job for premium bespoke, allows gradual transition and market testing.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When comparing production methods, always link back to the specific business context - there is no universally 'best' method.
  • Productivity questions: always show the formula and explain what affects the ratio (training, technology, motivation).
  • Lean production is about eliminating waste - always define what waste means in the specific context.

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When evaluating production methods, use the VCAF framework: Volume (how many?), Customisation (how unique?), Automation (how much tech?), Finance (what can the business afford?). The best method depends on market demand and business resources.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Flow production always produces the best-quality products. Correct: Flow production produces consistent products, not necessarily the best quality. Job production typically achieves higher quality because skilled workers focus on each individual item. Flow production's strength is consistency and low unit cost at high volume, but quality is standardised rather than exceptional. A luxury watch maker using job production will produce higher quality than a mass-produced watch factory.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether a craft bakery should switch from batch production to flow production to meet growing demand from supermarkets.

A grade 9 response will: analyse current batch production (quality, variety, flexibility, but limited volume); flow production benefits (high volume, low unit cost, consistent output for supermarket contracts); flow production drawbacks (loss of artisan quality, high capital investment, inflexibility, potential brand damage); alternatives (expand batch capacity, invest in semi-automated hybrid). Conclude: switching fully to flow risks damaging the brand's artisan image - a phased approach adding some automation while retaining craft methods for premium lines would be more prudent.
