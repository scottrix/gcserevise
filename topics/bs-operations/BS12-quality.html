Quality

How businesses ensure quality: quality control, quality assurance, total quality management (TQM), quality standards (ISO 9001), and the relationship between quality and competitiveness.

Key Fact: Quality control (QC) inspects the finished product to detect defects - it's reactive and occurs after production. Defective items are rejected or reworked.
Key Fact: Quality assurance (QA) builds quality into every stage of the production process - it's proactive and aims to prevent defects before they occur.
Key Fact: Total quality management (TQM) involves every employee in continuous quality improvement: zero defects culture, employee empowerment, and customer focus throughout the organisation.
Key Fact: ISO 9001 is an international quality standard: businesses must document processes, demonstrate consistent quality, and pass regular audits. Certification builds customer trust.
Key Fact: Higher quality reduces costs long-term: fewer returns, less rework, better reputation, more repeat business, and ability to charge premium prices.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between quality control and quality assurance.

Q2: Describe two ways TQM can benefit a business.

Q3: Evaluate whether a small business should invest in ISO 9001 certification.

✅ Answers

  1. Quality control checks products AFTER production to find and remove defective items - it's a checkpoint at the end. Quality assurance builds quality INTO the process at every stage, checking throughout so defects are prevented, not just detected. QC = catching problems; QA = preventing problems.
  2. TQM benefits: (1) Every employee takes responsibility for quality, reducing defects at source and creating a culture of continuous improvement - this leads to fewer customer complaints and returns. (2) Empowered workers can stop production if they spot a defect, preventing waste of materials and time on faulty batches - reducing costs and improving efficiency.
  3. Arguments for ISO 9001: signals quality to customers and partners, required by some B2B clients and government contracts, improves internal processes through documentation, builds competitive advantage over non-certified rivals. Arguments against: certification costs £3,000-£10,000 plus annual audits, significant staff time for documentation, may add bureaucracy that slows decision-making, a small business with strong reputation may not need it. Conclusion: worth it if the business supplies to large organisations that require it, or if it needs to differentiate from competitors; not essential if customers are local and already trust the brand.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Remember: quality control is REACTIVE (after), quality assurance is PROACTIVE (during). This distinction is a common exam question.
  • Link quality to cost: it's cheaper to prevent defects than to fix them - the 'cost of quality' includes both prevention costs and failure costs.
  • TQM is a CULTURE, not just a system - it requires commitment from top management and involvement from every employee.

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
When evaluating quality systems, use the PAC framework: Prevention (how does it stop defects?), Assurance (how does it build customer confidence?), Cost (do the benefits outweigh the investment?). Quality always has a cost, but poor quality costs more.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: High-quality products always cost more to produce than low-quality products. Correct: While high-quality materials may cost more, quality systems often REDUCE total costs by eliminating: rework (fixing defects), returns and refunds, warranty claims, lost customers, and wasted materials. A business with effective QA may have lower total costs than one using only QC, because it prevents defects rather than finding and fixing them. Toyota's TQM system actually reduced production costs while improving quality.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether a food manufacturer should move from quality control to quality assurance.

A grade 9 response will: explain QC (end-of-line testing catches contaminated batches but they must be thrown away); explain QA (HACCP system checks at each stage - raw materials, cooking temperatures, packaging - preventing contamination before it occurs); consider costs (QA requires more training and monitoring systems but reduces waste); consider risks (food safety is critical - QC failures could lead to product recalls, legal action, and brand damage); conclude: QA is essential in food manufacturing where safety is non-negotiable - the cost of prevention is far less than the cost of a food safety scandal.
