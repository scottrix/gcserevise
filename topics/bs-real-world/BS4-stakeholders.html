Stakeholders

Internal and external stakeholders; stakeholder objectives and conflict; how businesses manage stakeholder interests; influence of different stakeholder groups.

Key Fact: Stakeholders: any person or group with an interest in a business. Internal: owners, employees, managers. External: customers, suppliers, local community, government, pressure groups.
Key Fact: Stakeholder objectives often conflict: owners want maximum profit (low wages, high prices); employees want higher wages and better conditions; customers want low prices and high quality; local community wants jobs but less pollution; government wants tax revenue and compliance.
Key Fact: Managing stakeholder conflict: compromise, negotiation, prioritisation. Businesses cannot satisfy all stakeholders equally - they must balance competing interests based on power and importance.
Key Fact: Stakeholder influence varies: owners/shareholders have high influence (can appoint directors); employees can take industrial action; customers can take their business elsewhere; pressure groups can damage reputation through social media campaigns.
Key Fact: Stakeholder mapping: power (ability to influence) vs interest (how much they care). High power + high interest = key players who must be managed closely.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Identify four stakeholders of a large supermarket and explain what each would want from the business.

Q2: Explain how a conflict might arise between employees and owners of a business, and how it could be resolved.

Q3: A factory wants to expand, creating 200 jobs but increasing traffic and noise. Evaluate the competing stakeholder interests.

✅ Answers

  1. Owners: maximum profit and return on investment. Employees: fair wages, job security, safe working conditions. Customers: low prices, wide product range, convenient location. Suppliers: reliable orders, prompt payment, long-term contracts.
  2. Employees want higher wages and better conditions (costs increase, profits decrease). Owners want to minimise costs and maximise profits. Resolution: negotiation (unions and management discuss), compromise (modest pay rise tied to productivity improvements), or consultation (explaining financial constraints to employees).
  3. Employees and local community support expansion (200 new jobs, economic boost). Local residents oppose it (more traffic, noise, pollution). Local council may support (business rates revenue) or oppose (planning concerns). The business must balance these through environmental mitigation (noise barriers, traffic management plans), community investment, and transparent communication.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always identify stakeholders as internal or external
  • For each stakeholder, state WHAT they want and WHY
  • Show both sides of any conflict before suggesting a resolution

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
For stakeholder questions: 1) Identify all relevant stakeholders, 2) State each one's objective, 3) Identify the conflict, 4) Suggest how the business could manage it, 5) Evaluate which stakeholder has the most influence and why.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: All stakeholders are equally important and businesses must treat them all the same. Correct: Stakeholders have different levels of power and interest. Shareholders in a plc have significant influence through voting rights; a local community group may have strong feelings but limited direct power (though social media can amplify their voice). Businesses prioritise stakeholders based on their power and importance - ignoring a powerful stakeholder like a major shareholder can have serious consequences, while a less influential group may receive less attention.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

A large retailer is planning to open a new superstore on the edge of a small town. Analyse the impact on four different stakeholder groups and evaluate whether the development should go ahead.

A grade 9 response will: analyse impact on local residents (concern: increased traffic, noise, small shops may close; benefit: convenience, jobs), employees (benefit: new jobs, career progression), local small businesses (concern: competition, possible closure), local council (benefit: business rates, employment; concern: infrastructure costs); evaluate overall: the development brings economic benefits (jobs, investment, consumer choice) but may harm the town centre and community character; conclude with a justified position considering both sides.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AQA GCSE Business 8132: 2 papers x 90 marks, each 50%. AOs: AO1 Demonstrate knowledge (35%), AO2 Apply knowledge (35%), AO3 Analyse and evaluate (30%). For grade 9, demonstrate perceptive analysis, sophisticated evaluation of trade-offs, and fluent use of business terminology.
