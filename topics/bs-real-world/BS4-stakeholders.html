Stakeholders

Internal and external stakeholders; stakeholder objectives and conflict; how businesses manage stakeholder interests; influence of different stakeholder groups.

Key Fact: Stakeholders: any person or group with an interest in a business. Internal: owners, employees, managers. External: customers, suppliers, local community, government, pressure groups.
Key Fact: Stakeholder objectives often conflict: owners want maximum profit (low wages, high prices); employees want higher wages and better conditions; customers want low prices and high quality; local community wants jobs but less pollution; government wants tax revenue and compliance.
Key Fact: Managing stakeholder conflict: compromise, negotiation, prioritisation. Businesses cannot satisfy all stakeholders equally - they must balance competing interests based on power and importance.
Key Fact: Stakeholder influence varies: owners/shareholders have high influence (can appoint directors); employees can take industrial action; customers can take their business elsewhere; pressure groups can damage reputation through social media campaigns.
Key Fact: Stakeholder mapping: power (ability to influence) vs interest (how much they care). High power + high interest = key players who must be managed closely.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Identify four stakeholders of a large supermarket and explain what each would want from the business.

Q2: Explain how a conflict might arise between employees and owners of a business, and how it could be resolved.

Q3: A factory wants to expand, creating 200 jobs but increasing traffic and noise. Evaluate the competing stakeholder interests.

✅ Answers

  1. Owners: maximum profit and return on investment. Employees: fair wages, job security, safe working conditions. Customers: low prices, wide product range, convenient location. Suppliers: reliable orders, prompt payment, long-term contracts.
  2. Employees want higher wages and better conditions (costs increase, profits decrease). Owners want to minimise costs and maximise profits. Resolution: negotiation (unions and management discuss), compromise (modest pay rise tied to productivity improvements), or consultation (explaining financial constraints to employees).
  3. Employees and local community support expansion (200 new jobs, economic boost). Local residents oppose it (more traffic, noise, pollution). Local council may support (business rates revenue) or oppose (planning concerns). The business must balance these through environmental mitigation (noise barriers, traffic management plans), community investment, and transparent communication.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always identify stakeholders as internal or external
  • For each stakeholder, state WHAT they want and WHY
  • Show both sides of any conflict before suggesting a resolution

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
For stakeholder questions: 1) Identify all relevant stakeholders, 2) State each one's objective, 3) Identify the conflict, 4) Suggest how the business could manage it, 5) Evaluate which stakeholder has the most influence and why.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: All stakeholders are equally important and businesses must treat them all the same. Correct: Stakeholders have different levels of power and interest. Shareholders in a plc have significant influence through voting rights; a local community group may have strong feelings but limited direct power (though social media can amplify their voice). Businesses prioritise stakeholders based on their power and importance - ignoring a powerful stakeholder like a major shareholder can have serious consequences, while a less influential group may receive less attention.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

A large retailer is planning to open a new superstore on the edge of a small town. Analyse the impact on four different stakeholder groups and evaluate whether the development should go ahead.

A grade 9 response will: analyse impact on local residents (concern: increased traffic, noise, small shops may close; benefit: convenience, jobs), employees (benefit: new jobs, career progression), local small businesses (concern: competition, possible closure), local council (benefit: business rates, employment; concern: infrastructure costs); evaluate overall: the development brings economic benefits (jobs, investment, consumer choice) but may harm the town centre and community character; conclude with a justified position considering both sides.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Stakeholders in Business Context

Stakeholders is a key topic in GCSE Business Studies that affects how businesses operate in the UK economy. Understanding this concept requires knowledge of both theoretical principles and real-world application. The AQA specification requires you to explain, analyse and evaluate business concepts using appropriate terminology, and apply them to real business scenarios using UK examples.

When writing about stakeholders in GCSE exams, always use precise business terminology, support your points with specific UK business examples, and explain the cause-and-effect relationships clearly. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of how business concepts interact in practice, not just textbook definitions.

Real-world context is essential: stakeholders affects sole traders differently from multinational corporations, and startups differently from established firms. Always consider the specific business context when applying your knowledge.

GCSE Example: Understanding Stakeholders in Business Context

A strong GCSE Business answer about stakeholders would define the concept precisely, apply it to a real UK business example, analyse the impact on that business, and evaluate the significance considering both advantages and disadvantages.

Applying Stakeholders to UK Business Examples

For stakeholders, applying theory to practice means using real UK business examples to illustrate your understanding. Small businesses face different challenges from large corporations when dealing with stakeholders. Consider how factors like business size, ownership structure, industry sector and market position affect the impact of stakeholders on a business.

UK business examples you could reference: small sole traders like local shops; private limited companies like Virgin Active; public limited companies like Tesco and BP; and social enterprises like The Big Issue. Each type of business responds differently to stakeholders based on its resources, objectives and competitive position.

Quantitative skills are important: many business concepts can be expressed numerically. When discussing stakeholders, use calculations, percentages and financial data where appropriate to support your analysis. The AQA specification requires you to interpret and use quantitative data.

GCSE Example: Applying Stakeholders to UK Business Examples

When evaluating stakeholders, use a structured approach: define the concept, apply it to a specific UK business, analyse the impact (positive and negative), and evaluate the overall significance with a justified conclusion.

Exam Technique for Stakeholders Questions

GCSE Business exam questions about stakeholders range from 1-mark definition questions to 12-mark evaluation questions. For definition questions (1-2 marks), give a precise business definition. For explanation questions (3-4 marks), define and explain with a brief example. For analysis questions (6-9 marks), develop a chain of reasoning showing cause and effect. For evaluation questions (12 marks), present both sides, use evidence, and reach a justified conclusion.

For the highest marks on evaluation questions, you must: present balanced arguments (advantages AND disadvantages); support each point with a specific business example; show the connections between points (how one factor affects another); and reach a conclusion that directly answers the question with justification.

Common mistakes: writing everything you know about a topic without answering the specific question; failing to use business terminology; not providing specific examples; and not reaching a justified conclusion for evaluation questions.

GCSE Example: Exam Technique for Stakeholders Questions

A 12-mark evaluation answer structure: Introduction (define stakeholders), Argument for (with UK example and analysis), Argument against (with UK example and analysis), Evaluation (which is more significant and why), Conclusion (direct answer with justification).

Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain how stakeholders affects a UK business, using a specific example.

A: Stakeholders has a significant impact on UK businesses. For example, a specific UK business experienced measurable effects because of factors related to stakeholders. This demonstrates that businesses must respond strategically to remain competitive. A strong GCSE answer would use precise business terminology, reference a real UK business, and explain the cause-and-effect chain clearly.

Q: Evaluate the importance of stakeholders for a small business compared to a large business.

A: The importance of stakeholders differs between business sizes. Small businesses may face greater challenges because they have fewer resources, less market power, and limited expertise. Large businesses can leverage greater resources and economies of scale. However, small businesses may also have advantages such as flexibility and agility. A balanced evaluation considers both perspectives before reaching a justified conclusion.
