Business Location and Expansion

Factors affecting location decisions; business plans; organic growth vs external growth; franchising, e-commerce, outsourcing; economies and diseconomies of scale; mergers and takeovers.

Key Fact: Location factors: proximity to customers/market, transport links, availability of suitable labour, cost of premises, competition, government incentives (grants, enterprise zones).
Key Fact: Business plan: a document setting out the business idea, market research, financial forecasts, marketing strategy. Essential for securing finance from banks or investors. Shows the business has thought through its strategy.
Key Fact: Organic growth: expanding from within - opening new outlets, increasing product range, growing online sales. Slow but controlled, less risky than external growth.
Key Fact: External growth: mergers (two firms agree to join) and takeovers (one firm buys another). Faster growth but risky: cultural clash, high cost, integration problems. Franchising: rapid expansion with lower capital risk.
Key Fact: Economies of scale: as a business grows, average cost per unit falls (bulk buying, technical efficiency, financial economies, marketing economies). Diseconomies of scale: beyond a certain size, average costs rise (poor communication, coordination problems, low morale).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain three factors a business should consider when choosing a location for a new factory.

Q2: Compare organic growth and external growth as ways for a business to expand.

Q3: Explain what is meant by economies of scale and give three examples.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Proximity to raw materials/suppliers (reduces transport costs for heavy/bulky inputs). 2) Transport links (access to motorways, ports, rail for distribution). 3) Availability of labour (skills in the local area, wage costs, unemployment levels). Other factors: cost of land, government grants, proximity to customers.
  2. Organic growth: slow, controlled, funded from profits or borrowing, lower risk, management retains full control. External growth: rapid expansion through merger/takeover, high cost, risk of cultural clash and integration problems, but achieves instant market share increase. Organic is safer for risk-averse businesses; external suits businesses wanting rapid market dominance.
  3. Economies of scale = as output increases, the average cost per unit falls. Examples: 1) Purchasing economies (bulk buying at discounted prices). 2) Technical economies (using larger, more efficient machinery). 3) Financial economies (large firms can borrow at lower interest rates). 4) Marketing economies (spreading advertising costs over more units).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always link location choice to the TYPE of business (retail needs customers; manufacturing needs transport)
  • Distinguish between mergers (agreed) and takeovers (hostile)
  • Explain economies of scale with the 'average cost per unit falls' formula - be precise

📝 Exam Technique

Business Exam Tips:
For expansion questions: 1) Identify the growth method, 2) Explain how it works, 3) Give advantages and disadvantages, 4) Consider the business's specific circumstances (size, industry, resources), 5) Recommend the most appropriate method with justification.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Bigger is always better for a business because economies of scale mean lower costs. Correct: Economies of scale reduce average costs up to a point, but diseconomies of scale can set in when a business becomes too large: communication breaks down, decisions take longer, staff feel disconnected and morale falls, coordination costs increase. Many large businesses actually split into smaller units to avoid diseconomies. The optimal size depends on the industry - some industries (utilities, car manufacturing) benefit from huge scale; others (creative industries, personal services) work better small.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

A successful local bakery wants to expand nationally. Evaluate franchising versus opening company-owned stores as growth strategies.

A grade 9 response will: evaluate franchising (rapid expansion with lower capital risk, franchisees invest their own money, motivated owner-operators, but less quality control, shares profits, brand risk if franchisee performs poorly); evaluate company-owned stores (full control, all profits retained, consistent quality, but high capital cost, slower growth, all risk on the business); conclude: franchising suits rapid national expansion with limited capital, but the bakery must invest in strong training and quality systems to protect its brand.

Understanding Business Location and Expansion in Business Context

Business Location and Expansion is a key topic in GCSE Business Studies that affects how businesses operate in the UK economy. Understanding this concept requires knowledge of both theoretical principles and real-world application. The AQA specification requires you to explain, analyse and evaluate business concepts using appropriate terminology, and apply them to real business scenarios using UK examples.

When writing about business location and expansion in GCSE exams, always use precise business terminology, support your points with specific UK business examples, and explain the cause-and-effect relationships clearly. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of how business concepts interact in practice, not just textbook definitions.

Real-world context is essential: business location and expansion affects sole traders differently from multinational corporations, and startups differently from established firms. Always consider the specific business context when applying your knowledge.

GCSE Example: Understanding Business Location and Expansion in Business Context

A strong GCSE Business answer about business location and expansion would define the concept precisely, apply it to a real UK business example, analyse the impact on that business, and evaluate the significance considering both advantages and disadvantages.

Applying Business Location and Expansion to UK Business Examples

For business location and expansion, applying theory to practice means using real UK business examples to illustrate your understanding. Small businesses face different challenges from large corporations when dealing with business location and expansion. Consider how factors like business size, ownership structure, industry sector and market position affect the impact of business location and expansion on a business.

UK business examples you could reference: small sole traders like local shops; private limited companies like Virgin Active; public limited companies like Tesco and BP; and social enterprises like The Big Issue. Each type of business responds differently to business location and expansion based on its resources, objectives and competitive position.

Quantitative skills are important: many business concepts can be expressed numerically. When discussing business location and expansion, use calculations, percentages and financial data where appropriate to support your analysis. The AQA specification requires you to interpret and use quantitative data.

GCSE Example: Applying Business Location and Expansion to UK Business Examples

When evaluating business location and expansion, use a structured approach: define the concept, apply it to a specific UK business, analyse the impact (positive and negative), and evaluate the overall significance with a justified conclusion.

Exam Technique for Business Location and Expansion Questions

GCSE Business exam questions about business location and expansion range from 1-mark definition questions to 12-mark evaluation questions. For definition questions (1-2 marks), give a precise business definition. For explanation questions (3-4 marks), define and explain with a brief example. For analysis questions (6-9 marks), develop a chain of reasoning showing cause and effect. For evaluation questions (12 marks), present both sides, use evidence, and reach a justified conclusion.

For the highest marks on evaluation questions, you must: present balanced arguments (advantages AND disadvantages); support each point with a specific business example; show the connections between points (how one factor affects another); and reach a conclusion that directly answers the question with justification.

Common mistakes: writing everything you know about a topic without answering the specific question; failing to use business terminology; not providing specific examples; and not reaching a justified conclusion for evaluation questions.

GCSE Example: Exam Technique for Business Location and Expansion Questions

A 12-mark evaluation answer structure: Introduction (define business location and expansion), Argument for (with UK example and analysis), Argument against (with UK example and analysis), Evaluation (which is more significant and why), Conclusion (direct answer with justification).
