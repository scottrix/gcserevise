Paper 1: Myth & Religion Technique

Specific techniques for the OCR J280 Myth & Religion paper

Key Fact: Paper 1 is worth 50% of the GCSE and lasts 1 hour 30 minutes
Key Fact: It covers both Greek and Roman mythology and religion — comparison between cultures is essential
Key Fact: The paper tests knowledge of myths, religious practices, temples, festivals and beliefs about death
Key Fact: Visual source analysis is central — you must discuss vase paintings, sculpture and architecture
Key Fact: Thematic questions require comparison of Greek and Roman approaches
Key Fact: Questions may ask you to evaluate how far a statement about myth or religion is true
Key Fact: You need specific examples from both cultures: Greek AND Roman temples, festivals and beliefs
Key Fact: The extended question usually asks 'how far' — requiring balanced argument with judgement
Key Fact: Always use technical vocabulary: xenia, pax deorum, prothesis, metamorphosis, aetiological
Key Fact: For visual sources: describe, identify, analyse, evaluate — in that order
Key Fact: Paper 1 rewards breadth of knowledge across both Greek and Roman culture
Key Fact: Practice timed essays — approximately 45 minutes for the extended question

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Paper 1: Myth & Religion Technique, you must know:

  • Myth and Religion: Paper 1 of OCR J280 covering Greek and Roman mythology, religious practices and beliefs about death
  • Thematic study: Comparing how different cultures treat the same theme — e.g. Greek vs Roman religion
  • Visual analysis: Examining images as evidence for classical culture
  • Cross-cultural comparison: Comparing Greek and Roman approaches — essential for Paper 1
  • Aetiological myth: A myth explaining the origin of something
  • Syncretism: The merging of different religious traditions — Romans blended Greek and native Italian practices

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Only discussing Greek culture and ignoring Roman ✓ Paper 1 requires comparison between Greek AND Roman culture

✗ Describing a visual source without analysing significance ✓ Describe first, then analyse what it means for understanding classical culture

✗ Treating Greek and Roman religion as identical ✓ Romans adopted Greek gods but emphasised different aspects — more practical and state-focused

✗ Focusing on one small topic instead of the full range ✓ Paper 1 tests breadth — cover the full specification in revision

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How far did Greek and Roman religious practices serve similar purposes? (15 marks)

Greek and Roman religious practices served broadly similar purposes — maintaining divine favour, reinforcing social bonds and providing cultural identity — but differed significantly in emphasis. Both used sacrifice as the central act of worship and sought to maintain a favourable relationship with the gods. Greek thusia and Roman sacrifice both involved burning offerings and sharing meat. Both built temples and held festivals combining worship with competition. However, Greek religion was more personal and emotional — the Eleusinian Mysteries promised a blessed afterlife, and the gods' human-like behaviour made them relatable. Roman religion was more practical and contractual: do ut des treated worship as a transaction, and the pax deorum was a formal agreement. The Roman Imperial Cult had no Greek equivalent, blending religion with political loyalty. Roman augury and haruspicy were systematic state practices, while Greek oracles like Delphi were more individual and poetic. Overall, both religions served similar fundamental purposes but with different emphases — Greek religion prioritised personal devotion and mythological meaning, while Roman religion prioritised state security and practical outcomes.
