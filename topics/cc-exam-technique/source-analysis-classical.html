Source Analysis Skills

How to analyse classical sources for OCR J280 — literary, visual and material evidence

Key Fact: Source analysis is a core skill in OCR J280 — especially for visual and material culture
Key Fact: Classical sources include literary texts, visual sources (vase paintings, sculpture) and material evidence
Key Fact: Always consider content, provenance and context when analysing a source
Key Fact: Literary sources may be biased by genre, purpose or audience
Key Fact: Visual sources reflect artistic conventions, not necessarily realistic depictions
Key Fact: Archaeological evidence provides physical proof but must be interpreted
Key Fact: Utility means how useful a source is for a specific question — not whether it is true
Key Fact: A biased source can be useful — bias reveals attitudes and cultural assumptions
Key Fact: Cross-referencing sources strengthens arguments
Key Fact: OCR J280 requires use of both literary and visual/material sources
Key Fact: For vase paintings, describe what you see before analysing significance
Key Fact: Never accept a source at face value — always question its purpose and limitations

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Source Analysis Skills, you must know:

  • Provenance: The origin of a source — who created it, when, where, and for what purpose
  • Utility: How useful a source is for answering a particular historical or cultural question
  • Visual source: Evidence from images — vase paintings, sculpture, mosaics, architecture and coins
  • Literary source: Evidence from written texts — epic poetry, histories, plays, inscriptions and documents
  • Material culture: Physical objects from the ancient world — buildings, tools, pottery and artefacts
  • Genre convention: The artistic or literary rules of a particular type — epic poetry differs from history

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What three elements should you consider when analysing a source?

Q: Why can a biased source still be useful?

Q: How should you approach a vase painting as a source?

Q: Why is cross-referencing important?

Q: What is the difference between reliability and utility?

✅ Answers

  1. Content (what it says or shows), provenance (who created it, when, why), and context (what else was happening).
  2. Bias reveals attitudes, values and cultural assumptions of the author or their society.
  3. Describe what you see first, then analyse significance, and evaluate limitations (artistic conventions may distort reality).
  4. Comparing different sources strengthens arguments — when literary and archaeological evidence agree, conclusions are more convincing.
  5. Reliability concerns accuracy; utility concerns how useful a source is for a specific question.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Start source answers by identifying provenance
  • For visual sources, describe before analysing
  • Use: 'This source is useful because... However, it is limited because...'
  • Cross-reference the source with your own knowledge
  • Ask 'what does this source NOT tell me?'

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Classical Civilisation Exam Tips:
Use precise classical terminology in all answers. When analysing sources, always consider provenance (who created it, when, and why). For literary questions, reference specific episodes and book numbers from set texts. For thematic questions, compare Greek and Roman approaches where relevant. For 'how far' questions, present a balanced argument with a clear judgement. OCR J280 rewards answers that demonstrate understanding of the classical world through use of specific evidence from both literary and visual/material sources.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Saying a source is reliable/unreliable without explaining why ✓ Justify your judgement with specific evidence from provenance and content

✗ Only summarising what a source shows without analysing ✓ Description alone earns few marks — evaluate provenance, purpose and usefulness

✗ Treating literary sources as factual accounts ✓ Homer's epics are poetry, not history — composed to entertain and explore themes

✗ Forgetting visual sources have artistic conventions ✓ Vase paintings may stylise, simplify or symbolise rather than depict reality

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How useful is the Parthenon frieze as a source for the Panathenaic procession? (12 marks)

The Parthenon frieze is useful as a source for the Panathenaic procession but has significant limitations. It provides the most detailed visual depiction surviving — showing horsemen, musicians, sacrificial animals, maidens carrying offerings and the presentation of the peplos. As a contemporary source from Pericles's building programme, it reflects mid-fifth century Athenian practice. The frieze's location on the Parthenon confirms the procession's religious significance. However, limitations are important. It is an idealised representation, not a documentary record — figures appear in perfect order without the chaos of a real procession. The frieze may depict a symbolic rather than actual procession. Artistic conventions show figures in composite poses rather than naturalistic positions. The frieze was placed high on the temple and barely visible, suggesting its purpose was to honour the gods rather than inform mortals. Despite these limitations, the frieze remains our best visual evidence for the procession's key elements, though it should be used alongside literary sources for fuller understanding.
