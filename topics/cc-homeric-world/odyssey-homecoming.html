Homecoming & Revenge

Odysseus's return to Ithaca, the contest of the bow, and his revenge on the suitors

Key Fact: Odysseus returns to Ithaca disguised as a beggar by Athena's guidance — he must assess the situation before revealing himself
Key Fact: Eumaeus the swineherd and Philoetius the cowherd remain loyal to Odysseus and help him against the suitors
Key Fact: Odysseus and Telemachus plot the suitors' downfall together — father and son are reunited in secret
Key Fact: Penelope sets the contest of the bow — only Odysseus can string his own great bow and shoot through twelve axes
Key Fact: The bow contest reveals Odysseus's true identity through his physical superiority and unique skill
Key Fact: Odysseus, Telemachus, Eumaeus and Philoetius slaughter the suitors in the great hall
Key Fact: The loyal servants are spared; the disloyal maidservants who consorted with the suitors are killed
Key Fact: Penelope tests Odysseus with the secret of their marriage bed — only the true Odysseus would know it cannot be moved
Key Fact: The recognition scene (anagnorismos) between Odysseus and Penelope is one of the most famous in literature
Key Fact: Odysseus's revenge is brutal but presented as justified by the suitors' violation of xenia
Key Fact: The ending of the Odyssey is ambiguous — Athena stops the families of the suitors from seeking further revenge
Key Fact: The theme of homecoming involves reclaiming not just a place but an identity, a family and a kingdom

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Homecoming & Revenge, you must know:

  • Anagnorismos: Recognition scene — the moment when a character's true identity is revealed, central to Greek drama and epic
  • Nostos: Homecoming — the central theme of the second half of the Odyssey
  • Mnesteres: The suitors — from the Greek word for 'wooers' who competed for Penelope's hand
  • Sema: A sign or token — Penelope uses the immovable bed as a sema to confirm Odysseus's identity
  • Mimesis: Imitation or representation — Odysseus's disguise as a beggar is a form of mimesis
  • Dike: Justice — the killing of the suitors is presented as dike for their crimes against xenia

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Why does Odysseus disguise himself as a beggar rather than returning openly?

Q: What is the significance of the bow contest?

Q: How does Penelope test Odysseus's identity?

Q: Is Odysseus's killing of the suitors justified?

Q: Why is the ending of the Odyssey ambiguous?

✅ Answers

  1. To assess who remains loyal, understand the suitors' strength, and plan his revenge without being targeted. Athena advises this strategy — it uses cunning rather than direct confrontation.
  2. Only Odysseus can string his own bow, proving his physical identity. The suitors cannot even bend it, showing their inferiority. Shooting through the twelve axes demonstrates unique skill that marks him as the true king.
  3. She mentions moving their marriage bed — but Odysseus knows it was built around a living olive tree and cannot be moved. This secret proves his identity more than any physical feat.
  4. In the moral world of the Odyssey, yes — they violated xenia, consumed his wealth, threatened his family and plotted to kill his son. Their punishment fits their crime and is sanctioned by the gods.
  5. Athena intervenes to stop further bloodshed, but the families of the dead suitors want revenge. The poem ends with a fragile peace, suggesting that homecoming does not automatically resolve all conflicts.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • The recognition scene is the most important moment — analyse how identity is confirmed through knowledge, not just physical proof
  • The immovable bed is the key symbol — it represents the unbreakable bond of marriage
  • Compare the loyalty of Eumaeus with the disloyalty of the suitors and maidservants
  • The violence of the revenge scene is shocking — be prepared to discuss whether it is justified
  • Always link the homecoming to the wider themes of the poem: identity, loyalty, justice

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Describing the killing of the suitors as murder without context ✓ In the Odyssey's moral world, the suitors' deaths are justified punishment (dike) for their violation of xenia, consumption of Odysseus's wealth and threat to his family

✗ Thinking Penelope immediately recognises Odysseus ✓ Penelope is cautious and tests him — the bed trick shows she will not be deceived, even by a god or a lookalike

✗ Forgetting the role of Telemachus in the revenge ✓ Telemachus actively participates — he locks the doors, arms the loyal servants and fights alongside his father

✗ Treating the ending as a simple happy conclusion ✓ The ending is ambiguous — Athena forces peace, but the families of the suitors are still grieving, and further conflict was only averted by divine intervention

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How far does the homecoming of Odysseus show that cunning is more important than strength? (15 marks)

Odysseus's homecoming demonstrates that cunning is essential, but it also shows that physical strength remains crucial — both are necessary for a complete hero. The entire strategy of the homecoming is built on cunning: Odysseus arrives in disguise, gathers intelligence from Eumaeus, tests the suitors' character as a beggar, and coordinates the ambush with Telemachus in secret. Without this clever planning, the suitors' superior numbers would have overwhelmed him. Penelope's own cunning — the shroud trick and the bow contest — creates the perfect opportunity for Odysseus to strike. The bed test is the ultimate proof of identity through knowledge, not strength. However, physical strength is also indispensable. Only Odysseus can string the great bow, a feat of pure physical power that no suitor can match. The actual battle in the great hall requires fighting skill and endurance — Odysseus, Telemachus and the two loyal servants must kill over a hundred suitors. Athena's intervention provides crucial divine support, but the combat is real and physically demanding. The homecoming succeeds precisely because Odysseus combines both qualities: cunning creates the opportunity and physical strength exploits it. Without cunning, strength would be overwhelmed by numbers; without strength, cunning would produce no result. Overall, the homecoming shows that cunning is the more distinctive and necessary quality — it is what makes Odysseus unique — but it is insufficient without the physical power to execute the plan.
