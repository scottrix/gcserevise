The Roman Army

Organisation, equipment, tactics and daily life of the Roman legions

Key Fact: The Roman army was the most effective military force in the ancient world, enabling Rome's imperial expansion
Key Fact: A legion consisted of approximately 5,000-6,000 soldiers divided into ten cohorts
Key Fact: Each cohort had six centuries of 80 men commanded by a centurion
Key Fact: Legionaries were Roman citizens who served 25 years; auxiliaries were non-citizens who gained citizenship on discharge
Key Fact: Roman discipline was legendary — decimation (killing one in ten) punished units that broke ranks
Key Fact: Standard equipment: lorica segmentata (armour), gladius (short sword), pilum (javelin), scutum (shield), helmet
Key Fact: The Romans built a fortified marching camp every night on campaign for security and routine
Key Fact: The testudo (tortoise) formation with overlapping shields protected against missile fire
Key Fact: Training was relentless — recruits marched 20 miles in five hours carrying full kit
Key Fact: The army was also an engineering force — building roads, bridges, forts and aqueducts
Key Fact: The Roman navy (classis) controlled the Mediterranean, securing supply lines
Key Fact: Soldiers were paid, received a pension on retirement and shared in booty from conquests

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For The Roman Army, you must know:

  • Legion: The basic large unit of the Roman army, approximately 5,000-6,000 men, all Roman citizens
  • Centurion: A senior officer commanding a century of 80 men — the key leadership rank
  • Auxiliary: Non-citizen soldiers who served alongside legionaries and received citizenship on discharge
  • Testudo: The tortoise formation — soldiers overlapped shields for protection against missiles
  • Gladius: The Roman short sword (about 60cm) designed for thrusting in close formation
  • Pilum: A heavy javelin thrown before close combat — its soft iron shank bent on impact

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Why was Roman discipline so effective?

Q: What was the difference between legionaries and auxiliaries?

Q: Why was the Roman marching camp important?

Q: How did the army function as an engineering force?

Q: What made the gladius effective?

✅ Answers

  1. Strict training created instinctive obedience and cohesion. Decimation deterred cowardice. The marching camp imposed order. Units that stood together survived; individuals who broke ranks endangered everyone.
  2. Legionaries were Roman citizens in the legions; auxiliaries were non-citizens from provinces providing specialist skills (cavalry, archers) who received citizenship on discharge.
  3. It provided security against surprise attacks, gave soldiers routine, and allowed the army to project power. The same layout every night created familiarity and discipline.
  4. Legionaries built roads, bridges, forts, walls like Hadrian's Wall, aqueducts and entire cities. Engineering was as important as combat in securing the empire.
  5. Its short length made it ideal for close-formation combat. Designed for thrusting (more lethal than slashing), it could be used behind the protection of the scutum.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Link military organisation to Rome's imperial success
  • Use specific technical terms: legion, cohort, century, pilum, testudo
  • The marching camp shows how discipline and engineering supported military power
  • Compare the Roman army with other ancient armies to show what made it unique
  • The army was a social institution — it integrated provinces and created citizens

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Thinking all Roman soldiers were legionaries ✓ Auxiliaries made up roughly half the army — non-citizens providing cavalry, archers and specialist roles

✗ Describing the army as relying only on discipline ✓ The army also relied on engineering, logistics, intelligence and adaptable tactics

✗ Confusing the Republican army with the Imperial army ✓ The Imperial army was a standing professional force; the Republican army was a citizen militia reformed by Marius

✗ Assuming Roman soldiers only fought ✓ Soldiers spent most time on construction, garrison duty, policing and administration

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How far was Roman military success due to organisation rather than tactics? (15 marks)

Roman military success was primarily due to organisation, though effective tactics and engineering were also essential. The legion structure with cohorts, centuries and a clear chain of command, combined with the professional standing army and relentless training, produced soldiers who could manoeuvre as a coherent unit under any conditions. The marching camp system meant Romans never slept undefended. Logistics — supply lines, road networks and naval support — enabled sustained campaigns. This organisational depth meant Rome could absorb defeats and raise new armies, while enemies who lost once often collapsed. However, tactics were also crucial. The pilum-and-gladius combination was devastating. The testudo, crescent formation and reserve line allowed flexible responses. Engineering turned terrain disadvantages into advantages through siege works, bridges and roads. Overall, organisation was the foundation — it created the disciplined, well-supplied force that made tactical innovation possible. Without organisation, brilliant tactics would fail; with it, even conventional tactics could overwhelm most opponents.
