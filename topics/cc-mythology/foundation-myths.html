Foundation Myths

How the Greeks and Romans used myths to explain the origins of cities, customs and the world

Key Fact: Foundation myths (aetiological myths) explained how cities, customs and natural features came to exist
Key Fact: Cadmus founded Thebes after following a cow to its resting place and sowing dragon's teeth to create warriors
Key Fact: Theseus was the legendary founder of Athens who united Attica (synoecism) and slew the Minotaur
Key Fact: Romulus and Remus founded Rome after being suckled by a she-wolf — Romulus killed Remus over the city walls
Key Fact: Aeneas fled Troy and journeyed to Italy, becoming the ancestor of the Roman people according to Virgil
Key Fact: Foundation myths gave cities divine authority and a heroic past that legitimised their power and identity
Key Fact: The myth of autochthony (born from the earth itself) was important to Athenians — they claimed to be indigenous
Key Fact: Foundation myths often involve violence, sacrifice and the establishment of laws or religious practices
Key Fact: Theseus's synoecism of Attica was both a political and a mythical event, combining history and legend
Key Fact: The story of Aeneas linked Rome to the prestige of Trojan and Greek heroic culture
Key Fact: Foundation myths helped unify communities by giving them a shared origin story and common ancestors
Key Fact: Roman foundation myths deliberately connected Rome to Greek culture while asserting Roman superiority

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Foundation Myths, you must know:

  • Foundation myth: A story that explains the origins of a city, people, custom or natural feature — also called an aetiological myth
  • Synoecism: The unification of separate communities into a single city-state — attributed to Theseus in Athens
  • Autochthony: The belief that a people originated from the earth itself — Athenians claimed they sprang from Attic soil
  • Aetiological myth: A myth that explains the cause or origin of something — from the Greek 'aition' meaning 'cause'
  • Ktisis: The Greek word for the founding of a city, often associated with a founder-hero
  • Pax deorum: The peace of the gods — the favourable relationship between a city and its patron deities, established through foundation rituals

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Why did Greek cities need foundation myths?

Q: How did the myth of Theseus serve Athenian political interests?

Q: What was the significance of Romulus killing Remus?

Q: Why did the Romans connect their origins to Aeneas and Troy?

Q: What is the difference between a foundation myth and a historical account?

✅ Answers

  1. Foundation myths gave cities divine authority, a heroic past, and a shared identity. They legitimised political power, explained customs, and unified communities under a common origin story.
  2. Theseus's synoecism legitimised Athens's control over Attica, his autochthony supported the claim that Athenians were indigenous, and his heroic deeds gave Athens prestige among Greek cities.
  3. It explained why Rome had only one founder, established the precedent that the city's needs came before family bonds, and foreshadowed Rome's history of civil conflict resolved by single authority.
  4. Linking Rome to Aeneas and Troy connected Roman identity to the prestigious world of Greek heroic myth while allowing Romans to claim they were not Greek but equal or superior in heritage.
  5. Foundation myths blend supernatural elements, symbolic narratives and cultural meaning with possible historical events, whereas historical accounts aim for factual accuracy.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always explain WHY a foundation myth was important, not just WHAT happened in the story
  • Connect foundation myths to political and social functions — identity, legitimacy, unity
  • The Theseus myth is particularly important for OCR J280 — know its details thoroughly
  • Compare Greek and Roman foundation myths to show how cultures used myth differently
  • Remember that foundation myths evolved over time to serve changing political needs

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Treating foundation myths as historical facts ✓ Foundation myths blend legend, symbolism and possible historical memory — they reveal cultural values, not literal history

✗ Only narrating the myth without explaining its significance ✓ Always explain why the myth mattered to the people who told it — its political, social or religious function

✗ Confusing the Greek and Roman versions of the Aeneas story ✓ Homer mentions Aeneas briefly; Virgil's Aeneid is the full Roman version — always specify which source you are using

✗ Forgetting that foundation myths served different purposes at different times ✓ Myths were adapted and reinterpreted — e.g., the Theseus myth was expanded during the Athenian democracy to serve new political needs

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain the importance of foundation myths to the ancient Greeks and Romans. (12 marks)

Foundation myths were crucial to both Greeks and Romans because they provided identity, legitimacy and unity. In Athens, the myth of Theseus established the city's political structure: his synoecism of Attica legitimised Athenian dominance over the surrounding region, and the claim of autochthony gave Athenians a unique status among Greeks. The Theseus myth also provided a heroic ancestor who could rival Heracles, giving Athens cultural prestige. In Rome, the myth of Romulus and Remus explained the city's origins and values: Romulus's establishment of laws and religious practices set the pattern for Roman pietas, and even his killing of Remus carried a message about the primacy of the state over family loyalty. The Aeneas myth, developed fully by Virgil under Augustus, connected Rome to the heroic age of Troy, giving it a pedigree equal to Greece while asserting a distinct Roman identity based on pietas and duty. Both cultures used foundation myths to unify diverse populations under a single origin story, legitimise territorial claims, and justify their political systems. Without such myths, neither Athens nor Rome would have had the shared narrative that bound their citizens together.
