Heracles and Heroes

The myth of Heracles, his Twelve Labours and the concept of the Greek hero

Key Fact: Heracles (Hercules in Roman) was the greatest Greek hero, son of Zeus and the mortal Alcmene
Key Fact: Hera hated Heracles because he was proof of Zeus's infidelity — she sent two snakes to kill him in his cradle
Key Fact: Heracles was driven mad by Hera and killed his wife Megara and their children
Key Fact: The Twelve Labours were his punishment and path to redemption, set by King Eurystheus of Tiryns
Key Fact: Key Labours include: Nemean Lion, Lernaean Hydra, Ceryneian Hind, Erymanthian Boar, Augean Stables, Stymphalian Birds, Cretan Bull, Mares of Diomedes, Belt of Hippolyta, Cattle of Geryon, Apples of the Hesperides, and Cerberus
Key Fact: Heracles represents the hero who suffers greatly but achieves glory through endurance and strength
Key Fact: Greek heroes were typically semi-divine, performed extraordinary deeds, suffered greatly, and faced death
Key Fact: Heracles was eventually deified — after his death on a funeral pyre, he was taken to Olympus and made immortal
Key Fact: The Labours took Heracles to the edges of the known world, symbolising civilisation overcoming chaos
Key Fact: Other key heroes include Perseus (slew Medusa), Theseus (killed the Minotaur) and Jason (sought the Golden Fleece)
Key Fact: Hero myths follow a pattern: divine parentage, extraordinary childhood feat, quest or challenge, suffering, and death or deification
Key Fact: Heracles was worshipped as both a god and a hero across the Greek world

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Heracles and Heroes, you must know:

  • Hero: In Greek culture, a semi-divine figure of extraordinary ability who performed great deeds and suffered greatly
  • Labour: One of the twelve seemingly impossible tasks Heracles had to complete as penance for killing his family
  • Eurystheus: King of Tiryns who set Heracles his Twelve Labours, following the oracle at Delphi's instruction
  • Deification: The process of making a mortal into a god — Heracles was taken to Olympus after his death
  • Xenia: The Greek concept of hospitality and guest-friendship, which heroes both protected and sometimes violated
  • Arete: Excellence or virtue — the quality of being the best, which heroes embodied through their deeds

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Why did Hera hate Heracles?

Q: What was the purpose of the Twelve Labours?

Q: How does Heracles fit the pattern of the Greek hero?

Q: What do the Labours symbolise in Greek culture?

Q: How was Heracles both a god and a hero?

✅ Answers

  1. Heracles was the son of Zeus and the mortal Alcmene, making him living proof of Zeus's infidelity. Hera's hatred was motivated by jealousy and a desire to punish Zeus's illegitimate offspring.
  2. They were Heracles' penance for killing his wife and children in a fit of Hera-induced madness. The oracle at Delphi instructed him to serve King Eurystheus for twelve years.
  3. He has divine parentage (son of Zeus), shows extraordinary strength from childhood, performs impossible deeds (the Labours), suffers greatly (madness, servitude, death), and achieves immortality.
  4. They represent civilisation overcoming chaos and barbarism — each Labour involves defeating a monster or taming a force of disorder at the edges of the known world.
  5. He was worshipped as a hero (a mortal of extraordinary deeds who died) and as a god (after his deification and ascent to Olympus). Some cults emphasised one aspect over the other.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When discussing Heracles, always connect his story to the wider concept of the Greek hero
  • Use specific Labours as evidence — don't just say 'he completed tasks'
  • The theme of suffering is central to Heracles — examiners want to see this recognised
  • Compare Heracles with other heroes if the question allows it
  • Remember that Heracles' story has a moral dimension: great deeds come at great personal cost

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Calling Heracles 'Hercules' in a Greek context ✓ Hercules is the Roman name — use Heracles for Greek topics in OCR J280

✗ Treating the Labours as just a list of adventures ✓ The Labours were penance for murder, symbolising redemption through suffering — their moral purpose is essential

✗ Forgetting Hera's role in Heracles' suffering ✓ Hera's persecution drives the entire narrative — her hatred causes both his madness and his Labours

✗ Describing heroes as simply 'brave' ✓ Greek heroes were defined by semi-divine birth, extraordinary deeds, extreme suffering, and facing death — courage alone is insufficient

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain why Heracles was considered the greatest of the Greek heroes. (12 marks)

Heracles was considered the greatest Greek hero because his deeds, suffering and eventual deification surpassed all other heroes. His Twelve Labours were the most extraordinary feats in Greek mythology: he defeated monsters that no other mortal could overcome, including the Nemean Lion whose skin was impervious to weapons, the many-headed Hydra, and Cerberus the guard-dog of the Underworld itself. These Labours took him to the very edges of the world and beyond, symbolising a reach no other hero achieved. His suffering was also unmatched — Hera's persecution drove him to murder his own family, he was forced into servitude under the inferior Eurystheus, and he ultimately died in agony from the poisoned blood of Nessus. Yet Heracles endured all of this, embodying the Greek concept of arete (excellence) through sheer perseverance. His deification was unique among heroes: he alone was granted full immortality and a place on Olympus, marrying Hebe and becoming the gatekeeper of the gods. Other heroes like Perseus and Theseus achieved great deeds but did not suffer as profoundly or attain godhood. Heracles thus represents the complete hero — one who reaches the absolute limits of human capability, endures the worst possible suffering, and transcends mortality itself.
