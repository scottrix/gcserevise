Myths of the Underworld

Greek beliefs about death, the Underworld and the afterlife

Key Fact: The Greek Underworld (Hades) was the realm of the dead, ruled by the god Hades and his queen Persephone
Key Fact: All souls went to the Underworld — it was not simply a place of reward or punishment
Key Fact: Charon the ferryman transported souls across the River Styx — the dead were buried with a coin for payment
Key Fact: The Underworld had distinct regions: Asphodel Meadows for ordinary souls, Elysium for heroes, and Tartarus for the wicked
Key Fact: The three judges of the dead (Minos, Rhadamanthus and Aeacus) decided where souls would go
Key Fact: Persephone's abduction by Hades explained the seasons — she spent part of the year in the Underworld
Key Fact: Orpheus descended to the Underworld to retrieve his wife Eurydice but lost her by looking back
Key Fact: Theseus and Pirithous attempted to abduct Persephone but were trapped in chairs of forgetfulness
Key Fact: The River Lethe caused souls to forget their past lives before reincarnation in some traditions
Key Fact: Hermes psychopompos (guide of souls) escorted the dead to the Underworld
Key Fact: The Eleusinian Mysteries promised initiates a better afterlife, suggesting hope beyond mere existence in Asphodel
Key Fact: Odysseus and Aeneas both visited the Underworld while still alive — katabasis or nekuia episodes

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Myths of the Underworld, you must know:

  • Hades: Both the name of the god of the Underworld and the Underworld itself — the realm of the dead
  • Katabasis: A descent to the Underworld — a journey made by a living person to the realm of the dead
  • Elysium: The paradise region of the Underworld reserved for heroes and the specially favoured
  • Tartarus: The deepest, darkest region of the Underworld where the wicked were punished
  • Charon: The ferryman who transported souls across the River Styx into the Underworld
  • Nekuia: A ritual calling up of the dead — Odysseus performs a nekuia in Book 11 of the Odyssey

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Why were the dead buried with a coin in their mouth?

Q: What were the three main regions of the Underworld?

Q: How did Persephone's story explain the seasons?

Q: Why did Orpheus lose Eurydice?

Q: What is the difference between katabasis and nekuia?

✅ Answers

  1. To pay Charon the ferryman for transport across the River Styx — souls without payment were said to wander the banks for a hundred years.
  2. The Asphodel Meadows (for ordinary souls), Elysium (for heroes and the favoured), and Tartarus (for the wicked and those who offended the gods).
  3. Persephone was abducted by Hades and ate pomegranate seeds, binding her to the Underworld for part of each year. Her mother Demeter's grief caused winter; her return brought spring.
  4. Hades allowed Orpheus to lead Eurydice out on condition he did not look back. At the last moment, Orpheus looked to check she was following, and she was dragged back forever.
  5. Katabasis is a physical journey down to the Underworld; nekuia is summoning the dead while remaining at the surface.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When discussing the Underworld, always specify which region you mean — don't treat it as one place
  • The Persephone myth is central — it connects the Underworld to Greek explanations of the natural world
  • Use the Orpheus myth to illustrate themes of love, loss and the power and limits of music
  • Katabasis episodes in Homer and Virgil are key literary evidence for Underworld beliefs
  • Remember that Underworld beliefs evolved over time — early traditions were bleaker than later ones

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Treating the Underworld as equivalent to the Christian Hell ✓ The Underworld was the home of ALL the dead — only Tartarus was a place of punishment; most souls went to the Asphodel Meadows

✗ Confusing Hades the god with Hades the place without clarifying which you mean ✓ Specify 'the god Hades' or 'the Underworld (Hades)' to avoid ambiguity

✗ Saying all Greeks believed in the same afterlife ✓ Beliefs evolved significantly — Homer's shade-like ghosts differ from the later Elysium/Tartarus system, and the Eleusinian Mysteries offered different hope

✗ Forgetting that Persephone ate the pomegranate seeds herself ✓ In most versions she ate the seeds willingly or accidentally — this detail is crucial because it bound her to the Underworld by her own action

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How far do Greek myths of the Underworld reveal a pessimistic view of death? (15 marks)

Greek Underworld myths reveal a fundamentally pessimistic view of death, though they also contain elements of hope. The earliest and most influential depiction — Homer's Odyssey Book 11 — is deeply bleak. Achilles tells Odysseus he would rather be a landless peasant on Earth than king of the dead, declaring that death strips all meaning from existence. The shades in Homer are witless, feeble remnants of their living selves. The Asphodel Meadows, where most ordinary souls dwelt, was a place of neutral nothingness — neither reward nor punishment, merely a joyless continuation. This reflects a view that death is the end of everything worthwhile about life. However, there were more hopeful traditions. Elysium offered a paradise for heroes and the favoured, suggesting that exceptional deeds or divine favour could be rewarded. The Eleusinian Mysteries promised initiates a blessed afterlife, indicating that Greek religion developed more optimistic beliefs over time. The myth of Persephone also introduces hope: she returns from the Underworld each year, symbolising the cycle of death and rebirth. Yet even these hopeful elements are limited — Elysium was for very few, and Persephone's return is always temporary. Overall, Greek Underworld myths are predominantly pessimistic, reflecting a culture that valued life intensely and viewed death as a devastating loss, with only limited routes to a better existence beyond it.
