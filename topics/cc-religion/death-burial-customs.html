Death & Burial Customs

Greek and Roman funeral rites, beliefs about the dead and the importance of proper burial

Key Fact: Proper burial was essential in both Greek and Roman culture — the unburied dead could not enter the Underworld
Key Fact: The Greek prothesis was the laying-out of the body, where family mourned and paid respects over several days
Key Fact: The ekphora was the funeral procession to the burial site, held before dawn
Key Fact: Greek funerals included sacrifices, libations and offerings of food and drink at the graveside
Key Fact: Antigone's defiance of Creon to bury her brother Polyneices shows how seriously Greeks took burial obligations
Key Fact: Greek tombstones (stelae) often depicted the deceased in everyday scenes and carried moving epitaphs
Key Fact: Roman funerals for elite families were elaborate public events with processions, masks of ancestors and eulogies
Key Fact: Roman tombs lined the roads outside cities — the Via Appia is lined with elaborate monuments
Key Fact: The Parentalia was a Roman festival in February when families visited tombs and made offerings to the dead
Key Fact: Lemuria was another Roman festival when ghosts of the dead were placated with offerings
Key Fact: Both cultures believed that neglecting the dead brought pollution (miasma) and divine punishment
Key Fact: Burial practices reflected social status — wealthy Greeks and Romans built elaborate tombs; the poor received simpler burials

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Death & Burial Customs, you must know:

  • Prothesis: The Greek ritual of laying out the dead body at home for mourning by family and friends
  • Ekphora: The Greek funeral procession carrying the body to the burial or cremation site, held before dawn
  • Miasma: Religious pollution caused by leaving the dead unburied or failing to perform proper rites
  • Stele: An upright stone slab used as a grave marker, often carved with images and inscriptions
  • Parentalia: A Roman festival (13-21 February) when families visited tombs and made offerings to honour the dead
  • Imagines: Wax masks of deceased ancestors worn by actors in elite Roman funeral processions

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Why was proper burial so important to the Greeks?

Q: What happened during the Greek prothesis?

Q: How did Antigone's story illustrate Greek burial beliefs?

Q: How did elite Roman funerals differ from Greek ones?

Q: What was the purpose of the Roman Parentalia?

✅ Answers

  1. Without proper burial, the soul could not enter the Underworld and would wander eternally. Unburied corpses also caused miasma (religious pollution) that offended the gods and endangered the community.
  2. The body was washed, anointed with oil, dressed in white and laid on a kline (couch). Family and friends gathered to mourn, sing dirges and pay respects over several days.
  3. Antigone defied King Creon's order to leave her brother Polyneices unburied, performing burial rites herself. She chose death rather than allow her brother to suffer the ultimate dishonour of remaining unburied.
  4. Elite Roman funerals were grand public spectacles with actors wearing wax ancestor masks (imagines), a procession through the Forum, and a public eulogy celebrating the family's achievements.
  5. Families visited ancestral tombs, made offerings of food, wine and flowers, and performed rites to honour and placate the dead, maintaining the bond between the living and deceased.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • The Antigone story is the key literary evidence for the importance of burial — know it well
  • Always explain the RELIGIOUS significance of burial practices, not just the social customs
  • Miasma is an important concept — neglecting the dead endangered the whole community, not just the family
  • Compare Greek and Roman burial customs when the question allows
  • Tomb art and epitaphs are valuable evidence for understanding attitudes to death

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Describing burial customs without explaining their religious significance ✓ Burial was not just social convention — it was a religious obligation. Unburied dead caused miasma and could not enter the Underworld

✗ Confusing prothesis (laying out) with ekphora (procession) ✓ Prothesis = laying out the body at home for mourning; ekphora = the funeral procession to the burial site

✗ Assuming all Greeks and Romans had the same burial practices ✓ Practices varied by period, region and social class — elite Roman funerals were far more elaborate than those of ordinary citizens

✗ Forgetting that burial customs served the living as well as the dead ✓ Funerals reinforced family bonds, displayed social status, and allowed the community to share grief — they were about the survivors as much as the deceased

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How far did burial customs reveal what the Greeks and Romans believed about death? (15 marks)

Burial customs revealed Greek and Roman beliefs about death in profound ways, showing that both cultures viewed death as a transition requiring ritual intervention rather than a simple end. The insistence on proper burial demonstrated the belief that the soul needed ritual to reach the Underworld — Antigone's willingness to die rather than leave her brother unburied shows this was a non-negotiable religious obligation. The prothesis and ekphora in Greek practice showed that death was a communal event requiring the participation of family and community. The offerings of food, drink and libations at the graveside revealed a belief that the dead continued to need sustenance, or at least that maintaining a relationship with the deceased was important. Roman practices went further: the Parentalia festival showed that the dead were honoured regularly, not just at the funeral, while the imagines at elite funerals revealed a belief in the ongoing presence and influence of ancestors. Both cultures' elaborate tomb art and epitaphs showed a desire to preserve the memory and identity of the dead, suggesting that personal identity mattered beyond death. However, burial customs also revealed social values as much as religious ones — the grandeur of elite tombs displayed wealth and status, and Roman funeral eulogies were political speeches as much as religious acts. The emphasis on ancestry in Roman funerals served the living family's political interests. Overall, burial customs revealed genuine beliefs about death, the afterlife and the ongoing relationship between the living and dead, but they also served the social and political interests of the survivors.
