Temples & Sanctuaries

The design, function and significance of Greek and Roman temples and sacred spaces

Key Fact: Temples were the houses of the gods, not places of congregational worship — rituals took place at outdoor altars
Key Fact: The Parthenon on the Athenian Acropolis was dedicated to Athena and housed her colossal chryselephantine statue
Key Fact: Greek temples used three architectural orders: Doric (simple, no base), Ionic (scroll capitals) and Corinthian (acanthus leaves)
Key Fact: The cella (naos) was the inner room housing the cult statue; the pronaos was the porch entrance
Key Fact: Roman temples borrowed Greek styles but often used the Corinthian order and were built on raised podiums
Key Fact: The Pantheon in Rome, rebuilt by Hadrian, featured a massive dome and oculus — a revolutionary design
Key Fact: Sanctuaries like Delphi and Olympia were pan-Hellenic sites attracting visitors from all Greek states
Key Fact: Temples displayed the wealth and power of their city — the Parthenon was funded by the Delian League
Key Fact: Sacrifice was the central act of worship: animal sacrifice (thusia) involved burning bones for the gods and sharing meat
Key Fact: Libations (liquid offerings of wine, oil or milk) and votive offerings were also important religious practices
Key Fact: Priests and priestesses served the cult of a specific deity — the Pythia at Delphi was the most famous priestess
Key Fact: Temples were decorated with sculpture depicting myths, battles and processions — the Parthenon frieze showed the Panathenaic procession

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Temples & Sanctuaries, you must know:

  • Cella (naos): The main inner room of a temple where the cult statue of the god was housed
  • Cult statue: A large statue of the deity housed inside the temple, usually made of precious materials
  • Chryselephantine: Made of gold and ivory — Phidias's Athena Parthenos and Zeus at Olympia were chryselephantine
  • Thusia: Animal sacrifice — the central ritual act of Greek religion, where bones and fat were burned for the gods
  • Libation: A liquid offering poured onto an altar or the ground — usually wine, oil, milk or honey
  • Votive offering: A gift left at a temple or shrine to thank a god or request a favour — could be small figurines or valuable objects

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When describing temples, always explain their function AND their cultural significance

  • Don't confuse Greek and Roman temple styles — they differ significantly
  • Use technical vocabulary (cella, pronaos, thusia) to show subject knowledge
  • Remember that temples were political statements as much as religious buildings

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Describing temples as 'churches' where people went to pray ✓ Temples were the god's house holding the cult statue; worship and sacrifice happened at the outdoor altar

✗ Confusing Doric and Ionic orders ✓ Doric = plain capital, no base, simple; Ionic = scroll capital, column base, more elegant

✗ Forgetting that temple-building was a political act ✓ The Parthenon was funded by the Delian League — it was a statement of Athenian imperial power as much as religious devotion

✗ Describing sacrifice as cruel or primitive ✓ Animal sacrifice was a meaningful communal ritual that reinforced social bonds and the relationship between mortals and gods

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How far did temples serve political as well as religious purposes in the ancient world? (15 marks)

Temples served political purposes just as much as religious ones in the ancient world, though their sacred function remained fundamental. The Parthenon is the clearest example: funded by the Delian League treasury, it was a monument to Athenian imperial power as much as to Athena. Its sculptural programme depicted Athenian myths, the Panathenaic procession, and battles against Amazons and Centaurs — all projecting messages about Athenian civilisation defeating barbarism. The sheer cost and scale of the building announced that Athens was the greatest Greek city. Similarly, Roman emperors used temple-building to legitimise their rule: the Temple of Mars Ultor in Augustus's Forum celebrated his avenging of Caesar's murder, and the Ara Pacis (Altar of Peace) linked Augustus's reign to divine favour and Roman peace. The Pantheon, rebuilt by Hadrian, displayed Roman engineering genius and imperial generosity. Sanctuaries like Delphi and Olympia were politically neutral spaces where rival states could meet, making them centres of diplomacy as well as religion. However, temples were genuinely sacred spaces where the gods were honoured. The rituals performed at temples were heartfelt acts of devotion, and the divine presence in the cult statue was taken seriously. The political and religious functions were not separate — political authority was legitimised by divine favour, and the gods were believed to actively support their chosen cities and leaders. Therefore, temples were simultaneously religious and political institutions, with neither function being merely a cover for the other.
