G35: Reducing the Development Gap

Foundation Higher All Boards

Strategies to reduce the development gap including investment, tourism, aid, intermediate technology, fairtrade, debt relief, and microfinance.

📉 The Development Gap

Key Concept: The development gap is the difference in levels of wealth and standard of living between the richest and poorest countries in the world. Reducing this gap requires strategies that address economic, social, and political inequality.

The development gap has many consequences including differences in health, education, and quality of life. There are multiple strategies that can be used to reduce the gap, each with strengths and limitations.

🏗️ Investment

Definition: Investment is when money is put into a country's economy to generate future returns. This can come from foreign direct investment (FDI), governments, or international organisations.

Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)

TNCs and foreign governments invest in factories, infrastructure, and businesses in LICs and NEEs. This brings jobs, technology, and skills.

Advantages Disadvantages
Creates employment and income for local people Profits often return to the investing country
Brings new technology and skills TNCs may exploit cheap labour and weak laws
Develops infrastructure (roads, ports, power) Jobs may be low-skilled and low-paid
Increases tax revenue for government Can lead to over-dependence on foreign companies
Example: Chinese Investment in Africa

China has invested over $300 billion in sub-Saharan Africa since 2005, building roads, railways, ports, and stadiums. In Ethiopia, Chinese investment helped build the Addis Ababa-Djibouti railway, improving trade links. However, critics argue this creates "debt-trap diplomacy" - Zambia owed over $6 billion to China by 2022, raising concerns about sovereignty.

🏭 Industrial Development

Developing manufacturing and service industries allows countries to move from producing low-value primary goods to higher-value products, increasing income and employment.

Key Point: Countries typically develop through the Clark-Fisher model: primary sector (farming, mining) → secondary sector (manufacturing) → tertiary sector (services) → quaternary sector (research, IT). Moving up this chain increases GNI per head.
Example: Bangladesh's Garment Industry

Bangladesh's ready-made garment industry accounts for over 80% of the country's exports and employs over 4 million people, mostly women. Wages are low (around $95/month minimum in 2023), but the industry has contributed to Bangladesh's GNI per head rising from $640 in 2000 to $2,824 in 2022. However, safety concerns persist - the 2013 Rana Plaza collapse killed 1,134 workers.

🏖️ Tourism

Definition: Tourism is travel for pleasure or business. It is one of the world's largest industries and can bring significant income and employment to LICs and NEEs.
Advantages of Tourism Disadvantages of Tourism
Creates jobs directly and indirectly (hotels, transport, food) Jobs are often seasonal and low-paid
Brings foreign exchange earnings Profits may leak to foreign-owned companies
Develops infrastructure (airports, roads, water supply) Environmental damage (pollution, habitat loss)
Encourages cultural preservation and understanding Cultural dilution and commodification
Can fund conservation and national parks Tourists' demands for water/energy strain local supply
Economic Leakage: A high percentage of tourism revenue may "leak" out of the destination country. In some LICs, leakage can be 70-80% of gross tourism revenue, going to foreign-owned airlines, hotel chains, and imported goods.
Case Study: Tourism in Jamaica

Background: Jamaica is a Caribbean island NEE with a population of 2.8 million. Tourism contributes approximately 30% of GDP and employs around 25% of the workforce. In 2019, Jamaica welcomed over 4.3 million visitors.

Advantages: Tourism employs over 350,000 Jamaicans directly and indirectly. It generates over $3.7 billion in foreign exchange earnings (2019). Infrastructure has improved - Montego Bay and Kingston airports were expanded, and new roads connect tourist areas. The industry has stimulated local businesses including craft markets, restaurants, and transport services. The All-Inclusive Hotel model (e.g. Sandals, Couples) guarantees visitor numbers.

Disadvantages: Economic leakage is high - much revenue goes to foreign-owned hotel chains and airlines. All-inclusive hotels mean tourists rarely spend money in local communities. Many tourism jobs are low-skilled and low-paid (housekeeping, waiting). Environmental damage includes reef destruction from boat anchors and coastal development. Crime rates in tourist areas deter some visitors. The industry is vulnerable to external shocks - COVID-19 caused visitor numbers to drop by 70% in 2020, devastating the economy.

🤝 Aid

Definition: Aid is the transfer of resources from one country to another, or from an organisation to a country, to help with development or emergencies.
Type of Aid Description Example
Short-term/emergency aid Food, medicine, shelter given after disasters UK aid after 2010 Haiti earthquake
Long-term/developmental aid Ongoing support for education, healthcare, infrastructure UK's Department for International Development (DFID) programmes
Bilateral aid Given directly from one government to another USA to Egypt (military and economic)
Multilateral aid Given through international organisations like the World Bank or UN World Bank loans for infrastructure in India
Tied aid Aid given with condition that recipient buys goods from donor UK aid requiring purchase of British engineering services
Advantages of Aid: Provides resources that LICs cannot afford; funds essential services like healthcare and education; responds to emergencies; builds infrastructure. Disadvantages: Can create dependency; may be misused by corrupt governments; tied aid benefits donor more than recipient; does not always reach the poorest people.

🔧 Intermediate Technology

Definition: Intermediate technology (also called appropriate technology) is technology that is suited to the needs, skills, and resources of the local community. It is simple, affordable, easy to maintain, and environmentally sustainable - unlike high-tech solutions that require expensive expertise and parts.
Examples of Intermediate Technology

Treadle pumps: Human-powered water pumps used in Bangladesh and India, allowing farmers to irrigate crops without expensive diesel pumps. Cost around $20-35 each.

Solar cookers: Use sunlight to cook food, reducing the need for firewood (which causes deforestation) and reducing smoke inhalation. Used in parts of Africa and India.

Rainwater harvesting: Simple systems collect rainwater from roofs for drinking and irrigation, addressing water scarcity without expensive infrastructure.

Bicycle-powered generators: Generate small amounts of electricity for lighting or charging phones without connection to a grid.

Why Intermediate Technology Works: It uses local materials and skills → Affordable to buy and maintain → Does not require expensive imported parts → Creates local employment → Environmentally sustainable → Empowers communities rather than creating dependency.

⚖️ Fairtrade

Definition: Fairtrade is a trading partnership that ensures producers in LICs receive a fair price for their products, decent working conditions, and a premium for community development. It is certified by the Fairtrade Foundation.
Example: Fairtrade Cocoa in Ghana

Ghana is the world's second-largest cocoa producer. The Kuapa Kokoo cooperative, with over 100,000 members, sells Fairtrade cocoa. Farmers receive the Fairtrade minimum price ($2,400 per tonne, compared to the world market price which can fall below $2,000) plus a Fairtrade Premium of $240 per tonne for community projects. The premium has funded schools, clean water wells, and mobile health clinics. However, Fairtrade represents only a small share of the global market, and some farmers outside the scheme struggle to compete.

Advantages Disadvantages
Guarantees a minimum price above market rate Only a small percentage of trade is Fairtrade
Community premium funds schools, wells, clinics Fairtrade products cost more for consumers
Improves working conditions and bans child labour Does not address root causes of trade inequality
Empowers farmers through cooperatives Non-Fairtrade farmers may be worse off by comparison

💳 Debt Relief

Definition: Debt relief is when some or all of a country's debt is cancelled or restructured, allowing the government to spend more on development rather than debt repayments.
Example: HIPC Initiative

The Heavily Indebted Poor Countries (HIPC) initiative, launched by the IMF and World Bank in 1996, has provided debt relief to 36 countries, mostly in Africa. Uganda was the first beneficiary in 1998 - its debt was reduced from $4.3 billion to $1.5 billion. The savings were used to double primary school enrolment and reduce HIV prevalence. By 2020, over $76 billion in debt had been relieved under HIPC. However, critics note that conditions attached (privatisation, free trade) can harm the poor, and some countries have fallen back into debt.

🏦 Microfinance

Definition: Microfinance provides small loans (microcredit) and financial services to people who cannot access traditional banking. It targets the poorest, especially women, enabling them to start small businesses.
Example: Grameen Bank, Bangladesh

Founded by Muhammad Yunus in 1983, Grameen Bank has provided microloans to over 9 million borrowers in Bangladesh, 97% of whom are women. Average loan size is around $100. Borrowers use loans for income-generating activities: buying chickens, sewing machines, or mobile phones to rent out. Repayment rates are over 97%. Yunus won the Nobel Peace Prize in 2006. However, critics argue microfinance can trap borrowers in cycles of debt if businesses fail, and that interest rates (around 20%) are still high for the very poor.

Advantages of Microfinance Disadvantages of Microfinance
Reaches people excluded from traditional banking Interest rates are high compared to normal loans
Empowers women and increases their status Can create debt if businesses fail
High repayment rates (often 95%+) Only provides small amounts - cannot fund large projects
Creates sustainable livelihoods Not all borrowers are entrepreneurs - some need wages

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain how tourism can help reduce the development gap. Use a named example.

Q2: What is intermediate technology? Give two examples and explain how they help development.

Q3: Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of using aid to reduce the development gap.

Q4: Explain how microfinance can help reduce the development gap.

Q5: Using the case study of Jamaica, evaluate the effectiveness of tourism as a strategy to reduce the development gap.

Q6: "Fairtrade is the most effective way to reduce the development gap." How far do you agree?

✅ Answers

  1. Tourism brings foreign exchange earnings and creates employment. In Jamaica, tourism contributes 30% of GDP and employs 25% of the workforce (350,000+ jobs). It also stimulates infrastructure development such as airports and roads. However, economic leakage means much of the revenue goes to foreign companies, and many jobs are low-paid and seasonal.
  2. Intermediate technology is simple, affordable technology suited to the skills and resources of local communities. Example 1: Treadle pumps in Bangladesh allow farmers to irrigate crops without expensive diesel pumps, increasing food production and income. Example 2: Solar cookers in Africa reduce the need for firewood (preventing deforestation) and reduce smoke inhalation, improving health.
  3. Advantages: Aid provides essential resources that LICs cannot afford, funding healthcare, education, and infrastructure; emergency aid saves lives after disasters; development aid can build long-term capacity. Disadvantages: Aid can create dependency where countries rely on external support rather than developing their own economies; it may be misused by corrupt governments; tied aid primarily benefits the donor country; and it does not always reach the poorest people who need it most.
  4. Microfinance provides small loans to people who cannot access traditional banking, especially women. The Grameen Bank in Bangladesh has lent to 9 million borrowers, enabling them to start small businesses (e.g. buying livestock or sewing machines). This creates sustainable income, empowers women, and has high repayment rates (97%). It helps reduce the development gap by building economic independence from the bottom up.
  5. Jamaica's tourism industry has been effective in some ways: it generates $3.7 billion in foreign exchange, employs 350,000 people, and has improved infrastructure. However, effectiveness is limited by high economic leakage (profits going to foreign hotel chains), low wages, seasonal employment, and vulnerability to external shocks (COVID-19 caused a 70% drop in visitors). Tourism alone is insufficient - Jamaica needs to diversify its economy and reduce leakage to make tourism more effective at reducing the development gap.
  6. Fairtrade is effective because it guarantees a minimum price, provides a community premium for development projects, and improves working conditions. Ghana's Kuapa Kokoo cooperative has used Fairtrade premiums for schools and clinics. However, Fairtrade represents only a small share of global trade, products cost more, and it does not address structural inequality in the global trading system. Other strategies may be equally or more important: debt relief frees up government revenue, microfinance empowers entrepreneurs, and industrial development creates higher-value jobs. A combination of strategies is likely most effective.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use a named case study with specific data when evaluating strategies
  • For "how far do you agree" questions, discuss multiple strategies and compare their effectiveness
  • Remember to consider both advantages AND disadvantages for every strategy
  • Use the Jamaica case study with specific statistics for tourism questions
  • Key terms to use: economic leakage, intermediate technology, Fairtrade premium, microcredit, HIPC
  • Higher mark questions require evaluation - don't just describe, assess how effective each strategy is

📝 Exam Technique

Geography Exam Tips:
Use specific development indicators with data. Know the DTM stages. For case studies, cover TNC impacts (advantages AND disadvantages), aid types and effectiveness, and UK economic change including the north-south divide.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often write vague answers without specific geographical evidence. Wrong: Writing generalised statements like 'it causes problems' Correct: Using specific data and named examples, e.g. 'the 2010 Haiti earthquake killed over 200,000 people due to poor building quality'

Students often confuse causes and effects. Wrong: Mixing up what caused the event with what resulted from it Correct: Clearly separate causes (why it happened) from effects (what happened as a result)

Students often describe rather than evaluate. Wrong: Listing strategies without assessing their effectiveness Correct: Weighing up strengths and weaknesses of each approach and reaching a supported judgement

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

6 marks: Explain the key factors affecting reducing the development gap.

Reducing the Development Gap involves multiple interconnected factors that geographers must understand. The key concepts include the processes that create and change reducing the development gap, the impacts on both people and environment, and the strategies used to manage associated challenges. For a comprehensive answer, specific case study evidence should be used throughout, with named examples and data to support each point. Geographical terminology should be used precisely, and the interrelationship between physical and human factors should be demonstrated. Top-level responses evaluate the relative importance of different factors and consider how the situation varies between locations.

Mark scheme: 2 marks for identifying key factors, 2 marks for explaining processes with detail, 2 marks for using specific evidence

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 requires knowledge of the key facts and processes related to reducing the development gap. AO2 demands understanding of how and why these processes operate, and their implications. AO3 asks you to analyse, evaluate and make judgements — this is where grade 9 answers stand out by weighing up competing perspectives and reaching supported conclusions. AO4 may involve interpreting maps, graphs or data related to this topic. To move from grade 5 to grade 9: use precise geographical terminology, support every point with specific case study evidence, and always evaluate rather than just describe.
