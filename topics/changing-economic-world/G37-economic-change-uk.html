G37: Economic Change in the UK

Foundation Higher All Boards

Understanding de-industrialisation, the post-industrial economy, the north-south divide, and the UK's role in the wider world economy.

🏭 De-industrialisation in the UK

Definition: De-industrialisation is the decline in the importance of the secondary sector (manufacturing) in a country's economy, measured by output or employment.

The UK was the first country to industrialise (from the 1760s) and one of the first to de-industrialise. Manufacturing employment has fallen dramatically:

  • In 1970, manufacturing employed 8.5 million people (33% of workforce)
  • By 2020, manufacturing employed only 2.7 million people (8% of workforce)
  • Manufacturing's share of GDP fell from over 30% in 1970 to around 10% in 2020

Causes of De-industrialisation

  • Globalisation: Cheaper labour abroad made UK manufacturing uncompetitive - textile mills moved to Bangladesh, car production to Eastern Europe and Asia
  • Mechanisation and automation: Technology replaced human workers in factories, mines, and shipyards
  • Government policy: Privatisation and closure of unprofitable industries (especially coal mines) under Thatcher in the 1980s
  • Rising service sector: The tertiary sector offered higher wages and attracted workers away from manufacturing
  • Foreign competition: Countries like China could produce goods at much lower cost due to lower wages and fewer regulations
Case Study: Decline of Coal Mining

British coal mining employed over 1 million people in the 1920s. By the 1980s, the industry was losing money as cheaper imported coal and natural gas replaced it. The 1984-85 miners' strike failed to prevent pit closures. By 2020, only a handful of surface mines remained. Entire communities in Yorkshire, South Wales, and Nottinghamshire lost their primary employer, causing unemployment, deprivation, and social problems that persist today.

💼 The Post-Industrial Economy

Definition: A post-industrial economy is one where the service sector (tertiary) and knowledge-based industries (quaternary) dominate, having replaced manufacturing as the main source of employment and GDP.
Sector % of UK GDP (2022) % of UK Employment (2022) Key Industries
Primary 1% 1.5% Agriculture, fishing, mining
Secondary 10% 8% Manufacturing, construction, energy
Tertiary 69% 72% Retail, healthcare, education, finance, tourism
Quaternary 20% 18.5% IT, research and development, biotechnology, consultancy

Growth of the Service Sector

  • Financial services: London is the world's second-largest financial centre after New York; the sector contributes over £160 billion to UK GDP
  • Technology: The UK tech sector was valued at $1 trillion in 2022, making it the third most valuable globally after the US and China
  • Creative industries: Worth over £115 billion, including film, TV, music, gaming, and advertising
  • Higher education: International students contribute over £25 billion annually to the UK economy

Science and Business Parks

Key Concept: Science parks are located near universities and focus on research and high-tech industries. Business parks are developments offering modern office space for service-sector companies. Both are typically located on the edge of cities where land is cheaper and transport links are good.
Example: Cambridge Science Park

Established in 1970 by Trinity College Cambridge, it is the UK's oldest and largest science park. It houses over 100 companies employing 7,000+ people in sectors including biotechnology, AI, and pharmaceuticals. Known as "Silicon Fen," the area around Cambridge has generated over 4,000 knowledge-intensive firms. The park benefits from proximity to Cambridge University for research talent and graduate recruitment.

Impacts of Post-Industrial Change

Positive Impacts Negative Impacts
Higher wages in service and knowledge sectors Job losses in manufacturing communities
Cleaner environment (fewer factories) Skill mismatch - former factory workers lack qualifications for service jobs
Growth of high-tech industries Regional inequality - service jobs concentrated in the south
More flexible working patterns Insecure work (zero-hours contracts, gig economy)

↔️ The North-South Divide

Key Concept: The north-south divide refers to the perceived economic and cultural differences between southern England (wealthier, service-based, London-centric) and northern England (historically industrial, lower average incomes, higher unemployment).
Indicator South East (incl. London) North East
Median weekly earnings £726 £565
Unemployment rate 3.6% 5.2%
% with degree-level qualifications 45% 33%
Life expectancy (males) 81 years 77 years
House prices (median) £425,000 £155,000
Number of businesses per 10,000 people 540 260

Causes of the North-South Divide

  • De-industrialisation hit the north harder: Mining, shipbuilding, and heavy industry were concentrated in the north - when they closed, replacement jobs were scarce
  • London's dominance: The capital's financial and service industries attract investment, talent, and government spending disproportionately
  • Transport investment: Crossrail (£19 billion), HS1, and major airport expansion have favoured the south; northern transport infrastructure lags behind
  • Brain drain: Educated young people leave northern towns for London and the south east

Government Strategies to Address the Divide

  • Northern Powerhouse (2015): Aimed to boost northern cities (Manchester, Leeds, Liverpool, Sheffield) through devolution, transport investment, and science investment
  • HS2 (High Speed 2): Planned high-speed rail linking London to Birmingham and the north (though the northern leg was cancelled in 2023)
  • Levelling Up Fund (2021): £4.8 billion allocated to local infrastructure projects across the UK, particularly in deprived areas
  • Devolution deals: Elected mayors for combined authorities (e.g. Greater Manchester, West Midlands) with powers over transport, housing, and skills
Example: Manchester's Regeneration

Manchester has been at the forefront of northern regeneration. The 1996 IRA bomb triggered city-centre rebuilding. MediaCityUK in Salford brought BBC and ITV operations, creating 7,000 jobs. Metrolink tram network expanded to 99 stops. The city's population grew from 430,000 (2001) to 560,000 (2021). However, central Manchester's regeneration contrasts with deprivation in areas like Moss Side and Longsight, showing that not all communities benefit equally.

🌐 The UK in the Wider World

The UK's Economic Role

  • Trade: The UK is the 5th largest economy globally. Main exports: machinery, vehicles, pharmaceuticals, financial services. Main imports: machinery, vehicles, food, clothing
  • EU membership (1973-2020): EU membership gave access to the single market; Brexit (2020) created new trade barriers but also new opportunities for independent trade deals
  • Commonwealth links: Historical ties with 56 Commonwealth nations provide trade and cultural connections
  • Foreign Direct Investment: The UK is the 4th largest recipient of FDI globally, particularly in financial services, technology, and pharmaceuticals

Transport and Communications

  • Heathrow Airport: One of the world's busiest international airports (62 million passengers in 2022), connecting the UK to global business centres
  • Port of Felixstowe: The UK's largest container port, handling over 40% of containerised trade
  • Digital connectivity: 97% of UK premises have superfast broadband access, supporting the growing digital economy
Post-Brexit Challenges: Leaving the EU single market has created trade friction (customs checks, regulatory divergence), labour shortages (especially in agriculture, hospitality, and logistics), and reduced EU research funding. However, the UK has signed trade deals with Australia, Japan, and CPTPP (Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership). The long-term economic impact remains debated.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the causes of de-industrialisation in the UK.

Q2: Describe the characteristics of a post-industrial economy using the UK as an example.

Q3: What is the north-south divide? Describe two ways the government has tried to reduce it.

Q4: Explain the advantages and disadvantages of science parks for local economies.

Q5: "De-industrialisation has had only negative impacts on the UK." How far do you agree?

Q6: Assess the UK's role in the global economy after Brexit.

✅ Answers

  1. De-industrialisation in the UK was caused by: globalisation (cheaper labour abroad made UK manufacturing uncompetitive); mechanisation (technology replaced human workers); government policy (privatisation and closure of unprofitable industries like coal under Thatcher); rising service sector wages attracting workers from manufacturing; and foreign competition (countries like China producing goods more cheaply). Manufacturing employment fell from 8.5 million in 1970 to 2.7 million in 2020.
  2. A post-industrial economy is dominated by the tertiary (services, 69% of GDP) and quaternary (knowledge-based, 20% of GDP) sectors. In the UK, financial services (London as a global hub), technology (valued at $1 trillion), and creative industries (£115 billion) have replaced manufacturing. Science parks like Cambridge Science Park drive innovation in biotechnology and AI. Manufacturing now accounts for only 10% of GDP and 8% of employment.
  3. The north-south divide is the economic disparity between southern England (wealthier, service-based, higher wages, more investment) and northern England (lower average incomes, higher unemployment, legacy of de-industrialisation). Government strategies: the Northern Powerhouse aimed to boost northern cities through devolution and transport investment; the Levelling Up Fund allocated £4.8 billion to deprived areas for local infrastructure projects.
  4. Advantages: Science parks create high-skilled, high-wage jobs; attract investment to an area; benefit from proximity to universities for research and talent; raise the area's profile as a centre of innovation. Cambridge Science Park employs 7,000+ people in high-tech industries. Disadvantages: Jobs require high qualifications, excluding local people without degrees; may widen inequality within the area; land and property prices rise, displacing existing residents; economic benefits may not spread to surrounding deprived communities.
  5. While de-industrialisation caused significant job losses and community decline (e.g. coal mining communities losing their primary employer), it also brought positive changes. The environment improved as polluting factories closed; the service and knowledge sectors offer higher wages and cleaner working conditions; science parks have driven innovation; and flexible working patterns have increased. However, the benefits have been uneven - the south gained service jobs while the north suffered disproportionately. The overall impact depends on location, skills, and the ability of communities to adapt.
  6. After Brexit, the UK faces challenges including trade friction with the EU (its largest trading partner), labour shortages in key sectors, and reduced research funding. However, the UK remains the 5th largest economy, is the 4th largest recipient of FDI, and has signed new trade deals (CPTPP, Australia, Japan). London remains a global financial centre, and the tech sector continues to grow. The long-term impact depends on how the UK adapts its trade relationships, attracts skilled workers, and invests in innovation. The picture is mixed - some sectors have struggled while others have adapted successfully.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Use specific UK examples: Cambridge Science Park, Manchester regeneration, coal mining decline
  • Include data in your answers (percentages, employment figures, GDP contributions)
  • For north-south divide questions, compare specific indicators (wages, unemployment, life expectancy)
  • Always evaluate both positive and negative impacts of economic change
  • Know the difference between tertiary and quaternary sectors
  • For Brexit questions, present a balanced view with both challenges and opportunities
  • Higher mark questions: consider whether government strategies to reduce the divide have been effective

📝 Exam Technique

Geography Exam Tips:
Use specific development indicators with data. Know the DTM stages. For case studies, cover TNC impacts (advantages AND disadvantages), aid types and effectiveness, and UK economic change including the north-south divide.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often write vague answers without specific geographical evidence. Wrong: Writing generalised statements like 'it causes problems' Correct: Using specific data and named examples, e.g. 'the 2010 Haiti earthquake killed over 200,000 people due to poor building quality'

Students often confuse causes and effects. Wrong: Mixing up what caused the event with what resulted from it Correct: Clearly separate causes (why it happened) from effects (what happened as a result)

Students often describe rather than evaluate. Wrong: Listing strategies without assessing their effectiveness Correct: Weighing up strengths and weaknesses of each approach and reaching a supported judgement

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

6 marks: Explain the key factors affecting economic change in the uk.

Economic Change in the UK involves multiple interconnected factors that geographers must understand. The key concepts include the processes that create and change economic change in the uk, the impacts on both people and environment, and the strategies used to manage associated challenges. For a comprehensive answer, specific case study evidence should be used throughout, with named examples and data to support each point. Geographical terminology should be used precisely, and the interrelationship between physical and human factors should be demonstrated. Top-level responses evaluate the relative importance of different factors and consider how the situation varies between locations.

Mark scheme: 2 marks for identifying key factors, 2 marks for explaining processes with detail, 2 marks for using specific evidence

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 requires knowledge of the key facts and processes related to economic change in the uk. AO2 demands understanding of how and why these processes operate, and their implications. AO3 asks you to analyse, evaluate and make judgements — this is where grade 9 answers stand out by weighing up competing perspectives and reaching supported conclusions. AO4 may involve interpreting maps, graphs or data related to this topic. To move from grade 5 to grade 9: use precise geographical terminology, support every point with specific case study evidence, and always evaluate rather than just describe.
