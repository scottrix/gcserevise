Pure Substances

A pure substance is a single element or compound. It has a sharp, fixed melting point and boiling point. In everyday language, "pure" often means nothing added, but in chemistry it means only one substance is present.
  • A pure element contains only one type of atom (e.g. gold, oxygen).
  • A pure compound contains only one type of molecule (e.g. distilled water, sodium chloride).
  • Mixtures are not pure substances — they contain two or more different substances.
Worked Example 1: Identifying pure substances

Question: A sample of water melts at exactly 0 °C and boils at exactly 100 °C. Is it pure? Explain your answer.

Answer: Yes, the water is pure. Pure substances have sharp melting and boiling points. The fact that it melts at exactly 0 °C and boils at exactly 100 °C with no range of temperatures indicates no impurities are present.

Purity Analysis: Melting and Boiling Points

Impure substances melt and boil over a range of temperatures, and their melting/boiling points are different from the pure substance. Impurities lower the melting point and raise the boiling point.
Feature Pure Substance Impure Substance (Mixture)
Melting point Sharp, fixed temperature Lower than expected; melts over a range
Boiling point Sharp, fixed temperature Higher than expected; boils over a range
Heating/cooling curve Flat horizontal section at melting/boiling point No completely flat section; gradual change
Worked Example 2: Interpreting melting point data

Question: A student measures the melting point of a sample of aspirin. It starts to melt at 124 °C and is fully liquid by 130 °C. Pure aspirin melts at 136 °C. What can you conclude?

Answer: The sample is impure. Two pieces of evidence: (1) It melts over a range of temperatures (124–130 °C) rather than at a single sharp point. (2) The melting point is lower than the pure value of 136 °C. Both observations indicate the presence of impurities.

Formulations

A formulation is a mixture that has been designed as a useful product, made by mixing the ingredients in specific, measured quantities so the product performs its intended function.
Formulation Key Ingredients Purpose of Each Ingredient
Medicines / drugs Active drug, binder, filler, coating Drug treats illness; others control release and stability
Paints Pigment, solvent, binder Pigment gives colour; solvent lets it flow; binder sticks it to surface
Alloys Metal + other elements Improve strength, hardness, corrosion resistance
Fertilisers N, P, K compounds in specific ratios Provide essential nutrients for plant growth
Cleaning products Surfactant, solvent, fragrance, thickener Surfactant removes grease; others aid application
Worked Example 3: Explaining formulations

Question: Explain why paint is described as a formulation and why the ratio of ingredients matters.

Answer: Paint is a formulation because it is a mixture designed for a useful purpose with specific quantities of ingredients. The pigment provides colour, the solvent allows the paint to be spread easily, and the binder holds the pigment on the surface after the solvent evaporates. If the ratios were wrong (e.g. too much solvent), the paint might be too thin and runny; if too little solvent, it would be too thick to apply.

Chromatography

Chromatography is a technique used to separate and identify substances in a mixture. A substance can be identified by its Rf value, which is calculated from the distances moved.
Rf = distance moved by substance ÷ distance moved by solvent
  • The Rf value is always between 0 and 1.
  • The Rf value for a particular substance is the same in the same solvent at the same temperature.
  • Different substances have different Rf values, so Rf can be used to identify them.
  • A pure substance produces one spot on a chromatogram; a mixture produces multiple spots.
Worked Example 4: Calculating Rf values

Question: In a chromatogram, substance A moves 3.6 cm from the baseline and the solvent front moves 6.0 cm. Calculate the Rf value of substance A.

Answer:
Rf = distance moved by substance ÷ distance moved by solvent
Rf = 3.6 ÷ 6.0 = 0.60

Worked Example 5: Identifying substances using Rf

Question: A mixture is analysed by chromatography. Substance X has an Rf value of 0.45. The known Rf values in this solvent are: glucose = 0.33, sucrose = 0.45, fructose = 0.58. What is substance X?

Answer: Substance X has Rf = 0.45, which matches sucrose. Since the Rf value is the same in the same solvent, substance X is sucrose.

Practice Questions

F H

Q1. Define the term "pure substance" in chemistry. How can you tell if a sample is pure using its melting point?

Show Answer

A pure substance is a single element or compound with nothing else mixed in. A pure substance has a sharp, fixed melting point. If the sample melts over a range of temperatures, or at a lower temperature than expected, it is impure.

F H

Q2. A sample of a compound starts to melt at 110 °C and finishes melting at 118 °C. The pure compound melts at 125 °C. What do these results tell you about the sample?

Show Answer

The sample is impure. It melts over a range of temperatures (110–118 °C) rather than at a single sharp point. The melting point is also lower than the pure value of 125 °C. Both observations indicate impurities are present.

F H

Q3. Explain what a formulation is and give two examples. Why must the ingredients be in specific measured quantities?

Show Answer

A formulation is a mixture designed as a useful product, with ingredients in specific measured quantities. Examples: medicines (drug + binder + filler), paints (pigment + solvent + binder). The quantities must be measured carefully so the product works as intended — wrong ratios could make the product ineffective or unsafe.

F H

Q4. In chromatography, a substance moves 4.2 cm from the baseline and the solvent front moves 7.0 cm. Calculate the Rf value. If the reference Rf for aspirin in this solvent is 0.60, is the substance aspirin?

Show Answer

Rf = 4.2 ÷ 7.0 = 0.60. Since this matches the reference Rf value for aspirin (0.60) in the same solvent, the substance is aspirin.

H

Q5. A chromatogram of a food colouring shows three spots. Explain what this tells you about the food colouring. How could you identify each substance?

Show Answer

Three spots mean the food colouring is a mixture of three different substances (a pure substance would give only one spot). To identify each substance, calculate the Rf value for each spot and compare with known reference Rf values in the same solvent. Matching Rf values identify the substances.

Exam tip: When answering purity questions, always state both observations — that impure substances melt over a range and at a lower temperature than pure. For Rf calculations, always show your working and give the answer to 2 decimal places. Remember that Rf has no units. A common mistake is dividing the solvent distance by the substance distance — always put the substance distance on top.
