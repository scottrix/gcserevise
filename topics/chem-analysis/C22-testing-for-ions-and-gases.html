C22: Testing for Ions and Gases

← C21: Pure Substances and Formulations

Flame Tests

Flame tests identify metal ions by the colour they produce in a Bunsen burner flame. Different metal ions give characteristic flame colours.
Metal Ion Flame Colour
Lithium (Li⁺) Crimson red
Sodium (Na⁺) Yellow
Potassium (K⁺) Lilac
Calcium (Ca²⁺) Orange-red
Copper (Cu²⁺) Green-blue

Method: Clean a nichrome wire loop by dipping in concentrated HCl and heating. Dip the loop in the sample and place in the blue Bunsen flame. Observe the colour.

Worked Example 1: Identifying a metal ion by flame test

Question: A compound produces a lilac flame in a flame test. Which metal ion is present?

Answer: A lilac flame indicates the presence of potassium ions (K⁺).

Limitation: Flame tests can be unreliable because the yellow sodium flame can mask other colours. Also, some flame colours are similar (e.g. lithium crimson and calcium orange-red). Using instrumental methods (e.g. flame emission spectroscopy) is more accurate and sensitive.

Sodium Hydroxide Tests for Metal Ions

Adding sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution to a solution containing metal ions produces a precipitate (insoluble solid). The colour of the precipitate identifies the metal ion.
Metal Ion Precipitate Colour Additional Observation
Copper(II) (Cu²⁺) Blue Does not dissolve in excess NaOH
Iron(II) (Fe²⁺) Green Does not dissolve in excess NaOH
Iron(III) (Fe³⁺) Brown Does not dissolve in excess NaOH
Aluminium (Al³⁺) White Dissolves in excess NaOH (forms clear solution)
Calcium (Ca²⁺) White Does not dissolve in excess NaOH
Magnesium (Mg²⁺) White Does not dissolve in excess NaOH
Both aluminium and calcium/magnesium give white precipitates. To distinguish aluminium from calcium and magnesium: add excess NaOH. The aluminium precipitate dissolves; the calcium and magnesium precipitates do not.
Worked Example 2: Identifying metal ions with NaOH

Question: NaOH is added to an unknown solution. A white precipitate forms, which dissolves when excess NaOH is added. Which metal ion is present?

Answer: A white precipitate that dissolves in excess NaOH indicates aluminium ions (Al³⁺). Neither calcium nor magnesium white precipitates dissolve in excess NaOH.

Worked Example 3: Writing ionic equations

Question: Write an ionic equation for the reaction between copper(II) sulfate solution and sodium hydroxide solution.

Answer:
Cu²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Cu(OH)₂(s)
The blue precipitate is copper(II) hydroxide. Na⁺ and SO₄²⁻ are spectator ions.

Testing for Carbonates

To test for carbonates, add dilute acid (e.g. HCl). If the substance is a carbonate, it will fizz as carbon dioxide gas is produced.
Carbonate + Acid → Salt + Water + Carbon dioxide
e.g. Na₂CO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂

Confirm CO₂ by passing the gas through limewater — it turns milky/cloudy.

Worked Example 4: Testing a carbonate

Question: A student adds dilute HCl to a white powder and observes fizzing. The gas produced turns limewater milky. What is the conclusion?

Answer: The fizzing and the CO₂ test (limewater turns milky) confirm the white powder contains carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻).

Testing for Halides (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻)

To test for halide ions: add nitric acid (to remove other ions that could interfere), then add silver nitrate solution. The colour of the precipitate identifies the halide.
Halide Ion Precipitate with AgNO₃ Ionic Equation
Chloride (Cl⁻) White precipitate Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)
Bromide (Br⁻) Cream precipitate Ag⁺(aq) + Br⁻(aq) → AgBr(s)
Iodide (I⁻) Yellow precipitate Ag⁺(aq) + I⁻(aq) → AgI(s)
Worked Example 5: Identifying a halide ion

Question: A solution is tested with nitric acid followed by silver nitrate. A cream precipitate forms. Which halide ion is present?

Answer: A cream precipitate with silver nitrate indicates bromide ions (Br⁻). The ionic equation is: Ag⁺(aq) + Br⁻(aq) → AgBr(s).

Testing for Sulfates

To test for sulfate ions: add dilute hydrochloric acid (to remove carbonate ions that would also give a white precipitate), then add barium chloride solution. A white precipitate of barium sulfate confirms sulfate ions.
Ba²⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) → BaSO₄(s)
Worked Example 6: Testing for sulfate

Question: Explain why HCl is added before barium chloride when testing for sulfates.

Answer: HCl is added first to remove carbonate ions that may be present. Carbonate ions would also produce a white precipitate with barium chloride (BaCO₃), giving a false positive. The acid reacts with any carbonate to produce CO₂, preventing interference.

Gas Tests

Gases can be identified using simple chemical tests. Each gas gives a characteristic result.
Gas Test Positive Result
Hydrogen (H₂) Place a lit splint at the mouth of the test tube Squeaky "pop" sound
Oxygen (O₂) Place a glowing splint into the gas Splint relights
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) Bubble the gas through limewater (calcium hydroxide solution) Limewater turns milky/cloudy
Chlorine (Cl₂) Place damp blue litmus paper in the gas Litmus paper is bleached white (may turn red first)
Worked Example 7: Identifying gases

Question: A gas is produced in a reaction. A glowing splint relights when placed in the gas. What is the gas? Write the test for hydrogen and state the result you would expect.

Answer: A glowing splint relighting identifies oxygen (O₂). The test for hydrogen is to place a lit splint at the mouth of the test tube — a squeaky pop sound would confirm hydrogen is present.

Practice Questions

F H

Q1. Describe how to carry out a flame test and state the flame colour for lithium, sodium and potassium ions.

Show Answer

Clean a nichrome wire loop by dipping in concentrated HCl and heating in a Bunsen flame. Dip the loop in the sample, then place in the blue Bunsen flame. Observe the colour. Lithium = crimson red, sodium = yellow, potassium = lilac.

F H

Q2. Sodium hydroxide is added to solutions containing different metal ions. State the colour of the precipitate formed for: (a) Cu²⁺, (b) Fe²⁺, (c) Fe³⁺, (d) Al³⁺.

Show Answer

(a) Cu²⁺ = blue precipitate
(b) Fe²⁺ = green precipitate
(c) Fe³⁺ = brown precipitate
(d) Al³⁺ = white precipitate (that dissolves in excess NaOH)

H

Q3. An unknown solution gives a white precipitate with NaOH. How could you determine whether the ion is Al³⁺, Ca²⁺ or Mg²⁺?

Show Answer

Add excess NaOH. If the white precipitate dissolves, the ion is Al³⁺ (aluminium). If it does not dissolve, the ion is Ca²⁺ or Mg²⁺. To distinguish between Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺, carry out a flame test: calcium gives an orange-red flame, while magnesium does not produce a distinctive flame colour.

F H

Q4. Describe the test for sulfate ions and explain why dilute HCl is added before barium chloride.

Show Answer

Add dilute HCl to the solution, then add barium chloride solution. A white precipitate confirms sulfate ions are present. HCl is added first to remove carbonate ions that would also produce a white precipitate with barium chloride (BaCO₃), giving a false positive result.

F H

Q5. Describe the tests and positive results for: (a) hydrogen, (b) oxygen, (c) carbon dioxide, (d) chlorine.

Show Answer

(a) Hydrogen: place a lit splint at the mouth of the tube → squeaky pop.
(b) Oxygen: place a glowing splint in the gas → relights.
(c) Carbon dioxide: bubble through limewater → turns milky.
(d) Chlorine: place damp litmus paper in the gas → bleached white.

H

Q6. A solution contains both carbonate ions and sulfate ions. Explain how you would test for sulfate ions without interference from the carbonate.

Show Answer

Add dilute HCl first. The acid reacts with the carbonate ions to produce CO₂ gas (which can be confirmed by passing through limewater), removing the carbonate ions from solution. Then add barium chloride. Any white precipitate now must be due to barium sulfate, confirming sulfate ions, as the carbonate has already been removed.

Exam tip: Learn the sequence for halide tests: nitric acid first, then silver nitrate. For sulfate tests: HCl first, then barium chloride. Always explain why the acid is added (to remove interfering ions), not just that it is added. For NaOH tests, remember the key distinguishing feature of aluminium: the white precipitate dissolves in excess NaOH. Use ionic equations when asked for equations in ion test questions.
