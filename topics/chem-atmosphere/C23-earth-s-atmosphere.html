The Early Atmosphere

The Earth's early atmosphere was formed by intense volcanic activity. It contained large amounts of carbon dioxide and water vapour, with nitrogen and small amounts of ammonia and methane. There was little or no oxygen.
  • Volcanoes released gases including CO₂, water vapour, nitrogen, ammonia and methane.
  • This is similar to the atmospheres of Venus and Mars today (mostly CO₂).
  • There was no oxygen in the early atmosphere — it is wrong to think oxygen was always present.
Worked Example 1: Describing the early atmosphere

Question: Describe the composition of the Earth's early atmosphere and explain how it was formed.

Answer: The early atmosphere was formed by volcanic activity. It contained large amounts of carbon dioxide and water vapour, along with nitrogen and small amounts of ammonia and methane. There was little or no oxygen. The gases came from volcanoes which released them during intense volcanic activity as the Earth cooled.

How the Atmosphere Changed

The atmosphere changed over billions of years through several key processes: condensation of water vapour, dissolving of CO₂ in oceans, and photosynthesis by plants and algae.

1. Water vapour condensed to form oceans

As the Earth cooled, water vapour in the atmosphere condensed to form liquid water, creating the oceans.

2. Carbon dioxide was removed

  • CO₂ dissolved in the oceans.
  • Dissolved CO₂ formed carbonate sediments on the sea floor (e.g. calcium carbonate from shells and skeletons of marine organisms).
  • This locked up CO₂ in rocks, reducing the amount in the atmosphere.

3. Photosynthesis produced oxygen

Algae and plants evolved and carried out photosynthesis. This removed CO₂ from the atmosphere and produced oxygen. Over time, oxygen built up in the atmosphere.
Photosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen

4. The ozone layer formed

As oxygen levels increased, some oxygen formed ozone (O₃) in the upper atmosphere. The ozone layer shields the Earth's surface from harmful UV radiation, allowing more complex organisms to evolve and survive on land.
Worked Example 2: Explaining atmospheric change

Question: Explain how the amount of oxygen in the atmosphere increased over time.

Answer: Oxygen increased because algae and plants evolved and carried out photosynthesis. Photosynthesis takes in CO₂ and releases oxygen. Over millions of years, as more plants and algae grew, the oxygen produced built up in the atmosphere. The formation of the ozone layer then protected organisms from UV radiation, allowing life to thrive and continue producing oxygen.

Worked Example 3: Explaining the decrease in CO₂

Question: Describe two processes that reduced the amount of CO₂ in the atmosphere.

Answer: (1) CO₂ dissolved in the oceans and formed carbonate sediments on the sea floor, which locked CO₂ away in rocks. (2) Photosynthesis by algae and plants removed CO₂ from the atmosphere and used it to make glucose and other organic compounds.

Current Composition of the Atmosphere

The current atmosphere is approximately: nitrogen ~78%, oxygen ~21%, argon ~0.96%, CO₂ ~0.04%, plus trace amounts of other gases and water vapour.
Gas Approximate % Origin / Role
Nitrogen (N₂) ~78% Present from volcanic activity; largely inert
Oxygen (O₂) ~21% Produced by photosynthesis
Argon (Ar) ~0.96% Noble gas, from radioactive decay
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) ~0.04% Residual from early atmosphere; increased by human activity
Worked Example 4: Comparing early and current atmospheres

Question: Compare the composition of the early atmosphere with the current atmosphere.

Answer: The early atmosphere had lots of CO₂ and water vapour but little or no oxygen. The current atmosphere has mostly nitrogen (~78%) and oxygen (~21%), with only ~0.04% CO₂ and very little water vapour. CO₂ decreased dramatically due to dissolving in oceans and photosynthesis. Oxygen increased due to photosynthesis by plants and algae.

The Greenhouse Effect

The greenhouse effect is a natural process that keeps the Earth warm enough to support life. Greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane and water vapour) absorb and re-radiate infrared radiation from the Earth's surface, trapping heat in the atmosphere.
  • Short-wavelength radiation from the Sun passes through the atmosphere and is absorbed by the Earth's surface.
  • The Earth radiates longer-wavelength infrared radiation.
  • Greenhouse gases absorb some of this infrared radiation and re-radiate it back towards the surface, warming the Earth.
  • Without the greenhouse effect, the Earth would be too cold for life.

Q5. Describe the greenhouse effect and name three greenhouse gases.

Show Answer

The greenhouse effect is a natural process that warms the Earth. Short-wavelength radiation from the Sun passes through the atmosphere and warms the Earth's surface. The Earth emits longer-wavelength infrared radiation. Greenhouse gases absorb this infrared radiation and re-radiate it in all directions, including back towards the surface, keeping the Earth warm. Three greenhouse gases are carbon dioxide, methane and water vapour.

H

Q6. Explain the role of the ozone layer in the evolution of life on Earth.

Show Answer

As oxygen levels increased in the atmosphere, some oxygen formed ozone (O₃) in the upper atmosphere. The ozone layer absorbs harmful UV radiation from the Sun. Before the ozone layer formed, UV radiation would have damaged organisms, preventing complex life from surviving on land. Once the ozone layer was in place, it protected the Earth's surface, allowing organisms to live on land and evolve into more complex life forms.

Exam tip: When explaining how the atmosphere changed, structure your answer chronologically: (1) water vapour condensed → oceans, (2) CO₂ dissolved → carbonate sediments, (3) photosynthesis → oxygen increased, (4) ozone formed → protected life. Always emphasise that the early atmosphere had no oxygen — this is a common misconception. For the greenhouse effect, make sure you describe the process (Sun's radiation absorbed, Earth re-emits infrared, greenhouse gases absorb and re-radiate) rather than just naming it.
