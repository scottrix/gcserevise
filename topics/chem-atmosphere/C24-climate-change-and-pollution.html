C24: Climate Change and Pollution

← C23: Earth's Atmosphere

Greenhouse Gases and Human Activities

Human activities are increasing the concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere. The main greenhouse gases are carbon dioxide (CO₂) and methane (CH₄). Water vapour is also a greenhouse gas.
Greenhouse Gas Sources (Human Activities)
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) for energy and transport; deforestation (trees that absorbed CO₂ are removed)
Methane (CH₄) Farming (cattle and rice paddies produce methane); landfill sites (decomposition of organic waste); extraction of fossil fuels
Worked Example 1: Explaining the increase in greenhouse gases

Question: Explain how human activities have increased the amount of CO₂ in the atmosphere.

Answer: CO₂ has increased because of: (1) Burning fossil fuels for electricity, heating and transport — combustion of coal, oil and natural gas releases CO₂ that was previously locked underground. (2) Deforestation — cutting down trees reduces the amount of CO₂ absorbed by photosynthesis. When trees are burned or decompose, stored carbon is released as CO₂.

Worked Example 2: Sources of methane

Question: Give two human activities that release methane into the atmosphere.

Answer: (1) Farming — cattle and other livestock produce methane during digestion, and rice paddies produce methane from anaerobic decomposition. (2) Landfill sites — organic waste decomposes anaerobically, producing methane gas.

Climate Change

Increased greenhouse gas concentrations are causing global warming — an increase in the average temperature of the Earth. This leads to climate change, with significant consequences.
Consequence Explanation
Rising sea levels Thermal expansion of oceans and melting of ice caps and glaciers
Changing weather patterns More extreme weather events (floods, droughts, hurricanes)
Habitat loss Ecosystems change faster than species can adapt (e.g. coral bleaching, Arctic ice loss)
Species migration and extinction Species move to cooler areas; those that cannot adapt or migrate may die out
Impact on food production Changing rainfall and temperature affect crop yields; some areas become unfarmable
Worked Example 3: Consequences of climate change

Question: Describe three consequences of global warming and explain how each occurs.

Answer: (1) Rising sea levels — as temperatures rise, ocean water expands (thermal expansion) and ice caps/glaciers melt, adding more water to the oceans. (2) Habitat loss — changing temperatures and conditions alter ecosystems faster than species can adapt, e.g. coral reefs bleach and die when water is too warm. (3) Impact on food production — changing rainfall patterns and increased droughts reduce crop yields in some regions, while floods in other areas also destroy crops.

Carbon Footprint

A carbon footprint is the total amount of CO₂ and other greenhouse gas emissions caused by a person, organisation, event or product, expressed as a CO₂ equivalent.
  • It includes direct emissions (e.g. from driving a car) and indirect emissions (e.g. from manufacturing the products you buy).
  • Everything we do that uses energy from fossil fuels contributes to our carbon footprint.
Worked Example 4: Understanding carbon footprints

Question: Explain what is meant by the carbon footprint of a product, giving examples of activities that contribute to it.

Answer: The carbon footprint of a product is the total CO₂ equivalent emissions from all stages of its life. This includes emissions from: extracting raw materials, manufacturing the product, transporting it, using the product, and disposing of or recycling it. For example, the carbon footprint of a car includes emissions from making the steel, assembling the car, driving it (burning fuel), and scrapping it.

Reducing Carbon Footprints

Reducing carbon footprints involves reducing greenhouse gas emissions. Methods include using renewable energy, improving energy efficiency, carbon capture, and changing agricultural practices.
  • Replace fossil fuels with renewable energy sources (wind, solar, hydroelectric, geothermal).
  • Improve insulation and energy efficiency in homes and industry.
  • Use carbon capture and storage (CCS) — capture CO₂ from power stations and store it underground.
  • Reduce deforestation and plant more trees (afforestation) to absorb CO₂.
  • Reduce consumption of meat and dairy (less methane from farming).
  • Use more public transport, electric vehicles, and cycling.

Difficulties in reducing carbon footprints: scientific disagreement over causes and effects, economic considerations (fossil fuels are cheap and established), lack of political will, incomplete international cooperation, and reluctance of individuals to change lifestyle.

Air Pollution

Burning fossil fuels also releases pollutants that harm the environment and human health: sulfur dioxide, oxides of nitrogen, and particulates (carbon particles/soot).

Sulfur Dioxide (SO₂)

  • Produced when sulfur impurities in fossil fuels burn.
  • SO₂ dissolves in moisture in the atmosphere to form sulfuric acid, contributing to acid rain.
  • Acid rain damages buildings (corrodes limestone and metal), kills trees and aquatic life, and makes soil and lakes too acidic.

Oxides of Nitrogen (NOₓ)

  • Produced when nitrogen and oxygen in the air react at high temperatures in engines.
  • NOₓ also contributes to acid rain (forms nitric acid).
  • NOₓ reacts with sunlight and other pollutants to form photochemical smog.

Particulates (Carbon/soot)

  • Tiny solid particles produced from incomplete combustion of fossil fuels.
  • Can cause respiratory problems (asthma, lung disease) when inhaled.
  • Contribute to global dimming — particulates reflect sunlight back into space, reducing the amount of light reaching the Earth's surface.
Pollutant Source Environmental Problem Health Problem
Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) Burning sulfur-containing fossil fuels Acid rain (damages buildings, ecosystems) Respiratory irritation
Oxides of nitrogen (NOₓ) High-temperature combustion in engines Acid rain, photochemical smog Respiratory problems
Particulates (soot) Incomplete combustion of fossil fuels Global dimming Respiratory problems (asthma, lung disease)
Carbon monoxide (CO) Incomplete combustion of fossil fuels Toxic — binds to haemoglobin, reduces oxygen in blood Fatal in high concentrations
Worked Example 5: Explaining acid rain

Question: Explain how burning fossil fuels leads to acid rain and describe two effects of acid rain.

Answer: Fossil fuels contain sulfur impurities. When burned, sulfur forms SO₂. In the atmosphere, SO₂ dissolves in moisture to form sulfuric acid. High-temperature combustion also produces NOₓ, which forms nitric acid. These fall as acid rain. Effects: (1) Acid rain corrodes buildings and statues made of limestone or metal. (2) Acid rain makes lakes and soil too acidic, killing fish, plants and trees.

Worked Example 6: Global dimming

Question: What is global dimming and what causes it?

Answer: Global dimming is the reduction in sunlight reaching the Earth's surface. It is caused by particulates (tiny solid particles from incomplete combustion of fossil fuels) which reflect and scatter sunlight back into space. This means less energy reaches the surface, which can cause a cooling effect — though it does not cancel out global warming.

Practice Questions

F H

Q1. Name two greenhouse gases increased by human activity and state one source of each.

Show Answer

CO₂ — burning fossil fuels / deforestation. Methane — farming (cattle) / landfill sites.

F H

Q2. Describe three consequences of global warming / climate change.

Show Answer

(1) Rising sea levels due to thermal expansion and melting ice. (2) More extreme weather — floods, droughts, storms. (3) Habitat loss and species extinction — ecosystems change too quickly for species to adapt (e.g. coral bleaching). Also acceptable: impact on food production, migration of species to cooler areas.

F H

Q3. Define the term "carbon footprint" and suggest two ways an individual could reduce their carbon footprint.

Show Answer

A carbon footprint is the total amount of CO₂ and other greenhouse gas emissions caused by a person, organisation or product. Two ways to reduce it: (1) Use public transport, walk or cycle instead of driving. (2) Use renewable energy sources at home (e.g. solar panels, green energy tariff). Other valid answers: reduce meat consumption, improve home insulation, buy local products.

H

Q4. Explain how sulfur dioxide and oxides of nitrogen cause acid rain. Describe two effects of acid rain on the environment.

Show Answer

SO₂ from burning sulfur-containing fossil fuels and NOₓ from high-temperature combustion dissolve in moisture in the atmosphere to form sulfuric acid and nitric acid. These fall as acid rain. Effects: (1) Corrodes buildings and statues, especially those made of limestone/metal. (2) Makes lakes and soil acidic, killing fish, aquatic organisms and trees.

F H

Q5. Explain what particulates are, how they are produced, and two problems they cause.

Show Answer

Particulates are tiny solid particles (soot/carbon) produced by incomplete combustion of fossil fuels. Problems: (1) They cause respiratory problems such as asthma and lung disease when inhaled. (2) They contribute to global dimming by reflecting sunlight back into space, reducing the amount of light reaching the Earth's surface.

H

Q6. Suggest why it is difficult to reduce global carbon dioxide emissions, giving at least three reasons.

Show Answer

(1) Economic factors — fossil fuels are cheap and established; switching to renewables costs money. (2) Lack of international cooperation — not all countries agree on targets or implement them. (3) Dependence on fossil fuels — transport, industry and electricity generation still rely heavily on coal, oil and gas. (4) Reluctance to change lifestyles — individuals are unwilling to reduce car use, meat consumption, etc. (5) Incomplete scientific understanding in some areas leads to disagreement over the urgency of action.

Exam tip: When asked about climate change consequences, be specific — don't just say "it gets warmer". Mention sea level rise, extreme weather, habitat loss, species extinction, and food production impacts. For pollution questions, always link the pollutant to its source AND its effect (SO₂ → acid rain → corrodes buildings/kills aquatic life). Remember that global dimming is caused by particulates reflecting sunlight, not by greenhouse gases.
