C19: Hydrocarbons and Crude Oil

Crude Oil

Crude oil is a mixture of hydrocarbons that formed from the remains of ancient biomass (mostly plankton) over millions of years under high pressure and temperature. It is a finite resource — it cannot be replaced once used.
  • Crude oil is found in porous rocks beneath the Earth's surface.
  • It is a mixture, not a pure substance — it contains many different hydrocarbons with different chain lengths.
  • Because it is finite, we must use it carefully and find alternative resources.
Worked Example 1: Explaining crude oil formation

Question: Explain how crude oil was formed and why it is considered a finite resource.

Answer: Crude oil formed from ancient biomass (mainly plankton) that died and was buried under layers of sediment. Over millions of years, high pressure and temperature converted the biomass into crude oil. It is finite because it takes millions of years to form and we are using it much faster than it can be produced naturally — once supplies are used up, they cannot be replaced.

Fractional Distillation

Fractional distillation separates crude oil into fractions (groups of hydrocarbons with similar boiling points). The crude oil is heated and vapours rise up a fractionating column. Fractions condense at different heights depending on their boiling points.
  • The column is hottest at the bottom and coolest at the top.
  • Short-chain hydrocarbons have low boiling points — they condense near the top.
  • Long-chain hydrocarbons have high boiling points — they condense near the bottom.
Fraction Chain Length Boiling Point Range (°C) Uses
Petroleum gases C₁–C₄ Below 40 Heating, cooking (LPG)
Petrol (gasoline) C₅–C₁₀ 40–150 Fuel for cars
Kerosene C₁₀–C₁₆ 150–250 Aircraft fuel
Diesel oil C₁₄–C₂₀ 250–350 Fuel for lorries, trains
Fuel oil C₂₀–C₃₀ 350–500 Ship engines, power stations
Bitumen C₃₀+ Over 500 Road surfacing, roofing
Worked Example 2: Fractional distillation explanation

Question: Explain how fractional distillation separates the fractions in crude oil.

Answer: Crude oil is heated and vaporised. The vapours enter a fractionating column which is hot at the bottom and cool at the top. As the vapours rise, they cool down. Each fraction condenses at a different height because hydrocarbons have different boiling points. Long-chain hydrocarbons have high boiling points so they condense near the bottom (e.g. bitumen). Short-chain hydrocarbons have low boiling points so they condense near the top (e.g. petroleum gases).

Hydrocarbons

A hydrocarbon is a compound of carbon and hydrogen only. No other elements are present.

Alkanes

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons — they contain only single carbon–carbon bonds. The general formula for alkanes is CₙH₂ₙ₊₂.
Alkane general formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
Alkane Molecular Formula n Value Check
Methane CH₄ n = 1 2(1) + 2 = 4 H
Ethane C₂H₆ n = 2 2(2) + 2 = 6 H
Propane C₃H₈ n = 3 2(3) + 2 = 8 H
Butane C₄H₁₀ n = 4 2(4) + 2 = 10 H
Worked Example 3: Using the alkane formula

Question: An alkane has 8 carbon atoms. What is its molecular formula?

Answer: Using CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ with n = 8:
Number of H = 2(8) + 2 = 18
Molecular formula = C₈H₁₈ (octane)

Properties of Hydrocarbon Fractions

The properties of a hydrocarbon fraction depend on the length of the carbon chain.
Property Shorter Chains Longer Chains
Boiling point Lower Higher
Viscosity (how runny) More runny (less viscous) Less runny (more viscous)
Flammability More flammable Less flammable
Volatility (how easily evaporates) More volatile Less volatile
Worked Example 4: Comparing fractions

Question: Petrol is a shorter-chain fraction than diesel. Compare the viscosity, flammability and boiling point of petrol and diesel.

Answer: Petrol has shorter chains so it is less viscous (more runny), more flammable, and has a lower boiling point than diesel. Diesel has longer chains so it is more viscous (thicker), less flammable, and has a higher boiling point.

Cracking

Cracking breaks long-chain hydrocarbons into shorter, more useful molecules. It always produces at least one alkene (which has a C=C double bond) and an alkane.
  • Thermal cracking — high temperature (up to 750 °C) and high pressure.
  • Catalytic cracking — moderate temperature (~500 °C) with a catalyst (zeolite).
  • Cracking is needed because there is high demand for short-chain fractions (e.g. petrol) but supply from fractional distillation is limited.
Worked Example 5: Cracking equations

Question: Write a balanced equation for the cracking of C₁₄H₃₀ to produce ethene and one other product.

Answer:
C₁₄H₃₀ → C₂H₄ + C₁₂H₂₆
Check: C — 14 = 2 + 12 ✓   H — 30 = 4 + 26 ✓
Ethene (C₂H₄) is an alkene and C₁₂H₂₆ is an alkane (satisfies CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ with n = 12).

Alkenes

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons — they contain a C=C double bond. The general formula for alkenes is CₙH₂ₙ. Alkenes are more reactive than alkanes because of the double bond.
Alkene general formula: CₙH₂ₙ
Property Alkanes Alkenes
Bond type C—C single bonds only (saturated) Contains C=C double bond (unsaturated)
General formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ CₙH₂ₙ
Reactivity Less reactive More reactive
Bromine water test No change (stays orange) Decolourises (orange → colourless)
Bromine water test for unsaturation: Add bromine water (orange) to the hydrocarbon. If it decolourises (goes colourless), the substance is an alkene (unsaturated). If it stays orange, it is an alkane (saturated).
Worked Example 6: Identifying alkenes

Question: A hydrocarbon has the formula C₃H₆. Is it an alkane or an alkene? How would bromine water confirm this?

Answer: For an alkane with n = 3, the formula would be C₃H₈ (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂). C₃H₆ matches CₙH₂ₙ (with n = 3), so it is an alkene (propene). Bromine water would be decolourised from orange to colourless because the C=C double bond opens and reacts with bromine.

Practice Questions

F H

Q1. What is crude oil a mixture of? Explain why crude oil is described as a finite resource.

Show Answer

Crude oil is a mixture of hydrocarbons (compounds of carbon and hydrogen only). It is finite because it was formed from ancient biomass over millions of years and cannot be replaced within a human lifetime — once used, supplies are gone.

F H

Q2. Describe how fractional distillation separates crude oil into fractions. Explain why fractions condense at different heights in the column.

Show Answer

Crude oil is heated and vaporised, then the vapours enter a fractionating column. The column is hot at the bottom and cool at the top. As vapours rise they cool. Each fraction condenses at a different height because hydrocarbons have different boiling points — long-chain hydrocarbons have high boiling points and condense near the bottom, while short-chain hydrocarbons have low boiling points and condense near the top.

F H

Q3. An alkane has 5 carbon atoms. Use the general formula to work out its molecular formula and name it.

Show Answer

Using CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ with n = 5: H = 2(5) + 2 = 12. Molecular formula = C₅H₁₂. The name is pentane.

F H

Q4. Compare the properties of a short-chain hydrocarbon fraction (e.g. petrol) with a long-chain fraction (e.g. bitumen) in terms of boiling point, viscosity, flammability and volatility.

Show Answer

Petrol (short-chain) has a lower boiling point, is less viscous (more runny), is more flammable, and is more volatile than bitumen. Bitumen (long-chain) has a higher boiling point, is more viscous (thicker/sticky), is less flammable, and is less volatile.

H

Q5. Write a balanced symbol equation for the cracking of C₁₀H₂₂ to produce ethene and one other product. State whether each product is an alkane or an alkene.

Show Answer

C₁₀H₂₂ → C₂H₄ + C₈H₁₈
C₂H₄ (ethene) is an alkene — it has a C=C double bond and fits the formula CₙH₂ₙ.
C₈H₁₈ is an alkane — it has only single bonds and fits the formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ (n = 8, 2×8+2 = 18).

F H

Q6. Describe the bromine water test and explain how it distinguishes between an alkane and an alkene.

Show Answer

Add orange bromine water to the hydrocarbon and shake. If the bromine water is decolourised (turns from orange to colourless), the hydrocarbon is an alkene — the C=C double bond opens to react with bromine. If the bromine water stays orange, the hydrocarbon is an alkane — with only single bonds, it cannot react with the bromine in this way.

Exam tip: When asked about fractional distillation, always mention that the column has a temperature gradient (hot at bottom, cool at top) and that separation happens because different fractions have different boiling points. When writing cracking equations, always check your carbon and hydrogen atoms balance on both sides, and remember that cracking always produces at least one alkene.
