Alcohols

Alcohols are a homologous series with the functional group –OH (hydroxyl group). Their general formula is CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH. They are used as solvents, fuels and in alcoholic drinks.
Alcohol Formula Uses
Methanol CH₃OH Industrial solvent, chemical feedstock
Ethanol C₂H₅OH Alcoholic drinks, fuel, solvent
Propanol C₃H₇OH Solvent, cleaning agent
Worked Example 1: Naming alcohols

Question: Write the formula and name for the alcohol with 4 carbon atoms.

Answer: Using CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH with n = 4: H = 2(4) + 1 = 9. Formula = C₄H₉OH (butanol).

Reactions of Alcohols

Alcohols undergo three key reactions: combustion, oxidation and reaction with sodium.

1. Combustion

Alcohol + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water
e.g. C₂H₅OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O

Alcohols burn cleanly, making them useful fuels.

2. Oxidation

Alcohols are oxidised to form carboxylic acids. Oxidising agents such as acidified potassium dichromate(VI) are used. Ethanol oxidises to ethanoic acid.
C₂H₅OH + [O] → CH₃COOH + H₂O
ethanol → ethanoic acid + water

3. Reaction with Sodium

2C₂H₅OH + 2Na → 2C₂H₅ONa + H₂
ethanol + sodium → sodium ethoxide + hydrogen

Sodium sinks in ethanol, fizzes steadily (hydrogen gas), and dissolves. This is less vigorous than sodium with water.

Worked Example 2: Alcohol reactions

Question: Write balanced equations for (a) the complete combustion of methanol and (b) the oxidation of methanol to a carboxylic acid.

Answer:
(a) 2CH₃OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 4H₂O
(b) CH₃OH + [O] → HCOOH + H₂O (methanol → methanoic acid + water)

Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids have the functional group –COOH. They dissolve in water to give acidic solutions (pH below 7). They are weak acids because they only partially ionise in water.
Carboxylic Acid Formula Common Name
Methanoic acid HCOOH Found in ant stings
Ethanoic acid CH₃COOH Vinegar (dilute solution)
Propanoic acid C₂H₅COOH Food preservative

Reactions of Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids react like typical acids:

  • With metals: carboxylic acid + metal → salt + hydrogen
    e.g. 2CH₃COOH + Mg → (CH₃COO)₂Mg + H₂
  • With bases: carboxylic acid + base → salt + water
    e.g. CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O
  • With carbonates: carboxylic acid + carbonate → salt + water + CO₂
    e.g. 2CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂
Carboxylic acids are weak acids because they partially ionise in water. This means fewer H⁺ ions are released compared to strong acids like HCl, so their reactions are less vigorous.
Worked Example 3: Carboxylic acid reactions

Question: Ethanoic acid is added to sodium carbonate. Describe what you would observe and write a balanced equation.

Answer: You would observe fizzing (effervescence) as carbon dioxide gas is produced. Equation: 2CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂

Esters

An ester is formed when an alcohol reacts with a carboxylic acid. Water is also produced. Esters have pleasant fruity smells and are used in perfumes and flavourings.
Alcohol + Carboxylic Acid → Ester + Water
e.g. C₂H₅OH + CH₃COOH → CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O
ethanol + ethanoic acid → ethyl ethanoate + water

An acid catalyst (e.g. concentrated sulfuric acid) is usually needed for this reaction.

Worked Example 4: Ester formation

Question: Write the word equation for the reaction between methanol and ethanoic acid. Name the ester produced.

Answer: Methanol + Ethanoic acid → Methyl ethanoate + Water. The ester is methyl ethanoate.

Addition Polymers

Addition polymers are made from alkene monomers. The C=C double bond opens and the monomers join together in a long chain. No other substance is produced.
Monomer Polymer Uses
Ethene (C₂H₄) Polyethene (polythene) Carrier bags, bottles
Propene (C₃H₆) Polypropene (polypropylene) Rope, crates, car parts
Chloroethene PVC (polychloroethene) Window frames, pipes
n C₂H₄ → (—CH₂—CH₂—)ₙ
n ethene → polyethene
Worked Example 5: Drawing polymer from monomer

Question: Propene (CH₂=CHCH₃) undergoes addition polymerisation. Draw the repeating unit of poly(propene).

Answer: Open the C=C double bond and draw the repeating unit:
(—CH₂—CH(CH₃)—)ₙ
The double bond becomes a single bond, and the monomers join head-to-tail. The side group (—CH₃) stays attached to the carbon.

Condensation Polymers

Condensation polymers are made from two different monomers that join together with the loss of a small molecule (usually water). A common example is polyesters.

In polyester formation:

  • One monomer has a diol (two –OH groups)
  • The other monomer has a dicarboxylic acid (two –COOH groups)
  • Each time they join, a molecule of water is lost
Diol + Dicarboxylic acid → Polyester + Water
Feature Addition Polymers Condensation Polymers
Monomers One type (alkene) Two different types
By-product None Water (or other small molecule)
Bond formed C—C from opened C=C Ester linkage (—COO—)
Example Polyethene Polyester (e.g. Terylene)

Combustion of Alcohols Practical

In the combustion of alcohols practical, you measure the temperature rise of water when different alcohols are burned. This allows you to compare the energy released per mole.
Worked Example 6: Combustion practical

Question: In an experiment, 0.32 g of methanol (Mᵣ = 32) heated 100 g of water by 24 °C. The specific heat capacity of water is 4.2 J/g°C. Calculate the energy transferred per mole of methanol.

Answer:
Energy = mass of water × SHC × ΔT = 100 × 4.2 × 24 = 10 080 J
Moles of methanol = 0.32 ÷ 32 = 0.01 mol
Energy per mole = 10 080 ÷ 0.01 = 1 008 000 J/mol = 1008 kJ/mol

Practice Questions

F H

Q1. Name the functional group present in alcohols and give one use of ethanol.

Show Answer

The functional group in alcohols is the hydroxyl group (–OH). Ethanol is used as a fuel, a solvent, or in alcoholic drinks (any one).

F H

Q2. Write a balanced symbol equation for the complete combustion of ethanol.

Show Answer

C₂H₅OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O

F H

Q3. Explain why carboxylic acids are described as weak acids.

Show Answer

Carboxylic acids are weak acids because they partially ionise in water. This means only a small proportion of the acid molecules dissociate to release H⁺ ions, so the concentration of H⁺ is lower than in a strong acid of the same concentration. This makes their reactions less vigorous than strong acids.

H

Q4. Write a word equation for the formation of an ester from propanol and ethanoic acid. State the conditions needed.

Show Answer

Propanol + Ethanoic acid → Propyl ethanoate + Water. An acid catalyst (e.g. concentrated sulfuric acid) is needed, and the mixture is heated.

F H

Q5. Describe how addition polymers are formed from alkene monomers. Use ethene and polyethene as an example.

Show Answer

Addition polymerisation involves opening the C=C double bond in each alkene monomer. The monomers then join together in a long chain. For ethene: the C=C double bond in each ethene molecule opens, and the molecules link together to form a long chain of polyethene, (—CH₂—CH₂—)ₙ. No other substance is produced.

H

Q6. Compare addition polymerisation and condensation polymerisation in terms of the monomers used, the by-products, and the type of bond formed.

Show Answer

Addition polymerisation uses one type of monomer (alkenes); condensation uses two different monomers (e.g. a diol and a dicarboxylic acid). Addition polymerisation produces no by-product; condensation produces a small molecule (water). Addition polymers form C—C bonds from opened C=C double bonds; condensation polymers form new functional linkages such as ester bonds (—COO—).

Exam tip: When writing equations for alcohol combustion, always balance C first, then H, then O. For polymerisation questions, remember: addition polymers come from alkenes (double bond opens), condensation polymers come from two different monomers (water lost). In the combustion practical, common improvements include: using a draft shield, using a lid on the water, and reducing the distance between the flame and the beaker.
