C10: Concentration and Yield

FoundationHigher All Boards

Concentration, percentage yield and atom economy

📋 Key Definitions

Concentration: The amount of solute dissolved in a given volume of solution. Measured in g/dm³ or mol/dm³.
Percentage yield: The percentage of the theoretical maximum amount of product actually obtained from a reaction.
Atom economy: The percentage of atoms in the reactants that end up in the desired product. A measure of how efficiently atoms are used.

Key Formulae:

Concentration (g/dm³) = mass of solute (g) ÷ volume of solution (dm³)

Concentration (mol/dm³) = moles of solute ÷ volume of solution (dm³)

Percentage yield = (actual yield ÷ theoretical yield) × 100

Atom economy = (Mr of desired product ÷ sum of Mr of all products) × 100

📝 Concentration

The concentration of a solution tells you how much solute is dissolved in a certain volume of solvent. A more concentrated solution has more solute per unit volume.

Concentration UnitMeaningFormula
g/dm³Grams of solute per dm³ of solutionmass (g) ÷ volume (dm³)
mol/dm³Moles of solute per dm³ of solutionmoles ÷ volume (dm³)

Note: 1 dm³ = 1000 cm³. To convert cm³ to dm³, divide by 1000.

Worked Example 1: Calculating concentration in g/dm³

8 g of sodium hydroxide is dissolved in 250 cm³ of solution. Calculate the concentration in g/dm³.

Volume = 250 ÷ 1000 = 0.25 dm³

Concentration = 8 ÷ 0.25 = 32 g/dm³

Worked Example 2: Calculating concentration in mol/dm³

0.2 mol of HCl is dissolved in 500 cm³ of solution. Calculate the concentration in mol/dm³.

Volume = 500 ÷ 1000 = 0.5 dm³

Concentration = 0.2 ÷ 0.5 = 0.4 mol/dm³

Worked Example 3: Calculating mass from concentration

A solution of NaCl has a concentration of 20 g/dm³. What mass of NaCl is in 150 cm³ of this solution?

Volume = 150 ÷ 1000 = 0.15 dm³

Mass = concentration × volume = 20 × 0.15 = 3 g

📊 Percentage Yield

The theoretical yield is the maximum amount of product possible from the given amounts of reactants (calculated from the balanced equation). The actual yield is the amount of product actually obtained. The actual yield is always less than the theoretical yield.

Why actual yield is always less than theoretical:
  • Incomplete reactions: Not all reactants are converted to products
  • Product lost: Some product is lost during separation and purification (e.g. left on filter paper)
  • Reversible reactions: Some products react back to form reactants
  • Side reactions: Unexpected reactions produce unwanted by-products
Worked Example 4: Calculating percentage yield

The theoretical yield of a reaction is 50 g but only 40 g of product is obtained. Calculate the percentage yield.

Percentage yield = (40 ÷ 50) × 100 = 80%

Worked Example 5: Percentage yield from reacting masses

10 g of calcium carbonate is heated. The theoretical yield of calcium oxide is 5.6 g, but only 4.2 g is collected. Calculate the percentage yield.

Percentage yield = (4.2 ÷ 5.6) × 100 = 75%

♻️ Atom Economy

Atom economy measures how efficiently the reactant atoms end up in the desired product. A high atom economy means less waste, which is better for the environment and more sustainable.

MeasuresHow much product you actually make vs theoryHow efficiently atoms are used

Worked Example 6: Calculating atom economy

In the reaction CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂, the desired product is CaO. Calculate the atom economy.

Mr(CaO) = 56, Mr(CO₂) = 44

Atom economy = (56 ÷ (56 + 44)) × 100 = (56 ÷ 100) × 100 = 56%

Worked Example 7: Atom economy comparison

Compare the atom economy of two routes to produce hydrogen:

Route 1: CH₄ + H₂O → CO + 3H₂ (desired product: H₂)

Atom economy = (3 × 2) ÷ (28 + 6) × 100 = 6 ÷ 34 × 100 = 17.6%

Route 2: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂ (desired product: H₂)

Atom economy = 2 ÷ (136 + 2) × 100 = 2 ÷ 138 × 100 = 1.4%

Route 1 has the higher atom economy and is more sustainable.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Foundation 10 g of sugar is dissolved in 200 cm³ of solution. Calculate the concentration in g/dm³.

Q2: Foundation A reaction has a theoretical yield of 80 g. The actual yield is 60 g. Calculate the percentage yield.

Q3: Higher In the reaction C₂H₄ + Br₂ → C₂H₄Br₂, the desired product is C₂H₄Br₂. Calculate the atom economy. (Mr: C₂H₄Br₂ = 188)

Q4: Higher Explain two reasons why the actual yield of a reaction is always less than the theoretical yield.

Q5: Foundation A solution of HCl has a concentration of 0.5 mol/dm³. How many moles of HCl are in 100 cm³ of this solution?

✅ Answers

  1. Volume = 200 ÷ 1000 = 0.2 dm³. Concentration = 10 ÷ 0.2 = 50 g/dm³
  2. Percentage yield = (60 ÷ 80) × 100 = 75%
  3. This is an addition reaction — there is only one product. Atom economy = (188 ÷ 188) × 100 = 100%
  4. Any two from: incomplete reactions (not all reactants convert to products); product lost during separation/purification; reversible reactions (some products reform reactants); side reactions producing unwanted by-products.
  5. Volume = 100 ÷ 1000 = 0.1 dm³. Moles = 0.5 × 0.1 = 0.05 mol

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always convert cm³ to dm³ before calculating concentration (divide by 1000)
  • Atom economy uses the Mr of products only, not reactants
  • Percentage yield and atom economy are different — a reaction can have a high yield but low atom economy
  • Addition reactions always have 100% atom economy because all reactant atoms end up in the product
  • In atom economy questions, identify which product is the "desired" one carefully — the rest are waste
  • When asked to explain why yield is less, give specific reasons not just "some is lost"
