C25: Water and Waste

C26: Sustainable Chemistry →

Potable Water

Potable water is water that is safe to drink. It is NOT the same as pure water — potable water contains dissolved substances. It must have low enough levels of dissolved salts and microbes to be safe for human consumption.
Term Definition
Pure water Contains only H₂O molecules (no dissolved substances); boils at exactly 100 °C
Potable water Safe to drink; contains some dissolved substances but at safe levels; no harmful microbes
Distilled water Pure water produced by distillation — no dissolved substances at all
Worked Example 1: Potable vs pure water

Question: Explain why potable water is not the same as pure water.

Answer: Potable water is safe to drink but it is not pure because it contains dissolved substances (e.g. mineral ions) at safe levels. Pure water contains only H₂O molecules with nothing dissolved — it has a boiling point of exactly 100 °C. Potable water would boil at a slightly higher temperature due to dissolved substances.

Water Treatment

In the UK, fresh water from lakes, rivers and underground aquifers is treated to make it potable. The process involves: filtration, sedimentation, and chlorination.
  1. Filtration — water passes through filters (sand and gravel beds) to remove solid particles.
  2. Sedimentation — aluminium sulfate is added, which causes fine particles to clump together (flocculation) and settle to the bottom as sediment.
  3. Chlorination — chlorine is added to kill microbes (bacteria and other harmful microorganisms).
Worked Example 2: Describing water treatment

Question: Describe the three main stages of water treatment in the UK and explain the purpose of each stage.

Answer: (1) Filtration — water is passed through sand and gravel filters to remove solid particles and debris. (2) Sedimentation — aluminium sulfate is added to make fine particles clump together and settle out, removing them from the water. (3) Chlorination — chlorine gas is added to kill harmful microbes, making the water safe to drink.

Desalination

Desalination removes salt from sea water to produce potable water. Methods include reverse osmosis and distillation. Both are expensive because they require a lot of energy. Desalination is used in dry countries where fresh water is scarce.
Method How It Works Advantages Disadvantages
Distillation Boil sea water, condense the pure water vapour Produces very pure water Very energy-intensive and expensive
Reverse osmosis Sea water is forced through a semi-permeable membrane under high pressure; salt is left behind Does not require heating Requires high pressure (energy); membrane can become blocked
Worked Example 3: Comparing desalination methods

Question: Explain why desalination is more expensive than treating fresh water and why some countries still use it.

Answer: Desalination is expensive because both distillation (heating water to boiling) and reverse osmosis (generating high pressure) require large amounts of energy. Some countries still use desalination because they are in dry/arid regions where there is not enough fresh water from rivers, lakes and aquifers to supply the population, so they must obtain potable water from the sea.

Waste Water Treatment

Waste water (sewage) must be treated before being released into the environment. Treatment involves: screening, sedimentation, biological (aerobic) treatment, and anaerobic digestion.
  1. Screening — removes large solid objects (e.g. sticks, rags) using grids.
  2. Sedimentation — solids settle to the bottom forming sludge; the liquid above is effluent.
  3. Biological treatment (aerobic) — aerobic bacteria digest dissolved organic matter in the effluent, breaking it down. Air is bubbled through to supply oxygen.
  4. Anaerobic sludge digestion — the sludge is broken down by anaerobic bacteria in the absence of oxygen. This produces methane gas, which can be burned to generate electricity or for heating. The remaining digested sludge can be used as fertiliser.
Worked Example 4: Waste water treatment process

Question: Describe the stages of waste water treatment and explain how energy can be generated from the process.

Answer: (1) Screening removes large solids. (2) Sedimentation separates sludge from effluent. (3) Aerobic bacteria digest organic matter in the effluent. (4) Anaerobic bacteria digest the sludge, producing methane gas. The methane can be burned as fuel to generate electricity or for heating, making the treatment process more energy-efficient.

Fertilisers and Eutrophication

NPK fertilisers provide plants with the essential elements nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P) and potassium (K). They increase crop yields but can cause eutrophication if they enter waterways.
  • Nitrogen is needed for making proteins and growth.
  • Phosphorus is needed for roots and DNA.
  • Potassium is needed for flowers and fruit.

Eutrophication

Eutrophication occurs when fertilisers are washed into rivers and lakes. The nutrients cause algae to grow rapidly (algal bloom). When the algae die, bacteria decompose them, using up oxygen in the water. This causes fish and other organisms to die from lack of oxygen.
  1. Fertilisers run off into rivers and lakes.
  2. Excess nutrients cause rapid growth of algae (algal bloom) on the water surface.
  3. Algae block sunlight, so plants below the surface die.
  4. When the algae die, bacteria decompose them using aerobic respiration.
  5. Bacteria use up dissolved oxygen in the water.
  6. Fish and other aquatic organisms die due to lack of oxygen.

Q1. Explain the difference between potable water and pure water.

Show Answer

Potable water is safe to drink but is not pure — it contains dissolved substances at safe levels and no harmful microbes. Pure water contains only H₂O molecules with no dissolved substances; it boils at exactly 100 °C.

Q2. Describe the three stages of water treatment used to produce potable water from fresh water sources.

Show Answer

(1) Filtration — water passes through sand/gravel filters to remove solid particles. (2) Sedimentation — aluminium sulfate is added so fine particles clump and settle out. (3) Chlorination — chlorine is added to kill microbes, making the water safe to drink.

H

Q3. Compare distillation and reverse osmosis as methods of desalination. Explain why desalination is more expensive than treating fresh water.

Show Answer

Distillation involves boiling sea water and condensing the vapour; reverse osmosis forces water through a semi-permeable membrane at high pressure, leaving salt behind. Both are expensive because they require large amounts of energy — distillation needs heat to boil water, reverse osmosis needs high pressure. Treating fresh water is cheaper because it only involves filtration, sedimentation and chlorination, which use much less energy.

F H

Q4. Describe the stages of waste water treatment. How can energy be produced during the process?

Show Answer

(1) Screening removes large solids. (2) Sedimentation separates sludge from effluent. (3) Aerobic bacteria digest organic matter in the effluent. (4) Anaerobic bacteria digest the sludge, producing methane gas. The methane can be burned to generate electricity or heat, providing energy from the treatment process.

F H

Q5. Explain how the use of fertilisers can lead to eutrophication and the death of fish in a lake.

Show Answer

Fertilisers run off farmland into lakes. The excess nutrients (N and P) cause rapid growth of algae (algal bloom) on the surface. The algae block sunlight so underwater plants die. When the algae die, bacteria decompose them, using aerobic respiration which consumes dissolved oxygen from the water. The oxygen levels fall so low that fish and other organisms die.

H

Q6. Explain why NPK fertilisers contain nitrogen, phosphorus and potassium. Why might a farmer still choose to use fertilisers despite the risk of eutrophication?

Show Answer

N is needed for proteins and growth; P is needed for healthy roots and DNA; K is needed for flowers and fruit. Farmers use fertilisers because they significantly increase crop yields, providing more food and income. The economic benefit of higher yields may outweigh the risk of eutrophication, especially if the farmer manages fertiliser application carefully to minimise run-off.

Exam tip: Don't confuse potable water with pure water — potable water is safe to drink but NOT pure. For water treatment, learn the three stages in order: filtration → sedimentation → chlorination. For eutrophication, use the full sequence: fertiliser run-off → algal bloom → algae die → bacteria decompose → oxygen used up → fish die. Don't skip steps — examiners look for the complete chain.
