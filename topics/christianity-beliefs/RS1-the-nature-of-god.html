RS1: The Nature of God

All Boards

God as omnipotent, loving and just; the problem of evil and suffering; the oneness of God and the Trinity

The Nature of God

Christians believe in one God who is omnipotent (all-powerful), loving (benevolent) and just (fair). These attributes are central to Christian understanding of God and shape how Christians view the world and their relationship with God.

  • Omnipotent - God is all-powerful; nothing is impossible for God (Genesis 18:14: "Is anything too hard for the Lord?")
  • Loving - God is loving and cares for all people (John 3:16: "For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son")
  • Just - God is fair and will judge people righteously; God treats all people equally
Key Fact: The problem of evil and suffering challenges the idea of an omnipotent, loving God. If God is all-powerful and all-loving, why does suffering exist? This is a key philosophical debate in GCSE RS.

The Trinity

Christians believe in the Trinity — one God in three persons: Father, Son and Holy Spirit. This is not three gods (tritheism) but one God in three distinct persons.

PersonRoleDescription
God the FatherCreator and sustainerThe first person of the Trinity; creator of the universe; loving parent
God the SonSaviour and redeemerJesus Christ, the incarnation of God on Earth; fully human and fully divine
God the Holy SpiritGuide and comforterThe presence and power of God active in the world and in believers
Exam Tip: The word "Trinity" comes from "tri" meaning three and "unity" meaning one. The doctrine was formally established at the Council of Nicaea in 325 CE. The Trinity is not explicitly mentioned in the Bible but is inferred from passages like Matthew 28:19.

The Problem of Evil and Suffering

The existence of evil and suffering is one of the biggest challenges to belief in God. If God is omnipotent and loving, why does evil exist?

  • Moral evil - caused by human free will (e.g. murder, theft, war)
  • Natural evil - not caused by humans (e.g. earthquakes, disease, tsunamis)

Christian responses include:

  • The Free Will Defence: God gave humans free will; evil results from human choices, not God
  • Soul-making (Irenaean theodicy): Suffering helps humans grow spiritually and develop moral character
  • The Fall: Evil entered the world through Adam and Eve's disobedience (Genesis 3)
  • God shares in human suffering through Jesus on the cross

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Explain two Christian beliefs about the nature of God.

Q: How does the problem of evil challenge Christian beliefs about God?

✅ Answers

  1. 1) God is omnipotent — Christians believe God is all-powerful and nothing is beyond God's ability, as shown in Genesis where God creates the universe from nothing. 2) God is loving — Christians believe God demonstrates love through sending Jesus to save humanity (John 3:16), showing that God's love is selfless and sacrificial.
  2. The problem of evil challenges the idea that God is both omnipotent and loving. If God is all-powerful, God could prevent suffering; if God is all-loving, God would want to. The existence of suffering seems to contradict both attributes. Christians respond with the Free Will Defence (evil results from human choice, not God) and the soul-making theodicy (suffering develops moral character).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Christianity beliefs questions: Always use specific biblical references (e.g. Genesis 1, John 1:1-3, John 3:16). Explain different Christian interpretations (literal vs non-literal, Catholic vs Protestant). Use key terms precisely (omnipotent, Trinity, incarnation, atonement). For evaluation questions, consider both religious and secular viewpoints before reaching a justified conclusion.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse the Trinity as three gods rather than one God in three persons. Wrong: "Christians believe in three gods." Correct: "Christians believe in one God who exists as three persons — Father, Son and Holy Spirit."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that god as omnipotent, loving and just; the problem of evil and suffering; the oneness of god and the trinity.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
