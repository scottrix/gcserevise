RS1: The Nature of God

All Boards

God as omnipotent, loving and just; the problem of evil and suffering; the oneness of God and the Trinity

The Nature of God

Christians believe in one God who is omnipotent (all-powerful), loving (benevolent) and just (fair). These attributes are central to Christian understanding of God and shape how Christians view the world and their relationship with God.

  • Omnipotent - God is all-powerful; nothing is impossible for God (Genesis 18:14: "Is anything too hard for the Lord?")
  • Loving - God is loving and cares for all people (John 3:16: "For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son")
  • Just - God is fair and will judge people righteously; God treats all people equally
Key Fact: The problem of evil and suffering challenges the idea of an omnipotent, loving God. If God is all-powerful and all-loving, why does suffering exist? This is a key philosophical debate in GCSE RS.

The Trinity

Christians believe in the Trinity — one God in three persons: Father, Son and Holy Spirit. This is not three gods (tritheism) but one God in three distinct persons.

PersonRoleDescription
God the FatherCreator and sustainerThe first person of the Trinity; creator of the universe; loving parent
God the SonSaviour and redeemerJesus Christ, the incarnation of God on Earth; fully human and fully divine
God the Holy SpiritGuide and comforterThe presence and power of God active in the world and in believers
Exam Tip: The word "Trinity" comes from "tri" meaning three and "unity" meaning one. The doctrine was formally established at the Council of Nicaea in 325 CE. The Trinity is not explicitly mentioned in the Bible but is inferred from passages like Matthew 28:19.

The Problem of Evil and Suffering

The existence of evil and suffering is one of the biggest challenges to belief in God. If God is omnipotent and loving, why does evil exist?

  • Moral evil - caused by human free will (e.g. murder, theft, war)
  • Natural evil - not caused by humans (e.g. earthquakes, disease, tsunamis)

Christian responses include:

  • The Free Will Defence: God gave humans free will; evil results from human choices, not God
  • Soul-making (Irenaean theodicy): Suffering helps humans grow spiritually and develop moral character
  • The Fall: Evil entered the world through Adam and Eve's disobedience (Genesis 3)
  • God shares in human suffering through Jesus on the cross

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Explain two Christian beliefs about the nature of God.

Q: How does the problem of evil challenge Christian beliefs about God?

✅ Answers

  1. 1) God is omnipotent — Christians believe God is all-powerful and nothing is beyond God's ability, as shown in Genesis where God creates the universe from nothing. 2) God is loving — Christians believe God demonstrates love through sending Jesus to save humanity (John 3:16), showing that God's love is selfless and sacrificial.
  2. The problem of evil challenges the idea that God is both omnipotent and loving. If God is all-powerful, God could prevent suffering; if God is all-loving, God would want to. The existence of suffering seems to contradict both attributes. Christians respond with the Free Will Defence (evil results from human choice, not God) and the soul-making theodicy (suffering develops moral character).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

2. Explain both religious and non-religious viewpoints

5. Link your discussion of The Nature of God to its real-world impact on believers and society

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse the Trinity as three gods rather than one God in three persons. Wrong: "Christians believe in three gods." Correct: "Christians believe in one God who exists as three persons — Father, Son and Holy Spirit."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that god as omnipotent, loving and just; the problem of evil and suffering; the oneness of god and the trinity.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

Detailed Notes

Understanding The Nature of God in Christian Practice

The Nature of God is a key aspect of Christian practice that GCSE Religious Studies students must understand in depth. Christianity is the world’s largest religion with over 2 billion followers, and its practices shape the daily lives of believers. The AQA specification requires you to explain Christian practices, analyse their significance, and evaluate different Christian perspectives with reference to biblical and theological sources.

When writing about the nature of god, always refer to specific biblical teachings, denominational differences (Catholic, Anglican, Orthodox, Protestant), and the lived experience of Christians today. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of diversity within Christianity — not all Christians interpret or practice their faith in the same way.

Christian practices are rooted in the life and teachings of Jesus Christ as recorded in the Bible. The New Testament, particularly the Gospels and the Acts of the Apostles, provides the primary sources for understanding how Christians are called to live. When discussing the nature of god, connect the practice to its biblical foundation.

GCSE Example: Understanding The Nature of God in Christian Practice

A strong GCSE RS answer about the nature of god would: explain the practice with reference to biblical teaching, describe how it is observed by different Christian denominations, analyse its significance for Christians, and evaluate different perspectives with justified reasoning.

Biblical and Theological Foundations of The Nature of God

Christian practices are grounded in the Bible and developed through church tradition. When discussing the nature of god, identify the relevant biblical passages: the Old Testament provides the Jewish context that Christianity grew from; the Gospels record Jesus’ life and teachings; the Epistles (letters) of Paul and others explain theological significance; and the Acts of the Apostles describes the early church’s practices.

Key Christian beliefs that inform the nature of god include: the nature of God (loving, just, omnipotent), the incarnation (God becoming human in Jesus), the atonement (Jesus’ death reconciling humanity with God), the resurrection (Jesus rising from the dead), and the Holy Spirit (God’s active presence in the world and in believers). These doctrines shape how Christians understand and practice their faith.

Different denominations interpret biblical teaching differently. Catholic Christians give authority to both Scripture and Tradition (the teaching authority of the Church). Protestant Christians emphasise sola scriptura (Scripture alone). Orthodox Christians emphasise the continuity of apostolic tradition. These differences affect how the nature of god is understood and practiced.

GCSE Example: Biblical and Theological Foundations of The Nature of God

When explaining the nature of god, reference specific Bible passages: for worship, cite John 4:24 (‘God is spirit, and his worshippers must worship in the Spirit and in truth’); for prayer, cite Matthew 6:9-13 (the Lord’s Prayer). Always explain what the passage means in context and how it influences Christian practice today.

The Impact of The Nature of God on Christians Today

Understanding the nature of god in the modern world means considering how Christians practice their faith in contemporary Britain and globally. Christianity in the UK is diverse: the Church of England (Anglican) is the established church, but there are also Catholic, Methodist, Baptist, Pentecostal and Orthodox communities, each with distinct approaches to the nature of god.

Christian practices like the nature of god have both personal and communal dimensions. Personally, they shape individual spiritual life, ethical decision-making and relationship with God. Communally, they build church community, strengthen social bonds, and provide a witness to wider society. Understanding both dimensions is essential for GCSE.

When evaluating the significance of the nature of god for Christians today, consider: why do some Christians find this practice more meaningful than others? How does the practice express Christian beliefs about God, Jesus and the Holy Spirit? What challenges do Christians face in maintaining this practice in contemporary secular society?

GCSE Example: The Impact of The Nature of God on Christians Today

A balanced evaluation of the nature of god might argue: for many Christians, this practice is central to their faith because [reason with biblical reference]. However, other Christians may prioritise different aspects of their faith because [reason]. Ultimately, the significance of the nature of god depends on [factor] because [justification].

Catholics approach the nature of god by [specific Catholic practice], while Protestants [specific Protestant approach]. This denominational difference exists because [theological reason]. Despite differences, all Christians value the nature of god because [shared reason].

A: The Nature of God remains significant for many Christians in Britain today because [reason with evidence]. However, in a increasingly secular society, some Christians find it challenging to maintain this practice because [specific challenge]. Different denominations respond differently: [specific examples]. Overall, while the nature of god remains important for committed Christians, its expression may adapt to contemporary British life because [justified conclusion].
