RS2: Creation

All Boards

Different Christian beliefs about creation including Genesis 1:1-3 and John 1:1-3; the role of Word and Spirit

Creation in Genesis

Christians look to the Bible for teachings about how the world was created. There are two creation accounts in Genesis.

  • Genesis 1 - God creates the universe in six days and rests on the seventh. Each day God creates something new: light, sky, land, plants, stars, animals, and finally humans. God declares creation "very good" (Genesis 1:31).
  • Genesis 2 - A more detailed account focusing on the creation of humans. God forms Adam from dust and breathes life into him. God creates Eve from Adam's rib. Humans are given responsibility to care for the Garden of Eden.
Key Fact: Some Christians read Genesis literally (creationist view) while others read it as symbolic or metaphorical, compatible with scientific theories like the Big Bang and evolution.

Creation and John 1:1-3

The Gospel of John opens with a different perspective on creation:

"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. He was with God in the beginning. Through him all things were made." (John 1:1-3)

  • The "Word" (Logos in Greek) refers to Jesus Christ as the agent of creation
  • John presents Jesus as pre-existing with God from the beginning
  • This connects creation to the Trinity — God the Son was active in creation
  • The Holy Spirit is also present at creation (Genesis 1:2: "the Spirit of God was hovering over the waters")

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Explain two different Christian beliefs about creation.

Q: How does John 1:1-3 contribute to Christian understanding of creation?

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Literalist Christians believe Genesis describes actual events — God literally created the world in six 24-hour days, making all life forms directly. 2) Non-literalist Christians read Genesis as conveying theological truth (God is creator) but not scientific fact; they accept the Big Bang and evolution as the means God used to create, viewing the "days" as symbolic periods of time.
  2. John 1:1-3 presents Jesus (the Word/Logos) as the agent of creation, existing with God from the beginning. This expands the Genesis account by connecting creation to the Trinity — God the Son was active in creation, not just God the Father. It shows that creation was a Trinitarian act and that Jesus was not just a human teacher but divine from the beginning.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Christianity beliefs questions: Always use specific biblical references (e.g. Genesis 1, John 1:1-3, John 3:16). Explain different Christian interpretations (literal vs non-literal, Catholic vs Protestant). Use key terms precisely (omnipotent, Trinity, incarnation, atonement). For evaluation questions, consider both religious and secular viewpoints before reaching a justified conclusion.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse the Trinity as three gods rather than one God in three persons. Wrong: "Christians believe in three gods." Correct: "Christians believe in one God who exists as three persons — Father, Son and Holy Spirit."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that different christian beliefs about creation including genesis 1:1-3 and john 1:1-3; the role of word and spirit.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
