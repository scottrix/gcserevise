RS2: Creation

All Boards

Different Christian beliefs about creation including Genesis 1:1-3 and John 1:1-3; the role of Word and Spirit

Creation in Genesis

Christians look to the Bible for teachings about how the world was created. There are two creation accounts in Genesis.

  • Genesis 1 - God creates the universe in six days and rests on the seventh. Each day God creates something new: light, sky, land, plants, stars, animals, and finally humans. God declares creation "very good" (Genesis 1:31).
  • Genesis 2 - A more detailed account focusing on the creation of humans. God forms Adam from dust and breathes life into him. God creates Eve from Adam's rib. Humans are given responsibility to care for the Garden of Eden.
Key Fact: Some Christians read Genesis literally (creationist view) while others read it as symbolic or metaphorical, compatible with scientific theories like the Big Bang and evolution.

Creation and John 1:1-3

The Gospel of John opens with a different perspective on creation:

"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. He was with God in the beginning. Through him all things were made." (John 1:1-3)

  • The "Word" (Logos in Greek) refers to Jesus Christ as the agent of creation
  • John presents Jesus as pre-existing with God from the beginning
  • This connects creation to the Trinity — God the Son was active in creation
  • The Holy Spirit is also present at creation (Genesis 1:2: "the Spirit of God was hovering over the waters")

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Explain two different Christian beliefs about creation.

Q: How does John 1:1-3 contribute to Christian understanding of creation?

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Literalist Christians believe Genesis describes actual events — God literally created the world in six 24-hour days, making all life forms directly. 2) Non-literalist Christians read Genesis as conveying theological truth (God is creator) but not scientific fact; they accept the Big Bang and evolution as the means God used to create, viewing the "days" as symbolic periods of time.
  2. John 1:1-3 presents Jesus (the Word/Logos) as the agent of creation, existing with God from the beginning. This expands the Genesis account by connecting creation to the Trinity — God the Son was active in creation, not just God the Father. It shows that creation was a Trinitarian act and that Jesus was not just a human teacher but divine from the beginning.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

2. Explain both religious and non-religious viewpoints

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse the Trinity as three gods rather than one God in three persons. Wrong: "Christians believe in three gods." Correct: "Christians believe in one God who exists as three persons — Father, Son and Holy Spirit."

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that different christian beliefs about creation including genesis 1:1-3 and john 1:1-3; the role of word and spirit.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

Detailed Notes

Understanding Creation in Christian Practice

Creation is a key aspect of Christian practice that GCSE Religious Studies students must understand in depth. Christianity is the world’s largest religion with over 2 billion followers, and its practices shape the daily lives of believers. The AQA specification requires you to explain Christian practices, analyse their significance, and evaluate different Christian perspectives with reference to biblical and theological sources.

When writing about creation, always refer to specific biblical teachings, denominational differences (Catholic, Anglican, Orthodox, Protestant), and the lived experience of Christians today. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of diversity within Christianity — not all Christians interpret or practice their faith in the same way.

Christian practices are rooted in the life and teachings of Jesus Christ as recorded in the Bible. The New Testament, particularly the Gospels and the Acts of the Apostles, provides the primary sources for understanding how Christians are called to live. When discussing creation, connect the practice to its biblical foundation.

GCSE Example: Understanding Creation in Christian Practice

A strong GCSE RS answer about creation would: explain the practice with reference to biblical teaching, describe how it is observed by different Christian denominations, analyse its significance for Christians, and evaluate different perspectives with justified reasoning.

Biblical and Theological Foundations of Creation

Christian practices are grounded in the Bible and developed through church tradition. When discussing creation, identify the relevant biblical passages: the Old Testament provides the Jewish context that Christianity grew from; the Gospels record Jesus’ life and teachings; the Epistles (letters) of Paul and others explain theological significance; and the Acts of the Apostles describes the early church’s practices.

Key Christian beliefs that inform creation include: the nature of God (loving, just, omnipotent), the incarnation (God becoming human in Jesus), the atonement (Jesus’ death reconciling humanity with God), the resurrection (Jesus rising from the dead), and the Holy Spirit (God’s active presence in the world and in believers). These doctrines shape how Christians understand and practice their faith.

Different denominations interpret biblical teaching differently. Catholic Christians give authority to both Scripture and Tradition (the teaching authority of the Church). Protestant Christians emphasise sola scriptura (Scripture alone). Orthodox Christians emphasise the continuity of apostolic tradition. These differences affect how creation is understood and practiced.

GCSE Example: Biblical and Theological Foundations of Creation

When explaining creation, reference specific Bible passages: for worship, cite John 4:24 (‘God is spirit, and his worshippers must worship in the Spirit and in truth’); for prayer, cite Matthew 6:9-13 (the Lord’s Prayer). Always explain what the passage means in context and how it influences Christian practice today.

The Impact of Creation on Christians Today

Understanding creation in the modern world means considering how Christians practice their faith in contemporary Britain and globally. Christianity in the UK is diverse: the Church of England (Anglican) is the established church, but there are also Catholic, Methodist, Baptist, Pentecostal and Orthodox communities, each with distinct approaches to creation.

Christian practices like creation have both personal and communal dimensions. Personally, they shape individual spiritual life, ethical decision-making and relationship with God. Communally, they build church community, strengthen social bonds, and provide a witness to wider society. Understanding both dimensions is essential for GCSE.

When evaluating the significance of creation for Christians today, consider: why do some Christians find this practice more meaningful than others? How does the practice express Christian beliefs about God, Jesus and the Holy Spirit? What challenges do Christians face in maintaining this practice in contemporary secular society?

GCSE Example: The Impact of Creation on Christians Today

A balanced evaluation of creation might argue: for many Christians, this practice is central to their faith because [reason with biblical reference]. However, other Christians may prioritise different aspects of their faith because [reason]. Ultimately, the significance of creation depends on [factor] because [justification].

Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain the importance of creation for Christians, with reference to biblical teachings and denominational differences.

A: Creation is important for Christians because [specific biblical teaching]. For example, [specific Bible passage] teaches that [specific principle]. Catholics approach creation by [specific Catholic practice], while Protestants [specific Protestant approach]. This denominational difference exists because [theological reason]. Despite differences, all Christians value creation because [shared reason].

Q: Evaluate the significance of creation for Christians living in Britain today.

A: Creation remains significant for many Christians in Britain today because [reason with evidence]. However, in a increasingly secular society, some Christians find it challenging to maintain this practice because [specific challenge]. Different denominations respond differently: [specific examples]. Overall, while creation remains important for committed Christians, its expression may adapt to contemporary British life because [justified conclusion].
