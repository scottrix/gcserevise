RS4: The Crucifixion and Resurrection

All Boards

Beliefs about the crucifixion, resurrection and ascension of Jesus and their significance

The Crucifixion

The crucifixion is the event where Jesus was executed by being nailed to a cross. It is central to Christian belief because it is through Jesus' death that Christians believe salvation was made possible.

  • Jesus was betrayed by Judas, tried before the Sanhedrin and Pontius Pilate, and sentenced to death
  • He was crucified at Golgotha alongside two criminals
  • Jesus' last words: "It is finished" (John 19:30) — meaning his mission of salvation was complete
  • The curtain of the temple was torn in two, symbolising that the barrier between God and humanity was removed
Key Fact: The crucifixion demonstrates the depth of God's love — God was willing to suffer the most painful and humiliating death to save humanity. Christians remember this through the symbol of the cross.

The Resurrection

On the third day after his death, Christians believe Jesus rose from the dead. The resurrection is the most important event in Christianity.

  • The tomb was found empty by women followers (Mark 16:1-8)
  • Jesus appeared to his disciples multiple times over 40 days
  • Thomas touched Jesus' wounds and believed (John 20:24-29)
  • The resurrection proves Jesus' divinity and power over death

Without the resurrection, Christianity has no foundation. As St Paul wrote: "If Christ has not been raised, our preaching is useless and so is your faith" (1 Corinthians 15:14).

The Ascension

After 40 days of appearing to his disciples, Jesus ascended to heaven (Acts 1:6-11).

  • Jesus was taken up into heaven before the disciples' eyes
  • Two angels promised that Jesus would return in the same way
  • The ascension marks the end of Jesus' earthly ministry
  • It prepared the way for the Holy Spirit to come at Pentecost

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Explain why the resurrection is important for Christians.

Q: Explain two ways the crucifixion is significant for Christians.

✅ Answers

  1. The resurrection is the cornerstone of Christian faith because: it proves Jesus' divinity — rising from the dead demonstrates power over death that only God could have; it confirms that Jesus' sacrifice was accepted by God, making salvation possible; it gives Christians hope of eternal life — if Jesus conquered death, believers will too; and it transforms the cross from a symbol of defeat into one of victory. Without the resurrection, as Paul says, Christian faith is meaningless.
  2. 1) The crucifixion brings about atonement — Jesus' death pays the price for human sin, reconciling humanity with God. 2) It demonstrates God's love — that God was willing to suffer the most painful death shows the depth of divine love for humanity, as John 3:16 affirms.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse the Trinity as three gods rather than one God in three persons. Wrong: "Christians believe in three gods." Correct: "Christians believe in one God who exists as three persons — Father, Son and Holy Spirit."

Detailed Notes

Understanding The Crucifixion and Resurrection in Christian Practice

The Crucifixion and Resurrection is a key aspect of Christian practice that GCSE Religious Studies students must understand in depth. Christianity is the world’s largest religion with over 2 billion followers, and its practices shape the daily lives of believers. The AQA specification requires you to explain Christian practices, analyse their significance, and evaluate different Christian perspectives with reference to biblical and theological sources.

When writing about the crucifixion and resurrection, always refer to specific biblical teachings, denominational differences (Catholic, Anglican, Orthodox, Protestant), and the lived experience of Christians today. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of diversity within Christianity — not all Christians interpret or practice their faith in the same way.

Christian practices are rooted in the life and teachings of Jesus Christ as recorded in the Bible. The New Testament, particularly the Gospels and the Acts of the Apostles, provides the primary sources for understanding how Christians are called to live. When discussing the crucifixion and resurrection, connect the practice to its biblical foundation.

GCSE Example: Understanding The Crucifixion and Resurrection in Christian Practice

A strong GCSE RS answer about the crucifixion and resurrection would: explain the practice with reference to biblical teaching, describe how it is observed by different Christian denominations, analyse its significance for Christians, and evaluate different perspectives with justified reasoning.

Biblical and Theological Foundations of The Crucifixion and Resurrection

Christian practices are grounded in the Bible and developed through church tradition. When discussing the crucifixion and resurrection, identify the relevant biblical passages: the Old Testament provides the Jewish context that Christianity grew from; the Gospels record Jesus’ life and teachings; the Epistles (letters) of Paul and others explain theological significance; and the Acts of the Apostles describes the early church’s practices.

Key Christian beliefs that inform the crucifixion and resurrection include: the nature of God (loving, just, omnipotent), the incarnation (God becoming human in Jesus), the atonement (Jesus’ death reconciling humanity with God), the resurrection (Jesus rising from the dead), and the Holy Spirit (God’s active presence in the world and in believers). These doctrines shape how Christians understand and practice their faith.

Different denominations interpret biblical teaching differently. Catholic Christians give authority to both Scripture and Tradition (the teaching authority of the Church). Protestant Christians emphasise sola scriptura (Scripture alone). Orthodox Christians emphasise the continuity of apostolic tradition. These differences affect how the crucifixion and resurrection is understood and practiced.

GCSE Example: Biblical and Theological Foundations of The Crucifixion and Resurrection

When explaining the crucifixion and resurrection, reference specific Bible passages: for worship, cite John 4:24 (‘God is spirit, and his worshippers must worship in the Spirit and in truth’); for prayer, cite Matthew 6:9-13 (the Lord’s Prayer). Always explain what the passage means in context and how it influences Christian practice today.

The Impact of The Crucifixion and Resurrection on Christians Today

Understanding the crucifixion and resurrection in the modern world means considering how Christians practice their faith in contemporary Britain and globally. Christianity in the UK is diverse: the Church of England (Anglican) is the established church, but there are also Catholic, Methodist, Baptist, Pentecostal and Orthodox communities, each with distinct approaches to the crucifixion and resurrection.

Christian practices like the crucifixion and resurrection have both personal and communal dimensions. Personally, they shape individual spiritual life, ethical decision-making and relationship with God. Communally, they build church community, strengthen social bonds, and provide a witness to wider society. Understanding both dimensions is essential for GCSE.

When evaluating the significance of the crucifixion and resurrection for Christians today, consider: why do some Christians find this practice more meaningful than others? How does the practice express Christian beliefs about God, Jesus and the Holy Spirit? What challenges do Christians face in maintaining this practice in contemporary secular society?

GCSE Example: The Impact of The Crucifixion and Resurrection on Christians Today

A balanced evaluation of the crucifixion and resurrection might argue: for many Christians, this practice is central to their faith because [reason with biblical reference]. However, other Christians may prioritise different aspects of their faith because [reason]. Ultimately, the significance of the crucifixion and resurrection depends on [factor] because [justification].

Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain the importance of the crucifixion and resurrection for Christians, with reference to biblical teachings and denominational differences.

A: The Crucifixion and Resurrection is important for Christians because [specific biblical teaching]. For example, [specific Bible passage] teaches that [specific principle]. Catholics approach the crucifixion and resurrection by [specific Catholic practice], while Protestants [specific Protestant approach]. This denominational difference exists because [theological reason]. Despite differences, all Christians value the crucifixion and resurrection because [shared reason].

Q: Evaluate the significance of the crucifixion and resurrection for Christians living in Britain today.

A: The Crucifixion and Resurrection remains significant for many Christians in Britain today because [reason with evidence]. However, in a increasingly secular society, some Christians find it challenging to maintain this practice because [specific challenge]. Different denominations respond differently: [specific examples]. Overall, while the crucifixion and resurrection remains important for committed Christians, its expression may adapt to contemporary British life because [justified conclusion].
