RS5: Sin and Salvation

All Boards

Original sin; the means of salvation including law, grace and Spirit; atonement

Sin and Original Sin

Key Fact: Sin is any action that goes against God's will. Christians believe that sin separates humans from God and that all people have a tendency to sin (original sin), which stems from Adam and Eve's disobedience in Genesis 3.
  • Original sin — the first sin committed by Adam and Eve; its consequences affect all humanity
  • Some Christians believe original sin is inherited (Augustine's view); others see it as the tendency to sin that all humans share
  • Original sin explains why humans are imperfect and need salvation
  • The doctrine of original sin is particularly important in Catholic theology

Salvation

Salvation means being saved from sin and its consequences. Christians believe salvation is made possible through Jesus Christ.

  • Through law — following God's commandments and living a moral life
  • Through grace — God's unmerited favour; salvation is a gift from God that cannot be earned (Ephesians 2:8-9: "For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith — and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God")
  • Through the Spirit — the Holy Spirit empowers Christians to live transformed lives
Exam Tip: Different Christian traditions emphasise different aspects of salvation. Protestants tend to emphasise grace and faith alone (sola fide), while Catholics emphasise the importance of both faith and good works cooperating with grace.

Atonement

Atonement means making amends for sin and restoring the relationship between God and humanity. Christians believe Jesus achieved this through his death on the cross.

  • Substitutionary atonement: Jesus died in the place of sinners, taking the punishment that humanity deserved
  • Sacrifice: Jesus is described as the "Lamb of God" (John 1:29) — the perfect sacrifice that ends the need for all other sacrifices
  • Moral influence: Jesus' self-sacrificial love inspires humans to turn to God and live differently
  • Victory: Through the cross and resurrection, Jesus defeated sin and death

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Explain two Christian beliefs about salvation.

Q: "People can be good without God." Evaluate this statement with reference to Christian teaching.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Salvation through grace — Christians believe that humans cannot earn salvation through their own efforts; it is a free gift from God, received through faith (Ephesians 2:8-9). 2) Salvation through Christ's atonement — Jesus' death on the cross paid the penalty for sin, making it possible for humans to be reconciled with God. Without Jesus' sacrifice, the gap between a holy God and sinful humanity could not be bridged.
  2. Many would agree that non-religious people can live morally good lives, showing kindness, justice and compassion. However, from a Christian perspective, true goodness comes from God, and without God there is no ultimate standard of morality. Christians believe that even "good" actions fall short of God's standard due to sin, and salvation requires God's grace, not just human effort. The question depends on whether "goodness" is defined by human standards or God's standards.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse the Trinity as three gods rather than one God in three persons. Wrong: "Christians believe in three gods." Correct: "Christians believe in one God who exists as three persons — Father, Son and Holy Spirit."

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. Detailed Notes

Understanding Sin and Salvation in Christian Practice

Sin and Salvation is a key aspect of Christian practice that GCSE Religious Studies students must understand in depth. Christianity is the world’s largest religion with over 2 billion followers, and its practices shape the daily lives of believers. The AQA specification requires you to explain Christian practices, analyse their significance, and evaluate different Christian perspectives with reference to biblical and theological sources.

When writing about sin and salvation, always refer to specific biblical teachings, denominational differences (Catholic, Anglican, Orthodox, Protestant), and the lived experience of Christians today. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of diversity within Christianity — not all Christians interpret or practice their faith in the same way.

Christian practices are rooted in the life and teachings of Jesus Christ as recorded in the Bible. The New Testament, particularly the Gospels and the Acts of the Apostles, provides the primary sources for understanding how Christians are called to live. When discussing sin and salvation, connect the practice to its biblical foundation.

GCSE Example: Understanding Sin and Salvation in Christian Practice

A strong GCSE RS answer about sin and salvation would: explain the practice with reference to biblical teaching, describe how it is observed by different Christian denominations, analyse its significance for Christians, and evaluate different perspectives with justified reasoning.

Biblical and Theological Foundations of Sin and Salvation

Christian practices are grounded in the Bible and developed through church tradition. When discussing sin and salvation, identify the relevant biblical passages: the Old Testament provides the Jewish context that Christianity grew from; the Gospels record Jesus’ life and teachings; the Epistles (letters) of Paul and others explain theological significance; and the Acts of the Apostles describes the early church’s practices.

Key Christian beliefs that inform sin and salvation include: the nature of God (loving, just, omnipotent), the incarnation (God becoming human in Jesus), the atonement (Jesus’ death reconciling humanity with God), the resurrection (Jesus rising from the dead), and the Holy Spirit (God’s active presence in the world and in believers). These doctrines shape how Christians understand and practice their faith.

Different denominations interpret biblical teaching differently. Catholic Christians give authority to both Scripture and Tradition (the teaching authority of the Church). Protestant Christians emphasise sola scriptura (Scripture alone). Orthodox Christians emphasise the continuity of apostolic tradition. These differences affect how sin and salvation is understood and practiced.

GCSE Example: Biblical and Theological Foundations of Sin and Salvation

When explaining sin and salvation, reference specific Bible passages: for worship, cite John 4:24 (‘God is spirit, and his worshippers must worship in the Spirit and in truth’); for prayer, cite Matthew 6:9-13 (the Lord’s Prayer). Always explain what the passage means in context and how it influences Christian practice today.

The Impact of Sin and Salvation on Christians Today

Understanding sin and salvation in the modern world means considering how Christians practice their faith in contemporary Britain and globally. Christianity in the UK is diverse: the Church of England (Anglican) is the established church, but there are also Catholic, Methodist, Baptist, Pentecostal and Orthodox communities, each with distinct approaches to sin and salvation.

Christian practices like sin and salvation have both personal and communal dimensions. Personally, they shape individual spiritual life, ethical decision-making and relationship with God. Communally, they build church community, strengthen social bonds, and provide a witness to wider society. Understanding both dimensions is essential for GCSE.

When evaluating the significance of sin and salvation for Christians today, consider: why do some Christians find this practice more meaningful than others? How does the practice express Christian beliefs about God, Jesus and the Holy Spirit? What challenges do Christians face in maintaining this practice in contemporary secular society?

GCSE Example: The Impact of Sin and Salvation on Christians Today

A balanced evaluation of sin and salvation might argue: for many Christians, this practice is central to their faith because [reason with biblical reference]. However, other Christians may prioritise different aspects of their faith because [reason]. Ultimately, the significance of sin and salvation depends on [factor] because [justification].

Q: Explain the importance of sin and salvation for Christians, with reference to biblical teachings and denominational differences.

A: Sin and Salvation is important for Christians because [specific biblical teaching]. For example, [specific Bible passage] teaches that [specific principle]. Catholics approach sin and salvation by [specific Catholic practice], while Protestants [specific Protestant approach]. This denominational difference exists because [theological reason]. Despite differences, all Christians value sin and salvation because [shared reason].

Q: Evaluate the significance of sin and salvation for Christians living in Britain today.

A: Sin and Salvation remains significant for many Christians in Britain today because [reason with evidence]. However, in a increasingly secular society, some Christians find it challenging to maintain this practice because [specific challenge]. Different denominations respond differently: [specific examples]. Overall, while sin and salvation remains important for committed Christians, its expression may adapt to contemporary British life because [justified conclusion].
