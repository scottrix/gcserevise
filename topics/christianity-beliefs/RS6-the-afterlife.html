RS6: The Afterlife

All Boards

Different Christian beliefs about the afterlife: resurrection, judgement, heaven and hell

Christian Beliefs About the Afterlife

Christians believe that physical death is not the end — there is life after death. This belief is based on Jesus' resurrection and biblical teaching.

  • Resurrection — Christians believe the dead will be raised to new life, just as Jesus was raised from the dead
  • Judgement — God will judge all people based on their actions and faith (Matthew 25:31-46, the parable of the sheep and goats)
  • Heaven — eternal life in the presence of God; a place of joy, peace and fulfilment
  • Hell — separation from God; different Christians understand this differently (eternal punishment vs. annihilation vs. metaphor for separation)
Key Fact: Catholic Christians also believe in purgatory — a state of purification after death for those who will eventually enter heaven but need to be cleansed of venial sins.

The Impact of Afterlife Beliefs

  • Belief in judgement motivates Christians to live morally and follow Jesus' teachings
  • Belief in heaven provides comfort when facing death or losing loved ones
  • Belief in the sanctity of life influences Christian views on issues like abortion and euthanasia
  • Belief that life has eternal purpose gives meaning to daily decisions and actions

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Explain two Christian beliefs about the afterlife.

Q: "Belief in the afterlife gives life meaning." Evaluate this statement.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Resurrection — Christians believe that, like Jesus, the dead will be raised to new life. This is a physical resurrection, not just spiritual survival. 2) Judgement — Christians believe God will judge all people, as described in the parable of the sheep and goats (Matthew 25). Those who have lived according to God's will enter eternal life; those who have rejected God face separation.
  2. Many Christians would agree — without belief in eternal life, earthly existence could seem pointless. The afterlife gives meaning to suffering, motivates moral behaviour, and provides comfort. However, some argue that meaning can be found in this life alone through relationships, achievement and contribution to society. Atheists and humanists find meaning without afterlife beliefs. The question is whether eternal significance is necessary for meaning, or whether finite existence can be meaningful in itself.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Christianity beliefs questions: Always use specific biblical references (e.g. Genesis 1, John 1:1-3, John 3:16). Explain different Christian interpretations (literal vs non-literal, Catholic vs Protestant). Use key terms precisely (omnipotent, Trinity, incarnation, atonement). For evaluation questions, consider both religious and secular viewpoints before reaching a justified conclusion.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse the Trinity as three gods rather than one God in three persons. Wrong: "Christians believe in three gods." Correct: "Christians believe in one God who exists as three persons — Father, Son and Holy Spirit."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that different christian beliefs about the afterlife: resurrection, judgement, heaven and hell.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
