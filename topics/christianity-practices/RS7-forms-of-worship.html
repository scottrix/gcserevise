RS7: Forms of Worship

All Boards

Liturgical, non-liturgical and informal worship; private worship; the role of the Bible

✝️ Forms of Worship

Worship is the way Christians show their love and devotion to God. There are different forms of worship in Christianity:

  • Liturgical worship — follows a set structure and ritual (e.g. Catholic Mass, Anglican Holy Communion). Uses written prayers, set readings and formal ceremony.
  • Non-liturgical worship — more spontaneous and flexible. Often centred on a sermon with extemporary prayer and modern worship songs. Common in Baptist and Pentecostal churches.
  • Informal worship — may include modern music, clapping, dancing and speaking in tongues (glossolalia). Often found in charismatic and evangelical churches.
  • Private worship — individual prayer, Bible study and meditation at home. Christians believe they can worship God anywhere, not just in church.
Key Fact: Jesus said "Where two or three gather in my name, there am I with them" (Matthew 18:20). Christians believe worship can be both corporate (together) and private (individual).

📖 The Role of the Bible in Worship

The Bible is central to all forms of Christian worship:

  • Readings — passages from the Old and New Testament are read aloud during services
  • Sermons — the priest or pastor explains and applies a Bible passage to daily life
  • Songs and hymns — many are based directly on Bible passages (e.g. Psalm 23)
  • Personal study — Christians read the Bible privately for guidance and inspiration
Exam Tip: When comparing worship styles, remember that both have the same purpose (honouring God) but different methods. Catholic and Anglican Christians tend to prefer liturgical worship; Baptist, Methodist and Pentecostal Christians prefer non-liturgical or informal worship.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two different forms of Christian worship.

Q2: "Liturgical worship is better than non-liturgical worship." Evaluate this statement.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Liturgical worship follows a set structure with formal prayers, readings and sacraments. The Catholic Mass is an example — it has a fixed order including the Kyrie, Gloria, readings, Eucharistic prayer and Holy Communion. 2) Non-liturgical worship is more spontaneous and informal. Baptist and Pentecostal services often include modern worship songs, extemporary prayer and a sermon, without a fixed order of service.
  2. Some Christians prefer liturgical worship because it connects them to centuries of tradition, provides structure and reverence, and follows Jesus' instruction to "do this in remembrance of me" at the Last Supper. Others prefer non-liturgical worship because it feels more personal and authentic, allows the Holy Spirit to guide the service, and uses contemporary language that speaks to modern people. Both forms honour God — the choice depends on denomination and personal preference.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Christianity practices questions: Name specific sacraments and explain their significance. Compare different Christian traditions (Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant). Use real examples (e.g. Lourdes, Iona, CAFOD, street pastors). For worship questions, distinguish between liturgical, non-liturgical and informal worship with specific examples.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think all Christians celebrate the Eucharist in the same way. Wrong: "All Christians believe the bread and wine literally become Jesus' body and blood." Correct: "Catholics believe in transubstantiation; Protestants have different views including consubstantiation and memorial symbolism."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that liturgical, non-liturgical and informal worship; private worship; the role of the bible.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
