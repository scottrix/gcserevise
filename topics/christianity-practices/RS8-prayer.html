RS8: Prayer

All Boards

The significance of prayer; the Lord's Prayer; set prayers and informal prayer

✝️ Prayer in Christianity

Prayer is communication with God — Christians use it to praise, thank, confess and ask for help. Prayer is essential to Christian life and practice.

  • Praise — telling God how wonderful he is
  • Thanksgiving — thanking God for his blessings
  • Confession — saying sorry for sin and asking forgiveness
  • Intercession — praying for the needs of others
  • Petition — asking God for something for yourself
Key Fact: The Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:9-13) is the most important Christian prayer. Jesus taught it to his disciples when they asked him how to pray. It includes praise ("Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name"), petition ("Give us today our daily bread") and confession ("Forgive us our debts").

🙏 The Significance of Prayer

  • Prayer strengthens the relationship between Christians and God
  • It provides comfort and guidance in difficult times
  • Jesus himself prayed regularly — he withdrew to pray before important decisions (Luke 6:12-13)
  • Paul instructed Christians to "pray without ceasing" (1 Thessalonians 5:17)
  • Some Christians believe God answers prayer through miracles, inner peace, or changed circumstances
  • Others believe prayer changes the person praying, giving them strength and perspective

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two types of Christian prayer with examples.

Q2: "Prayer does not work." Evaluate this statement.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Set prayers are written prayers used repeatedly in worship. The Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:9-13) is the most important set prayer — Jesus taught it to his disciples and it is used in almost every Christian denomination. 2) Extemporary prayer is spontaneous and informal — Christians speak to God in their own words about current needs and concerns. This is common in Baptist and Pentecostal churches where prayer is seen as a personal conversation with God.
  2. Some people argue prayer doesn't work because bad things still happen despite prayers, and there is no scientific evidence that prayer changes outcomes. However, Christians would respond that prayer is not like a vending machine — God answers in his own way, which may be "yes", "no" or "wait". Prayer also changes the person praying, giving them peace, strength and perspective. Millions of Christians testify that prayer has transformed their lives. The value of prayer may be in the relationship with God rather than guaranteed outcomes.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think all Christians celebrate the Eucharist in the same way. Wrong: "All Christians believe the bread and wine literally become Jesus' body and blood." Correct: "Catholics believe in transubstantiation; Protestants have different views including consubstantiation and memorial symbolism."

Detailed Notes

Understanding Prayer in Christian Practice

Prayer is a key aspect of Christian practice that GCSE Religious Studies students must understand in depth. Christianity is the world’s largest religion with over 2 billion followers, and its practices shape the daily lives of believers. The AQA specification requires you to explain Christian practices, analyse their significance, and evaluate different Christian perspectives with reference to biblical and theological sources.

When writing about prayer, always refer to specific biblical teachings, denominational differences (Catholic, Anglican, Orthodox, Protestant), and the lived experience of Christians today. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of diversity within Christianity — not all Christians interpret or practice their faith in the same way.

Christian practices are rooted in the life and teachings of Jesus Christ as recorded in the Bible. The New Testament, particularly the Gospels and the Acts of the Apostles, provides the primary sources for understanding how Christians are called to live. When discussing prayer, connect the practice to its biblical foundation.

GCSE Example: Understanding Prayer in Christian Practice

A strong GCSE RS answer about prayer would: explain the practice with reference to biblical teaching, describe how it is observed by different Christian denominations, analyse its significance for Christians, and evaluate different perspectives with justified reasoning.

Biblical and Theological Foundations of Prayer

Christian practices are grounded in the Bible and developed through church tradition. When discussing prayer, identify the relevant biblical passages: the Old Testament provides the Jewish context that Christianity grew from; the Gospels record Jesus’ life and teachings; the Epistles (letters) of Paul and others explain theological significance; and the Acts of the Apostles describes the early church’s practices.

Key Christian beliefs that inform prayer include: the nature of God (loving, just, omnipotent), the incarnation (God becoming human in Jesus), the atonement (Jesus’ death reconciling humanity with God), the resurrection (Jesus rising from the dead), and the Holy Spirit (God’s active presence in the world and in believers). These doctrines shape how Christians understand and practice their faith.

Different denominations interpret biblical teaching differently. Catholic Christians give authority to both Scripture and Tradition (the teaching authority of the Church). Protestant Christians emphasise sola scriptura (Scripture alone). Orthodox Christians emphasise the continuity of apostolic tradition. These differences affect how prayer is understood and practiced.

GCSE Example: Biblical and Theological Foundations of Prayer

When explaining prayer, reference specific Bible passages: for worship, cite John 4:24 (‘God is spirit, and his worshippers must worship in the Spirit and in truth’); for prayer, cite Matthew 6:9-13 (the Lord’s Prayer). Always explain what the passage means in context and how it influences Christian practice today.

The Impact of Prayer on Christians Today

Understanding prayer in the modern world means considering how Christians practice their faith in contemporary Britain and globally. Christianity in the UK is diverse: the Church of England (Anglican) is the established church, but there are also Catholic, Methodist, Baptist, Pentecostal and Orthodox communities, each with distinct approaches to prayer.

Christian practices like prayer have both personal and communal dimensions. Personally, they shape individual spiritual life, ethical decision-making and relationship with God. Communally, they build church community, strengthen social bonds, and provide a witness to wider society. Understanding both dimensions is essential for GCSE.

When evaluating the significance of prayer for Christians today, consider: why do some Christians find this practice more meaningful than others? How does the practice express Christian beliefs about God, Jesus and the Holy Spirit? What challenges do Christians face in maintaining this practice in contemporary secular society?

GCSE Example: The Impact of Prayer on Christians Today

A balanced evaluation of prayer might argue: for many Christians, this practice is central to their faith because [reason with biblical reference]. However, other Christians may prioritise different aspects of their faith because [reason]. Ultimately, the significance of prayer depends on [factor] because [justification].

Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain the importance of prayer for Christians, with reference to biblical teachings and denominational differences.

A: Prayer is important for Christians because [specific biblical teaching]. For example, [specific Bible passage] teaches that [specific principle]. Catholics approach prayer by [specific Catholic practice], while Protestants [specific Protestant approach]. This denominational difference exists because [theological reason]. Despite differences, all Christians value prayer because [shared reason].

Q: Evaluate the significance of prayer for Christians living in Britain today.

A: Prayer remains significant for many Christians in Britain today because [reason with evidence]. However, in a increasingly secular society, some Christians find it challenging to maintain this practice because [specific challenge]. Different denominations respond differently: [specific examples]. Overall, while prayer remains important for committed Christians, its expression may adapt to contemporary British life because [justified conclusion].
