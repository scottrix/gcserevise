RS9: Sacraments: Baptism and Eucharist

All Boards

The meaning of sacrament; infant and believers' baptism; Holy Communion and its significance

✝️ Sacraments: Baptism and Eucharist

Key Fact: A sacrament is an outward sign of an inward grace — a visible ceremony that Christians believe channels God's grace (favour) to the believer. Most Christians recognise two sacraments (Baptism and Eucharist) but Catholics and Orthodox recognise seven.

💧 Baptism

Baptism is the sacrament of initiation into the Christian faith. It involves water and the words "I baptise you in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit."

FeatureInfant BaptismBelievers' Baptism
WhoBabies, on behalf of parents' faithAdults/young people who choose it themselves
DenominationsCatholic, Anglican, Methodist, OrthodoxBaptist, Pentecostal, some evangelical
MethodSprinkling with water or pouringFull immersion in a pool or river
GodparentsYes — make promises on behalf of the childNo — the person makes their own promises
MeaningCleansing from original sin; welcome into the ChurchPublic declaration of personal faith; following Jesus' example

🍞 The Eucharist (Holy Communion)

The Eucharist commemorates the Last Supper, when Jesus shared bread and wine with his disciples before his crucifixion. "Do this in remembrance of me" (1 Corinthians 11:24-25).

ViewDenominationBelief about Bread and Wine
TransubstantiationCatholicThe bread and wine literally become the body and blood of Christ (though they still look and taste like bread and wine)
ConsubstantiationLutheranChrist's body and blood are present alongside the bread and wine
Memorial/SymbolicBaptist, ZwingliThe bread and wine are symbols that remind believers of Christ's sacrifice
Spiritual presenceMethodist, ReformedChrist is spiritually present but the elements remain bread and wine
Exam Tip: The Eucharist is the most important act of worship for Catholics — they believe they receive Christ himself. For Protestants, it is a memorial that strengthens faith and unity. Be ready to explain both views with specific theological terms.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two different Christian views about the Eucharist.

Q2: Explain why baptism is important for Christians.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Catholics believe in transubstantiation — the bread and wine literally become the body and blood of Christ during the Mass, even though they still appear as bread and wine. This is based on Jesus' words "This is my body" at the Last Supper. 2) Baptists and many Protestants see the Eucharist as a memorial — the bread and wine are symbols that remind believers of Christ's sacrifice on the cross. They follow Jesus' instruction to "do this in remembrance of me" but do not believe the elements change.
  2. Baptism is important because: it follows Jesus' own baptism by John (Mark 1:9-11); Jesus commanded his disciples to baptise (Matthew 28:19); it symbolises the washing away of sin and new life in Christ; it initiates the person into the Christian community; and Catholics believe it removes original sin. Different denominations practise baptism differently but all see it as a key Christian rite.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think all Christians celebrate the Eucharist in the same way. Wrong: "All Christians believe the bread and wine literally become Jesus' body and blood." Correct: "Catholics believe in transubstantiation; Protestants have different views including consubstantiation and memorial symbolism."

Detailed Notes

Understanding Sacraments: Baptism and Eucharist in Christian Practice

Sacraments: Baptism and Eucharist is a key aspect of Christian practice that GCSE Religious Studies students must understand in depth. Christianity is the world’s largest religion with over 2 billion followers, and its practices shape the daily lives of believers. The AQA specification requires you to explain Christian practices, analyse their significance, and evaluate different Christian perspectives with reference to biblical and theological sources.

When writing about sacraments: baptism and eucharist, always refer to specific biblical teachings, denominational differences (Catholic, Anglican, Orthodox, Protestant), and the lived experience of Christians today. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of diversity within Christianity — not all Christians interpret or practice their faith in the same way.

Christian practices are rooted in the life and teachings of Jesus Christ as recorded in the Bible. The New Testament, particularly the Gospels and the Acts of the Apostles, provides the primary sources for understanding how Christians are called to live. When discussing sacraments: baptism and eucharist, connect the practice to its biblical foundation.

GCSE Example: Understanding Sacraments: Baptism and Eucharist in Christian Practice

A strong GCSE RS answer about sacraments: baptism and eucharist would: explain the practice with reference to biblical teaching, describe how it is observed by different Christian denominations, analyse its significance for Christians, and evaluate different perspectives with justified reasoning.

Biblical and Theological Foundations of Sacraments: Baptism and Eucharist

Christian practices are grounded in the Bible and developed through church tradition. When discussing sacraments: baptism and eucharist, identify the relevant biblical passages: the Old Testament provides the Jewish context that Christianity grew from; the Gospels record Jesus’ life and teachings; the Epistles (letters) of Paul and others explain theological significance; and the Acts of the Apostles describes the early church’s practices.

Key Christian beliefs that inform sacraments: baptism and eucharist include: the nature of God (loving, just, omnipotent), the incarnation (God becoming human in Jesus), the atonement (Jesus’ death reconciling humanity with God), the resurrection (Jesus rising from the dead), and the Holy Spirit (God’s active presence in the world and in believers). These doctrines shape how Christians understand and practice their faith.

Different denominations interpret biblical teaching differently. Catholic Christians give authority to both Scripture and Tradition (the teaching authority of the Church). Protestant Christians emphasise sola scriptura (Scripture alone). Orthodox Christians emphasise the continuity of apostolic tradition. These differences affect how sacraments: baptism and eucharist is understood and practiced.

GCSE Example: Biblical and Theological Foundations of Sacraments: Baptism and Eucharist

When explaining sacraments: baptism and eucharist, reference specific Bible passages: for worship, cite John 4:24 (‘God is spirit, and his worshippers must worship in the Spirit and in truth’); for prayer, cite Matthew 6:9-13 (the Lord’s Prayer). Always explain what the passage means in context and how it influences Christian practice today.

The Impact of Sacraments: Baptism and Eucharist on Christians Today

Understanding sacraments: baptism and eucharist in the modern world means considering how Christians practice their faith in contemporary Britain and globally. Christianity in the UK is diverse: the Church of England (Anglican) is the established church, but there are also Catholic, Methodist, Baptist, Pentecostal and Orthodox communities, each with distinct approaches to sacraments: baptism and eucharist.

Christian practices like sacraments: baptism and eucharist have both personal and communal dimensions. Personally, they shape individual spiritual life, ethical decision-making and relationship with God. Communally, they build church community, strengthen social bonds, and provide a witness to wider society. Understanding both dimensions is essential for GCSE.

When evaluating the significance of sacraments: baptism and eucharist for Christians today, consider: why do some Christians find this practice more meaningful than others? How does the practice express Christian beliefs about God, Jesus and the Holy Spirit? What challenges do Christians face in maintaining this practice in contemporary secular society?

GCSE Example: The Impact of Sacraments: Baptism and Eucharist on Christians Today

A balanced evaluation of sacraments: baptism and eucharist might argue: for many Christians, this practice is central to their faith because [reason with biblical reference]. However, other Christians may prioritise different aspects of their faith because [reason]. Ultimately, the significance of sacraments: baptism and eucharist depends on [factor] because [justification].
