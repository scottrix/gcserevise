CI19: What Is Active Citizenship?

Foundation Higher AQA 8100, OCR J270, Edexcel 1CS0

What active citizenship means, why it matters, different forms of participation, barriers to participation, and how it strengthens democracy.

What Is Active Citizenship?

What active citizenship means, why it matters, different forms of participation, barriers to participation, and how it strengthens democracy.

Key Fact: Active citizenship means taking responsible action to address community or social issues — it goes beyond voting and includes volunteering, campaigning, and community action.
Key Fact: Formal participation: voting, joining a political party, standing for election, contacting elected representatives, attending public meetings.
Key Fact: Informal participation: volunteering, community organising, campaigning, protesting, signing petitions, using social media for civic purposes.
Key Fact: The GCSE specification requires students to carry out a citizenship action project — addressing a real issue through research, planning, action, and evaluation.
Key Fact: Volunteering contributes to communities and develops skills such as teamwork, communication, and empathy. Over 20 million people volunteer in the UK each year.
Key Fact: Campaigning raises awareness and can influence decision-makers. Methods include petitions, social media campaigns, public meetings, and lobbying elected representatives.
Key Fact: Community action brings people together to address local needs collectively — food banks, youth clubs, environmental clean-ups, and mutual aid networks.
Key Fact: Digital citizenship involves responsible online participation: combating misinformation, engaging in respectful debate, and using social media for civic engagement.
Key Fact: Active citizenship strengthens democracy by holding those in power to account, ensuring diverse voices are heard, and filling gaps in public services.
Key Fact: Barriers to participation include: lack of time (especially for low-paid workers), lack of confidence or knowledge, lack of resources, language barriers, and feeling that participation will not make a difference.
Key Fact: Young people can be active citizens through school councils, youth parliament, volunteering, campaigning, and community projects — age is not a barrier.
Key Fact: Active citizenship benefits both society and the individual: it develops skills, builds social connections, increases political understanding, and gives a sense of purpose.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is active citizenship and why is it important?

Q: Give three examples of active citizenship.

Q: What are the barriers to active citizenship?

✅ Answers

  1. Active citizenship means taking part in community and political life to improve society. It is important because it strengthens democracy, holds those in power to account, addresses social issues directly, and empowers individuals and communities to make a difference.
  2. 1) Volunteering at a food bank to help people experiencing poverty. 2) Campaigning for better recycling facilities by organising a petition and meeting the local council. 3) Voting in elections and encouraging others to do the same through a voter registration drive.
  3. Barriers include lack of time (especially for people in low-paid work), lack of confidence or knowledge about how to participate, lack of financial resources, language barriers for non-native speakers, and a sense that participation will not make a difference (political disengagement).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Active citizenship is both a topic and a practical requirement — know the theory AND the process
  • Use real examples of citizenship action to illustrate your answers
  • AQA requires you to describe and evaluate your own citizenship action in Paper 2 Section B

📝 Exam Technique

Citizenship Studies Exam Tips:
Use precise legal and political terminology. Always support arguments with specific examples (e.g. named laws, cases, or organisations). For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Active citizenship questions require you to demonstrate practical understanding, not just theoretical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Active citizenship only means voting in elections. ✓ Voting is one form, but active citizenship also includes volunteering, campaigning, joining pressure groups, contacting representatives, community organising, and digital participation.

✗ Only adults can be active citizens. ✓ Young people can be active citizens through school councils, youth parliament, volunteering, campaigning, and community projects.

✗ Active citizenship always involves politics. ✓ Active citizenship includes non-political actions like volunteering at a charity shop, organising a community clean-up, or mentoring younger students.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain why active citizenship is important for a healthy democracy. [9 marks]

Active citizenship is essential for a healthy democracy because it ensures power remains in the hands of the people. When citizens participate — by voting, campaigning, or holding representatives to account — they ensure government reflects the will of the people. Without participation, democracy becomes symbolic rather than real. Active citizenship provides a check on power. Investigative journalism, pressure groups, and public protests expose government failures and demand accountability. The Windrush scandal was exposed through active citizenship by campaigners and journalists. Furthermore, active citizenship addresses issues that government alone cannot solve. Food banks, youth clubs, and environmental clean-ups are often run by volunteers who fill gaps in public services. This strengthens communities and builds social cohesion. However, barriers to participation mean not all citizens can be equally active, meaning democracy may disproportionately reflect the voices of more privileged citizens.

AO2 (Analysis & Evaluation — 40%): You must analyse and evaluate different viewpoints, policies, and approaches related to what is active citizenship?. Consider strengths and limitations, compare perspectives, and reach reasoned judgements.

AO3 (Active Citizenship — 30%): Apply your understanding of what is active citizenship? to real-world citizenship contexts. Demonstrate how citizens can engage with this topic through participation, campaigning, or community action.
