Citizenship Action Planning

How to plan a citizenship action project: identifying issues, researching, setting SMART targets, choosing methods, planning resources and timescales, and documenting evidence.

Key Fact: Identify a genuine citizenship issue: something related to rights, democracy, equality, or community participation that you care about and that affects real people.
Key Fact: Research the issue thoroughly using reliable sources: government statistics, charity reports, academic research, surveys, and expert interviews. Avoid relying on social media alone.
Key Fact: Set SMART targets: Specific (clear and focused), Measurable (can track progress), Achievable (realistic given your resources), Relevant (linked to citizenship), Time-bound (with deadlines).
Key Fact: Identify stakeholders: who is affected by the issue, who has the power to change it, and who might support or oppose your action. Understanding power dynamics is key.
Key Fact: Choose appropriate methods: petitions, letter-writing, public meetings, social media campaigns, awareness events, lobbying decision-makers. Match methods to your target audience.
Key Fact: Plan resources: people (volunteers and supporters), money (budget for materials), materials (printing, equipment), and permissions (school approval, risk assessments for events).
Key Fact: Set a realistic timescale with milestones and deadlines. Break the project into phases: research, planning, action, and evaluation.
Key Fact: Consider potential obstacles and plan how to overcome them: lack of engagement, unresponsive decision-makers, negative publicity, resource shortages.
Key Fact: Involve others — collective action is usually more effective than individual action. Build a team with different skills and delegate responsibilities.
Key Fact: Ensure your action is lawful, safe, and respectful. Protests must be notified to police; safeguarding procedures apply if working with vulnerable groups.
Key Fact: Document everything for your GCSE portfolio: photos, screenshots, petition signatures, meeting minutes, feedback forms, media coverage.
Key Fact: AQA citizenship action must demonstrate: research into the issue, planned strategies, the action itself, and evaluation of impact — all four elements are assessed.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What are the key stages in planning a citizenship action?

Q: Why is it important to identify stakeholders when planning citizenship action?

Q: Give an example of a SMART target for a citizenship action.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Identify a citizenship issue, 2) Research the issue thoroughly, 3) Set clear aims and SMART targets, 4) Decide on methods and actions, 5) Plan resources, timescales, and roles, 6) Carry out the action, 7) Evaluate the impact and reflect on learning.
  2. Stakeholders are the people or groups affected by or with power over the issue. Identifying them helps you understand who to influence, who to involve, and who might support or oppose your action, making your campaign more strategic and effective.
  3. To collect 200 signatures on a petition for improved recycling facilities in the school by the end of November, present it to the school council in December, and achieve a commitment from the headteacher by the end of term.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • AQA Paper 2 Section B asks about your citizenship action — prepare detailed notes in advance
  • Always link your action to citizenship concepts (rights, democracy, participation, equality)
  • Evaluation is worth the most marks — explain what worked, what didn't, and why

📝 Exam Technique

Citizenship Studies Exam Tips:
Use precise legal and political terminology. Always support arguments with specific examples (e.g. named laws, cases, or organisations). For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Active citizenship questions require you to demonstrate practical understanding, not just theoretical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Any project counts as citizenship action. ✓ Citizenship action must address a genuine citizenship issue related to rights, democracy, or community participation — not just a charity fundraiser or personal project.

✗ The more ambitious the action, the better the grade. ✓ Marks are awarded for the quality of planning, research, and evaluation — not the scale. A well-evaluated small project can score higher than a poorly evaluated large one.

✗ You only need to describe what you did in the exam. ✓ You need to analyse and evaluate your action — explain why you chose your methods, what worked and what didn't, and what you would do differently.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe how you would plan a citizenship action to address a local issue. [9 marks]

To plan a citizenship action on a local issue such as lack of youth facilities, I would first research the issue by surveying young people in my area and gathering data on existing facilities and funding. I would identify which local authority department is responsible and who the relevant decision-makers are. Next, I would set SMART targets: to collect 300 signatures on a petition within three weeks, present findings to the local council within six weeks, and secure a meeting with the cabinet member for communities within two months. I would plan my methods: creating an online petition, running a social media campaign, organising a community meeting, and writing to my local councillor. I would recruit volunteers, assign roles, and set a budget for materials. Finally, I would plan for obstacles — if the council is unresponsive, I would escalate by contacting the local MP or newspaper. Throughout, I would document evidence including petition signatures, meeting notes, and media coverage.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 (Knowledge & Understanding — 30%): You need to demonstrate knowledge and understanding of citizenship action planning, including key concepts, legal frameworks, and institutional structures.

AO2 (Analysis & Evaluation — 40%): You must analyse and evaluate different viewpoints, policies, and approaches related to citizenship action planning. Consider strengths and limitations, compare perspectives, and reach reasoned judgements.

AO3 (Active Citizenship — 30%): Apply your understanding of citizenship action planning to real-world citizenship contexts. Demonstrate how citizens can engage with this topic through participation, campaigning, or community action.
