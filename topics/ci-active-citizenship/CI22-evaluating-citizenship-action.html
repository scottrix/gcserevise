Evaluating Citizenship Action

How to evaluate your citizenship action: measuring impact, using evidence, reflecting on what worked and what didn't, and connecting to citizenship concepts. Essential for AQA Paper 2 Section B.

Key Fact: Evaluation means assessing what worked, what didn't, and why — it is the most important part of citizenship action and worth the most marks in the exam.
Key Fact: Evaluate by comparing outcomes against original aims: Did you achieve what you set out to do? How do you know? What evidence supports your assessment?
Key Fact: Consider both intended outcomes (what you planned) and unintended outcomes (unexpected results, positive or negative).
Key Fact: Use quantitative evidence (numbers: petition signatures, survey responses, attendance figures) and qualitative evidence (quotes, testimonials, observations, feedback).
Key Fact: Evaluate your methods: Were petitions, social media, or meetings the most effective? Why? Would different methods have worked better?
Key Fact: Assess impact on stakeholders: How were different groups affected? Did the action benefit some more than others? Were any groups excluded?
Key Fact: Identify what worked well and explain why — was it good planning, effective methods, strong teamwork, or fortunate timing?
Key Fact: Identify what didn't work and analyse reasons — was it poor research, unrealistic targets, lack of engagement, or external obstacles?
Key Fact: Reflect on what you would do differently with hindsight — this demonstrates learning and gains high marks for evaluation.
Key Fact: Connect your evaluation to citizenship concepts: How did your action relate to democracy, rights, participation, or accountability? What did you learn about how change happens?
Key Fact: Honest evaluation is more valuable than claiming success where there was little — examiners reward critical reflection, not exaggeration.
Key Fact: AQA Paper 2 Section B is worth 24 marks, with the majority for evaluation. Prepare detailed notes about your action before the exam.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Why is evaluation important in citizenship action?

Q: What is the difference between quantitative and qualitative evidence in evaluation?

Q: How should you structure an evaluation of your citizenship action?

✅ Answers

  1. Evaluation is important because it assesses whether the action achieved its aims, identifies what worked and what didn't, provides evidence of impact, and helps improve future actions. In the AQA exam, evaluation is worth the most marks (up to 24 marks on Paper 2 Section B).
  2. Quantitative evidence is numerical — e.g. 250 signatures collected, 80% of students supported the campaign. Qualitative evidence is descriptive — e.g. quotes from stakeholders about changing attitudes, observations about increased awareness, testimonials from participants.
  3. Restate your original aims, assess whether each aim was met using evidence, discuss which methods worked well and why, identify what didn't work and analyse reasons, explain what you would do differently, and reflect on what you learned about citizenship and democracy.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • AQA Paper 2 Section B is worth 24 marks — most marks are for evaluation, not description
  • Always use specific evidence from your action, not vague generalisations
  • Honest reflection scores higher than exaggerated claims of success

📝 Exam Technique

Citizenship Studies Exam Tips:
Use precise legal and political terminology. Always support arguments with specific examples (e.g. named laws, cases, or organisations). For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Active citizenship questions require you to demonstrate practical understanding, not just theoretical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Evaluation means just describing what you did. ✓ Description is only the starting point. Evaluation requires analysis — explaining why things happened, assessing effectiveness, and reflecting on learning.

✗ If your action didn't achieve its aims, you will lose marks. ✓ Marks are for the quality of evaluation, not whether the action succeeded. An honest evaluation of a partially successful action that analyses reasons will score highly.

✗ You only need to evaluate whether you achieved your aims. ✓ You should also evaluate: your choice of methods, impact on stakeholders, unintended outcomes, what you would do differently, and what you learned about citizenship.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the citizenship action you have carried out. In your answer you should refer to the research you undertook, the strategies you chose, the action you took and the impact of the action. [24 marks]

My citizenship action aimed to improve recycling facilities in my school, where a survey showed 78% of students wanted better options. My research included surveying 150 students, interviewing the site manager, and reviewing the school's current recycling provision. My strategy was to combine a petition with a presentation to the school council and a social media campaign. I chose these methods because they involved different stakeholders and created multiple routes to influence the headteacher. The petition collected 210 signatures in two weeks, exceeding my target of 150. The social media campaign reached 500 views on Instagram but engagement was low — only 30 likes and 5 shares. This showed that while awareness was raised, it did not translate into active support. Presenting to the school council was effective — they voted to support the proposal, giving it legitimacy. The headteacher agreed to fund three new recycling bins and a poster campaign, partially meeting my aim. However, my action had limitations. I focused on bins but did not address the lack of education about what can be recycled. I struggled to maintain momentum after the launch. With hindsight, I would have involved the eco-club from the start. The impact was measurable — recycling rates increased by 25% in the first month. However, long-term impact depends on whether the school maintains the facilities and educates students. I learned that combining methods is more effective than relying on one approach, and that citizenship action requires persistence.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 (Knowledge & Understanding — 30%): You need to demonstrate knowledge and understanding of evaluating citizenship action, including key concepts, legal frameworks, and institutional structures.

AO2 (Analysis & Evaluation — 40%): You must analyse and evaluate different viewpoints, policies, and approaches related to evaluating citizenship action. Consider strengths and limitations, compare perspectives, and reach reasoned judgements.

AO3 (Active Citizenship — 30%): Apply your understanding of evaluating citizenship action to real-world citizenship contexts. Demonstrate how citizens can engage with this topic through participation, campaigning, or community action.
