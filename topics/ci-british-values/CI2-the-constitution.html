The UK Constitution & Devolution

How the UK is governed: the uncodified constitution, parliamentary sovereignty, devolution to Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland, and how power is distributed.

Key Fact: The UK has an uncodified (unwritten) constitution: no single document sets out the rules of government. Instead, the constitution comes from: statutes (laws passed by Parliament), common law (judicial precedent), conventions (unwritten rules like the PM being an MP), and authoritative works (A.V. Dicey). This makes the constitution flexible but also less clear.
Key Fact: Parliamentary sovereignty: Parliament is the supreme legal authority - it can make or unmake any law. No Parliament can bind its successor. However, EU membership (1973-2020) limited sovereignty; devolution has transferred some powers; and the Human Rights Act 1998 means UK courts can declare legislation incompatible with human rights.
Key Fact: Devolution: the transfer of powers from Westminster to Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland. Scotland Act 1998: created the Scottish Parliament with powers over health, education, justice and environment (but not defence, foreign policy). Wales Act 1998/2017: Senedd Cymru has powers over health, education, some taxation. Northern Ireland Act 1998: Stormont has powers over local matters (suspended at times due to political crisis).
Key Fact: The West Lothian Question: Scottish, Welsh and Northern Irish MPs can vote on English-only matters at Westminster, but English MPs cannot vote on devolved matters. English Votes for English Laws (EVEL) was introduced in 2015 but abolished in 2021. This remains an unresolved constitutional issue.
Key Fact: Constitutional reform debates: should the UK adopt a codified constitution (clearer rights, limits on government power) or keep the current system (flexible, pragmatic)? Should the UK become a federal state? Should the House of Lords be reformed or abolished? Should proportional representation replace First Past the Post?

🎯 Exam Tips

  • UK constitution = UNCODIFIED (statutes + common law + conventions). Flexible but unclear.
  • Devolution: Scotland, Wales, NI have their own parliaments/assemblies with LIMITED powers. Westminster retains defence, foreign policy.
  • Parliamentary sovereignty: still exists in LAW but limited in PRACTICE by devolution, human rights, international agreements.

📝 Exam Technique

Citizenship Studies Exam Tips:
Use precise legal and political terminology. Always support arguments with specific examples (e.g. named laws, cases, or organisations). For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Active citizenship questions require you to demonstrate practical understanding, not just theoretical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ The UK does not have a constitution because it is not written down in a single document. ✓ The UK DOES have a constitution - it is uncodified rather than unwritten. It exists across multiple sources: Acts of Parliament (e.g. Human Rights Act 1998), common law (judicial decisions), conventions (PM must be an MP), and authoritative works. It is just as real and binding as a codified constitution, though less accessible to ordinary citizens.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether the UK should adopt a codified constitution.

A grade 9 response will: define codified and uncodified constitutions; present arguments for codification (clear rights, limits on power, citizen reference point, modern democratic standard); present arguments against (flexibility loss, difficult to agree, judicial activism risk, UK system works well); evaluate using international comparisons (USA, Germany have codified constitutions); conclude: a codified constitution would strengthen rights protection and democratic accountability, but the current system's flexibility is valuable. A compromise might be a written constitution that preserves some flexibility through amendment procedures.
