Democracy in Britain

How democracy works in the UK: representative democracy, the role of Parliament, elections, and how citizens can participate in the democratic process.

Key Fact: Representative democracy: citizens elect MPs to represent them in Parliament. The UK is a parliamentary democracy where the government is drawn from and accountable to Parliament. General elections must be held at least every 5 years (Fixed-term Parliaments Act 2011, largely repealed 2022).
Key Fact: Parliament: House of Commons (650 elected MPs), House of Lords (unelected - hereditary peers, life peers, bishops), and the monarch (largely ceremonial - Royal Assent). Parliament scrutinises the government, passes laws, approves taxation, and represents the public.
Key Fact: How a law is made: Green Paper (consultation) -> White Paper (proposals) -> Bill -> First Reading -> Second Reading (debate) -> Committee Stage (detailed examination) -> Report Stage -> Third Reading -> House of Lords -> Royal Assent -> Act of Parliament.
Key Fact: Elections: general elections (MPs), devolved elections (MSPs, MSs, MLAs), local elections (councillors), and elections for Police and Crime Commissioners. Voting age: 18 for general elections (16 for Scottish Parliament and Welsh Senedd elections). First Past the Post (FPTP) is used for general elections.
Key Fact: Voter turnout and participation: turnout has declined from 84% in 1950 to 67% in 2019. Reasons: disengagement, lack of trust, feeling votes don't matter. Ways to increase participation: lower voting age, proportional representation, online voting, compulsory voting, and citizenship education.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Parliament = Commons (elected) + Lords (unelected) + Monarch (ceremonial). Commons is supreme.
  • Law-making: Green Paper -> White Paper -> Bill -> Readings + Committee -> Lords -> Royal Assent.
  • FPTP = simple but UNFAIR. PR = fairer but may create instability. Trade-off between stability and proportionality.

📝 Exam Technique

Citizenship Studies Exam Tips:
Use precise legal and political terminology. Always support arguments with specific examples (e.g. named laws, cases, or organisations). For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Active citizenship questions require you to demonstrate practical understanding, not just theoretical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ The UK is a full democracy because every citizen can vote and the government is elected. ✓ While the UK has democratic structures, there are significant democratic deficits: the House of Lords is unelected; FPTP produces disproportionate results; voter turnout has declined; the government can bypass Parliament through secondary legislation; and the Prime Minister has extensive powers (calling elections, appointing peers). A true democracy requires not just elections but meaningful participation, accountability and proportionality.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate how effectively the UK Parliament holds the government to account.

A grade 9 response will: describe scrutiny mechanisms (PMQs, select committees, debates, votes); argue Parliament IS effective (committees expose failings, debates shape policy, rebellions can defeat government); argue Parliament is NOT effective (whips control MPs, large majorities mean government always wins, Lords can only delay); evaluate: effectiveness varies with government majority, media pressure and public opinion; conclude: Parliament has significant scrutiny tools but their effectiveness depends on political circumstances. Stronger scrutiny would require: free votes, empowered committees, and Lords reform.
