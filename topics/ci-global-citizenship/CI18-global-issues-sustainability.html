Global Issues & Sustainability

Global challenges including climate change, poverty, conflict, and sustainability. The UN Sustainable Development Goals and citizens' responsibilities as global citizens.

Key Fact: Climate change is one of the greatest global challenges, caused by greenhouse gas emissions from burning fossil fuels. The Paris Agreement (2015) aims to limit warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels.
Key Fact: The UK has committed to net zero carbon emissions by 2050 — the first major economy to do so. This requires transforming energy, transport, and industry. The Climate Change Committee monitors progress.
Key Fact: Extreme poverty has declined globally but inequality persists both between and within nations. The richest 1% own nearly half the world's wealth. Global cooperation on trade, aid, and debt relief is essential.
Key Fact: The UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) set 17 targets for a better world by 2030, including: no poverty, zero hunger, quality education, gender equality, clean water, climate action, and reduced inequalities.
Key Fact: Global health crises like COVID-19 demonstrate the need for international cooperation — viruses do not respect borders, and vaccine distribution requires global coordination.
Key Fact: Conflicts and refugees are global issues: over 100 million people are forcibly displaced worldwide. The UNHCR coordinates refugee protection, but host countries bear most responsibility.
Key Fact: Sustainable development balances three pillars: economic growth, social inclusion, and environmental protection. Progress in one area should not come at the expense of the others.
Key Fact: Individual actions (recycling, reducing energy use, choosing sustainable products) contribute to sustainability, but systemic change through government policy and corporate responsibility is also essential.
Key Fact: Fair trade ensures producers in developing countries receive fair prices, decent working conditions, and sustainable farming practices. Look for the Fairtrade Mark on products.
Key Fact: Water scarcity, deforestation, and biodiversity loss are interconnected environmental challenges that require coordinated international action.
Key Fact: Global citizenship means taking responsibility for the impact of our choices on others worldwide — recognising that our consumption, travel, and political choices affect people in other countries.
Key Fact: The UK has a key role in addressing global issues through its aid budget, diplomatic influence, military capability, and leadership in international organisations.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What are the UN Sustainable Development Goals?

Q: Explain why climate change requires international cooperation.

Q: Evaluate the effectiveness of individual actions vs government policy in addressing sustainability.

✅ Answers

  1. The SDGs are 17 goals adopted by all UN member states in 2015, aiming to end poverty, protect the planet, and ensure peace and prosperity by 2030. They include goals on no poverty, zero hunger, quality education, gender equality, clean water, climate action, and reduced inequalities.
  2. Climate change is a global problem — emissions in one country affect the entire planet. No single nation can solve it alone. International agreements like the Paris Agreement are needed because countries must commit to reducing emissions collectively, and developing nations need financial and technical support from wealthier countries.
  3. Individual actions like recycling and reducing energy use raise awareness and reduce personal carbon footprints. However, individual action alone is insufficient — about 70% of global emissions come from 100 companies. Government policy can drive systemic change through regulation, green investment, and international agreements. The most effective approach combines both.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always link sustainability to the three pillars: economic, social, environmental
  • Use specific SDG numbers and names when referencing them (e.g. SDG 13: Climate Action)
  • When evaluating climate action, consider both mitigation (reducing emissions) and adaptation (preparing for effects)

📝 Exam Technique

Citizenship Studies Exam Tips:
Use precise legal and political terminology. Always support arguments with specific examples (e.g. named laws, cases, or organisations). For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Active citizenship questions require you to demonstrate practical understanding, not just theoretical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Sustainability is only about the environment. ✓ Sustainability has three pillars: environmental protection, social inclusion, and economic development. All three must be balanced for true sustainability.

✗ Individual actions cannot make any difference to global issues. ✓ While individual actions alone cannot solve global issues, collective individual action can drive market changes and influence government policy. Every social movement starts with individuals.

✗ The UK is already doing enough to meet its net zero target. ✓ The Climate Change Committee has warned the UK is not on track, with insufficient progress on home insulation, transport electrification, and agricultural emissions.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that global citizenship requires individuals to take action on climate change. [12 marks]

Global citizenship implies individuals have responsibilities beyond their own nation, and climate change is the defining global challenge. As global citizens, individuals should take action because everyday choices — energy use, travel, diet, consumption — directly contribute to carbon emissions. Individual action demonstrates commitment and can influence others. Democratic participation is also individual action: citizens can vote for parties with strong climate policies, join environmental pressure groups, and petition government. These individual political actions can drive systemic change that personal lifestyle changes alone cannot achieve. However, individual action has clear limitations. About 70% of global emissions come from just 100 companies. One person's recycling is insignificant compared to industrial emissions. Placing responsibility on individuals can distract from the need for government regulation and corporate accountability. In conclusion, while individual action is necessary to demonstrate commitment and build political demand for change, it is not sufficient on its own. Effective global citizenship on climate change requires both personal action and political engagement to drive the systemic changes the crisis demands.
