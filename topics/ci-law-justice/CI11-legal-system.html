The Legal System in the UK

How the UK legal system works: the rule of law, sources of law (statute and common law), the court hierarchy, the role of judges and juries, and legal aid.

Key Fact: The rule of law is a fundamental principle: no one is above the law, including the government. Laws must be clear, public, and applied equally to everyone.
Key Fact: Parliamentary sovereignty means Parliament is the supreme law-making body. No Parliament can bind its successors, and courts cannot overrule primary legislation.
Key Fact: Statute law is written law passed by Parliament through the legislative process (Bill → Royal Assent → Act). Common law is developed through court decisions and judicial precedent.
Key Fact: Precedent: lower courts must follow decisions of higher courts in similar cases. This ensures consistency but can mean outdated rulings persist until overturned by a higher court or Parliament.
Key Fact: The court hierarchy: Magistrates' Court (minor criminal and some civil) → Crown Court (serious criminal) → High Court (major civil and appeals) → Court of Appeal → Supreme Court.
Key Fact: Magistrates are volunteer judges (no legal qualification required) who hear most criminal cases. Judges in higher courts are legally qualified and independent of government.
Key Fact: Juries of 12 randomly selected citizens decide the verdict in Crown Court trials. Jury service is a civic duty and a safeguard against state oppression.
Key Fact: The Supreme Court is the highest court for most matters. It can declare government actions unlawful and issue declarations of incompatibility with the European Convention on Human Rights.
Key Fact: Legal aid provides government funding for those who cannot afford legal representation, but cuts since 2012 have significantly reduced eligibility, particularly for civil cases.
Key Fact: The Ministry of Justice oversees the courts, sentencing, and rehabilitation. The Lord Chancellor is the cabinet minister responsible for the justice system.
Key Fact: Scotland and Northern Ireland have separate legal systems with some different procedures and courts, though the UK Supreme Court remains the highest court for all.
Key Fact: The Judicial Appointments Commission ensures judges are appointed on merit, maintaining judicial independence from political influence.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the rule of law and why is it important?

Q: Explain the difference between statute law and common law.

Q: Describe the role of the Supreme Court in the UK legal system.

✅ Answers

  1. The rule of law means everyone, including the government, is subject to the same laws and can be held accountable. It is important because it prevents arbitrary power, ensures equality before the law, and protects citizens' rights from being overridden by those in authority.
  2. Statute law is written law passed by Parliament through the legislative process (a Bill receives Royal Assent to become an Act). Common law is developed through court decisions, where judges apply and extend legal principles through precedent. Statute law takes priority over common law.
  3. The Supreme Court is the highest court in the UK for most matters. It hears appeals on points of law of general public importance, and its decisions bind all lower courts. It can declare government actions unlawful but cannot overrule primary legislation passed by Parliament.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always distinguish between criminal and civil law in your answers
  • Use correct court names: 'Crown Court' not 'criminal court', 'High Court' not 'civil court'
  • The rule of law is a fundamental concept that appears across multiple topics — know it thoroughly

📝 Exam Technique

Citizenship Studies Exam Tips:
Use precise legal and political terminology. Always support arguments with specific examples (e.g. named laws, cases, or organisations). For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Active citizenship questions require you to demonstrate practical understanding, not just theoretical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ The Supreme Court can overrule Parliament's laws. ✓ The Supreme Court cannot overrule primary legislation. It can declare government actions unlawful and issue declarations of incompatibility with the ECHR, but Parliament remains sovereign.

✗ Common law is less important than statute law. ✓ Both are important sources of law. Many fundamental legal principles originated in common law, and statute law often builds on common law foundations. Statute law takes priority where they conflict.

✗ Judges are appointed by the government to follow its policies. ✓ Judges are independent and must decide cases based on law, not government policy. The Judicial Appointments Commission selects judges on merit, free from political influence.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain why the rule of law is essential for a fair society. [9 marks]

The rule of law is essential for a fair society because it ensures everyone, including those in power, is accountable under the same laws. Without the rule of law, governments could act arbitrarily, favouring some citizens over others and punishing opponents without legal basis. The rule of law means laws must be clear, publicly available, and predictable, so citizens can understand their rights and obligations. This prevents secret or retroactive laws that could trap people unfairly. Furthermore, the rule of law guarantees access to independent courts where disputes can be resolved impartially. This means that if someone's rights are violated, they can seek justice regardless of their wealth or status. Legal aid was designed to support this principle. However, the rule of law is not always perfectly applied. Legal aid cuts have reduced access to justice for many, and some groups may face bias within the system. Nevertheless, the principle remains fundamental to preventing tyranny and ensuring fairness.
