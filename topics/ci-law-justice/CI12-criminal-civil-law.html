Criminal & Civil Law

The differences between criminal law (offences against the state) and civil law (disputes between individuals): standards of proof, courts, procedures, and outcomes.

Key Fact: Criminal law deals with offences against the state and society. The state prosecutes, and penalties include imprisonment, fines, and community orders.
Key Fact: Civil law resolves disputes between individuals or organisations. The claimant sues, and remedies include damages (compensation), injunctions, and specific performance.
Key Fact: Standard of proof: criminal cases require proof 'beyond reasonable doubt'; civil cases require proof on the 'balance of probabilities' (more likely than not).
Key Fact: In criminal cases, the Crown Prosecution Service (CPS) brings the case on behalf of the state. The defendant is presumed innocent until proven guilty.
Key Fact: In civil cases, the claimant brings the case and must prove their claim. There is no presumption of innocence — the court weighs the evidence from both sides.
Key Fact: Criminal cases: Magistrates' Court (summary offences) and Crown Court (indictable offences, trial by jury). Civil cases: County Court and High Court.
Key Fact: Criminal penalties aim to punish, deter, and rehabilitate. Civil remedies aim to compensate the injured party or prevent further harm.
Key Fact: Some acts can be both a crime and a civil wrong: e.g. assault (criminal prosecution + civil claim for damages), fraud (criminal + civil recovery).
Key Fact: Legal aid is available for some criminal and civil cases but has been significantly reduced since 2012, particularly for family and civil matters.
Key Fact: Tort law is a major branch of civil law covering negligence, defamation, nuisance, and trespass. It provides remedies when someone's actions cause loss or harm to another.
Key Fact: The police investigate crimes and gather evidence. Civil disputes do not involve police investigation — the claimant must gather their own evidence.
Key Fact: A person found not guilty in a criminal court can still be sued in a civil court because the standard of proof is lower (O.J. Simpson case principle).

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the main difference between criminal and civil law?

Q: Why is the standard of proof higher in criminal cases?

Q: Give an example where an act could be both a crime and a civil wrong.

✅ Answers

  1. Criminal law deals with offences against the state, where the CPS prosecutes and guilt must be proven beyond reasonable doubt, with penalties including prison. Civil law resolves disputes between individuals, where the claimant sues and must prove their case on the balance of probabilities, with remedies like damages or injunctions.
  2. The standard of proof is higher in criminal cases because the consequences are more severe — a defendant could lose their liberty through imprisonment. The 'beyond reasonable doubt' standard protects innocent people from wrongful conviction, reflecting the principle that it is better for the guilty to go free than for the innocent to be wrongly convicted.
  3. If someone assaults another person, they could be prosecuted in a criminal court for the offence of assault and sentenced to prison. The victim could also sue them in a civil court for compensation for injuries, lost earnings, and emotional distress.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always specify whether you are discussing criminal or civil law
  • Use precise legal vocabulary — 'claimant' not 'plaintiff', 'prosecution' not 'the government'
  • When comparing, consider: who brings the case, standard of proof, type of court, and outcome

📝 Exam Technique

Citizenship Studies Exam Tips:
Use precise legal and political terminology. Always support arguments with specific examples (e.g. named laws, cases, or organisations). For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Active citizenship questions require you to demonstrate practical understanding, not just theoretical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Civil law is less important than criminal law. ✓ Both are equally important. Civil law protects property rights, enforces contracts, provides remedies for harm, and regulates relationships — all essential for an orderly society.

✗ If someone is found not guilty in a criminal court, they cannot be sued. ✓ A person found not guilty criminally can still be sued in a civil court because the standard of proof is lower (balance of probabilities vs beyond reasonable doubt).

✗ The police investigate both criminal and civil cases. ✓ The police only investigate criminal cases. In civil disputes, each party must gather their own evidence and present it to the court.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Compare criminal and civil law in the UK. [12 marks]

Criminal and civil law serve different purposes in the UK legal system. Criminal law deals with offences against the state and society as a whole. Cases are brought by the Crown Prosecution Service on behalf of the state, and guilt must be proven beyond reasonable doubt. Penalties include imprisonment, fines, and community orders, and the aim is to punish and deter offenders. Civil law resolves disputes between individuals or organisations. Cases are brought by a claimant who has suffered harm or loss, and liability must be proven on the balance of probabilities — a lower standard than criminal law. Remedies include damages, injunctions, and specific performance, and the aim is to put the injured party in the position they would have been in had the wrong not occurred. Despite these differences, the two systems overlap. An assault can lead to criminal prosecution and a civil claim for damages. Both systems operate through independent courts and are underpinned by the rule of law. Both have been affected by legal aid cuts, which some argue undermines access to justice.
