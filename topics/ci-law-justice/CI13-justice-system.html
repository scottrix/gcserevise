The Justice System

How the UK justice system works: the police, CPS, courts, sentencing, prisons, probation, youth justice, and restorative justice.

Key Fact: The criminal justice system involves: police (investigate), CPS (prosecute), courts (try), sentencing (punish), probation (supervise in community), prison (custodial sentences).
Key Fact: The police are responsible for preventing and detecting crime, maintaining public order, and protecting life and property. Police officers have powers of arrest, stop and search, and detention.
Key Fact: The Crown Prosecution Service (CPS) reviews evidence gathered by the police and decides whether to charge — applying the Full Code Test (sufficient evidence + public interest).
Key Fact: Trial by jury is available for serious criminal offences ('indictable offences') in the Crown Court. The jury of 12 citizens decides the verdict; the judge decides the sentence.
Key Fact: Sentencing aims: retribution (punishment), deterrence (discouraging reoffending and others), rehabilitation (reforming the offender), and protection of the public.
Key Fact: Prison serves to protect the public and punish serious offenders, but the UK has one of the highest prison populations in Western Europe and reoffending rates are around 46% within one year.
Key Fact: Community orders require offenders to complete requirements in the community: unpaid work, curfew, rehabilitation programmes, or drug treatment. They are cheaper and can be more effective than short prison sentences.
Key Fact: Restorative justice brings offenders and victims together to address the harm caused. It can give victims closure and reduce reoffending, but is not appropriate for all cases.
Key Fact: Youth courts handle cases involving 10-17 year olds. They are less formal than adult courts: no public gallery, no wigs, parents must attend, and reporting restrictions apply.
Key Fact: The probation service supervises offenders serving community sentences and those released from prison on licence, monitoring compliance and providing rehabilitation support.
Key Fact: Victims have rights under the Victims' Code including being informed about their case, making a Victim Personal Statement, and being told about an offender's release.
Key Fact: Anti-social behaviour powers include Community Protection Notices, Criminal Behaviour Orders, and dispersal powers, replacing the previous ASBO regime.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the role of the Crown Prosecution Service?

Q: Explain two purposes of sentencing.

Q: What is restorative justice and what are its advantages?

✅ Answers

  1. The CPS reviews evidence gathered by the police and decides whether there is enough evidence and whether prosecution is in the public interest. It then presents the case in court on behalf of the state. The CPS does not investigate crimes — that is the police's role.
  2. Retribution: the offender deserves to be punished for the harm they have caused, reflecting society's condemnation. Deterrence: the punishment discourages the offender from reoffending (individual deterrence) and discourages others from committing similar crimes (general deterrence).
  3. Restorative justice brings offenders and victims together so offenders can understand the impact of their actions and victims can have a say in how harm is repaired. Advantages include higher victim satisfaction, lower reoffending rates, giving victims closure, and being more cost-effective than prison.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know the roles of different criminal justice agencies: police → CPS → courts → probation/prison
  • When evaluating sentencing, consider all four purposes: punishment, deterrence, rehabilitation, protection
  • Use statistics where possible — e.g. 46% reoffending rate — to support evaluation

📝 Exam Technique

Citizenship Studies Exam Tips:
Use precise legal and political terminology. Always support arguments with specific examples (e.g. named laws, cases, or organisations). For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Active citizenship questions require you to demonstrate practical understanding, not just theoretical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ The police decide whether someone is charged with a crime. ✓ The police investigate and can arrest, but the CPS decides whether to bring criminal charges based on the Full Code Test (evidence + public interest).

✗ Restorative justice means the offender gets a lighter sentence. ✓ Restorative justice is a process that can run alongside the justice system. It does not replace sentencing but can be taken into account by the court.

✗ Juries decide the sentence in criminal cases. ✓ Juries decide the verdict (guilty or not guilty). The judge decides the sentence based on sentencing guidelines and the circumstances of the case.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the effectiveness of the UK justice system in achieving fairness. [12 marks]

The UK justice system has strengths in achieving fairness. Trial by jury allows ordinary citizens to decide verdicts, protecting against state oppression. The rule of law ensures all are equal before the courts, and judicial independence means judges cannot be influenced by government. Legal aid was designed to ensure access to justice regardless of wealth. However, significant fairness issues remain. Legal aid cuts since 2012 have denied many people access to legal representation, particularly in civil cases. This means wealthy individuals and corporations have an advantage. Ethnic minorities are disproportionately represented in the criminal justice system — they are more likely to be stopped, arrested, and receive harsher sentences. The prison population is high but reoffending rates remain around 46%, suggesting the system fails at rehabilitation. Youth justice has improved with a focus on rehabilitation over punishment for young offenders, but concerns remain about the age of criminal responsibility (10 in England and Wales, one of the lowest in Europe). Overall, while the justice system has strong principles of fairness, practical issues around access, disproportionality, and rehabilitation mean it does not always deliver justice equally to all citizens.
