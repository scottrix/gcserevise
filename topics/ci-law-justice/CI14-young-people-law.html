Young People & the Law

How the law affects young people: the age of criminal responsibility, youth courts, young offenders, legal ages for rights and responsibilities, and safeguarding.

Key Fact: The age of criminal responsibility in England and Wales is 10 — one of the lowest in Europe. In Scotland it was raised to 12 in 2021 (from 8). Many argue it should be raised to 14.
Key Fact: Children aged 10-17 are dealt with in youth courts, not adult courts. Youth courts are less formal: no public gallery, judges don't wear wigs, parents must attend, and reporting restrictions protect the young person's identity.
Key Fact: Young offenders can receive: Referral Orders (first offence, attend a youth offender panel), Youth Rehabilitation Orders (community sentence with requirements), or Detention and Training Orders (custodial, from age 12).
Key Fact: ASSET assessments identify the needs of young offenders and the factors contributing to their offending behaviour — such as family problems, education, substance misuse, and mental health.
Key Fact: At 16: can work full-time, join the armed forces with parental consent, consent to medical treatment, and marry with parental consent.
Key Fact: At 17: can drive a car (with a provisional licence), and can be remanded to a Young Offender Institution.
Key Fact: At 18: full adult legal rights and responsibilities apply — can vote, serve on a jury, buy alcohol, and be tried in an adult court.
Key Fact: Stop and search disproportionately affects young people from ethnic minority backgrounds — Black people are around 7 times more likely to be stopped than white people.
Key Fact: Schools have legal duties regarding bullying, exclusion, safeguarding, and the wellbeing of students. The Children Act 1989 and 2004 set out these responsibilities.
Key Fact: Anti-social behaviour impacts young people both as perpetrators and victims. Young people are often perceived as causing ASB but are also frequently victims of it.
Key Fact: Youth offending teams (YOTs) are multi-agency teams that work with young offenders to address the causes of offending and prevent reoffending.
Key Fact: The UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (UNCRC) sets out children's rights, though the UK has not fully incorporated it into domestic law.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the age of criminal responsibility in England and Wales?

Q: How does the youth justice system differ from the adult system?

Q: Evaluate whether the age of criminal responsibility should be raised.

✅ Answers

  1. The age of criminal responsibility in England and Wales is 10. This means children aged 10 and over can be arrested, charged, and prosecuted for criminal offences. Critics argue this is too low compared to other European countries where it is typically 14.
  2. Youth courts are less formal with no public gallery and no wigs. Parents or guardians must attend. Sentences focus on rehabilitation rather than punishment, with options like Referral Orders and Youth Rehabilitation Orders instead of prison for most offences. Reporting restrictions protect the young person's identity.
  3. Arguments for raising: 10-year-olds may not fully understand the consequences of their actions; other European countries set it at 14 or higher; it could prevent criminalising vulnerable children who need support not punishment. Arguments against: serious offences still need consequences; victims deserve justice; a lower age allows early intervention through the youth justice system.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always specify which part of the UK you are referring to — ages differ between England, Scotland, and NI
  • When evaluating the youth justice system, balance welfare (rehabilitation) vs justice (accountability)
  • Use specific examples of youth sentences — Referral Orders, YROs — not just general statements

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Children under 18 cannot be sent to prison. ✓ Young people aged 12-17 can receive custodial sentences served in Young Offender Institutions, though this is a last resort for serious or persistent offending.

✗ The age of criminal responsibility is the same across the UK. ✓ It is 10 in England and Wales, 12 in Scotland (raised from 8 in 2021), and 10 in Northern Ireland.

✗ Youth courts are exactly the same as adult courts. ✓ Youth courts are less formal: no public gallery, judges don't wear wigs, parents must attend, and reporting restrictions protect the young person's identity.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether the youth justice system in England and Wales effectively balances rehabilitation and punishment. [12 marks]

The youth justice system attempts to prioritise rehabilitation over punishment, reflecting that young people may not fully understand the consequences of their actions. Youth courts use less formal procedures, and sentences such as Referral Orders and Youth Rehabilitation Orders focus on addressing the causes of offending rather than simply punishing. Multi-agency youth offending teams work with families and communities to prevent reoffending. This approach has had some success. Youth offending rates have fallen significantly since 2007, suggesting rehabilitation-focused interventions can work. Referral Orders have relatively high completion rates and victim satisfaction. However, the system is not wholly rehabilitative. The age of criminal responsibility at 10 is one of the lowest in Europe, meaning very young children can be drawn into the criminal justice system. Custodial sentences are still available and used for serious offences, and conditions in Young Offender Institutions have been criticised. Reoffending rates remain high at around 40% within 12 months. Overall, while the youth justice system has a stronger rehabilitative focus than the adult system, the persistence of a low criminal responsibility age and high reoffending rates suggest it does not yet fully achieve the balance it aims for.

Detailed Notes

Understanding Young People & the Law in British Context

Young People & the Law is a core topic in GCSE Citizenship Studies that requires understanding of how the UK political and legal systems work, and how citizens can participate effectively. The specification requires you to know how British democracy functions, understand your rights and responsibilities as a citizen, and be able to take informed and responsible action on citizenship issues.

When writing about young people & the law in GCSE exams, always refer to specific UK examples: the UK Parliament at Westminster, the devolved legislatures (Scottish Parliament, Welsh Senedd, Northern Ireland Assembly), the court system (magistrates’ courts, Crown Court, High Court, Supreme Court), and real examples of active citizenship campaigns. Examiners reward answers that show understanding of how systems work in practice, not just textbook descriptions.

British Values underpin GCSE Citizenship: democracy, the rule of law, individual liberty, and mutual respect and tolerance of different faiths and beliefs. Understanding young people & the law requires connecting these values to the specific systems and processes you are studying.

When discussing young people & the law, a strong GCSE answer would: define the concept precisely, explain how it operates in the UK using a specific example, evaluate its effectiveness, and consider how citizens can engage with it through active citizenship.

Young People & the Law and UK Democratic Processes

The UK is a parliamentary democracy where citizens exercise power through elected representatives. Understanding young people & the law requires knowledge of how democratic processes work: general elections (first past the post), devolved elections (additional member system for Scotland and Wales), local elections, and referendums. The UK does not have a written constitution — instead, constitutional principles are drawn from statute law, common law, conventions and authoritative works.

The UK Parliament consists of the House of Commons (650 elected MPs), the House of Lords (appointed and hereditary members), and the monarch. A Bill becomes an Act of Parliament after passing through both Houses and receiving Royal Assent. Understanding how laws are made is essential context for young people & the law, as it determines how citizens can influence the democratic process.

Active citizenship means participating in democratic processes beyond just voting: contacting your MP, joining pressure groups, participating in consultations, organising campaigns, and using legal rights such as freedom of information requests. GCSE Citizenship requires evidence of active citizenship involvement.

GCSE Example: Young People & the Law and UK Democratic Processes

A group of GCSE Citizenship students concerned about young people & the law could organise a campaign: research the issue, write to their MP, create a petition on the parliament.uk website, arrange a meeting with local councillors, and document the entire process as evidence of active citizenship.

Rights, Responsibilities and Young People & the Law

UK citizens have both rights and responsibilities. Key rights include: the right to vote, freedom of speech, freedom of religion, the right to a fair trial, and the right to privacy. These rights are protected by the Human Rights Act 1998, which incorporates the European Convention on Human Rights into UK law. The Supreme Court is the highest court in the UK and can declare government actions incompatible with human rights.

Key responsibilities include: obeying the law, paying taxes, serving on a jury if called, and respecting the rights of others. Citizenship is not just about rights — it requires active participation and contribution to the community. The balance between rights and responsibilities is a key theme in GCSE Citizenship.

When evaluating young people & the law, consider different perspectives: how might different groups in society be affected differently? What are the competing rights and responsibilities? How do democratic processes ensure that different views are heard and balanced?

GCSE Example: Rights, Responsibilities and Young People & the Law

When discussing rights and responsibilities related to young people & the law, a balanced answer would consider: what rights are at stake, who holds them, what responsibilities accompany those rights, and how the UK legal and political systems protect and enforce them.
