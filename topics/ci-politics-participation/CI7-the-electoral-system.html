The Electoral System & Voting

How elections work: voting systems, the franchise, different types of elections, and debates about electoral reform.

Key Fact: First Past the Post (FPTP): used for UK general elections. The country is divided into 650 constituencies; each elects one MP by simple plurality (most votes wins). Strengths: simple to understand, produces strong majority governments, clear MP-constituency link. Weaknesses: disproportional (winning party often gets a majority of seats with less than 50% of votes), wasted votes in safe seats, smaller parties under-represented.
Key Fact: Proportional representation (PR): party seats match their vote share. Used in many European countries. The Additional Member System (AMS) is used for Scottish Parliament and Welsh Senedd elections. The Single Transferable Vote (STV) is used in Northern Ireland. PR is fairer but often produces coalition governments.
Key Fact: The franchise (right to vote): expanded over centuries: 1832 (property-owning men), 1867 (urban working men), 1918 (all men over 21, women over 30), 1928 (equal suffrage at 21), 1969 (voting age reduced to 18), 2015 (16 for Scottish independence referendum). Debates: should the voting age be lowered to 16 for all UK elections?
Key Fact: Types of elections: general elections (MPs, FPTP), devolved elections (MSPs/MSs/MLAs, AMS/STV), local elections (councillors, FPTP), European elections (closed list PR, now irrelevant post-Brexit), Police and Crime Commissioner elections (supplementary vote), and by-elections.
Key Fact: Electoral reform: the 2011 AV referendum was rejected (68% against). Arguments for PR: fairer, more representative, every vote counts. Arguments against: FPTP produces stable governments, PR produces weak coalitions, and FPTP gives a clear MP-constituency link. The Electoral Reform Society campaigns for PR.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • FPTP = simple, strong government, UNFAIR. PR = fair, representative, potentially UNSTABLE.
  • Franchise expanded: property men -> all men -> women -> age 18 -> age 16 (Scotland). Each step was contested.
  • 2019 result shows FPTP distortion: 44% votes -> 56% seats. PR would be fairer but may reduce stability.

📝 Exam Technique

Citizenship Studies Exam Tips:
Use precise legal and political terminology. Always support arguments with specific examples (e.g. named laws, cases, or organisations). For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Active citizenship questions require you to demonstrate practical understanding, not just theoretical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ First Past the Post is the fairest system because the candidate with the most votes wins. ✓ While FPTP seems fair at constituency level (most votes wins), it is unfair nationally: a party can win a majority of seats with well under 50% of votes, and millions of votes for losing candidates have no effect on the result. In 2019, it took an average of 38,000 votes to elect one Conservative MP but over 300,000 to elect one Liberal Democrat MP. The system systematically disadvantages smaller parties and over-rewards larger ones.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether the UK should replace First Past the Post with proportional representation for general elections.

A grade 9 response will: describe both systems; present FPTP strengths (simplicity, strong government, constituency link) and weaknesses (disproportionality, wasted votes, small party disadvantage); present PR strengths (fairness, representation) and weaknesses (coalition instability, weak government, broken constituency link); evaluate using 2019 election data and international comparisons; conclude: PR would be fairer and more representative, but FPTP's stability advantage is valued. A hybrid system like AMS might achieve the best balance of fairness, stability and accountability.
