Equality & Diversity

Equality and diversity in the UK: the Equality Act 2010, protected characteristics, direct and indirect discrimination, institutional racism, and multiculturalism.

Key Fact: The Equality Act 2010 protects people from discrimination based on 9 protected characteristics: age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage/civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation.
Key Fact: Direct discrimination: treating someone less favourably because of a protected characteristic, e.g. refusing to hire someone because of their race.
Key Fact: Indirect discrimination: a policy that applies to everyone but disproportionately disadvantages a particular group, e.g. a height requirement that excludes more women than men.
Key Fact: The Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) enforces equality law, provides guidance, and can investigate systemic discrimination.
Key Fact: Positive action allows employers to take steps to encourage underrepresented groups to apply for jobs or promotions, but positive discrimination (quotas) is generally unlawful in the UK.
Key Fact: The Public Sector Equality Duty requires public bodies to have due regard to the need to eliminate discrimination, advance equality, and foster good relations between groups.
Key Fact: Despite legal protections, discrimination persists: the gender pay gap is around 7.7% (2023), ethnicity pay gaps exist, and hate crimes have increased in recent years.
Key Fact: The Macpherson Report (1999) found the Metropolitan Police was institutionally racist — defined as collective failure to provide appropriate service due to prejudice. The concept applies to other institutions too.
Key Fact: Multiculturalism promotes the coexistence of different cultural identities within a society. The UK is one of the most diverse countries in Europe, particularly in major cities.
Key Fact: Diversity benefits society by bringing different perspectives, fostering innovation, reducing prejudice through contact, and enriching culture. Research shows diverse teams make better decisions.
Key Fact: Citizens have a responsibility to challenge discrimination and promote inclusion — this is part of active citizenship and the British values of mutual respect and tolerance.
Key Fact: The Windrush scandal (2018) showed how legal status alone did not protect people from discrimination — members of the Windrush generation were wrongly detained, denied rights, and some deported.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What are the 9 protected characteristics in the Equality Act 2010?

Q: Explain the difference between direct and indirect discrimination.

Q: Evaluate the effectiveness of the Equality Act 2010 in preventing discrimination.

✅ Answers

  1. Age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, and sexual orientation.
  2. Direct discrimination is deliberately treating someone less favourably because of a protected characteristic, e.g. refusing to hire someone because of their race. Indirect discrimination is a policy that applies to everyone but disproportionately disadvantages a particular group, e.g. a height requirement that excludes more women than men.
  3. The Act provides comprehensive legal protection across 9 characteristics and gives individuals legal recourse. However, discrimination persists — pay gaps remain, hate crime has increased, and the burden of proving discrimination falls on the victim. Legal protection is necessary but not sufficient to achieve equality.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use correct legal terms — 'protected characteristics' not 'minority groups'
  • When evaluating equality law, consider both the law itself and its practical enforcement
  • Statistics on pay gaps and hate crime provide strong evidence for evaluation

📝 Exam Technique

Citizenship Studies Exam Tips:
Use precise legal and political terminology. Always support arguments with specific examples (e.g. named laws, cases, or organisations). For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Active citizenship questions require you to demonstrate practical understanding, not just theoretical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ The Equality Act means everyone must be treated exactly the same. ✓ The Equality Act protects against discrimination. Sometimes treating people differently (e.g. reasonable adjustments for disability) is necessary to achieve equality of opportunity.

✗ Discrimination is no longer a problem in the UK. ✓ Despite legal protections, discrimination persists — the gender pay gap is around 7.7%, ethnicity pay gaps exist, and hate crimes have increased in recent years.

✗ Positive action is the same as positive discrimination. ✓ Positive action allows measures to encourage underrepresented groups to apply; positive discrimination (quotas) is generally unlawful in the UK.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that laws alone cannot create an equal society. [12 marks]

Laws such as the Equality Act 2010 are essential but insufficient for an equal society. Legal protection gives individuals recourse against discrimination and signals that discrimination is unacceptable. The Act covers 9 protected characteristics comprehensively. However, laws alone cannot change deep-rooted attitudes. Despite legal protections, the gender pay gap persists at 7.7%, ethnic minorities face hiring discrimination (shown in CV studies), and hate crime has increased. The Windrush scandal showed legal status alone did not protect people from institutional discrimination. Enforcement is also a challenge. The burden of proving discrimination falls on the individual, who may lack resources. Legal aid cuts have made this harder. The EHRC has limited resources to investigate systemic discrimination. Education, cultural change, and economic policies are needed alongside laws. Schools teaching diversity, employers auditing pay gaps, and media challenging stereotypes all contribute. Laws set the framework but achieving equality requires sustained action across all areas of life. In conclusion, while laws are a necessary foundation, they are not sufficient. Legal change must be accompanied by cultural, educational, and economic change to address root causes of inequality.
