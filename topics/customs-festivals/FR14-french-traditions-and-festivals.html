⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often use the present tense instead of the perfect for describing past festivals. Wrong: Pour le 14 juillet, on danse dans les rues (present — implies habit). Correct: Pour le 14 juillet, on a dansé dans les rues (perfect — specific past event). Students also confuse quand (when) with quant (how much/many).

Le 14 Juillet et les Fêtes Nationales

The most important French national celebration is le 14 juillet (Bastille Day), commemorating the storming of the Bastille prison in 1789, which launched the French Revolution. The day begins with le défilé militaire (military parade) on les Champs-Élysées in Paris — the oldest and largest regular military parade in the world. The President reviews the troops, and military aircraft fly over, trailing le tricolore (blue, white and red smoke). In the evening, le bal du 14 juillet (dance) is held in every town, followed by le feu d'artifice (fireworks).

Other key French traditions: la Toussaint (1 November — All Saints' Day), when families visit cemeteries and lay des chrysanthèmes (chrysanthemums) on graves. Noël (Christmas) features le réveillon (Christmas Eve feast), with la bûche de Noël (Yule log cake) and le foie gras. In many French families, le Père Noël brings gifts on the night of 24 December, and children place leurs souliers (shoes) by the fireplace rather than hanging stockings.

Use the perfect tense to describe festival experiences: L'année dernière, j'ai vu le défilé du 14 juillet à Paris — c'était incroyable (Last year, I saw the 14 July parade in Paris — it was incredible). Use the imperfect for descriptions: Il y avait des gens partout, les rues étaient décorées en bleu, blanc, rouge (There were people everywhere, the streets were decorated in blue, white, red).

Exemple

Le 14 juillet, il y a un défilé militaire sur les Champs-Élysées à Paris. Le soir, on danse au bal populaire et on regarde le feu d'artifice. C'est la fête nationale la plus importante! — On 14 July, there is a military parade on the Champs-Élysées in Paris. In the evening, people dance at the public ball and watch the fireworks. It's the most important national celebration!

Les Fêtes et Traditions Saisonnières

L'Épiphanie (6 January) celebrates the Three Kings with la galette des rois (King's cake) — a puff pastry pie filled with la frangipane (almond cream). A small figurine (la fève) is hidden inside; whoever finds it becomes le roi or la reine (king or queen) and wears la couronne (crown). This tradition is beloved across France and lasts throughout January.

La Chandeleur (2 February — Candlemas) is the day of les crêpes. Families make crêpes, and there's a tradition of flipping a crêpe with your left hand while holding a gold coin in your right — if you succeed, you'll have prosperity all year! Pâques (Easter) features les cloches de Pâques (Easter bells) — in French tradition, church bells fly to Rome on Good Friday and return on Easter Sunday dropping chocolate eggs for children. This differs from the British Easter Bunny tradition.

Le 1er mai (May Day) is a public holiday. People offer du muguet (lily of the valley) to loved ones for good luck. This tradition dates from 1561 when King Charles IX received a sprig of muguet and decided to give one to every lady at court each year. La Fête de la Musique (21 June) fills every street with free music on the first day of summer.

Exemple

Pour l'Épiphanie, on mange la galette des rois. Celui qui trouve la fève devient le roi ou la reine et porte la couronne. Pour la Chandeleur, on fait des crêpes — c'est délicieux! — For Epiphany, we eat the King's cake. Whoever finds the fève becomes king or queen and wears the crown. For Candlemas, we make crêpes — it's delicious!

Les Traditions Francophones

Francophone traditions extend far beyond France. In Sénégal, la Tabaski (Eid al-Adha) is one of the biggest celebrations — families sacrifice a sheep and share the meat with the community. Le Magal de Touba is a massive annual pilgrimage attracting millions to the holy city of Touba. La fête de l'Indépendance (4 April) celebrates independence from France in 1960.

In Québec, la Fête nationale du Québec (24 June — Saint-Jean-Baptiste Day) is the province's biggest celebration. Concerts, bonfires (les feux de joie), and parades take place across Québec. Le Carnaval de Québec (January–February) is the world's largest winter carnival, with ice sculptures, le palais de glace (ice palace), and Bonhomme Carnaval — the carnival's snowman mascot. In Belgium, le Carnaval de Binche is a UNESCO Masterpiece of Oral and Intangible Heritage, featuring les Gilles — masked performers in elaborate costumes who throw oranges.

Comparing traditions across francophone countries shows cultural depth: Tandis qu'en France, le 14 juillet célèbre la Révolution, au Québec, la Fête nationale le 24 juin célèbre la culture québécoise. En Belgique, le Carnaval de Binche est classé par l'UNESCO. Use tandis que, cependant, and en revanche to contrast.

Exemple

Au Québec, la Fête nationale est le 24 juin — on célèbre la culture québécoise avec des concerts et des feux de joie. En Belgique, le Carnaval de Binche avec les Gilles est classé par l'UNESCO. — In Québec, the national holiday is 24 June — they celebrate Québécois culture with concerts and bonfires. In Belgium, the Carnival of Binche with the Gilles is classified by UNESCO.
