Analysing Dance Performance

How to analyse live or recorded dance effectively

Key Fact: Analysing dance performance involves watching closely and breaking down what you observe into component parts
Key Fact: Live performance offers a full sensory experience — you feel the energy, scale and atmosphere of the work
Key Fact: Recorded performance allows repeated viewing, enabling closer analysis of specific moments and details
Key Fact: A structured approach uses the four content categories: action, dynamics, space and relationships
Key Fact: When analysing, always consider the choreographic intention as the central reference point
Key Fact: Effective note-taking during or after viewing helps capture observations before they are forgotten
Key Fact: Analysis should consider design elements: costume, lighting, set and aural setting alongside movement
Key Fact: The AO4 framework requires analysis to explain how and why choreographic choices create meaning
Key Fact: Comparing sections of a dance reveals structure, contrast and development over time
Key Fact: Analysis of professional works must reference specific choreographic features and their purpose
Key Fact: When watching live, focus on the whole stage first, then zoom in on specific dancers or moments
Key Fact: AQA exam questions often ask you to analyse a specific moment, section or feature of a work

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Analysing Dance Performance, you must know:

  • Live performance: A dance performed in real time in front of an audience, offering full sensory and atmospheric experience
  • Recorded performance: A dance captured on video, allowing repeated viewing and detailed analysis of specific moments
  • Observation: The act of carefully watching and noting what happens in a dance performance as the basis for analysis
  • Structured analysis: A systematic approach to breaking down a dance using frameworks such as the four content categories
  • Design elements: The visual and aural components of a dance: costume, lighting, set/staging and aural setting
  • Aural setting: The sound world of the dance including music, silence, spoken word or environmental sounds

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the advantage of watching a live performance over a recorded one?

Q: Why is repeated viewing useful when analysing a recorded dance?

Q: How do the four content categories help structure an analysis?

Q: Why should analysis always reference the choreographic intention?

Q: Name two design elements you should consider alongside movement when analysing a dance.

✅ Answers

  1. Live performance provides the full sensory experience — you feel the energy, scale and atmosphere in real time, and the audience-performer relationship is immediate and tangible.
  2. Repeated viewing lets you focus on different elements each time — first the overall structure, then specific movement details, design features or dancer relationships.
  3. They provide a systematic framework so you cover all aspects: action (what), dynamics (how), space (where) and relationships (who), ensuring nothing is missed.
  4. Every choreographic choice serves the intention — linking your analysis to it shows you understand why decisions were made, not just what they are.
  5. Costume and lighting — though set/staging and aural setting are also valid design elements that contribute to meaning.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When analysing a recorded work, watch it at least twice — once for overall impression, once for specific detail
  • Structure your analysis using the four content categories to ensure a thorough response
  • Always link what you observe to the choreographic intention — explain how features serve the purpose
  • Include design elements in your analysis when relevant — costume, lighting and sound contribute to meaning
  • In exam answers, use specific examples from the work rather than vague generalisations

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ You can only analyse live performances effectively. ✓ Both live and recorded performances can be analysed effectively — recorded performance offers the advantage of repeated viewing for closer detail.

✗ Analysis means just listing what happens in the dance. ✓ Listing is description; analysis means explaining how and why choreographic choices work to create meaning and effect.

✗ Design elements are not important in dance analysis. ✓ Costume, lighting, set and aural setting are integral to the work and contribute to meaning — they must be included in a thorough analysis.

✗ You only need to watch a dance once to analyse it. ✓ Effective analysis usually requires multiple viewings — first for overall structure, then for specific details, relationships and design elements.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how you would approach analysing a professional dance work for the AQA 8236 written exam. Describe your method and give examples. [6 marks]

When analysing a professional dance work, I use a structured method that ensures thorough coverage. First, I watch the entire work to understand its overall structure, mood and choreographic intention. For example, watching 'Within Her Eyes' I note it explores love and loss, with a narrative structure. Second, I watch again focusing on specific elements using the four content categories. In action content, I note the use of gesture and contact between the two dancers; in dynamic content, I observe the contrast between sustained, intimate lifts and sudden, sharp separations; in spatial content, I note the intimate use of personal space; in relationship content, I identify the dependent, trust-based partnership. Third, I consider design elements: the costume of everyday clothes makes the dancers relatable, and the aural setting of spoken text adds emotional depth. Throughout, I link each observation to the choreographic intention — for example, the sustained lifts and close contact communicate love and trust, while the sudden separations suggest loss. This systematic approach ensures my analysis is detailed, well-supported and directly addresses the AQA assessment objectives.
