Dance Terminology & Action Words

Action, dynamic, spatial and relationship content vocabulary

Key Fact: Dance terminology is the specialist vocabulary used to describe, analyse and evaluate movement accurately
Key Fact: Action content refers to what the body does: travel, turn, jump, gesture, stillness, transfer of weight
Key Fact: Dynamic content describes how the body moves: fast/slow, sudden/sustained, strong/light, direct/indirect
Key Fact: Spatial content describes where the body moves: levels, directions, pathways, formations, personal and general space
Key Fact: Relationship content describes who the body moves with: unison, canon, contact, contrast, complement, leader-follower
Key Fact: Accurate use of terminology is essential for both written exam answers and NEA written tasks
Key Fact: AQA expects precise vocabulary — words like 'fast' should be refined to 'sudden' or 'quick' depending on context
Key Fact: Action words are the foundation: every movement can be categorised as one of the six action content types
Key Fact: Dynamic words add quality and character to movement descriptions, distinguishing between efforts and energies
Key Fact: Spatial words locate movement in the performance space and describe the dancer's relationship to it
Key Fact: Relationship words describe interactions between dancers and are key to analysing group choreography
Key Fact: Using the four content categories ensures your appreciation is thorough and covers all aspects of the dance

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Dance Terminology & Action Words, you must know:

  • Action content: What the body does in dance: travel, turn, jump, gesture, stillness, transfer of weight
  • Dynamic content: How the body moves: qualities of speed, energy, weight and flow such as sudden, sustained, strong or light
  • Spatial content: Where the body moves: levels, directions, pathways, formations and use of personal and general space
  • Relationship content: Who the body moves with: interactions such as unison, canon, contact, contrast and complement
  • Transfer of weight: Shifting the body's weight from one foot or body part to another, a fundamental action in dance
  • Effort actions: Movement qualities derived from Laban's effort framework combining weight, time and space to describe dynamics

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Name the six types of action content.

Q: How does dynamic content differ from action content?

Q: Give an example of spatial content in a dance.

Q: Name three types of relationship content.

Q: Why is it important to use all four content categories when analysing a dance?

✅ Answers

  1. Travel, turn, jump, gesture, stillness and transfer of weight.
  2. Action content describes what the body does (e.g. jump); dynamic content describes how it is performed (e.g. sudden, strong jump versus sustained, light rise).
  3. A dancer moving in a circular pathway at a high level across the general space, using diagonal direction towards downstage left.
  4. Unison, canon and contact — though contrast, complement and leader-follower are also valid.
  5. Using all four categories ensures you cover every aspect of the movement — action, dynamics, space and relationships — giving a thorough and complete analysis.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use the four content categories (action, dynamic, spatial, relationships) as a checklist in appreciation answers
  • Upgrade everyday words to dance terminology — say 'sudden' not 'fast', 'sustained' not 'slow'
  • When describing movement, state the action first, then add dynamics and spatial information for precision
  • Relationship content is often forgotten — always check if dancers interact and name the relationship type
  • In NEA written tasks, use terminology consistently to show confident subject knowledge

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Dance Exam Tips:
Use precise classical terminology in all answers. When analysing sources, always consider provenance (who created it, when, and why). For literary questions, reference specific episodes and book numbers from set texts. For thematic questions, compare Greek and Roman approaches where relevant. For 'how far' questions, present a balanced argument with a clear judgement. AQA 8236 rewards answers that demonstrate understanding of the classical world through use of specific evidence from both literary and visual/material sources.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Action content only means big movements like jumps. ✓ Action content includes all six types: travel, turn, jump, gesture, stillness and transfer of weight — gesture and stillness are equally valid actions.

✗ Dynamic content just means fast or slow. ✓ Dynamic content covers speed, energy, weight and flow — it includes sudden/sustained, strong/light, direct/indirect and bound/free qualities.

✗ Spatial content is only about where the dancer stands. ✓ Spatial content includes levels, directions, pathways, formations, personal space and general space — it describes all spatial aspects of movement.

✗ Relationship content only applies to duets. ✓ Relationship content applies to any number of dancers — it includes unison, canon, contrast and complement in solos with implied relationships, duets and groups.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Using the four content categories, analyse a moment of dance where two dancers run towards each other, leap and land in a lift. [6 marks]

In action content, both dancers travel across the space, jump into a leap and use transfer of weight as they land into the lift, where one dancer supports the other. In dynamic content, the running is quick and sustained, building momentum; the leap is sudden and strong, requiring explosive energy; the landing into the lift is sustained and controlled, showing strength and careful weight management. In spatial content, the dancers travel along direct pathways towards each other, the leap rises to a high level and the lift extends vertically, making the spatial design dramatic and expansive. In relationship content, the dancers move in complementary pathways that converge, the lift requires trust and physical contact, and their timing must be coordinated — the supported dancer depends on the base dancer for safety and balance. Together, the four content categories show how the choreographer creates a moment of powerful connection and trust, using speed, height and physical contact to communicate partnership and reliance.
