Evaluating Dance Works

Making judgements about effectiveness and quality of dance works

Key Fact: Evaluation means making informed judgements about how effectively a dance communicates its intention
Key Fact: Effective evaluation requires clear criteria — such as how well movement, structure and design serve the intention
Key Fact: Evaluation builds on description and analysis — you must first understand what happens and how it works
Key Fact: A judgement must always be supported with specific evidence from the dance
Key Fact: You can evaluate a dance as highly effective, partially effective or ineffective, but must explain why
Key Fact: Evaluation considers whether the choreographic choices successfully communicate meaning to the audience
Key Fact: When evaluating, consider the whole work as well as specific moments or sections
Key Fact: The effectiveness of a dance depends on how well action, dynamic, spatial and relationship content work together
Key Fact: Design elements — costume, lighting, set and aural setting — also contribute to overall effectiveness
Key Fact: AQA marks evaluation that shows independent thinking and personal response supported by evidence
Key Fact: Evaluating your own choreography requires honesty about strengths and weaknesses with specific examples
Key Fact: Strong evaluation uses comparative language: 'more effective because…', 'less successful as…'

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Evaluating Dance Works, you must know:

  • Evaluation: Making an informed judgement about the effectiveness and quality of a dance work, supported by specific evidence
  • Effectiveness: How well a dance communicates its choreographic intention to the audience through its creative choices
  • Criteria: The standards or measures used to judge a dance, such as clarity of intention, cohesion or audience impact
  • Informed judgement: A reasoned opinion based on knowledge, analysis and evidence rather than personal preference alone
  • Cohesion: How well the elements of a dance work together as a unified whole to support the choreographic intention
  • Audience impact: The effect a dance has on its viewers, including emotional, intellectual and aesthetic responses

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the difference between analysis and evaluation?

Q: Why must evaluation be supported by evidence?

Q: Give two criteria you could use to evaluate a dance work.

Q: How would you evaluate a section of a dance as partially effective?

Q: Why is it important to evaluate your own choreography honestly?

✅ Answers

  1. Analysis explains how and why choreographic choices work; evaluation judges how effective they are — analysis is about understanding, evaluation is about judging success.
  2. Evidence grounds your judgement in fact; without it, evaluation is mere opinion and gains no marks in the AQA exam.
  3. Clarity of intention (how clearly the purpose is communicated) and cohesion (how well the elements work together as a unified whole).
  4. State what works well with evidence, then explain what is less successful with evidence — for example, 'the lift effectively shows trust but the transition into it is unclear, weakening the overall impact.'
  5. Honest evaluation identifies genuine strengths and areas for improvement, showing the examiner mature reflective practice rather than uncritical self-praise.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always start evaluation by stating the choreographic intention — then judge how well it is achieved
  • Use phrases like 'highly effective because…' or 'less successful as…' to show clear evaluative judgement
  • Support every judgement with at least one specific example from the dance
  • In self-evaluation, balance strengths and weaknesses — examiners value reflective honesty
  • Remember: evaluation is the highest-level skill — it demonstrates mature, independent critical thinking

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Dance Exam Tips — Evaluating Dance Works:

4. Reference relevant cases and statutes when discussing Evaluating Dance Works
5. Consider reform proposals and alternative approaches where relevant

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Evaluation means saying whether you liked the dance. ✓ Evaluation is an informed judgement based on evidence and criteria, not personal preference — liking or disliking is not evaluation.

✗ You can evaluate a dance without analysing it first. ✓ Evaluation builds on analysis — you must understand how the dance works before you can judge how effective it is.

✗ Every dance must be evaluated as effective. ✓ Evaluation can judge a dance as effective, partially effective or ineffective — the key is justifying your judgement with evidence.

✗ Self-evaluation should focus only on what went well. ✓ Honest self-evaluation identifies both strengths and weaknesses — examiners reward reflective, balanced judgement over uncritical praise.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how to evaluate a dance work effectively. Describe the process and give an example of evaluating a specific choreographic feature. [6 marks]

Effective evaluation of a dance work follows a clear process: first, identify the choreographic intention as the basis for judgement; second, analyse how the choreographic features serve that intention; third, make an informed judgement supported by specific evidence. For example, when evaluating the use of unison in a dance about community unity, I would first establish the intention is to show solidarity. I would then analyse how the unison works — all dancers performing the same bold, sustained arm movements together creates a powerful visual image of togetherness. My evaluation would be that the unison is highly effective because it directly and clearly communicates the intention of unity to the audience; the shared timing and matching dynamics make the group appear as one body, which powerfully reinforces the idea of solidarity. However, if the unison was occasionally imperfect, I might evaluate it as partially effective — the intention is clear when the unison is clean, but moments of misalignment weaken the image of unity. In both cases, the judgement is grounded in specific evidence and linked to the choreographic intention.
