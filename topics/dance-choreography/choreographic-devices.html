Choreographic Devices

Repetition, unison, canon, accumulation, retrograde and other devices

Key Fact: Choreographic devices are tools used to develop and structure movement material
Key Fact: Repetition involves repeating a movement or phrase to create emphasis or recognition
Key Fact: Unison means all dancers performing the same movement at the same time
Key Fact: Canon is when dancers perform the same movement one after another in sequence
Key Fact: Accumulation builds a phrase by adding one movement at a time, retaining earlier movements
Key Fact: Retrograde means performing a movement phrase in reverse order, like rewinding a video
Key Fact: Fragmentation breaks a phrase into smaller parts which can be rearranged
Key Fact: Development uses devices to transform original material so it remains connected but varied
Key Fact: Choreographers choose devices to support their intention and communicate meaning
Key Fact: Different devices create different effects — unison shows unity, canon shows ripple or wave
Key Fact: In AQA 8236, you must use choreographic devices in your NEA choreography and identify them in the written exam
Key Fact: Professional choreographers use devices to maintain audience interest and create variety

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Choreographic Devices, you must know:

  • Choreographic device: A tool or method used to develop, manipulate and structure movement material
  • Repetition: Repeating a movement or phrase to create emphasis, pattern or recognition
  • Unison: Two or more dancers performing the same movement at exactly the same time
  • Canon: Dancers performing the same movement phrase starting at different times, like a round in music
  • Accumulation: A device where movements are added one at a time so the phrase gradually grows longer
  • Retrograde: Performing a movement phrase in reverse order, as if rewinding the movement

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the difference between unison and canon?

Q: Describe how accumulation works as a choreographic device.

Q: Why might a choreographer use retrograde in a dance?

Q: How does repetition help communicate choreographic intention?

Q: Explain how fragmentation can be used to develop a dance phrase.

✅ Answers

  1. In unison, all dancers move at the same time; in canon, dancers start the same phrase at different times in sequence.
  2. Accumulation starts with one movement, then adds a second so both are performed, then a third, and so on — the phrase gradually grows longer with each repetition.
  3. Retrograde creates a striking visual effect, like rewinding time. It can symbolise memory, returning to the past, or give a phrase a surprising twist that maintains audience interest.
  4. Repetition draws attention to key movements, making them memorable and emphasising their importance to the audience, reinforcing the meaning the choreographer wants to convey.
  5. Fragmentation breaks an existing phrase into smaller parts — these fragments can be rearranged, performed in a different order, or isolated to create new material while keeping a link to the original.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always name the specific device — do not just describe what happens
  • Link each device to its effect on the audience and the choreographic intention
  • In NEA written tasks, identify the device and explain why you chose it
  • For professional works, identify devices used and explain how they contribute to the piece
  • Remember: devices develop material, they do not replace it — the original movement is always the foundation

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Dance Exam Tips:
Use precise classical terminology in all answers. When analysing sources, always consider provenance (who created it, when, and why). For literary questions, reference specific episodes and book numbers from set texts. For thematic questions, compare Greek and Roman approaches where relevant. For 'how far' questions, present a balanced argument with a clear judgement. AQA 8236 rewards answers that demonstrate understanding of the classical world through use of specific evidence from both literary and visual/material sources.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Canon means dancers perform different movements at the same time. ✓ Canon means dancers perform the same movement phrase but start at different times in sequence.

✗ Repetition is just doing the same thing over and over with no purpose. ✓ Repetition is a deliberate device used to create emphasis, pattern, recognition or build intensity.

✗ Accumulation and canon are the same thing. ✓ Accumulation gradually adds movements to build a longer phrase; canon staggers the timing of the same phrase across dancers.

✗ Retrograde means performing a movement very slowly. ✓ Retrograde means performing the movement phrase in reverse order — the last movement becomes the first.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe two choreographic devices and explain how each contributes to the development of a dance. Give a specific example for each. [6 marks]

Canon is a choreographic device where dancers perform the same movement phrase starting at different times, creating a wave-like or ripple effect. For example, in a group of four, each dancer begins the phrase two counts after the previous one. This creates visual interest and can suggest the spreading of an idea or emotion through a group, as seen in works like 'Swansong' by Christopher Bruce where canon is used to show the prisoners responding to one another. Accumulation is a device where a phrase is built up by adding one movement at a time. For example, movement A is performed, then A + B, then A + B + C, and so on. This creates a sense of growing complexity and momentum. It can communicate building tension or the layering of experiences. Trisha Brown famously used accumulation in her work 'Accumulation', where a simple gesture is gradually expanded. Both devices transform original material to create variety and maintain audience interest while staying connected to the choreographic intention.
