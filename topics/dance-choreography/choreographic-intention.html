Choreographic Intention & Communication

How choreographers communicate meaning, mood and intention

Key Fact: Choreographic intention is the purpose or message behind a dance — what the choreographer wants to communicate
Key Fact: Every choreographic decision should serve the intention, from movement choices to structure and staging
Key Fact: Mood is the atmosphere or feeling a dance creates — for example, tense, joyful, melancholic
Key Fact: Meaning is the deeper message or idea the dance conveys to the audience
Key Fact: Motifs are key movement phrases that represent the core idea of the dance
Key Fact: Developing and varying motifs throughout a dance reinforces the intention
Key Fact: Dynamics, space and relationships all contribute to communicating intention
Key Fact: The audience's interpretation may differ from the choreographer's intention — this is valid
Key Fact: In AQA 8236, you must state and explain your choreographic intention clearly
Key Fact: Professional choreographers often explain their intention in programme notes or interviews
Key Fact: Choreographic intention should be identifiable from the movement itself, not just the title
Key Fact: Evaluating how effectively a dance communicates its intention is a key exam skill

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Choreographic Intention & Communication, you must know:

  • Choreographic intention: The purpose, message or meaning that the choreographer aims to communicate through the dance
  • Motif: A key movement phrase that represents the core idea or theme of the dance
  • Mood: The atmosphere or emotional quality created in a dance, such as tense, joyful or eerie
  • Meaning: The deeper message, idea or concept that a dance conveys to the audience
  • Communication: The process of conveying intention to the audience through movement, structure and design
  • Interpretation: How the audience understands or makes sense of a dance, which may differ from the choreographer's intention

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is choreographic intention?

Q: How do motifs help communicate choreographic intention?

Q: Why might an audience interpret a dance differently from the choreographer's intention?

Q: How do dynamics contribute to communicating mood in a dance?

Q: Explain why a choreographer must ensure the intention is clear from the movement itself.

✅ Answers

  1. Choreographic intention is the purpose or message the choreographer wants to communicate through the dance — it guides every creative decision.
  2. Motifs are key movement phrases that embody the core idea. By developing and varying motifs throughout the dance, the choreographer reinforces the intention so the audience recognises and understands it.
  3. Each audience member brings their own experiences and perspectives, so they may understand the movement in a different way. Multiple interpretations are valid and enrich the dance.
  4. Dynamics such as sudden, sharp movements can create a tense or aggressive mood, while sustained, flowing movements can create a calm or lyrical mood — the quality of movement directly affects atmosphere.
  5. If the intention can only be understood through a title or programme note, the movement has not effectively communicated. A successful dance conveys its meaning through the choreography so the audience can engage with it directly.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always state the choreographic intention clearly before analysing how it is communicated
  • Use specific movement examples to show how intention is conveyed — do not be vague
  • Remember that mood and meaning are different — mood is atmosphere, meaning is the deeper idea
  • In NEA tasks, write your intention as a clear statement and refer back to it throughout
  • For professional works, quote the choreographer's stated intention and link it to what you see

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Choreographic intention is the same as the dance title. ✓ The title may hint at the intention, but the intention is the deeper purpose or message the choreographer wants to communicate through movement.

✗ Mood and meaning are the same thing. ✓ Mood is the atmosphere or emotional quality (e.g. tense, joyful); meaning is the deeper message or idea the dance conveys.

✗ If the audience does not understand the choreographer's intention, the dance has failed. ✓ Audience interpretation may differ from the choreographer's intention and this is valid — dance can communicate on many levels.

✗ A motif is just any movement in the dance. ✓ A motif is a specific, key movement phrase that represents the core idea of the dance and is developed and varied to reinforce the intention.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain what choreographic intention is and discuss how a choreographer can communicate it effectively to an audience. Give specific examples. [6 marks]

Choreographic intention is the purpose or message the choreographer aims to communicate through the dance. It is the driving idea behind every choreographic decision. To communicate intention effectively, a choreographer uses several tools. First, motifs — key movement phrases that embody the core idea — are created and then developed and varied throughout the dance to reinforce the intention. For example, a choreographer exploring 'imprisonment' might create a motif of clasped hands and contracted body, returning to it in different forms. Second, dynamics communicate mood: sharp, sudden movements could convey panic, while slow, sustained movement might suggest helplessness. Third, spatial design — such as restricting dancers to a small area — can physically represent confinement. Fourth, the choice of structure supports intention; a narrative structure might tell the story of escape, while an abstract structure could explore the feeling of being trapped. Finally, relationships between dancers — such as one controlling others — can communicate power and oppression. All these elements must work together so the audience can sense the intention from the movement itself, not just from a programme note. The most effective dances communicate on both an emotional and intellectual level.
