Stimulus & Starting Points

Types of stimulus: visual, auditory, kinaesthetic, ideational and literary

Key Fact: A stimulus is the starting point or inspiration for creating a dance
Key Fact: There are five main types of stimulus: visual, auditory, kinaesthetic, ideational and literary
Key Fact: A visual stimulus is something you see — a painting, photograph, sculpture or film
Key Fact: An auditory stimulus is something you hear — music, natural sounds, spoken word or silence
Key Fact: A kinaesthetic stimulus comes from physical sensation — a gesture, body action or quality of movement
Key Fact: An ideational stimulus is a concept or theme — an emotion, issue, memory or philosophical idea
Key Fact: A literary stimulus comes from words — a poem, story, newspaper article or quote
Key Fact: One stimulus can inspire movement in many different ways depending on the choreographer
Key Fact: Choreographers may combine more than one type of stimulus to enrich their work
Key Fact: The choice of stimulus influences the mood, style and content of the final dance
Key Fact: In AQA 8236 NEA, you must clearly identify and describe your chosen stimulus
Key Fact: Understanding different stimulus types helps you analyse professional works in the written exam

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Stimulus & Starting Points, you must know:

  • Stimulus: A starting point that inspires the creation of dance movement and ideas
  • Visual stimulus: A stimulus based on something seen, such as a painting, photograph or sculpture
  • Auditory stimulus: A stimulus based on something heard, such as music, sound effects or spoken word
  • Kinaesthetic stimulus: A stimulus based on physical sensation or body awareness, such as a gesture or touch
  • Ideational stimulus: A stimulus based on a concept or theme, such as an emotion, social issue or memory
  • Literary stimulus: A stimulus based on written or spoken words, such as a poem, story or quote

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Name the five types of stimulus used in dance choreography.

Q: Give an example of a visual stimulus and explain how it could inspire movement.

Q: How does an ideational stimulus differ from a literary stimulus?

Q: Why might a choreographer choose to combine more than one type of stimulus?

Q: Explain how a kinaesthetic stimulus could be used to create movement material.

✅ Answers

  1. Visual, auditory, kinaesthetic, ideational and literary.
  2. A painting of a stormy sea could inspire swirling, expansive movements with sudden changes in dynamics to represent crashing waves.
  3. An ideational stimulus is a concept or theme (e.g. loneliness), whereas a literary stimulus is based on specific words (e.g. a poem about loneliness).
  4. Combining stimuli can create richer, more layered choreography — for example, using both a visual image and a piece of music to inspire contrasting movement qualities.
  5. A kinaesthetic stimulus such as the feeling of tension in the shoulders could be explored and developed into movement phrases that show struggle or restriction.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always name the specific type of stimulus in exam answers — visual, auditory, kinaesthetic, ideational or literary
  • When describing a stimulus, give a concrete example and link it to the movement it inspired
  • Remember that one type of stimulus can lead to many different choreographic outcomes
  • In NEA tasks, clearly state your stimulus type and explain why you chose it
  • For professional works questions, identify the choreographer's stimulus and how it shaped the piece

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Dance Exam Tips:
Use precise classical terminology in all answers. When analysing sources, always consider provenance (who created it, when, and why). For literary questions, reference specific episodes and book numbers from set texts. For thematic questions, compare Greek and Roman approaches where relevant. For 'how far' questions, present a balanced argument with a clear judgement. AQA 8236 rewards answers that demonstrate understanding of the classical world through use of specific evidence from both literary and visual/material sources.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ A stimulus is the music used for a dance. ✓ Music can be an auditory stimulus, but a stimulus can be any of five types: visual, auditory, kinaesthetic, ideational or literary.

✗ Ideational and literary stimuli are the same thing. ✓ An ideational stimulus is a concept or theme; a literary stimulus is specifically words such as a poem, story or quote.

✗ You can only use one stimulus when choreographing. ✓ Choreographers can combine multiple stimuli to create richer, more layered work.

✗ A kinaesthetic stimulus means watching someone else move. ✓ A kinaesthetic stimulus comes from your own physical sensation or body awareness, such as a gesture, breath or feeling of weight.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Choose two different types of stimulus and explain how each could inspire choreographic movement. Give a specific example for each. [6 marks]

A visual stimulus is something seen that inspires movement. For example, a photograph of a bird in flight could inspire light, soaring movements with extended limbs and swift, sweeping pathways through space. The choreographer might explore the shapes of wings through arm gestures and the quality of gliding through sustained, flowing dynamics. An auditory stimulus is something heard that inspires movement. For example, the sound of a heartbeat could inspire pulsing, rhythmic movement with sudden accents matching the beat. The choreographer might use contraction and release in the torso to echo the rhythm and create a tense, urgent quality. Both types of stimulus provide a clear starting point, but they lead to different movement qualities — the visual stimulus produced expansive, airborne qualities while the auditory stimulus produced rhythmic, grounded qualities. In both cases, the choreographer would explore, select and refine the movement to communicate their intention effectively.
