Social & Cultural Influences on Dance

How society, culture, technology and gender have shaped dance: understanding the external factors that influence choreographic decisions and dance development

Key Fact: Dance reflects the society in which it is created, responding to political, economic and social conditions
Key Fact: Cultural traditions shape dance vocabulary, from Bharatanatyam's Hindu storytelling to Flamenco's Romani heritage
Key Fact: Technology has expanded dance through lighting, projection, digital music and motion capture
Key Fact: Gender roles in dance have evolved — from male-only court ballet to challenging stereotypes in contemporary work
Key Fact: War, protest and social movements have inspired choreographers such as Christopher Bruce's Ghost Dances
Key Fact: Multiculturalism has led to fusion styles that blend techniques from different cultural traditions
Key Fact: Social media and online platforms have democratised dance, spreading styles like hip-hop globally
Key Fact: The commercial dance industry influences style through music videos, television and advertising
Key Fact: Religion and spirituality have historically shaped dance, from sacred temple dances to liturgical movement
Key Fact: Funding and patronage affect which dance styles thrive — government arts policy shapes the dance landscape
Key Fact: Body image and representation in dance have become important cultural conversations
Key Fact: AQA 8236 requires understanding of how social and cultural contexts inform professional works

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Social & Cultural Influences on Dance, you must know:

  • Social context: The societal conditions — political, economic and cultural — in which a dance is created and performed
  • Cultural heritage: The traditions, customs and artistic practices passed down through generations that shape dance identity
  • Democratisation of dance: The process of making dance accessible to wider audiences and participants beyond elite institutions
  • Gender stereotype: A fixed, oversimplified view of how men and women should behave or move in dance
  • Fusion: The blending of elements from different cultural dance traditions to create a new hybrid form
  • Patronage: Financial support from individuals, organisations or governments that enables dance creation and performance

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How did social and political conditions influence Christopher Bruce's Ghost Dances?

Q: Explain one way technology has changed how dance is created or performed.

Q: How have gender roles in dance changed over time?

Q: Why is understanding social and cultural context important for AQA 8236?

Q: Give an example of how multiculturalism has influenced contemporary dance.

✅ Answers

  1. Ghost Dances was inspired by the political oppression in Latin America. Bruce used traditional folk dance imagery and the motif of ghostly figures to represent the disappeared, reflecting the social injustice and violence of the period.
  2. Projection and digital scenography allow choreographers to create immersive environments that interact with dancers, such as in Akram Khan's work where digital backdrops extend the narrative beyond the physical body.
  3. Historically, ballet portrayed women as delicate and ethereal while men displayed strength. Contemporary choreographers such as Matthew Bourne have challenged this — his male Swan Lake redefines gender representation, and many works now explore fluid and non-traditional gender roles.
  4. The AQA specification requires students to analyse how context informs choreographic intention and meaning. Understanding the social and cultural influences behind prescribed works enables deeper, more informed analysis.
  5. Akram Khan's work blends Kathak with contemporary dance, creating a fusion that draws on his Bangladeshi heritage and Western training. This cross-cultural approach produces a unique movement vocabulary that communicates themes of identity and belonging.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always link social or cultural factors to specific choreographic choices in the prescribed work
  • When discussing gender, use examples from professional works rather than general statements
  • Technology questions should focus on how it enhances or changes the audience's experience
  • Use the phrase 'social and cultural context' directly — it is key AQA terminology
  • Be prepared to discuss multiple influences on one work, not just a single factor

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Social context just means when the dance was made. ✓ Social context includes the political climate, social movements, economic conditions and cultural values that shaped the choreographer's decisions.

✗ Technology in dance is only about lighting. ✓ Technology includes projection, digital scenography, motion capture, amplified sound, interactive sensors and online platforms that spread dance globally.

✗ Gender in dance is only about men vs women. ✓ Gender in dance includes representation, stereotypes, non-binary identity, and how choreographers reinforce or challenge gender norms through movement.

✗ Cultural influence means the choreographer just copied traditional dance. ✓ Cultural influence means drawing on heritage, values and traditions as inspiration, often blending them with contemporary approaches to create something new.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how social and cultural influences have shaped the creation of a professional dance work you have studied. [6 marks]

Social and cultural influences are central to the creation of professional dance works. Christopher Bruce's Ghost Dances (1981) was directly shaped by the political oppression in Latin America. The social context of military dictatorships and the disappearance of civilians informed Bruce's choreographic intention to represent the suffering of ordinary people. Culturally, Bruce drew on Latin American folk dance motifs — the dead spirits perform folk-style steps with skulled masks, blending traditional dance vocabulary with contemporary technique to create a powerful image of oppression. The work's cultural references make the political message accessible and emotionally resonant. Additionally, the gender representation is significant: both male and female dancers perform the same ghost motifs, suggesting that political violence affects all people regardless of gender. The work demonstrates how social conditions provide the stimulus while cultural traditions provide the movement vocabulary, together shaping choreographic decisions and creating meaning that is both specific and universal.
