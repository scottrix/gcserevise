Comparing & Evaluating Dance

Comparing professional works and own choreography: identifying similarities and differences, and making supported judgements about effectiveness

Key Fact: Comparing requires identifying both similarities and differences — not just one or the other
Key Fact: Use linking words: 'similarly', 'in contrast', 'whereas', 'both', 'however' to show comparison clearly
Key Fact: Always compare the same element — for example, compare use of dynamics in both works
Key Fact: Evaluating means making a judgement about effectiveness, supported by specific evidence
Key Fact: A strong evaluation considers strengths and limitations before reaching a conclusion
Key Fact: When evaluating your own work, be honest and specific about what worked and what could improve
Key Fact: Use criteria for evaluation: intention, audience impact, technical execution, choreographic structure
Key Fact: Comparison questions may ask about two professional works or your work versus a professional work
Key Fact: Always reference specific moments or sections from the works you are comparing
Key Fact: Evaluation answers should end with a clear, stated judgement, not just a list of points
Key Fact: Avoid vague statements like 'they are different' — state exactly how and why
Key Fact: AQA 8236 assessment rewards depth of analysis over breadth of coverage

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Comparing & Evaluating Dance, you must know:

  • Similarity: A feature or quality shared by two or more works that can be directly compared
  • Difference: A feature or quality where two works contrast or diverge from each other
  • Judgement: A reasoned conclusion about the effectiveness or quality of a work, supported by evidence
  • Criterion: A standard or principle used as the basis for making an evaluation, such as clarity of intention
  • Effectiveness: How well a choreographic choice communicates the intended meaning or achieves its purpose
  • Evidence: Specific examples from the dance that support a point, comparison or judgement

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the key difference between comparing and evaluating dance?

Q: Name three linking words that help structure a comparison answer.

Q: What criteria could you use to evaluate the effectiveness of a choreographic work?

Q: Why is it important to reference specific moments when comparing two works?

Q: How should you conclude an evaluation answer?

✅ Answers

  1. Comparing identifies similarities and differences between works. Evaluating goes further by making a judgement about effectiveness, weighing evidence to reach a supported conclusion about quality or impact.
  2. Similarly, in contrast, whereas. These signal to the examiner that you are making direct comparisons rather than writing about each work separately.
  3. Clarity of intention, audience impact, technical execution, use of choreographic devices, structure, creativity and how well movement communicates meaning are all valid evaluation criteria.
  4. Specific references demonstrate detailed knowledge and prevent vague generalisations. They allow you to make precise comparisons — for example, 'Both works use canon, but in Work A it creates urgency while in Work B it creates fluidity.'
  5. State a clear judgement that directly answers the question, summarising the key evidence that supports your conclusion. Avoid sitting on the fence — the examiner wants to see you have reached a reasoned decision.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Plan comparison answers in a table before writing — list similarities and differences for each element
  • Never write about one work then the other separately — interleave your points with linking words
  • For evaluation, introduce your criteria early so the examiner knows your basis for judgement
  • Support every comparative point with at least one specific example from each work
  • In evaluation answers, always finish with a clear, one-sentence judgement that answers the question

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Dance Exam Tips:
Use precise classical terminology in all answers. When analysing sources, always consider provenance (who created it, when, and why). For literary questions, reference specific episodes and book numbers from set texts. For thematic questions, compare Greek and Roman approaches where relevant. For 'how far' questions, present a balanced argument with a clear judgement. AQA 8236 rewards answers that demonstrate understanding of the classical world through use of specific evidence from both literary and visual/material sources.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Writing about Work A, then writing about Work B, without linking them. ✓ Interleave your points — compare the same element in both works using linking words like 'whereas' or 'similarly' within the same paragraph.

✗ Only identifying differences when asked to compare. ✓ Compare means identify both similarities and differences. If you only discuss differences, you have not fully answered the question.

✗ Giving an opinion without evidence when evaluating. ✓ Every judgement must be supported by specific evidence from the dance. Say what is effective and why, referencing particular moments or techniques.

✗ Ending an evaluation without a clear judgement. ✓ The final sentence should state your judgement clearly — for example, 'Overall, Work A is more effective because its use of canon creates greater emotional impact.'

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Compare the use of choreographic devices in two professional works you have studied and evaluate which is more effective in communicating the choreographic intention. [6 marks]

Both Ghost Dances and A Linha Curva use choreographic devices to communicate their intentions, but they do so differently. Both works use unison to create a powerful group presence: in Ghost Dances, the ghosts perform unison folk steps to represent oppressive unity, whereas in A Linha Curva, the unison percussive hip movements celebrate communal energy and joy. Similarly, both use canon — Ghost Dances uses it to show the ghosts' influence spreading, while A Linha Curva uses canon to create visual ripple effects that enhance the rhythmic drive. A key difference is that Ghost Dances uses motif development more extensively: the folk motif is repeated, varied and fragmented to show how oppression persists, while A Linha Curva relies more on contrast between sections of different dynamic quality. Evaluating effectiveness, Ghost Dances' use of devices is more effective in communicating intention because the motif development directly represents the theme of recurring oppression, giving the choreographic devices narrative meaning beyond their visual impact. A Linha Curva's devices are visually spectacular but serve a celebratory intention that requires less thematic depth, making Ghost Dances the more effective work in terms of purposeful choreographic communication.
