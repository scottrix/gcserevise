Writing accurately about dance using correct terminology: describing movement, dynamics, space and relationships precisely and effectively

Describing Dance in Writing

Writing accurately about dance using correct terminology: describing movement, dynamics, space and relationships precisely and effectively

Key Fact: Accurate written description requires precise dance terminology, not everyday language
Key Fact: Movement description should cover action, dynamics, space and relationships (A D S R)
Key Fact: Action vocabulary includes actions of the body: turn, jump, travel, gesture, fall, stillness
Key Fact: Dynamics describe how movements are performed: fast, slow, sustained, sudden, heavy, light
Key Fact: Spatial description includes levels, directions, pathways, formations and use of stage space
Key Fact: Relationships describe how dancers interact: unison, canon, contact, complement, contrast
Key Fact: Always describe what you see in specific, concrete terms rather than vague impressions
Key Fact: Avoid subjective language like 'beautiful' or 'amazing' — use technical observations instead
Key Fact: Use the present tense when describing what happens in a dance: 'the dancer leaps', not 'leapt'
Key Fact: Reference specific moments, counts or sections to show detailed knowledge of the work
Key Fact: Link descriptive points to the choreographic intention for higher-mark questions
Key Fact: Practise writing movement descriptions from memory to build fluency with terminology

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Describing Dance in Writing, you must know:

  • Action: What the body does — the movement categories of travel, turn, jump, gesture, fall and stillness
  • Dynamics: How the movement is performed — the quality, speed, weight and flow of the action
  • Spatial design: The use of space including levels, directions, pathways and formations in the performance area
  • Unison: Two or more dancers performing the same movement at the same time
  • Canon: A structure where dancers perform the same movement phrase one after another with a time delay
  • Complement: A relationship where dancers' movements fit together, enhancing each other's quality

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What are the four key areas you should cover when describing dance movement?

Q: Give an example of describing dynamics accurately rather than vaguely.

Q: Why should you avoid subjective language like 'beautiful' in exam answers?

Q: What is the difference between unison and canon?

Q: How can you show detailed knowledge of a professional work in your written description?

✅ Answers

  1. Action, dynamics, space and relationships (A D S R). Together, they provide a complete description of what happens, how it happens, where it happens and how dancers interact.
  2. Vague: 'The dancer moved nicely.' Accurate: 'The dancer performed a sustained, slow développé with light, flowing quality, gradually extending through the leg and arm.'
  3. Subjective language does not describe what actually happens in the dance. Examiners reward precise technical observation — describing dynamics, actions and spatial design — rather than personal opinion.
  4. In unison, dancers perform the same movement at exactly the same time. In canon, dancers perform the same movement but start at different times, creating a ripple or wave effect.
  5. Reference specific moments, sections or counts — for example, 'In Section 2, the trio performs canon with sharp, percussive arm gestures while travelling in a circular pathway at mid level.'

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Use A D S R (Action, Dynamics, Space, Relationships) as a checklist for complete descriptions
  • Always pair a technical term with a specific example from the work you are describing
  • Write in present tense: 'The dancer extends' not 'The dancer extended'
  • Replace vague words with precise ones — 'sustained' not 'slow', 'percussive' not 'hard'
  • Practise writing timed descriptions of short sections to build speed and accuracy

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Writing 'the dance was good' or 'it looked nice'. ✓ Use specific technical language: 'The dancer performed sustained, flowing movements at a low level with smooth transitions between gestures.'

✗ Describing only what the dancers do, not how they do it. ✓ Always include dynamics — the quality of movement is as important as the action itself for a complete description.

✗ Confusing unison and canon. ✓ Unison means same movement at the same time; canon means same movement at different times with a time delay between dancers.

✗ Using past tense when describing dance. ✓ Use present tense for dance description: 'The dancer leaps' not 'The dancer leapt', because the work exists as a living performance.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe the movement in a section of a professional dance work you have studied, using accurate dance terminology. [6 marks]

In the opening section of Christopher Bruce's Ghost Dances, the three ghost figures enter in unison, performing folk-influenced action including travelling steps with bent knees and rhythmic arm swings. The dynamics are heavy and earthy, with a driving, percussive quality as their feet strike the floor in sync with the panpipe music. Spatially, they move along a diagonal pathway from upstage right to downstage left, maintaining a close formation at a low-to-mid level. Their relationship is one of unison — they perform identical movement phrases simultaneously, creating an intimidating, unified presence. The stillness moments are sudden and suspended, the ghosts freezing mid-gesture with arms extended, creating contrast between the driving folk steps and the eerie pauses. This combination of percussive folk action, heavy dynamics, diagonal spatial design and unison relationship establishes the ghosts as an oppressive, inescapable force within the work.
