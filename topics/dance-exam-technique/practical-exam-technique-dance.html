AQA 8236

Maximising marks in performance and choreography components: preparation, rehearsal strategies, performance quality and choreographic evidence

Practical Exam Technique

Maximising marks in performance and choreography components: preparation, rehearsal strategies, performance quality and choreographic evidence

Key Fact: The practical component is worth 60% of the total GCSE — performance and choreography combined
Key Fact: Performance (30%) includes one solo and one duet/trio performance of set phrases
Key Fact: Choreography (30%) requires a solo or group choreography in response to a chosen stimulus
Key Fact: Performance marks reward technical accuracy, physical skills, expressive skills and mental skills
Key Fact: Technical accuracy includes alignment, coordination, control, balance and spatial awareness
Key Fact: Expressive skills include projection, focus, musicality, communication of choreographic intent
Key Fact: Mental skills include commitment, concentration, confidence and responsiveness to feedback
Key Fact: Choreography marks reward creative response, choreographic intention, structure and use of devices
Key Fact: Keep a choreographic diary — it provides evidence of your process and supports the written programme note
Key Fact: Rehearse under exam conditions to build confidence and reduce nerves on the day
Key Fact: Video yourself in rehearsal to identify areas for improvement that you may not feel from inside the movement
Key Fact: The programme note is assessed alongside your choreography — write it clearly, linking intention to movement

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Practical Exam Technique, you must know:

  • Technical accuracy: The precision of physical execution including alignment, coordination, control, balance and use of space
  • Projection: The ability to reach out to the audience and communicate the dance's meaning confidently and clearly
  • Musicality: The sensitivity and responsiveness to the music's phrasing, dynamics and rhythm in performance
  • Choreographic diary: A written record of the choreographic process documenting decisions, experiments and development
  • Programme note: A written description of the choreographic intention and key features submitted alongside the practical exam
  • Expressive skills: The performance qualities that communicate meaning to the audience: projection, focus, musicality and engagement

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What are the four categories of performance skills assessed in AQA 8236?

Q: Why is it important to rehearse under exam conditions?

Q: What should you include in your programme note?

Q: How can videoing rehearsals improve your practical work?

Q: What is the purpose of keeping a choreographic diary?

✅ Answers

  1. Technical accuracy, physical skills (flexibility, strength, stamina), expressive skills (projection, musicality, focus) and mental skills (commitment, concentration, confidence, responsiveness).
  2. Rehearsing under exam conditions builds familiarity with the performance environment, reduces anxiety, and ensures you can perform at your best without the support of mirrors or teacher prompts. It develops the confidence and mental preparation needed for the actual exam.
  3. Your programme note should state your choreographic intention, describe how the stimulus inspired your ideas, explain key choreographic choices such as devices, structure and dynamics, and link these to how you want the audience to respond.
  4. Watching video allows you to see technical inaccuracies, unconvincing performance quality and unclear choreographic ideas that you may not feel from inside the movement. It enables you to set specific improvement targets for subsequent rehearsals.
  5. A choreographic diary documents your creative process — experiments, decisions, refinements and feedback. It provides evidence of your understanding of choreographic principles and supports the programme note by showing intentionality behind your choices.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Warm up thoroughly before performing — physical and vocal preparation prevents injury and focuses the mind
  • Perform to the back row of the audience space — project your energy and intention beyond the examiner
  • If you make a mistake, keep going without stopping — continuity and commitment are assessed
  • Write your programme note early and refine it — it is assessed alongside the choreography
  • Practise your choreography in different spaces so you can adapt to the exam room on the day

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Dance Exam Tips:
Use precise classical terminology in all answers. When analysing sources, always consider provenance (who created it, when, and why). For literary questions, reference specific episodes and book numbers from set texts. For thematic questions, compare Greek and Roman approaches where relevant. For 'how far' questions, present a balanced argument with a clear judgement. AQA 8236 rewards answers that demonstrate understanding of the classical world through use of specific evidence from both literary and visual/material sources.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Stopping and restarting when you make a mistake in performance. ✓ Keep going — continuity is part of the assessment. Examiners reward resilience and commitment, and a small mistake recovered from is better than a broken performance.

✗ Writing the programme note at the last minute without linking it to the choreography. ✓ Write the programme note during the choreographic process, linking intention directly to specific movement choices so it reads as a genuine reflection of the work.

✗ Only rehearsing the sections you enjoy or find easy. ✓ Devote extra rehearsal time to weak sections. The examiner sees the whole piece — a strong opening undermined by a weak middle section will lower your overall mark.

✗ Focusing only on technique and forgetting expressive skills in performance. ✓ Both technical accuracy and expressive skills are assessed. A technically clean but emotionally flat performance will lose marks for projection, musicality and communication.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how preparation and rehearsal strategies can maximise your marks in the AQA 8236 practical components. [6 marks]

Preparation and rehearsal strategies are essential for maximising practical marks because they develop the four assessed skill areas: technical accuracy, physical skills, expressive skills and mental skills. Rehearsing under exam conditions builds the mental preparation and confidence needed to perform without teacher prompts or mirrors, reducing anxiety on the day. Videoing rehearsals allows you to identify technical inaccuracies and expressive weaknesses that are not felt from inside the movement, enabling targeted improvement. Keeping a choreographic diary documents the creative process, providing evidence of intentionality that supports the programme note — an assessed component alongside the choreography itself. Additionally, devoting extra time to weaker sections ensures consistent quality throughout the piece rather than a strong opening followed by a decline. Physical preparation through thorough warm-ups prevents injury and ensures the body is ready for technical demands. Finally, rehearsing in different performance spaces develops spatial awareness and adaptability, so the exam room environment does not disrupt your performance. Together, these strategies ensure you are technically prepared, expressively engaged and mentally confident, maximising marks across all assessment criteria.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 (Performance): Demonstrate knowledge and understanding of practical exam technique, including choreographic processes, performing skills and dance appreciation relevant to AQA 8236.

AO2 (Choreography): Apply knowledge and understanding of practical exam technique through the creative process of choreography, selecting and using movement material and choreographic devices.

AO3 (Appreciation): Analyse, evaluate and critique aspects of practical exam technique, including professional works, performance quality and choreographic intention, constructing supported arguments.
