Written Exam Technique

Techniques for the AQA 8236 written paper: time management, answer structure, planning and revision strategies to maximise marks

Key Fact: The AQA 8236 written paper is 1 hour 30 minutes and is worth 40% of the total GCSE grade
Key Fact: Read all questions before starting — plan your time based on marks available per question
Key Fact: Spend approximately 1.5 minutes per mark as a rough guide for timing
Key Fact: Always plan extended answers before writing — a brief outline saves time and improves structure
Key Fact: Start with the question you feel most confident about to build momentum and confidence
Key Fact: Use the PEEL structure for paragraphs: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link to intention
Key Fact: Short-answer questions need concise, precise responses — one mark equals one clear point
Key Fact: Annotation questions require specific dance terminology placed accurately on the diagram
Key Fact: Leave time to check answers — look for missing command word responses and vague language
Key Fact: Practise timed responses to build speed and familiarity with exam conditions
Key Fact: Keep a glossary of key terms and revise terminology regularly — precise vocabulary earns marks
Key Fact: Use bullet points for planning but write in full sentences in your final answer

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Written Exam Technique, you must know:

  • PEEL paragraph: A structured paragraph format: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link — ensuring each point is developed and connected to intention
  • Time allocation: The strategy of dividing exam time proportionally based on the marks available for each question
  • Annotation: A question type where you label a diagram or image with specific dance terminology to show understanding
  • Extended response: A longer written answer worth 6 or more marks requiring developed, structured analysis or evaluation
  • Mark scheme: The criteria used by examiners to award marks, showing what each level of response requires
  • Command word: The instructional verb in a question (describe, explain, analyse) that determines the required response type

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How should you manage your time in the AQA 8236 written exam?

Q: What is the PEEL structure and why is it useful?

Q: How should you approach an annotation question?

Q: Why is it important to plan extended answers before writing?

Q: What should you check for when reviewing your answers at the end of the exam?

✅ Answers

  1. Allocate approximately 1.5 minutes per mark. Read all questions first, then start with your strongest topic. For a 6-mark question, allow about 9 minutes including planning time. Leave 5 minutes at the end for checking.
  2. PEEL stands for Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link. It ensures each paragraph has a clear point, is supported by specific evidence from the work, explains how the evidence supports the point, and links back to the choreographic intention or question focus.
  3. Use precise dance terminology — not vague labels. Place labels accurately on the correct part of the diagram. Cover action, dynamics, space and relationships where relevant, and ensure each annotation is specific rather than general.
  4. Planning ensures your answer is structured, covers all required elements and directly addresses the command word. A brief outline prevents waffle, ensures you include evidence and keeps your answer focused on the question.
  5. Check that you have answered the command word correctly, used specific dance terminology, supported points with evidence from the work, written in full sentences, and not left any questions blank.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Read the whole paper first and mark up command words before writing anything
  • Use the 1.5 minutes per mark rule — a 6-mark question gets 9 minutes maximum
  • Plan extended answers with 3–4 key points before writing in full sentences
  • Never leave a question blank — even a rough answer may pick up marks for knowledge
  • Check that every paragraph links back to the question focus or choreographic intention

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Dance Exam Tips:
Use precise classical terminology in all answers. When analysing sources, always consider provenance (who created it, when, and why). For literary questions, reference specific episodes and book numbers from set texts. For thematic questions, compare Greek and Roman approaches where relevant. For 'how far' questions, present a balanced argument with a clear judgement. AQA 8236 rewards answers that demonstrate understanding of the classical world through use of specific evidence from both literary and visual/material sources.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Spending too long on early questions and rushing later ones. ✓ Allocate time per mark at the start and stick to it. Move on when your time is up — you can return to unfinished answers during checking time.

✗ Writing everything you know about a topic without addressing the command word. ✓ Identify the command word first and structure every point around it. Knowledge is only credited when it answers the question as asked.

✗ Using vague language instead of precise dance terminology. ✓ Replace 'the dancer moved well' with 'the dancer performed a sustained, flowing développé at a high level' — precision earns marks.

✗ Skipping the planning stage for extended answers. ✓ A two-minute plan saves time overall by preventing rambling and ensuring your answer is structured, focused and complete.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how effective time management and answer planning can improve your performance in the AQA 8236 written exam. [6 marks]

Effective time management ensures you attempt every question and maximise marks across the whole paper. The AQA 8236 written exam is 90 minutes, so allocating roughly 1.5 minutes per mark prevents spending too long on early questions and rushing later ones. For example, a 6-mark question should take approximately 9 minutes, including planning. Reading the whole paper first allows you to identify the most straightforward questions and start confidently. Answer planning is equally important for extended responses: spending two minutes outlining key points using the PEEL structure ensures each paragraph has a clear point, evidence, explanation and link to intention. Without planning, answers tend to be disorganised and repetitive, missing key assessment criteria. Planning also ensures the command word is addressed throughout, rather than drifting into description when the question asks for analysis. Together, time management and planning mean every answer is focused, structured and complete, giving the examiner clear, markable content throughout the paper.
