Expressive Skills in Dance

Projection, focus, musicality, communication and phrasing

Key Fact: Expressive skills enable a dancer to communicate meaning, mood and intention to the audience
Key Fact: Projection is the ability to reach out to the audience with energy and presence, making the performance compelling to watch
Key Fact: Focus is the direction of the dancer's gaze and attention, guiding the audience's eye and expressing intention
Key Fact: Musicality is the dancer's response to the music, showing awareness of rhythm, tempo, accents and phrasing through movement
Key Fact: Communication is the overall ability to convey the choreographic intention and emotional content to the audience
Key Fact: Phrasing is the way a dancer shapes a sequence of movements, like sentences in speech, to create meaning and dynamics
Key Fact: A dancer with good projection fills the performance space and holds the audience's attention
Key Fact: Focus can be directed outward to the audience, inward to reflect emotion, or at a partner to show relationship
Key Fact: Musicality is not just about being on the beat — it includes highlighting accents, reflecting dynamics and using silence
Key Fact: Phrasing groups movements into meaningful units, using breath, dynamics and timing to create contrast and emphasis
Key Fact: Expressive skills depend on physical and technical skills being secure enough to allow the dancer to focus on communication
Key Fact: AQA 8236 examiners assess expressive skills as a key part of the performance component

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Expressive Skills in Dance, you must know:

  • Projection: The ability to extend energy and presence outward to reach and engage the audience
  • Focus: The direction of a dancer's gaze and attention, used to guide the audience and express intention
  • Musicality: The dancer's sensitive response to the music, including rhythm, tempo, accents and phrasing
  • Communication: The ability to convey the choreographic intention, mood and meaning to the audience through movement
  • Phrasing: The way a dancer shapes a sequence of movements into meaningful units using dynamics, breath and timing
  • Presence: The quality of commanding attention on stage through confidence, energy and expressive engagement

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is projection in dance and why does it matter?

Q: How can focus be used to show a dancer's relationship with a partner?

Q: Explain the difference between being on the beat and showing musicality.

Q: What is phrasing and how does it improve a performance?

Q: Why must physical and technical skills be secure before expressive skills can be effective?

✅ Answers

  1. Projection is the ability to extend energy and presence outward to engage the audience. It matters because it makes the performance compelling and ensures the audience connects with the dance.
  2. A dancer can direct their gaze at a partner to show connection, look away to show distance, or use shared focus to demonstrate unity in the relationship.
  3. Being on the beat means timing movements to the pulse; musicality goes further by responding to accents, dynamics, tempo changes and the emotional character of the music.
  4. Phrasing is shaping movement sequences into meaningful units using breath, dynamics and timing. It improves performance by creating contrast, emphasis and clarity, like sentences in speech.
  5. If a dancer is struggling with technique or physical demands, their attention is on the body rather than communication. Secure technique frees the dancer to focus on expression.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always link expressive skills back to the choreographic intention — do not describe them in isolation
  • When writing about projection, mention both physical energy (extending through the body) and mental focus (engaging with the audience)
  • Musicality questions often require specific references to how the dancer responds to rhythm, accents or dynamics
  • Use the analogy of speech for phrasing — movements are grouped like sentences with pauses, emphasis and flow
  • In 6-mark answers, cover at least two expressive skills and explain how each helps communicate meaning

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Projection just means dancing with lots of energy. ✓ Projection is about directing energy and presence outward to reach the audience — it is focused and intentional, not just loud or big movement.

✗ Focus means looking at the audience the whole time. ✓ Focus can be directed at the audience, at a partner, inward or at a point in space — the choice of focus communicates different meanings.

✗ Musicality is the same as counting the beats. ✓ Counting beats ensures timing, but musicality is a deeper response to the music including dynamics, accents, phrasing and emotional quality.

✗ Phrasing only refers to the choreography, not the dancer. ✓ While choreography creates movement phrases, the dancer shapes the phrasing through breath, dynamic emphasis and timing choices in performance.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Discuss two expressive skills and explain how they help a dancer communicate the choreographic intention to the audience. [6 marks]

Projection and musicality are two expressive skills that help a dancer communicate the choreographic intention. Projection is the ability to extend energy and presence outward to engage the audience. A dancer performing a bold, celebratory piece uses projection by directing energy through the whole body and face, filling the space so the audience feels the joy and confidence of the choreography. Without projection, even technically strong dancing can appear flat and fail to connect with the audience. Musicality is the dancer's sensitive response to the music, going beyond simply keeping time. For example, in a lyrical piece, a musical dancer highlights the swells in the music by expanding movements on crescendos and softening on decrescendos, showing the emotional arc of the work. They also respond to accents with sharp, precise actions and use silence or pauses to create dramatic tension. Together, projection ensures the audience is engaged and musicality ensures the movement reflects the mood and structure of the accompaniment, both communicating the choreographic intention clearly and powerfully.
