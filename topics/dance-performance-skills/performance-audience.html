Performance & the Audience

Performing in different venues, audience-performer relationship and performance quality

Key Fact: The relationship between performer and audience is central to the experience of dance
Key Fact: Different venues — studios, theatres, outdoor spaces — affect how a performance is presented and received
Key Fact: A proscenium arch theatre creates a clear separation between performer and audience, framing the dance like a picture
Key Fact: Thrust and in-the-round stages bring the audience closer, creating intimacy and changing the performer's focus
Key Fact: Site-specific performances use non-theatre spaces and respond directly to the environment, creating unique audience experiences
Key Fact: The audience-performer relationship involves energy exchange — the audience's attention and response affect the performer
Key Fact: Performance quality is the overall standard of a performance, shaped by physical, technical and expressive skills working together
Key Fact: A dancer must adapt their projection, focus and energy to suit the size and type of venue
Key Fact: Audience interpretation depends on their prior knowledge, cultural background and personal experience
Key Fact: Effective performers acknowledge the audience's presence while staying committed to the character and intention
Key Fact: GCSE Dance students perform in both studio and theatre settings and must understand how context affects their work
Key Fact: AQA 8236 assesses understanding of performance context and the ability to analyse how audiences experience dance

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Performance & the Audience, you must know:

  • Proscenium arch: A theatre stage frame that separates the performers from the audience, creating a 'fourth wall'
  • Thrust stage: A stage that extends into the audience area, with spectators on three sides
  • In-the-round: A staging arrangement where the audience surrounds the performance area on all sides
  • Site-specific: Performance created for and in response to a particular non-theatre location
  • Fourth wall: The invisible barrier between performers and audience in a proscenium theatre
  • Performance quality: The overall standard and impact of a performance, determined by the combination of physical, technical and expressive skills

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How does a proscenium arch theatre affect the audience-performer relationship?

Q: Why must a dancer adapt their performance for an in-the-round staging?

Q: What is a site-specific performance and how does it change the audience experience?

Q: Explain the idea of energy exchange between performer and audience.

Q: What factors contribute to overall performance quality?

✅ Answers

  1. It creates a clear separation with a 'fourth wall', framing the dance visually and directing the audience's gaze to the stage as if watching a picture.
  2. The audience is on all sides, so the dancer must adjust focus, projection and facings so that no part of the audience is excluded or sees only the back of the performer.
  3. A site-specific performance is created for a non-theatre space such as a gallery, street or ruin. It changes the experience by immersing the audience in the environment and breaking the traditional stage-audience boundary.
  4. The performer sends energy and intention outward through projection and expression; the audience responds with attention, silence or applause, which in turn motivates the performer — it is a two-way relationship.
  5. Performance quality is shaped by the combination of physical skills (strength, flexibility), technical skills (alignment, control) and expressive skills (projection, musicality) working together effectively.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always name the type of venue or staging when discussing audience-performer relationships
  • When comparing venues, mention specific practical adjustments the dancer must make — facings, projection, spacing
  • The term 'fourth wall' is useful for proscenium arch answers — show you understand how it affects the relationship
  • Performance quality questions require you to link physical, technical and expressive skills together
  • When discussing audience interpretation, acknowledge that different viewers may respond differently based on their experience

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ All performances are the same regardless of venue. ✓ The venue significantly affects how a dance is performed and received — dancers must adapt projection, focus, spacing and energy to suit each space.

✗ The audience has no effect on the performer. ✓ The audience-performer relationship is a two-way exchange — the audience's attention, energy and response influence the performer's energy and commitment.

✗ Site-specific just means performing outside. ✓ Site-specific means the choreography is created in response to a particular location's features — it is not just any outdoor performance.

✗ Performance quality depends only on technique. ✓ Performance quality is the result of physical, technical and expressive skills working together — technique alone does not create a compelling performance.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Compare how the audience-performer relationship differs in a proscenium arch theatre and an in-the-round staging. Explain how the dancer must adapt their performance for each. [6 marks]

In a proscenium arch theatre, the audience sits on one side facing the stage, with a clear frame separating performer and spectator. This creates a 'fourth wall' where the audience watches as if looking through a window. The dancer can direct projection and focus primarily forward, knowing the entire audience shares the same viewpoint. For example, a solo performer faces front and projects energy outward through the space to reach the back rows. In contrast, in-the-round staging places the audience on all four sides of the performance area, removing the fourth wall and creating a much more intimate relationship. The dancer must adapt by constantly rotating their focus so that every section of the audience is included, adjusting facings throughout the piece so no one is left watching only the back of the performer. Projection must be directed to all sides, and the dancer must be aware that every angle is visible, meaning technical execution such as alignment must be precise from all directions. Both stagings create powerful audience-performer relationships, but the proscenium allows controlled, directed focus while in-the-round demands 360-degree awareness and more dynamic use of space.
