Costume and Make-Up Design

Costume design: period, style, colour symbolism, status indicators, practical considerations; make-up: age, character, period, symbolic, special effects make-up; costume changes and quick changes.

Key Fact: Costume communicates instantly: period, status, character, relationships — the audience reads costume before the character speaks.
Key Fact: Colour symbolism: white = purity/innocence; black = death/mystery; red = passion/danger; purple = royalty/wealth.
Key Fact: Status through costume: wealthy fabrics (silk, velvet) vs. poor (cotton, worn); formal vs. casual; fitted vs. loose.
Key Fact: Make-up: naturalistic (age, period accuracy) vs. symbolic (exaggerated, stylised). Special effects make-up for injury, age.
Key Fact: Quick changes: multi-roling requires costume pieces that can be rapidly added or removed — hats, jackets, props.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: How can costume design communicate a character's social status without any dialogue? Give specific examples.

Q2: Explain how colour symbolism in costume can reinforce a play's themes. Use a specific example.

Q3: What practical considerations must a costume designer address for a production with multi-roling?

✅ Answers

  1. Fabric quality (silk vs. polyester), fit (tailored vs. ill-fitting), condition (pristine vs. worn), accessories (jewellery, watches). Each element connotes wealth, care and social position. E.g. a well-tailored suit vs. a frayed jacket instantly communicates class difference.
  2. A specific example: e.g. in Blood Brothers, Mrs Lyons is dressed in expensive, cold-coloured clothing while Mrs Johnstone wears warm but worn colours — the colour contrast reinforces the class theme. White for innocence, red for danger — always link to specific play.
  3. Quick changes: easily removable layers (jackets, hats, scarves); distinct accessories for each role; Velcro fastenings; different shoes. Backstage organisation: dressers, laid-out costumes, clear running order. Each role must be visually distinct for the audience.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Costume is SEMIOTIC — always discuss denotation and connotation
  • Colour symbolism questions need specific examples, not just 'red means danger'
  • Practical considerations matter: movement, quick changes, safety
  • Make-up is part of the overall design concept — it must be coherent with set and lighting

📝 Exam Technique

Drama Exam Tips:
For costume questions, always: 1) Describe the specific costume item, 2) Explain its denotation (what it literally is), 3) Explain its connotation (what it symbolises), 4) Link to character or theme, 5) Evaluate its effectiveness. E.g. 'Mrs Birling's elegant silk dress denotes wealth but connotes complacency and detachment from the working class — the costume reinforces the theme of social responsibility.'

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Costume is just about dressing the actors appropriately for the period — it's functional, not meaningful. Correct: Costume is a key meaning-making element. Every fabric choice, colour, fit, accessory and condition carries semiotic significance. A designer chooses between cotton and silk, between fitted and loose, between pristine and worn — each choice communicates character, status, relationships and themes. Costume is never just 'appropriate dressing'.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

As a costume and make-up designer, describe and justify your design choices for two contrasting characters in a play you have studied. Explain how costume communicates character and theme.

A grade 9 response will: describe specific costume choices for each character with detail; explain denotation and connotation; show how costume differences communicate relationship/status; link to the play's themes; describe make-up choices; evaluate the overall design coherence; use precise design terminology; consider practical requirements.

Mark scheme: Costume and make-up design questions test AO1 (design terminology), AO2 (analysing how design creates meaning), and AO3 ...

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

Costume and make-up design questions test AO1 (design terminology), AO2 (analysing how design creates meaning), and AO3 (applying design knowledge). The written exam may ask you to answer from a designer's perspective for the set text (Section A). You must show how specific costume choices communicate character, status and theme — not just describe what characters would wear.

Detailed Notes

Costume Design for Character and Context

Costume design communicates character identity, social class, historical period, occupation and personality before the performer speaks a single line. A character's costume tells the audience who they are, where they belong and how they see themselves. Key costume design considerations include: period accuracy (does the costume correctly represent the historical setting?), social signalling (does the costume communicate the character's class, profession and status?), character consistency (does the costume reflect the character's personality and values?), and practicality (can the performer move, sit and change as required by the action?). UK costume designer Vicki Mortimer, who designed the costumes for the National Theatre's production of The Deep Blue Sea, uses fabric choice, colour and silhouette to communicate subtle social distinctions that the audience reads instantly.

Colour psychology in costume design is a powerful tool: red connotes passion, danger or power; white connotes purity, innocence or sterility; black connotes authority, mourning or menace; blue connotes calm, trust or coldness. The audience reads these colour associations subconsciously, making costume colour choices immediately effective. In An Inspector Calls, contrasting the Birling family's formal evening wear (impeccable, status-conscious) with the Inspector's plain dark suit (functional, anonymous) visually establishes the opposition between social privilege and moral authority. GCSE students must justify colour and style choices with reference to the character and the production concept, not merely describe what the costume looks like.

Example

A GCSE student designing costumes for Blood Brothers specifies: Mrs Johnstone wears faded floral cotton (working-class, practical, warm), Mrs Lyons wears structured silk in muted tones (middle-class, controlled, emotionally restrained), Mickey's costume deteriorates through the play (from clean school uniform to worn work clothes to dishevelled desperation), whilst Eddie's costume becomes increasingly formal (school uniform to university blazer to business suit), visually charting their diverging trajectories.

Make-up Design and Character Transformation

Make-up design extends characterisation beyond costume, enabling transformations that fabric alone cannot achieve. Stage make-up techniques for GCSE include: straight make-up (enhancing natural features for visibility under stage lighting), age make-up (using highlights and shadows to create the appearance of ageing), character make-up (adding distinctive features like scars, wrinkles or unusual colouring), and period make-up (recreating the cosmetic styles of a specific historical era). UK make-up designer Ivana Primorac, whose work includes film and theatre productions, uses make-up to create character detail that survives even the back row of a large theatre. GCSE students should understand that stage make-up must be more defined than everyday make-up to counteract the flattening effect of stage lighting.

Specialist make-up techniques include: prosthetics (pre-made pieces applied to change facial structure, such as ageing nose tips or wound appliances), bruise and wound make-up (using red, purple, yellow and green colours to simulate injuries at different stages of healing), fantasy make-up (non-realistic designs for stylised or abstract productions), and face painting (bold, graphic designs that transform the performer's appearance completely). For GCSE devised work, simple make-up choices (dark circles under eyes for a tired character, pale foundation for illness, heavy eyeliner for menace) can significantly enhance characterisation. The RSC's make-up department provides excellent online resources showing professional techniques that GCSE students can adapt.

Example

A GCSE student designing make-up for a devised piece about war specifies: before the conflict scenes, all performers have natural, healthy make-up; after the conflict, specific performers show wounds (created with scar wax and theatrical blood), bruising (layered red-purple-yellow cream make-up), and exhaustion (dark shadows under eyes, pale foundation, cracked lips), using make-up to externalise the physical cost of violence.

Quick Changes and Costume Management

Quick changes are costume changes that must happen within seconds during a performance, often between scenes. Managing quick changes requires: advance planning (identifying which changes are needed and how long they take), costume design for speed (using Velcro, poppers and magnets instead of buttons and zips), dressing area setup (positioning costumes in the exact order needed, with all fastenings pre-opened), and rehearsal (practising the change until it is consistently achievable within the available time). UK West End productions employ dedicated dressers whose sole job is to manage quick changes, but GCSE students must manage their own changes, making advance preparation even more critical.

Costume management encompasses all aspects of organising costumes for a production: creating a costume plot (listing every character's costume for every scene), maintaining costume condition (laundering, repairing and pressing between performances), and managing costume storage and access. The costume plot should specify: the scene number, the character name, the costume description, and the change time available. GCSE students should create a costume plot for their production and allocate specific responsibilities for managing quick changes, as the chaos of unmanaged costume changes is a common source of performance disruption in school productions.

Example

A GCSE performer who must change from a school uniform (Scene 3) to a party outfit (Scene 4) in under 30 seconds plans the change meticulously: the party outfit is pre-positioned on a chair in the wings, pre-fastened with Velcro rather than buttons, and shoes are slip-ons rather than laced. They rehearse the change five times, reducing it from 45 seconds to 22 seconds, ensuring a clean transition that maintains the performance's pacing.
