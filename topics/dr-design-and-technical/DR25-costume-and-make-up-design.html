Costume and Make-Up Design

Costume design: period, style, colour symbolism, status indicators, practical considerations; make-up: age, character, period, symbolic, special effects make-up; costume changes and quick changes.

Key Fact: Costume communicates instantly: period, status, character, relationships — the audience reads costume before the character speaks.
Key Fact: Colour symbolism: white = purity/innocence; black = death/mystery; red = passion/danger; purple = royalty/wealth.
Key Fact: Status through costume: wealthy fabrics (silk, velvet) vs. poor (cotton, worn); formal vs. casual; fitted vs. loose.
Key Fact: Make-up: naturalistic (age, period accuracy) vs. symbolic (exaggerated, stylised). Special effects make-up for injury, age.
Key Fact: Quick changes: multi-roling requires costume pieces that can be rapidly added or removed — hats, jackets, props.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: How can costume design communicate a character's social status without any dialogue? Give specific examples.

Q2: Explain how colour symbolism in costume can reinforce a play's themes. Use a specific example.

Q3: What practical considerations must a costume designer address for a production with multi-roling?

✅ Answers

  1. Fabric quality (silk vs. polyester), fit (tailored vs. ill-fitting), condition (pristine vs. worn), accessories (jewellery, watches). Each element connotes wealth, care and social position. E.g. a well-tailored suit vs. a frayed jacket instantly communicates class difference.
  2. A specific example: e.g. in Blood Brothers, Mrs Lyons is dressed in expensive, cold-coloured clothing while Mrs Johnstone wears warm but worn colours — the colour contrast reinforces the class theme. White for innocence, red for danger — always link to specific play.
  3. Quick changes: easily removable layers (jackets, hats, scarves); distinct accessories for each role; Velcro fastenings; different shoes. Backstage organisation: dressers, laid-out costumes, clear running order. Each role must be visually distinct for the audience.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Costume is SEMIOTIC — always discuss denotation and connotation
  • Colour symbolism questions need specific examples, not just 'red means danger'
  • Practical considerations matter: movement, quick changes, safety
  • Make-up is part of the overall design concept — it must be coherent with set and lighting

📝 Exam Technique

Drama Exam Tips:
For costume questions, always: 1) Describe the specific costume item, 2) Explain its denotation (what it literally is), 3) Explain its connotation (what it symbolises), 4) Link to character or theme, 5) Evaluate its effectiveness. E.g. 'Mrs Birling's elegant silk dress denotes wealth but connotes complacency and detachment from the working class — the costume reinforces the theme of social responsibility.'

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Costume is just about dressing the actors appropriately for the period — it's functional, not meaningful. Correct: Costume is a key meaning-making element. Every fabric choice, colour, fit, accessory and condition carries semiotic significance. A designer chooses between cotton and silk, between fitted and loose, between pristine and worn — each choice communicates character, status, relationships and themes. Costume is never just 'appropriate dressing'.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

As a costume and make-up designer, describe and justify your design choices for two contrasting characters in a play you have studied. Explain how costume communicates character and theme.

A grade 9 response will: describe specific costume choices for each character with detail; explain denotation and connotation; show how costume differences communicate relationship/status; link to the play's themes; describe make-up choices; evaluate the overall design coherence; use precise design terminology; consider practical requirements.

Mark scheme: Costume and make-up design questions test AO1 (design terminology), AO2 (analysing how design creates meaning), and AO3 ...

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

Costume and make-up design questions test AO1 (design terminology), AO2 (analysing how design creates meaning), and AO3 (applying design knowledge). The written exam may ask you to answer from a designer's perspective for the set text (Section A). You must show how specific costume choices communicate character, status and theme — not just describe what characters would wear.
