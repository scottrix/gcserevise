Devising Process and Stimulus

Responding to stimuli (images, text, music, objects, themes); research and exploration; brainstorming and mind-mapping; initial ideas to structured drama; collaborative creation.

Key Fact: A stimulus can be anything: an image, a song, a news story, a poem, an object — it's the starting point for devising.
Key Fact: Research: explore the stimulus from multiple angles — personal, social, historical, political — to find depth.
Key Fact: Brainstorming: generate as many ideas as possible without judging them — quantity leads to quality.
Key Fact: Collaborative creation: everyone contributes — share ideas physically and verbally, build on each other's suggestions.
Key Fact: From stimulus to structure: move from exploration (what does this make us think/feel?) to selection (which ideas are strongest?) to structuring (how do we shape this into drama?).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the process you would use to develop a piece of drama from a stimulus. Use a specific example.

Q2: How can research enhance a devised piece? What types of research might you do?

Q3: Explain how collaborative devising is different from working with a script. What are the challenges and benefits?

✅ Answers

  1. Full process: receive stimulus → initial reactions → research → brainstorm → improvise → select strongest ideas → structure → rehearse → refine. Give a specific stimulus example showing each stage.
  2. Research adds depth and authenticity: factual research (statistics, history), personal research (interviews, experiences), artistic research (other plays, art works). It prevents cliches and enriches content.
  3. Collaborative: everyone contributes ideas, ownership is shared, multiple perspectives enrich the work. Challenges: compromise, conflicting visions, unequal contribution. Benefits: creativity, investment, diverse ideas.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always describe the FULL devising journey — don't just talk about the final piece
  • Show how the STIMULUS led to SPECIFIC dramatic choices
  • In your portfolio, document EVERY stage with evidence
  • Collaborative means shared ownership — show how everyone contributed

📝 Exam Technique

Drama Exam Tips:
For devising process questions, use a chronological structure: Stimulus → Response → Research → Exploration → Selection → Structure → Rehearsal → Performance. At each stage, describe what you DID, WHY you did it, and what EFFECT it had on the final piece.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Devising is just making up a play on the spot — you don't need to plan or research. Correct: Devising is a structured creative process. It begins with careful stimulus analysis and research, moves through collaborative exploration and improvisation, and requires deliberate selection and structuring. The best devised work combines creative freedom with rigorous development — it is never just improvised on the spot.

Detailed Notes

Responding to Stimuli: Methods and Approaches

Devising begins with the stimulus, which can be anything that sparks the creative process: an image, a piece of music, a news article, a poem, an object, a theme or a word. The GCSE devising component typically provides a stimulus pack from the exam board, and students must demonstrate how they have explored and responded to this material. Effective stimulus response moves beyond literal interpretation: a photograph of an empty room might inspire a piece about loneliness, memory, abandonment or anticipation, depending on the group's creative choices. UK company Complicite begins devising with extensive 'play' sessions, allowing the stimulus to generate multiple possible directions before the group commits to a single path.

Brainstorming and mind-mapping are essential early-stage devising tools. A mind map places the stimulus at the centre and radiates associated ideas, creating a visual network of potential themes, characters, situations and images. More sophisticated approaches include research-based exploration (gathering factual information about the stimulus context), personal association (connecting the stimulus to the group members' own experiences), and theatrical translation (asking 'how could this idea work on stage?'). The UK devising company Forced Entertainment uses a technique called 'dreamwalking,' where performers speak continuous improvised monologues inspired by the stimulus, recording and reviewing the material for promising moments. GCSE groups should document their stimulus response process thoroughly, as the portfolio must show how the starting point led to the finished performance.

Example

A GCSE group given a stimulus image of a closed door creates a mind map branching into: 'What's behind it?' (secrets, opportunities, danger), 'Who closed it?' (authority, fear, protection), 'Do we open it?' (curiosity, consequences, choice). The group then researches the theme of barriers, interviews family members about doors that were significant in their lives, and develops three short scenes exploring different aspects of the metaphor before selecting the most powerful for development.

Research and Exploration in Devising

Research transforms a vague initial response into a theatrically rich and intellectually grounded piece. Primary research involves gathering first-hand information: interviews with people connected to the theme, site visits to relevant locations, surveys and questionnaires. Secondary research uses published sources: newspaper articles, academic studies, literature, films and documentaries. For a piece about the NHS, a GCSE group might interview a local nurse (primary), research NHS funding statistics (secondary), visit a hospital reception area to observe atmosphere and interactions (primary), and read news coverage of healthcare debates (secondary). This research provides authentic detail that elevates the performance above generic assumptions.

Exploration through practical workshops generates theatrical material from the research. Physical exploration uses movement, stillness and spatial relationship to embody research findings: a piece about factory work might explore repetitive, mechanised movement patterns. Verbal exploration uses improvisation, hot-seating and role play to develop characters and dialogue. Visual exploration uses drawings, model-making and collage to develop design concepts. The UK company Gecko Theatre uses extensive physical exploration, allowing the body to generate ideas before the mind edits them. GCSE students should use multiple exploration methods, as over-reliance on one approach (typically discussion) limits the theatrical range of the devised piece. Documentation of the exploration process, including failed experiments, demonstrates the authentic creative journey that examiners reward.

Example

A GCSE group devising a piece about the Windrush scandal interviews a local community elder who migrated from Jamaica in the 1960s. His description of arriving at a cold, grey London railway station inspires a physical sequence where performers create the platform, the wind and the overwhelming noise using only their bodies and voices, creating a moment of genuine emotional power grounded in lived experience.

Structuring Devised Work

Moving from exploratory material to a structured performance requires editorial decisions about what to include, what to discard, and how to order the selected material. Common devised structures include: chronological narrative (following a story through time), thematic structure (organising scenes around ideas rather than plot), physical structure (using recurring movement motifs as the organising principle), and episodic structure (self-contained scenes linked by theme or character). The structure should serve the content, not constrain it: forcing material into a predetermined structure produces flat, formulaic work, whilst allowing the structure to emerge from the material produces organic, surprising theatre.

Transitions between scenes are crucial in devised work, where scenes are often created independently and must be connected coherently. Options include: direct transitions (one scene ends, the next begins), physical transitions (performers move into the next configuration as part of the choreography), sound transitions (music or soundscape bridges the gap), and narrative transitions (a narrator or character links the scenes verbally). UK company Kneehigh, based in Cornwall, is renowned for fluid, imaginative transitions that maintain theatrical momentum. GCSE students should give as much creative attention to transitions as to scenes, as poor transitions can undermine otherwise strong material. The portfolio should document structural decisions, explaining why specific material was included and why particular scene orders were chosen.

Example

A GCSE group devising a piece about social media structures their work thematically rather than chronologically: Scene 1 explores connection (positive), Scene 2 explores comparison (negative), Scene 3 explores confrontation (dangerous), Scene 4 explores choice (resolution). Each scene uses the same four performers but in different character configurations, with a recurring physical motif (hands reaching for phones) linking the scenes visually.
