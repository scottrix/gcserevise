Documenting and Evaluating Devised Work

Portfolio writing: documenting the process, reflecting on decisions, evaluating the final piece, analysing practitioner influence, justifying creative choices, structured written evidence.

Key Fact: The portfolio is worth as much as the performance — it must be detailed, reflective and analytical.
Key Fact: Document EVERY stage: stimulus response, research, exploration, rehearsal, refinement, performance.
Key Fact: Reflection: not just what you did, but WHY you did it and what you would change — show critical thinking.
Key Fact: Practitioner influence: explicitly show how Stanislavski, Brecht, Frantic Assembly etc. shaped your work.
Key Fact: Justify EVERY choice: 'We used cross-cutting because Brecht's V-effect encourages the audience to compare the two scenes and think critically about the social message.'

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What should you include in a devising portfolio to achieve a high mark?

Q2: Explain the difference between describing what you did and evaluating why you did it. Why is evaluation more important?

Q3: How would you document the influence of a practitioner on your devised piece?

✅ Answers

  1. Stimulus analysis, research evidence, exploration techniques used, rehearsal process, key decisions and their justification, practitioner influence with examples, reflection on strengths and weaknesses, evaluation of the final piece's impact on the audience.
  2. Description: 'We used a freeze-frame.' Evaluation: 'We used a freeze-frame to mark the moment when the truth was revealed, creating a pause that allowed the audience to absorb the significance — this Brechtian technique forced critical reflection rather than emotional response.'
  3. Name the practitioner; explain the specific technique or idea used; describe how you applied it in rehearsal; evaluate its effect on the final piece; analyse how it shaped the audience's experience.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • The portfolio is NOT a diary — it's an analytical document
  • Always justify: WHAT you did + WHY you did it + WHAT EFFECT it had
  • Use practitioner terminology throughout — 'alienation effect', 'given circumstances'
  • Include evidence: photos, diagrams, annotated scripts

📝 Exam Technique

Drama Exam Tips:
Structure each portfolio section using: What we did → Why we chose this approach → How we developed it → What effect we intended → How the audience responded → What we would improve. This systematic approach ensures full AO4 coverage.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The portfolio is just a record of what happened in rehearsals — you just need to describe the process. Correct: The portfolio is an EVALUATIVE document. Describing what happened earns basic marks; explaining WHY choices were made, HOW they developed the piece, and EVALUATING their effectiveness earns top marks. Every paragraph should contain analysis, not just description.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Write a portfolio entry evaluating how you used a practitioner's ideas to develop a key moment in your devised piece.

A grade 9 portfolio entry will: identify the practitioner and specific technique; describe the practical application in detail; explain the creative intention; analyse the effect on the audience; evaluate the success of the technique; suggest specific improvements; use precise theatrical terminology throughout.

Assessment Objective Analysis

The devising portfolio is assessed on AO4 (analyse and evaluate — 30% of the devising component). Marks are awarded for: research and exploration (how you developed ideas), development and refinement (how you improved the piece), and evaluation (how you assess the final outcome). The portfolio must show analytical depth, not just chronological description.

Detailed Notes

Portfolio and Documentation Requirements

The GCSE devising portfolio (or written evidence) documents the entire creative journey from initial stimulus to final performance. For AQA, this is worth 30 marks (part of the 60-mark devising component). The portfolio must demonstrate: how the stimulus was explored and developed, research conducted and how it influenced the piece, the contribution of each individual to the creative process, how practitioner theories were applied, the development and refinement of ideas, and evaluation of the final performance. Strong portfolios tell a coherent story of the creative journey, not just a chronological diary of what happened. AQA moderators report that the best portfolios are analytical rather than descriptive, explaining why decisions were made rather than merely recording what was done.

Effective documentation techniques include: annotated photographs of rehearsal work, video extracts of key developmental moments, annotated scripts showing changes and their reasons, research notes with analysis of relevance, and reflective journal entries written after each rehearsal. Digital portfolios can include embedded video and audio, which AQA encourages as evidence of the practical process. The portfolio should demonstrate the student's individual contribution within the group context, showing specific moments where their ideas, direction or performance choices shaped the piece. UK drama teachers recommend writing portfolio entries immediately after each rehearsal whilst the experience is fresh, rather than attempting to reconstruct the process retrospectively.

Example

A student's devising portfolio includes: a mind map of stimulus responses (Session 1), annotated photographs of a physical exploration exercise (Session 3), a video extract of an improvisation with a written analysis of why a particular moment was selected for development (Session 5), and a reflective entry explaining how audience feedback in Session 8 led to cutting a character and redistributing their dialogue (Session 9). Each entry is dated and explicitly connected to the marking criteria.

Evaluating Devised Work: Criteria and Methods

Evaluating devised work requires assessing both the process and the product against specific criteria. Process evaluation considers: how effectively the group collaborated, how thoroughly the stimulus was explored, how well research informed the piece, how appropriately practitioner techniques were applied, and how effectively the student contributed individually. Product evaluation considers: the quality of the final performance (acting, design, technical elements), the effectiveness of the structure and pacing, the clarity of the artistic intention, and the impact on the audience. GCSE exam criteria require students to evaluate both their own contribution and the overall piece, demonstrating analytical thinking rather than mere description.

Self-evaluation for GCSE devising should be specific and evidence-based. Rather than writing 'I think our piece went well,' a strong evaluation states: 'The opening physical sequence effectively established the theme of entrapment because the confined spatial arrangement forced the audience to share the characters' claustrophobia. However, the transition between Scene 2 and Scene 3 was too slow, losing dramatic momentum. If we were to rework the piece, I would create a sound bridge using a heartbeat that accelerates through the transition, maintaining tension and connecting the scenes thematically.' This level of specific, analytical evaluation, supported by evidence and suggesting concrete improvements, demonstrates the critical thinking that achieves higher grades.

Example

A student evaluating their devised performance writes: 'My use of Frantic Assembly's push-pull technique in Scene 2 was effective because it physically embodied the power dynamic between the two characters, making their conflict visible before any dialogue was spoken. However, I rushed the final pause, allowing only 3 seconds of stillness instead of the 8 seconds we had rehearsed. This reduced the impact of the climax because the audience had insufficient time to absorb the emotional shift. In future performances, I would count the pause internally to maintain the intended timing.'

Evidence and Assessment Preparation

GCSE devising assessment requires evidence of both the process and the final performance. Process evidence includes the portfolio, rehearsal footage, annotated scripts and reflective journals. Performance evidence includes a video recording of the final piece and, for some exam boards, a live performance for a visiting examiner. Students must ensure that all evidence clearly demonstrates their individual contribution within the group context. AQA's marking criteria specifically reward students who show 'perceptive' understanding and 'sophisticated' application of dramatic techniques, which requires evidence of deep engagement with the creative process rather than surface-level participation.

Preparing for the devising assessment involves ensuring all documentation is complete, well-organised and explicitly linked to the marking criteria. Each portfolio entry should identify which assessment objective it addresses. The final performance recording should be of good quality (clear sound, adequate lighting, stable camera position) and should represent the group's best achievable work. Some AQA centres invite an audience for the assessed performance, which raises the stakes and can improve the quality of the work. Students should also prepare for the possibility of being asked to discuss their devising process in a viva-style conversation with the teacher, explaining their creative choices with reference to practitioner theory and theatrical terminology.

Example

A student preparing their devising portfolio for submission creates a contents page mapping each section to the AQA marking criteria: Section 1 (Research and Exploration - AO1), Section 2 (Development and Shaping - AO2), Section 3 (Performance Skills - AO3), Section 4 (Analysis and Evaluation - AO4). Each section begins with a brief statement of what they will demonstrate, followed by dated, annotated evidence, and concludes with a reflective summary.
