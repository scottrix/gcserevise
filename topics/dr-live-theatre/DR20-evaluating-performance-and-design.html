Evaluating Performance and Design

Evaluating acting, directing and design choices; making judgements about effectiveness; supporting analysis with evidence; criteria for evaluation; linking choices to production intention.

Key Fact: Evaluation = analysis + judgement. You must say HOW effective something was AND WHY.
Key Fact: Criteria for evaluation: did the choice serve the production's intention? Did it communicate meaning clearly? Did it engage the audience?
Key Fact: Support every judgement with SPECIFIC evidence from the production — never make unsupported claims.
Key Fact: Performance evaluation: vocal skills, physical skills, characterisation, interaction. Design evaluation: set, costume, lighting, sound, props.
Key Fact: Link choices to the production's OVERALL INTENTION — a choice is effective if it serves the vision, not just if it was impressive.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What criteria would you use to evaluate whether a performer's choices were effective?

Q2: How would you evaluate a lighting design's contribution to a production? Give specific examples.

Q3: Explain the difference between analysis and evaluation. Why does evaluation require judgement?

✅ Answers

  1. Did the performer serve the character's truth? Were vocal and physical choices precise and consistent? Did they interact authentically with others? Did their choices serve the production's intention? Were they engaging for the audience?
  2. Describe the lighting design (colour, intensity, direction, movement); explain what each choice connoted; evaluate whether it served the production's intention; judge its effectiveness on the audience's experience; support with a specific moment as evidence.
  3. Analysis identifies WHAT was done and HOW it created meaning. Evaluation adds a JUDGEMENT about effectiveness. Analysis: 'The designer used red lighting.' Evaluation: 'The designer's use of red lighting was highly effective because it connoted danger and created an oppressive atmosphere that served the production's intention to unsettle the audience.'

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Evaluation REQUIRES judgement — you must say whether it was effective and why
  • Support every judgement with a specific moment from the production
  • Effectiveness = did it serve the INTENTION? Not just 'was it impressive?'
  • Be balanced — acknowledge both strengths and areas for improvement

📝 Exam Technique

Drama Exam Tips:
For evaluation questions, use the structure: What I saw (specific moment) → How it was done (technique) → What it meant (analysis) → How effective it was (judgement) → Why it was effective/ineffective (reasoning linked to intention). This ensures both analysis and evaluation are present.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Evaluating a production means saying whether it was good or bad and whether you enjoyed it. Correct: Evaluation is about EFFECTIVENESS, not personal taste. A choice is effective if it serves the production's intention, communicates meaning clearly and impacts the audience as intended. You can evaluate a choice as effective even if you didn't personally enjoy it. Professional evaluation uses criteria, not preference.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the effectiveness of the performance and design choices in a production you have seen. Support your evaluation with specific examples.

A grade 9 response will: identify the production's intention; evaluate at least two performance choices; evaluate at least two design choices; make clear judgements about effectiveness; support every judgement with specific evidence; link choices to the production's overall intention; use precise theatrical terminology; show balanced critical thinking.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

Live theatre evaluation tests AO2 (analyse and evaluate — 20% of the qualification). In Section B of the written exam, you must write about a production you have seen. Marks are awarded for: precise analysis of specific moments, supported judgements about effectiveness, use of theatrical terminology, and understanding of how choices serve the production's intention.
