Writing a Theatre Review

Structure of a theatre review; introduction, analysis and evaluation paragraphs; conclusion; using the SEE framework; writing in an analytical style; incorporating theatrical terminology.

Key Fact: Introduction: name the production, company, venue, date and the production's overall intention.
Key Fact: SEE framework: State the technique, Evidence from the production, Effect on the audience — use for every analytical point.
Key Fact: Analysis paragraphs: focus on one aspect per paragraph (e.g. performance, then design, then direction).
Key Fact: Conclusion: evaluate the overall effectiveness — did the production achieve its intention? Sum up your judgement.
Key Fact: Analytical style: precise terminology, specific moments, supported judgements — never vague or subjective language.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the structure of a theatre review for the GCSE written exam.

Q2: What is the SEE framework and how does it improve your written analysis?

Q3: How can you ensure your theatre review is analytical rather than just descriptive?

✅ Answers

  1. Introduction (production, company, venue, intention) → Performance analysis paragraph (specific vocal/physical choices) → Design analysis paragraph (specific set/lighting/sound/costume choices) → Direction analysis paragraph (staging/pacing) → Conclusion (overall effectiveness judgement linked to intention).
  2. SEE: State the technique used, Evidence from the production (specific moment), Effect on the audience. It improves analysis by forcing you to move beyond description ('the lighting was blue') to analysis ('the blue lighting connoted coldness and isolation, making the audience feel the character's loneliness').
  3. Use precise terminology instead of everyday language. Always explain the MEANING and EFFECT of choices, not just what you saw. Make judgements about effectiveness. Support every claim with evidence. Replace 'I liked it' with 'The designer's choice was effective because...'

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Introduction: name production, company, venue, date — essential facts first
  • Every paragraph must contain both ANALYSIS and EVALUATION
  • Use SEE for every point — State, Evidence, Effect
  • Conclusion: judge overall effectiveness against the production's stated intention

📝 Exam Technique

Drama Exam Tips:
Time management for Section B (live theatre): 5 minutes planning (which moments will you write about?), 30 minutes writing (structured paragraphs using SEE), 5 minutes checking (terminology, evidence, judgements). Plan your key moments BEFORE you start writing to ensure you have enough material.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: A theatre review should start with your overall opinion and then give reasons why you liked or disliked the show. Correct: A theatre review should start with FACTUAL CONTEXT (production, company, venue, intention) and then provide ANALYSIS and EVALUATION of specific choices. Your opinion only matters when it is supported by evidence and linked to the production's intention. The examiner wants to see analytical skill, not personal preference.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Write a structured theatre review of a production you have seen, evaluating the effectiveness of the performance and design choices.

A grade 9 review will: open with factual introduction; use structured paragraphs (performance, design, direction); apply the SEE framework consistently; make clear judgements about effectiveness; support every claim with specific evidence; use precise theatrical terminology; conclude with overall evaluation linked to intention; write in an analytical, not descriptive style.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

The live theatre review (Component 1 Section B) assesses AO2 (20%). The question asks you to analyse and evaluate a production you have seen. You must: name the production, analyse specific performance and design choices, evaluate their effectiveness, and use precise terminology. The mark scheme rewards: specific moments (not generalities), supported judgements (not unsupported opinions), and theatrical vocabulary (not everyday language).
