Writing a Theatre Review

Structure of a theatre review; introduction, analysis and evaluation paragraphs; conclusion; using the SEE framework; writing in an analytical style; incorporating theatrical terminology.

Key Fact: Introduction: name the production, company, venue, date and the production's overall intention.
Key Fact: SEE framework: State the technique, Evidence from the production, Effect on the audience — use for every analytical point.
Key Fact: Analysis paragraphs: focus on one aspect per paragraph (e.g. performance, then design, then direction).
Key Fact: Conclusion: evaluate the overall effectiveness — did the production achieve its intention? Sum up your judgement.
Key Fact: Analytical style: precise terminology, specific moments, supported judgements — never vague or subjective language.

Q1: Describe the structure of a theatre review for the GCSE written exam.

Q2: What is the SEE framework and how does it improve your written analysis?

Q3: How can you ensure your theatre review is analytical rather than just descriptive?

✅ Answers

  1. Introduction (production, company, venue, intention) → Performance analysis paragraph (specific vocal/physical choices) → Design analysis paragraph (specific set/lighting/sound/costume choices) → Direction analysis paragraph (staging/pacing) → Conclusion (overall effectiveness judgement linked to intention).
  2. SEE: State the technique used, Evidence from the production (specific moment), Effect on the audience. It improves analysis by forcing you to move beyond description ('the lighting was blue') to analysis ('the blue lighting connoted coldness and isolation, making the audience feel the character's loneliness').
  3. Use precise terminology instead of everyday language. Always explain the MEANING and EFFECT of choices, not just what you saw. Make judgements about effectiveness. Support every claim with evidence. Replace 'I liked it' with 'The designer's choice was effective because...'

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Introduction: name production, company, venue, date — essential facts first
  • Every paragraph must contain both ANALYSIS and EVALUATION
  • Use SEE for every point — State, Evidence, Effect
  • Conclusion: judge overall effectiveness against the production's stated intention

📝 Exam Technique

Drama Exam Tips:
Time management for Section B (live theatre): 5 minutes planning (which moments will you write about?), 30 minutes writing (structured paragraphs using SEE), 5 minutes checking (terminology, evidence, judgements). Plan your key moments BEFORE you start writing to ensure you have enough material.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: A theatre review should start with your overall opinion and then give reasons why you liked or disliked the show. Correct: A theatre review should start with FACTUAL CONTEXT (production, company, venue, intention) and then provide ANALYSIS and EVALUATION of specific choices. Your opinion only matters when it is supported by evidence and linked to the production's intention. The examiner wants to see analytical skill, not personal preference.

Detailed Notes

Structure and Content of a Theatre Review

A GCSE theatre review must be structured, analytical and evaluative, quite different from a subjective personal response. The review should open with a brief contextual introduction (production title, theatre, director, date), then analyse specific aspects of the production in organised paragraphs, and conclude with an overall evaluation of the production's effectiveness. Each analytical paragraph should follow the Point-Evidence-Explain-Evaluate structure: make a specific claim about the production, provide precise evidence from the performance, explain how the technique created meaning, and evaluate its effectiveness on the audience. UK newspaper reviewers like Arifa Akbar (The Guardian) and Nick Curtis (The Standard) provide excellent models of structured, evidence-based theatre criticism.

Content should cover the elements specified by the exam question, which typically asks about performance skills, design elements, or directorial choices. A strong review demonstrates theatrical understanding by using precise vocabulary (not 'the set was nice' but 'the traverse staging created an intimate, confrontational atmosphere') and by connecting production choices to the play's themes and the audience's experience. GCSE students should avoid plot summary (the examiner knows the story) and focus instead on how the production realised the text. The review should be specific enough that the examiner can visualise the moments described: naming specific scenes, describing exact lighting states, quoting particular line deliveries.

Example

A GCSE student writes: 'The set design by Rae Smith transformed the Lyric Hammersmith's stage into a war-torn landscape using debris-strewn earth and shattered walls that towered over the performers. This claustrophobic environment effectively communicated the theme of entrapment, as characters navigated narrow paths between rubble, their restricted movement physically embodying their lack of choices in wartime.'

Writing About Performance Skills

Writing about performance skills requires precise observation and theatrical vocabulary. Vocal skills to discuss include: projection (could the actor be heard clearly?), articulation (was every word intelligible?), pace and rhythm (did the vocal delivery match the character's emotional state?), accent and dialect (was the accent consistent and appropriate?), and vocal contrast (did the actor vary their delivery to maintain interest?). Physical skills include: spatial awareness (how did the actor use the stage space?), physical characterisation (did the body communicate character consistently?), interaction with others (was the actor genuinely responsive to scene partners?), and stillness and movement (were both used effectively?).

The most perceptive GCSE writing about performance analyses how vocal and physical skills work together to create holistic characterisation. Rather than treating voice and body as separate elements, the student identifies how they combine: 'When the Inspector first appeared, his physical stillness (standing motionless centre stage, hands clasped behind his back) combined with his measured, quiet vocal delivery to create an unsettling authority. The stillness prevented the audience from reading his emotions, whilst the quiet voice forced them to lean in, creating a powerful sense of controlled menace that made his eventual direct address to the audience feel genuinely threatening.'

Example

A GCSE student writing about a performer's creation of Lady Macbeth identifies three specific moments: the 'unsex me here' soliloquy (vocal intensity increased through ascending pitch and accelerating pace), the sleepwalking scene (physical disintegration shown through uncontrolled tremor and unfocused gaze), and the banquet scene (vocal control masking physical anxiety, creating dramatic irony where the audience sees what the other characters cannot).

Review Writing Techniques and Exam Preparation

Effective theatre reviews demonstrate several key writing techniques: precise descriptive language (avoiding vague adjectives like 'good' or 'interesting'), comparative analysis (how does this production compare to others of the same text or by the same company?), contextual awareness (how do the production choices relate to the play's historical, social or political context?), and balanced judgement (acknowledging both strengths and weaknesses rather than unqualified praise or criticism). UK professional critics like Kate Maltby and Holly Williams provide accessible models of this balanced, evidence-based approach that GCSE students can study.

Exam preparation for the live theatre review should include: organising notes from the performance into structured categories (performance, design, direction), selecting the strongest 4-5 moments for detailed analysis, practising timed writing using past questions, and developing a personal vocabulary list of precise theatrical terms. Students should be prepared to write about any element the question specifies, so their notes must cover performance, design and direction comprehensively. AQA's live theatre question is worth 32 marks and requires approximately 40 minutes of writing, so students must be efficient: planning for 3 minutes, writing for 35 minutes, and checking for 2 minutes.

Example

A student preparing for the live theatre exam creates a structured notes document organised by element: Performance (3 specific moments with vocal/physical analysis for each), Design (3 specific moments with set/costume/lighting/sound analysis), Direction (2 specific moments with interpretive choices and their effectiveness). This preparation allows them to answer any question the exam sets, drawing from their pre-selected evidence.
