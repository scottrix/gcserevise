DR16: Performing a Character from a Script

Performing a Character from a Script

Vocal and physical skills for scripted performance; line learning and delivery; intention and actioning; maintaining character; truthfulness in performance; performing for an examiner.

Key Fact: Actioning: assigning a transitive verb to each line — 'I WARN', 'I BEG', 'I THREATEN' — gives every line a purpose and energy.
Key Fact: Vocal skills: pace, pitch, pause, volume, tone, emphasis, accent — each must be chosen deliberately for character and situation.
Key Fact: Physical skills: posture, gesture, facial expression, proxemics, eye contact, movement — the body communicates as much as the voice.
Key Fact: Line learning: learn lines EARLY so you can focus on delivery, not recall. Learn intention, not just words.
Key Fact: Truthfulness: the audience must believe the character — this comes from understanding motivation, not from 'acting' emotions.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain what actioning is and how it helps an actor deliver lines more effectively.

Q2: Describe the vocal and physical skills you would use to perform a character experiencing a crisis of confidence.

Q3: How can an actor maintain truthfulness in performance without simply 'acting' emotions?

✅ Answers

  1. Actioning assigns a transitive verb to each line (I CONVINCE, I INTIMIDATE, I PLEAD). It gives every line a purpose, prevents flat delivery, and ensures the actor is always DOING something to the other character, not just saying words.
  2. Vocal: quieter volume, hesitant pace, rising pitch on questions, long pauses before responses. Physical: closed posture, avoiding eye contact, fidgeting hands, retreating spatially. Each choice justified by the character's internal state.
  3. Truthfulness comes from understanding what the character WANTS and pursuing it honestly, not from pretending to feel emotions. If you focus on the objective and the action, the emotion follows naturally. Stanislavski: play the action, not the feeling.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Actioning verbs must be TRANSITIVE (done TO someone): I WARN, not 'I am angry'
  • Always link vocal/physical choices to CHARACTER MOTIVATION
  • Performing for an examiner: confidence, energy, projection and focus from the start
  • Never describe emotions — describe the ACTIONS that show the emotions

📝 Exam Technique

Drama Exam Tips:
For performance questions, always describe specific, concrete choices: 'I would deliver this line at a slower pace, with dropped volume, avoiding eye contact with the other character and moving USR to create physical distance — this shows the character's withdrawal and loss of confidence.'

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Good acting is about showing strong emotions convincingly — you need to really feel the sadness or anger to perform well. Correct: Good acting is about pursuing objectives truthfully. If you focus on 'showing sadness', the performance looks fake. If you focus on what the character WANTS and the OBSTACLES they face, genuine emotion emerges naturally. Stanislavski's system is about action, not emotion. Act with purpose, not feeling.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe how you would use vocal and physical skills to perform a key moment for a character from a play you have studied. Justify each choice with reference to the character's motivation.

A grade 9 response will: identify the character's objective in the moment; describe specific vocal choices (pace, pitch, pause, emphasis); describe specific physical choices (posture, gesture, proxemics); explain how each choice reveals character and motivation; show understanding of the scene's context; evaluate the intended effect on the audience.

Detailed Notes

Performing Character from Script

Performing a character from a scripted text requires the actor to inhabit a role created by another mind whilst bringing their own truth and creativity to the interpretation. The starting point is always the text: what the playwright tells us about the character through dialogue, stage directions and other characters' references. However, the text provides only the skeleton; the actor must add flesh through imagination, research and rehearsal. GCSE students must balance fidelity to the playwright's intentions with the creative interpretation that makes a performance feel alive rather than merely recited. UK actor Mark Rylance exemplifies this balance, creating performances that are deeply faithful to the text yet startlingly original in interpretation.

The relationship between the actor and the role is central to performance quality. An actor who simply 'plays themselves' in every role lacks range; an actor who mimics a stereotypical version of the character lacks truth. The goal is to find the character within oneself, identifying personal connections that make the character's behaviour feel genuine rather than performed. Stanislavski's 'magic if' is the key tool: rather than asking 'How would this character behave?', the actor asks 'What would I do if I were in these circumstances?' This subtle shift ensures that the performance emerges from genuine personal engagement rather than external observation. GCSE examiners assess the quality of characterisation, looking for truthfulness, consistency and detail.

Example

A GCSE student performing Eric Birling draws on their own experience of making a mistake they were afraid to confess to their parents. They do not mimic Eric's behaviour from outside but use the 'magic if' to find genuine feelings of guilt, shame and relief within themselves, creating a performance that feels emotionally authentic rather than technically calculated.

Vocal and Physical Performance Techniques

Vocal performance for scripted text requires precision, variety and control. Key vocal techniques include: projection (filling the performance space without shouting), articulation (ensuring every consonant is crisp and every vowel is clear), pace variation (using speed changes to create emphasis and maintain interest), pitch variation (using the full range of the voice rather than speaking at one monotonous level), pause (using silence for dramatic effect), and emphasis (stressing key words to clarify meaning and intention). The National Theatre's voice coaches recommend warming up the voice before every performance with breathing exercises, resonance work and articulation drills. GCSE students should demonstrate vocal warm-up as part of their preparation routine.

Physical performance requires awareness of the whole body as an instrument of communication. Key physical techniques include: stillness (the ability to hold a position with purpose and presence), gesture (hand and arm movements that are specific to the character rather than habitual to the actor), facial expression (communicating thought and emotion through the face), proxemics (using spatial relationship to communicate character dynamics), and physical transformation (changing posture, gait and centre of gravity to become the character). Laban's effort actions (punch, slash, dab, glide, press, wring, flick, float) provide a useful vocabulary for describing and refining physical choices. GCSE students performing scripted text must demonstrate that their physical and vocal choices are integrated, creating unified characterisation.

Example

A GCSE student performing a monologue from DNA uses the Laban effort action 'press' (sustained, strong, direct) for Phil's controlled manipulation of the group, shifting to 'wring' (sustained, strong, indirect) when Phil's certainty begins to crack. This physical vocabulary, combined with a vocal delivery that moves from quiet authority to strained uncertainty, creates a nuanced characterisation that communicates Phil's psychological complexity.

Interaction and Relationship in Performance

Acting is fundamentally about interaction: characters exist in relation to each other, and the quality of a performance depends significantly on how performers respond to one another. Active listening is the most important skill: genuinely hearing what the other actor says and responding authentically, rather than simply waiting to deliver the next line. The American teacher Sanford Meisner developed repetition exercises that train this responsiveness, and UK actor training increasingly incorporates Meisner technique. GCSE students who genuinely listen and respond create scenes with spontaneous energy that examiners recognise as high-quality acting.

Status transactions, developed by Keith Johnstone, describe the constant negotiation of power between characters in every interaction. In a scene from An Inspector Calls, the status dynamic shifts continuously: Mr Birling starts with high status (successful businessman, head of family), but the Inspector systematically undermines him, and by the end Birling's status is demolished. GCSE performers must track these status shifts and communicate them physically and vocally: high status characters speak slowly, make direct eye contact and occupy central space; low status characters speak quickly, avoid eye contact and position at the margins. Understanding and performing status transactions transforms flat, static scenes into dynamic, engaging drama.

Example

In a GCSE performance of an extract from Blood Brothers, the two actors playing Mickey and Eddie track their changing status through the scene: Eddie begins with high status (confident, educated) and Mickey with low status (unemployed, desperate). As the scene progresses and Mickey reveals his pain, the audience's sympathy shifts, subtly elevating Mickey's emotional status whilst Eddie's material privilege becomes a source of discomfort rather than power. The performers communicate this shift through subtle changes in eye contact, posture and vocal pace.
