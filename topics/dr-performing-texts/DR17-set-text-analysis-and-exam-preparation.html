Set Text Analysis and Exam Preparation

Detailed study of set text for written exam; approaching extract questions; understanding the full play context; character, theme and staging analysis; structuring written exam responses.

Key Fact: Know the WHOLE play, not just the extracts — context matters for every question.
Key Fact: Extract questions: analyse the specific scene AND connect it to the whole play's themes and structure.
Key Fact: Three perspectives: you may be asked from performer, director or designer viewpoint — always answer from the specified perspective.
Key Fact: Structure your answer: point (identify technique/choice) + evidence (specific reference) + explanation (effect on audience) + link (to whole play themes).
Key Fact: Time management: Section A is 45 minutes — plan for 5 minutes, write for 35 minutes, check for 5 minutes.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: How would you approach a written exam question asking you to describe how you would perform an extract from your set text?

Q2: Why is it important to understand the whole play, even though the exam only asks about extracts?

Q3: Describe how you would answer a question from a director's perspective about staging a key scene.

✅ Answers

  1. Read the extract carefully; identify the key dramatic moment; choose a perspective (performer/director/designer as asked); make specific staging/performance choices; justify each with reference to the text; link to the play's themes and context.
  2. Extracts are shaped by the whole play — character motivations come from earlier scenes, themes run throughout, the extract's significance depends on its place in the overall structure. Without whole-play knowledge, your analysis will be superficial.
  3. Describe staging choices (stage type, positioning, levels); explain design choices (set, lighting, costume); justify how these communicate the scene's meaning; link to the director's concept and the play's themes; use precise theatrical vocabulary.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Read the question TWICE — check which perspective is required
  • Always refer to SPECIFIC moments in the extract — never write generically
  • Link extract choices to the WHOLE play — context marks are essential
  • Use PEEL paragraphs: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link

📝 Exam Technique

Drama Exam Tips:
For the written exam, use the SEE framework for each point: State your choice (e.g. 'I would position the character DS centre'), Evidence from the text ('because at this moment they are confronting the truth'), Effect on audience ('creating maximum focus and intimacy'), Link to theme ('which reflects the play's central theme of honesty').

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: For the written exam, you just need to learn the story of the play and describe what happens in the extract. Correct: The written exam tests your ABILITY TO REALISE the text, not just recall the plot. You must make specific performance, design or directorial choices, justify them with textual evidence, and link them to the whole play's themes. Describing what happens scores low marks; explaining how you would bring it to life scores high marks.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

As a performer, describe how you would use vocal and physical skills to realise a key moment in the given extract. Justify your choices with reference to the whole play.

A grade 9 response will: identify the key dramatic moment; describe specific vocal choices with justification; describe specific physical choices with justification; explain the intended effect on the audience; link choices to the character's development across the whole play; connect to the play's central themes; use precise theatrical terminology throughout.

Detailed Notes

Set Text Analysis Techniques

GCSE set text analysis requires a systematic approach that uncovers how the playwright creates meaning through structure, character, language and theatrical devices. Begin with an overview: what is the play about (theme), who is it about (character), and what happens (plot)? Then analyse the structure: how is the narrative organised, where are the key turning points, how does tension build and resolve? Next, examine the characters: what are their objectives, what obstacles do they face, how do they change? Finally, analyse the language: what stylistic features does the playwright use, what is distinctive about the dialogue, what dramatic devices are employed? UK exam boards require students to demonstrate understanding of how the play works as a text for performance, not merely as a literary work.

Extract analysis for GCSE exams requires focused attention on a short section of the text. Students should identify: the given circumstances of the extract (what has just happened, what is happening, what will happen next), the characters' objectives in the extract (what each character wants from the others), the key dramatic devices used (irony, symbolism, contrast, climax), the language features (register, pace, repetition, imagery), and the performance potential (how the extract could be staged, what design and directorial choices it suggests). This analytical framework produces comprehensive, well-organised responses that examiners can credit across multiple assessment objectives.

Example

A GCSE student analysing the opening of An Inspector Calls identifies: Priestley establishes a celebratory atmosphere (given circumstances) to create maximum contrast with the Inspector's arrival; Mr Birling's long, complacent speeches (language feature) reveal his arrogance and create dramatic irony; the single ring of the doorbell (dramatic device) shatters the complacent mood and introduces the play's moral reckoning; the scene demands a naturalistic performance style with carefully calibrated status shifts as the Inspector takes control.

Exam Preparation and Question Types

GCSE Drama set text questions typically fall into three categories: extract-based questions (analysing a specific section of the text), whole-play questions (evaluating a theme or character across the entire play), and performance questions (proposing how a section could be staged or performed). Each type requires a different approach but all demand textual evidence, analytical thinking and theatrical understanding. Extract questions require close reading of the set passage; whole-play questions require breadth of reference across multiple scenes; performance questions require practical staging knowledge. Students should prepare for all three types by annotating their text with key quotations, character arcs and structural turning points.

Effective revision for the set text exam involves: re-reading the play multiple times (each reading with a different focus), creating character maps (tracking relationships and arcs), identifying key quotations for each theme and character, practising timed essay responses, and studying past mark schemes to understand what examiners reward. AQA's set text questions typically ask students to consider the play from both a critical and a theatrical perspective, analysing the text as a piece of drama intended for performance rather than merely as a literary text. UK drama teacher standards recommend that students have seen at least one live production of their set text, as this provides invaluable concrete reference points for performance-based questions.

Example

A student preparing for a GCSE exam on DNA creates a character map showing the group's relationships: Phil (leader, manipulative), Leah (conscience, sidelined), John Tate (follower, violent), Brian (victim, scapegoat), Mark and Jan (enforcers). They annotate key quotations for each character and identify three key moments of power shift in the play, preparing analysis for each that they can adapt to whatever the exam question asks.

Practitioner Application to Set Texts

Applying practitioner theory to set texts demonstrates sophisticated understanding that GCSE examiners reward at higher grades. For each set text, students should consider how different practitioners would approach the play: a Stanislavskian production would emphasise psychological truth, given circumstances and emotion memory; a Brechtian production would use V-effects, placards and gestus to expose social themes; an Artaudian approach would use sensory assault and physical intensity to create visceral impact; a Frantic Assembly approach would physicalise the relationships through dynamic movement. The key is not to describe the practitioner's theory generically but to apply it specifically to the text being studied.

For GCSE exams, practitioner application questions typically ask how a specific practitioner's approach would influence the staging or performance of a section of the set text. Strong responses identify specific techniques from the practitioner's repertoire and explain exactly how they would be applied to the specific text, with reasoned justification of why the technique would be effective for communicating the play's themes. For example, applying Brecht's gestus to An Inspector Calls: when Mr Birling celebrates his knighthood whilst discussing the low wages he pays his workers, the gestus simultaneously reveals his social status and his economic exploitation, creating a moment of political clarity that Brecht would want the audience to analyse critically. UK drama teachers report that students who can apply practitioner theory with specific textual reference achieve significantly higher grades than those who write generically about the practitioner.

Example

A GCSE student applying Frantic Assembly to DNA identifies that the group dynamics could be physicalised through push-pull sequences: Phil physically directs the group's movement, pushing members into position, whilst Leah attempts to pull them away from Phil's control. This physical embodiment of the power dynamics makes the psychological manipulation visible and visceral, creating a production that communicates the play's themes through the body as well as the text.
