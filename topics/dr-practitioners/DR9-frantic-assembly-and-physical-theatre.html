Frantic Assembly and Physical Theatre

Physical theatre conventions; Frantic Assembly's techniques: chair work, push hands, lift and lean, hymns hands, unison, counterpoint; devising through movement; DV8 Physical Theatre.

Key Fact: Physical theatre: the body is the primary means of expression — movement communicates meaning beyond words.
Key Fact: Frantic Assembly's chair work: using chairs as the central prop — creates dynamic, rhythmic, often aggressive sequences.
Key Fact: Push hands: two performers pushing against each other's hands — creates tension, balance and relational dynamics.
Key Fact: Lift and lean: one performer supports another's weight — requires trust, shows relationship and dependency.
Key Fact: Unison and counterpoint: moving together (unison) shows unity; moving in opposition (counterpoint) shows conflict or individuality.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe three Frantic Assembly techniques and explain the dramatic effect each can create.

Q2: How can physical theatre communicate emotions and relationships more effectively than dialogue alone?

Q3: You are creating a devised piece about conflict. How would you use physical theatre techniques to explore this theme?

✅ Answers

  1. Three named techniques with dramatic effect: chair work (rhythm, aggression, status); push hands (tension, balance of power); lift and lean (trust, dependency, vulnerability). Each linked to specific dramatic meaning.
  2. Physical theatre bypasses language to communicate directly through the body — proximity, touch, weight and rhythm reveal relationship dynamics instantly. It can show conflict, intimacy or power shifts without a single word.
  3. Practical response showing: push hands to show conflict escalation; unison breaking into counterpoint to show unity fracturing; chair work to create aggressive confrontations; lift/lean to show shifting power. Each linked to the theme of conflict.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Name Frantic Assembly techniques precisely — 'chair duet', not 'chairs scene'
  • Physical theatre is about MEANING through MOVEMENT — not just 'it looks cool'
  • Always link physical choices to the emotional/relational content
  • Safety and trust are essential when discussing contact work

📝 Exam Technique

Drama Exam Tips:
For physical theatre questions, describe movement PRECISELY: who moves, how they move, what it represents, and the effect. 'Two performers stand close, hands pressed together, pushing rhythmically — the counter-tension represents their competing desires, the increasing force shows escalating conflict.'

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Physical theatre is just dancing — it doesn't have the same dramatic depth as scripted dialogue. Correct: Physical theatre uses movement as a primary language for dramatic meaning. Frantic Assembly's techniques communicate complex emotions and relationships through the body. Movement can be MORE powerful than words for showing conflict, intimacy and power because it bypasses intellectual processing and hits the audience viscerally.

Detailed Notes

Frantic Assembly's Physical Theatre Method

Frantic Assembly, founded in 1994 by Scott Graham, Steven Hoggett and Vicki Middleton, has become one of the UK's most influential physical theatre companies. Their technique combines dynamic physical movement with text and design to create visually striking, emotionally charged theatre. Unlike dance theatre, which prioritises choreographic beauty, Frantic Assembly's movement derives directly from character and dramatic situation: every lift, push, fall and counter-balance tells a story about the relationship between the performers. Their work on The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time (National Theatre) won the Olivier Award for Best New Play and demonstrated how physical theatre techniques can serve narrative drama at the highest level. GCSE students should understand that Frantic Assembly's physical vocabulary is accessible and can be adapted for school production work.

Frantic Assembly's devising process begins with physical tasks rather than text. Performers explore a theme through movement exercises, building a physical vocabulary that communicates character, relationship and emotion before any dialogue is introduced. Key techniques include: chair duets (two performers on chairs creating intimate relationship narratives through linked movement), push-pull sequences (performers physically supporting and resisting each other to explore power dynamics), and lift and counter-balance work (creating moments of trust, vulnerability and connection). These techniques are documented in Frantic Assembly's book The Frantic Assembly Book of Devising Theatre, which provides step-by-step guides that GCSE students can follow directly in their practical work.

Example

A GCSE group devising a piece about friendship uses Frantic Assembly's chair duet technique: two performers sit on adjacent chairs and develop a sequence of 8-10 linked movements that communicate their relationship (shared rhythm suggests closeness, an interrupted movement suggests tension, a physical push away suggests conflict). The sequence is repeated throughout the piece, with each repetition modified to reflect how the friendship has changed, creating a powerful physical through-line.

Key Productions and Techniques

Frantic Assembly's key productions demonstrate how physical theatre techniques serve diverse dramatic purposes. Beautiful Burnout (2006), created with playwright Bryony Lavery, used boxing training movements to explore the world of young Scottish boxers, with punch combinations becoming a physical language for ambition and self-destruction. Othello: Remixed (2015) used hip-hop and parkour movement to reimagine Shakespeare's tragedy for a contemporary urban context. The Believers (2023) explored religious faith through communal physical rituals. Each production begins with extended physical workshops where the company develops a movement vocabulary specific to the production's world, ensuring that the physicality is organic rather than imposed.

For GCSE practical work, students can adapt Frantic Assembly's approach by creating movement sequences that emerge from the specific world of their piece. A piece about exam stress might use repetitive writing gestures, clock-watching movements and chair-swinging restlessness as its physical vocabulary. A piece about family dynamics might use lift and counter-balance sequences to represent the shifting weight of responsibility between family members. The key principle is that physical movement must be motivated by character and situation, not simply decorative. UK schools increasingly use Frantic Assembly's published workshop resources to teach physical devising techniques that meet GCSE assessment criteria for demonstrating understanding of practitioner approaches.

Example

A GCSE student creating a solo piece about competitive swimming adapts Frantic Assembly's physical principles: a repetitive arm-stroke sequence establishes the swimmer's dedication, the sequence accelerates to suggest intensifying pressure, and eventually breaks down into exhausted, irregular movements that communicate the physical and mental toll of elite sport. The movement journey tells the story without any dialogue.

Physical Theatre in the UK Context

Physical theatre in the UK encompasses a range of companies and approaches beyond Frantic Assembly. DV8 Physical Theatre, founded by Lloyd Newson, creates work that combines movement with provocative social and political content, including Can We Talk About This? (examining multiculturalism and freedom of speech) and John (exploring addiction and sexuality). Gecko Theatre, founded by Amit Lahav, creates visually spectacular ensemble work like The Time of Your Life and Institute that uses physical metaphor to explore human experience. Complicite, led by Simon McBurney, creates intellectually rigorous physical theatre including The Master and Margarita and A Disappearing Number. These companies represent a distinctive British contribution to the international physical theatre movement.

The UK's physical theatre tradition draws on multiple influences: Jacques Lecoq's movement pedagogy (taught at the Lecoq school in Paris where many British practitioners trained), Grotowski's poor theatre principles, and the long tradition of British popular performance from music hall to circus to street theatre. GCSE students studying physical theatre should understand this lineage and be able to place Frantic Assembly's work in the broader context of British and European physical theatre. The University of Kent's drama department has been particularly influential in developing physical theatre research and training in the UK, and many current GCSE drama teachers were trained in physical theatre approaches at UK universities.

Example

A GCSE group creating a piece about migration draws on Gecko Theatre's approach, using the physical metaphor of a journey across a treacherous landscape created through performers' bodies (some form mountains, others form barriers, one becomes a river to be crossed). This extended physical metaphor communicates the experience of migration without reducing it to a single narrative, embodying Gecko's principle that 'the body knows things that the mind has not yet articulated.'
