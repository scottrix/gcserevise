Character Creation and Development

Character motivation, objectives, super-objectives, status, relationships, character arcs, empathy versus sympathy, stereotypes versus archetypes, multi-roling, characterization techniques.

Key Fact: Every character has a motivation — what they WANT drives every action and line.
Key Fact: Super-objective: the character's overarching goal across the whole play; scene objectives are smaller goals within each scene.
Key Fact: Status: the power dynamic between characters — it shifts moment to moment and is shown through body language, position and voice.
Key Fact: Character arc: how a character changes from beginning to end — static characters stay the same, dynamic characters transform.
Key Fact: Multi-roling: one actor playing multiple parts — requires clear physical and vocal differentiation between roles.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between a character's objective and their super-objective, using a character from a play you have studied.

Q2: How does an actor communicate a character's status change through performance? Give specific examples.

Q3: What is the effect of multi-roling on the audience? How must the actor prepare for this challenge?

✅ Answers

  1. Objective is the immediate want in a scene (e.g. to win an argument); super-objective is the overarching goal (e.g. to gain power). The character's scene objectives serve the super-objective.
  2. Status is shown through: body language (high status = open, still; low status = closed, fidgety); spatial positioning (higher = more powerful); vocal qualities (pace, pitch, volume). Give a specific example of a status shift.
  3. Multi-roling creates connections between characters, suggests social types, and challenges the audience. The actor must use clear physical and vocal switches — costume pieces, posture, accent, gesture.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always identify what a character WANTS — their motivation drives everything
  • Track status shifts within scenes — they reveal power dynamics
  • For multi-roling questions, discuss both the ACTOR's technique and the DIRECTOR's intention
  • Character arcs matter — show how characters change across the whole play

📝 Exam Technique

Drama Exam Tips:
When analysing character, use the motivation-objective-action chain: What does the character want? What do they do to get it? What prevents them? How do they change? This systematic approach covers all assessment objectives.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: A character's personality is fixed — they are either good or bad throughout the play. Correct: Characters are complex and can change. Their status shifts moment to moment; their understanding grows; their objectives clash with other characters'. The best character analysis tracks development and contradictions, not fixed labels.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe the character arc of a character from a play you have studied. How does the playwright use this character to communicate a key theme?

A grade 9 response will: track the character's journey from start to finish; identify key turning points; explain how the character's changes (or refusal to change) reflect the play's themes; use precise quotations or references; evaluate the playwright's purpose.

Mark scheme: Character analysis tests AO1 (knowledge of character terminology), AO2 (analysing how character communicates meaning), a...

Detailed Notes

Building a Character: From Text to Performance

Character creation begins with a thorough analysis of the script to identify the character's given circumstances, motivations, relationships and arc. The given circumstances are the factual information the playwright provides: the character's age, social class, occupation, family situation, historical period and the events that have shaped them before the play begins. Motivations are what the character wants (their objective) in each scene and across the whole play. Relationships define how the character relates to others, revealing power dynamics, emotional bonds and conflicts. The character arc traces the journey from who the character is at the beginning to who they become by the end. For GCSE set texts like An Inspector Calls, mapping Sheila Birling's arc from complicit privilege to moral awakening is central to understanding the play's meaning.

Stanislavski's system provides a structured approach to character building that GCSE students can apply directly. Given circumstances establish the factual foundation. The 'magic if' asks the actor: 'What would I do if I were in this situation?' Emotion memory draws on the actor's own experiences to find authentic emotional connections. Units and objectives break the script into manageable sections, each with a clear purpose. The through-line of action identifies the character's overarching objective that drives them through the entire play. Subtext recognises that what a character says is often different from what they mean. Tempo-rhythm acknowledges that emotional states have characteristic speeds and patterns. UK drama schools including RADA and LAMDA continue to teach Stanislavski's principles as the foundation of character work.

Example

A GCSE student preparing the role of Mrs Birling in An Inspector Calls creates a character profile identifying her given circumstances (upper-middle-class, magistrate, charity committee chair), her super-objective (to maintain social order and family reputation), and her character arc (from confident authority through defensive hostility to stubborn denial). They use emotion memory to connect Mrs Birling's defensive dismissiveness to their own experience of being caught in a mistake they refused to admit.

Physical and Vocal Characterisation

Physical characterisation transforms the actor's body into the character's through posture, gait, gesture and facial expression. A character's status, age, emotional state and social class are communicated physically before any dialogue is spoken. High-status characters occupy more space, move deliberately and maintain stillness; low-status characters make themselves smaller, fidget and avoid direct gaze. Laban's movement analysis (developed by Rudolf Laban, who worked in the UK) categorises movement qualities using four effort factors: weight (light/strong), time (sudden/sustained), space (direct/indirect) and flow (bound/free). Combining these factors creates eight effort actions that actors use to find a character's physical vocabulary.

Vocal characterisation uses pitch, pace, pause, volume, tone and emphasis to create a character's distinctive voice. Accent and dialect communicate geographic and social identity, and UK productions frequently require regional accents to establish character authenticity. Projection ensures the voice reaches the back row without amplification, a fundamental skill for GCSE performance. Articulation ensures every word is clearly heard and understood. The National Theatre's voice department, led by vocal coaches like Cicely Berry, developed techniques for freeing the natural voice that are now standard in UK actor training. GCSE students should demonstrate vocal variety in performance, using pitch and pace changes to communicate shifts in a character's emotional state.

Example

A student performing the role of Eric Birling in An Inspector Calls uses physical characterisation to show his guilt: initially standing with shoulders hunched and weight shifted away from the family group, with a vocal quality that starts loud and dismissive but becomes quieter and more hesitant as the Inspector's questioning intensifies. This physical and vocal journey externalises Eric's internal disintegration.

Character Relationships and Status

Character relationships are the dynamic connections between individuals in a play, defined by power, attraction, conflict and dependence. Status transactions, a concept developed by British theatre practitioner Keith Johnstone, describe how characters constantly negotiate relative power in every interaction. High-status behaviour includes maintaining eye contact, speaking slowly and clearly, and occupying central space. Low-status behaviour includes breaking eye contact, speaking quickly, and positioning at the edges of the stage. In GCSE performance work, understanding status transactions helps actors make scenes dynamic and believable, as every conversation involves subtle shifts in who holds power.

The relationship between characters often provides the central dramatic tension. In DNA by Dennis Kelly, the shifting power dynamic between Phil and Leah drives the group's behaviour. In An Inspector Calls, the Inspector systematically dismantles each character's status position, forcing them from confidence to vulnerability. GCSE students should map character relationships using status diagrams that track how power shifts through each scene, and use this analysis to inform their performance choices. UK director Max Stafford-Clark, founder of Out of Joint theatre company, uses status exercises extensively in rehearsal to help actors understand the power dynamics in a scene.

Example

A student directing a scene from Blood Brothers creates a status map showing how Mickey's status drops as he loses his job and becomes dependent on Eddie, whilst Eddie's status rises through education and wealth. In performance, the actor playing Mickey gradually adopts lower-status physicality (stooped posture, downward gaze), whilst Eddie's physicality becomes more expansive and confident, visually communicating their diverging social trajectories.
