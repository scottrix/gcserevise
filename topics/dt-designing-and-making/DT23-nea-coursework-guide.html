NEA Coursework Guide

AQA NEA structure: investigating, designing, making, analysing and evaluating; portfolio requirements, marking criteria, time management, evidence presentation.

Key Fact: NEA = Non-Exam Assessment, worth 50% of GCSE D&T. Students complete a single design-and-make task from a choice of contextual challenges set by AQA. Approximately 35 hours of supervised time.
Key Fact: NEA structure: Section 1 - Investigating (research, design brief, specification), Section 2 - Designing (ideas, development, modelling), Section 3 - Making (manufacturing the prototype), Section 4 - Analysing and Evaluating (testing against specification, improvements).
Key Fact: Portfolio: A3 format (or digital equivalent), 20-30 pages recommended. Each section must be clearly labelled. Include photographs of making, annotated sketches, test results, evaluation against specification points.
Key Fact: Marking criteria: AO1 - Investigating (10%), AO2 - Designing (20%), AO3 - Making (30%), AO4 - Analysing and Evaluating (15%). Each AO has specific mark bands - read the criteria and target the top band.
Key Fact: Time management: 35 hours total - Investigating approx 8 hrs, Designing approx 10 hrs, Making approx 12 hrs, Evaluating approx 5 hrs. Common mistake: spending too long on research and not enough on making and evaluation.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the four sections of the D&T NEA and what each must include.

Q2: Explain why it is important to evaluate the final prototype against the original specification.

Q3: A student has spent 15 hours on research and has only 5 hours left for making and evaluation. What advice would you give?

✅ Answers

  1. Section 1 - Investigating: research, user needs, existing products, design brief and specification. Section 2 - Designing: initial ideas (annotated sketches), development, modelling, final design. Section 3 - Making: step-by-step manufacture with photographic evidence, quality control, safety. Section 4 - Analysing and Evaluating: testing against each specification point, strengths, weaknesses, improvements.
  2. The specification is the benchmark the product was designed to meet - if you do not test against it, you cannot prove the product succeeds. Each specification point should have: a test method, the result, and a conclusion (met/not met/partially met). This directly addresses AO4 (Analysing and Evaluating) worth 15%.
  3. Stop researching immediately - you have enough data to design. Move to Section 2: generate 3-4 quick concept sketches with annotations, select the best idea, develop it. Section 3: prioritise key functional elements - the prototype needs to work, not be perfect. Section 4: test against specification, suggest two specific improvements. A complete portfolio across all four sections scores better than an incomplete one with excellent research.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Each NEA section must be clearly labelled with headings - moderators need to find evidence quickly
  • Photograph EVERY stage of making - you cannot go back and retake photos later
  • Evaluate against EVERY specification point - missed points mean missed marks

📝 Exam Technique

D&T Exam Tips:
For NEA questions: 1) Identify which section the question relates to, 2) Describe what that section requires, 3) Explain how to present evidence effectively, 4) Link to the marking criteria (AO1-AO4), 5) Give specific practical advice for maximising marks.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The NEA prototype needs to be a perfect, professional-quality product that looks like something from a shop. Correct: The NEA prototype is a proof-of-concept that demonstrates your design works - it does NOT need to look like a manufactured product. Marks are awarded for: demonstrating skills, showing the making process (photographic evidence), quality control, and evaluating the outcome. A working prototype with honest evaluation scores far more highly than a beautiful product with no evidence of the making process.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

A student has chosen the AQA contextual challenge 'Designing for a small space'. Write a suitable design brief, produce a specification with six measurable points, and outline what the student should include in their Section 1 (Investigating) portfolio.

A grade 9 response will: Brief: 'Design and make a compact, multi-functional storage and workspace unit for a student bedroom measuring 3 m x 3 m, that maximises vertical space and can be flat-packed for transport.' Specification: 1) Maximum footprint 600 mm x 400 mm. 2) At least 3 distinct storage zones. 3) Fold-down workspace of at least A3 size. 4) Support at least 15 kg when fully loaded. 5) Flat-pack for transport in a standard car. 6) Materials cost under 60 GBP. Section 1: user research, existing product analysis, anthropometric data, material research, environmental considerations.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AQA D&T 8552: Written exam 50% + NEA 50%. AOs: AO1 Recall (20%), AO2 Apply (30%), AO3 Analyse & evaluate (50%). The NEA specifically assesses AO1 Investigating (10%), AO2 Designing (20%), AO3 Making (30%), AO4 Analysing & Evaluating (15%).

Detailed Notes

NEA Structure and Marking Criteria

The AQA GCSE D&T NEA (Non-Exam Assessment) is worth 50% of the final grade and consists of a single design-and-make task completed in approximately 35 hours of supervised time. The NEA is structured into four sections: Investigating (16 marks), Designing (32 marks), Making (32 marks), and Analysing and Evaluating (12 marks), totalling 92 raw marks. Students choose from three contextual challenges set by AQA each year. The investigation section requires primary and secondary research, a design brief and a specification. The design section requires development sketches, modelling and final design proposals. The making section requires a working prototype with photographic evidence. The evaluation section tests the prototype against the specification and suggests improvements.

Marking criteria reward depth and specificity over breadth. A mid-range investigating response describes existing products, but a high-range response analyses them in detail, identifying specific strengths, weaknesses and opportunities for innovation. A mid-range making response shows basic competence with tools, but a high-range response demonstrates skilled, precise work with appropriate process selection and quality control. Students must read the marking criteria carefully and ensure their portfolio addresses every bullet point at the highest level they can achieve. UK schools typically allocate 12 weeks to the NEA, and time management is critical: a suggested schedule is 3 weeks investigating, 5 weeks designing, 3 weeks making, 1 week evaluating.

Example

A student targeting grade 9 writes an investigation that analyses four existing products using a detailed comparison grid, interviews six target users with recorded responses, and creates a specification with 12 testable points. Each specification point includes a measurable criterion (e.g. 'Must support loads up to 5kg' rather than 'Must be strong'), enabling objective evaluation in the final section.

Portfolio Presentation and Evidence

The NEA portfolio is typically presented as an A3 document (approximately 20 pages) or a digital equivalent. Each page should have a clear heading, organised content and concise annotation. Photographs must be high-quality, well-lit and clearly show the relevant detail. Sketches should be annotated with specific design decisions rather than generic labels. Manufacturing processes should be documented step-by-step with photographs and process notes. The portfolio is a communication document: if the moderator cannot understand what you did and why, the marks cannot be awarded, however good the physical prototype may be.

Digital portfolios are increasingly accepted by AQA, offering advantages including video evidence of testing, interactive 3D CAD models and easy image annotation. However, digital portfolios must still be concise and well-organised. Students should avoid including excessive research material that is not directly relevant to their design decisions. The NEA is assessed on the quality of analysis and decision-making, not the quantity of information gathered. Each page should earn its place by demonstrating specific marking criteria achievement. UK moderator feedback consistently identifies the most common weakness as insufficient development: students present a single design idea without showing the iterative exploration that demonstrates genuine designing.

Example

A student presents their NEA on 18 A3 pages, with each page focused on one marking criterion: pages 1-4 cover investigating (research, analysis, brief, specification), pages 5-12 cover designing (initial concepts, development, modelling, final design with technical drawing), pages 13-16 cover making (process sequence with photos, quality control notes), and pages 17-18 cover evaluating (testing against specification, improvements).

Common NEA Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

The most common NEA mistakes identified by UK moderators include: copying research without analysis (low marks because the student has not demonstrated their own thinking), writing vague specifications (e.g. 'must be lightweight' without specifying a target weight), presenting a single design without development (missing the iterative design process that earns higher marks), poor quality making (rushed work with visible defects), and weak evaluation (simply stating the product is good without testing it against specification points). Each of these pitfalls can be avoided by reading the marking criteria before starting and checking progress against the criteria at each stage.

Time management is the single biggest factor in NEA success. Students who fall behind in the investigating stage rush the making and evaluating stages, losing marks in these sections. UK teachers recommend setting internal deadlines for each section and sticking to them ruthlessly. Another common error is over-ambition: choosing a product that requires skills or processes beyond the student's capability or the school's resources. A simpler product executed to a high standard will always score more highly than a complex product poorly made. Students should discuss their design proposals with their teacher before committing to ensure the project is achievable within the time and resources available.

Example

A student who initially planned to design a motorised wheelchair ramp (complex electronics, heavy-duty mechanism, safety-critical) instead refines their project to a manual lightweight folding ramp (mechanical focus, achievable with school workshop processes, testable with realistic loads). The simpler project allows them to achieve higher marks across all criteria because they can produce a fully functional, well-finished prototype rather than an incomplete ambitious design.
