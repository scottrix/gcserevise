Selection of Materials and Components

Material selection criteria (function, aesthetics, cost, availability, sustainability, manufacturability); decision matrices; justified material choices.

Key Fact: Selection criteria: function, aesthetics, cost (material + processing + assembly), availability, sustainability (recyclability, material miles), manufacturability.
Key Fact: Decision matrix: weighted scoring system - list criteria, assign weights, score each option, multiply weight x score, total reveals best choice. Removes bias.
Key Fact: Working properties must match function: frying pan handle needs thermal insulation (thermoset/silicone), not conductivity (metal). Bicycle frame needs strength-to-weight (aluminium/CFRP).
Key Fact: Batch vs mass production affects material choice: mass production justifies injection moulding tooling; batch uses CNC or hand-laid composites.
Key Fact: Environmental considerations: full life cycle - extraction, processing, use phase, end of life. Cradle-to-cradle design aims for zero waste.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Create a simple decision matrix to compare acrylic, polycarbonate and glass for a display cabinet door.

Q2: A designer must choose between aluminium and polypropylene for a kitchen utensil handle. Justify using at least four criteria.

Q3: Explain how production volume (batch vs mass) influences material selection for a plastic product.

✅ Answers

  1. Criteria with weights: Safety (impact resistance) 5, Transparency 4, Cost 3, Weight 3, Scratch resistance 2. Acrylic: 58. Polycarbonate: 62. Glass: 50. Winner: polycarbonate (best safety and transparency despite higher cost).
  2. Function: handle must insulate - PP wins over aluminium. Weight: PP lightweight, aluminium heavier. Cost: PP cheap and easy to mould; aluminium requires die-casting. Durability: aluminium resists staining; PP may stain. Recommendation: PP for budget, aluminium with silicone overmould for premium.
  3. Mass production (100,000+ units): justifies expensive injection mould tooling - thermoplastics very cheap per unit. Batch (50-500 units): tooling cost per unit too high - use CNC, vacuum forming, or 3D printing.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Justify with SPECIFIC criteria - 'tensile strength of 500 MPa exceeds the requirement of 200 MPa'
  • Use a decision matrix when asked to compare or evaluate
  • Consider at least 4 criteria: function, cost, sustainability and one other

📝 Exam Technique

D&T Exam Tips:
For material selection: 1) Identify relevant criteria, 2) Weight by importance, 3) Score each option, 4) Total and compare, 5) State justified conclusion. This demonstrates AO3 evaluation.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The cheapest material is always the best choice because it maximises profit. Correct: Initial cost is only one factor. A cheap material may fail in use (warranty claims), require expensive processing, have limited availability, or be unsustainable. Total cost includes material + processing + assembly + QC + returns + disposal. A more expensive material may reduce total cost by simplifying processing.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

A company is designing a reusable water bottle for the premium market. Using a decision matrix, compare stainless steel, glass and Tritan across at least five criteria.

A grade 9 response will: identify criteria with weights - Durability 5, Aesthetics 4, Sustainability 4, Cost 3, Weight 3, Hygiene 3; score each; stainless steel: 84; glass: 72; Tritan: 70; conclude: stainless steel recommended for premium market.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AQA D&T 8552: Written exam 50% + NEA 50%. AOs: AO1 Recall (20%), AO2 Apply (30%), AO3 Analyse & evaluate (50%). For grade 9, demonstrate systematic decision-making using weighted criteria and justified conclusions.
