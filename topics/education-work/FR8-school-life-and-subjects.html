❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Write 4 sentences in French about school life and subjects.

Q2: Write a paragraph (40-60 words) giving your opinion on school life and subjects.

Q3: Translate into French: "I think school life and subjects is important because..."

✅ Answers

  1. Four sentences using topic vocabulary, with correct adjective agreement, articles and verb conjugation. Include at least two different tenses for Higher tier.
  2. A structured paragraph with: introduction (à mon avis...), opinion with reason (je pense que... parce que...), a different viewpoint (cependant...), and a conclusion (en conclusion...). Use connecting words and varied vocabulary.
  3. "Je pense que [topic] est important(e) parce que..." — ensure adjective agreement (important vs importante depending on noun gender), correct verb form, and give a specific reason.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Use a range of tenses (present, past, future) to access higher marks
  • Include opinions with reasons (je pense que... parce que...)
  • Check adjective agreement (gender and number) before finishing
  • Use complex structures: negation (ne...pas, ne...jamais), pronouns (le, la, les), conjunctions (cependant, donc, en plus)
  • Include cultural references to French-speaking countries
  • For writing: plan your response, check verb conjugations, re-read for errors

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse the conditional and future tenses. Wrong: Je voudrais (means "I would like" not "I will"). Correct: Je voudrais être = I would like to be (conditional); je serai = I will be (future). Students also forget to use the infinitive after modal verbs. Wrong: Je dois fais mes devoirs. Correct: Je dois faire mes devoirs.

Les Matières Scolaires et le Système Éducatif

School subjects in French: les mathématiques (maths), le français (French), l'anglais (English), les sciences (sciences), l'histoire-géographie (history-geography — taught as one subject in France), les SVT (sciences de la vie et de la Terre — biology/geology), la physique-chimie (physics-chemistry — also combined), les arts plastiques (art), l'EPS (sport). Note how French combines some subjects that are separate in the UK.

The French school system is highly structured. L'école primaire (ages 6–11) is followed by le collège (ages 11–15), where all students follow the same curriculum. Then le lycée (ages 15–18) offers different pathways: général (academic), technologique, or professionnel (vocational). Since 2021, the baccalauréat has been reformed — students now choose three spécialités in première and keep two in terminale, rather than the old S/ES/L streams.

The school day is different too: French students typically have une pause déjeuner of 1.5–2 hours, and many go home for lunch (or eat at la cantine). Le mercredi après-midi is often free for younger students. School generally runs from 8h to 17h with longer days but fewer days per year than the UK.

Exemple

Au collège, on étudie le français, les maths, l'histoire-géo, les SVT et la physique-chimie. Le déjeuner dure presque deux heures! — At collège, you study French, maths, history-geography, SVT and physics-chemistry. Lunch lasts almost two hours!

La Vie au Lycée et le Baccalauréat

The baccalauréat (le bac) is the defining exam of French secondary education. Unlike GCSEs and A-Levels, it is a single national exam taken at the end of la terminale. Under the reformed system, 40% of the final mark comes from le contrôle continu (continuous assessment) and 60% from les épreuves terminales (final exams). The bac is a rite de passage in French culture — passing it is celebrated widely.

French students also take le brevet at the end of la troisième (age 15), roughly equivalent to GCSEs. However, the bac is far more significant culturally. The phrase avoir le bac (to have passed the bac) is a milestone. Results are posted publicly outside schools, and les fêtes de fin d'année (end-of-year parties) are legendary.

In Sénégal, the education system follows the French model closely, with le baccalauréat sénégalais modelled on the French exam. In Belgium, le CESS (Certificat d'Enseignement Secondaire Supérieur) is the equivalent qualification. In Québec, le cégep (Collège d'enseignement général et professionnel) is a unique two-year pre-university or three-year vocational institution between secondary school and university.

Exemple

Le baccalauréat est l'examen le plus important en France. Les résultats sont affichés devant le lycée et les étudiants fêtent cette réussite! — The baccalauréat is the most important exam in France. Results are posted outside the lycée and students celebrate this achievement!

Exprimer des Opinions sur l'École

Discussing school requires opinion vocabulary and structures. Express likes and dislikes: J'adore les maths parce que c'est logique (I love maths because it's logical). Je déteste l'histoire — c'est trop ennuyeux (I hate history — it's too boring). Use more sophisticated expressions for Higher tier: Ce qui me plaît, c'est... (What I like is...), Ce qui me déplaît, c'est... (What I dislike is...), Je trouve que c'est difficile/intéressant (I find it difficult/interesting).

Use different tenses to discuss school experiences: Present for routine (Je vais au lycée tous les jours), imperfect for past habits (Quand j'étais au collège, je détestais les sciences), perfect for specific events (J'ai réussi mon contrôle de maths), future for plans (L'année prochaine, je vais choisir la spécialité SVT).

French school culture differs from British: there are no school uniforms (except in some private schools), students call teachers professeur or monsieur/madame, and the atmosphere is generally more formal. Le conseil de classe (class council) meets each term to discuss each student's progress — a unique French tradition.

Exemple

Ce qui me plaît au lycée, c'est la physique-chimie parce que les expériences sont fascinantes. Ce qui me déplaît, c'est la quantité de devoirs. Je trouve que le système français est trop stressant. — What I like at lycée is physics-chemistry because the experiments are fascinating. What I dislike is the amount of homework. I find the French system too stressful.

En revanche, les élèves britanniques ont plus de choix de matières au GCSE. — In France, the baccalauréat is a national exam at the end of terminale, whereas in the UK, pupils take GCSEs at sixteen and A-Levels at eighteen. On the other hand, British pupils have more subject choices at GCSE.
