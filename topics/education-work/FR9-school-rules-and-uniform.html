⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse the conditional and future tenses. Wrong: Je voudrais (means "I would like" not "I will"). Correct: Je voudrais être = I would like to be (conditional); je serai = I will be (future). Students also forget to use the infinitive after modal verbs. Wrong: Je dois fais mes devoirs. Correct: Je dois faire mes devoirs.

Notes Détaillées

Le Règlement Intérieur et les Règles

Every French school has le règlement intérieur (internal rules) that students and parents must sign. Common rules include: Il est interdit de fumer dans l'enceinte de l'établissement (It is forbidden to smoke on school premises), Il est obligatoire d'arriver à l'heure (It is compulsory to arrive on time), Les téléphones portables sont interdits en classe (Mobile phones are banned in class). Since 2018, a national law bans phone use in all écoles and collèges, though lycées can set their own rules.

Express rules using key structures: il faut / il ne faut pas (one must / one must not), il est interdit de / il est obligatoire de (it is forbidden to / it is compulsory to), on doit / on ne doit pas (one must / one must not), on a le droit de / on n'a pas le droit de (one has the right to / one doesn't have the right to). These impersonal structures are essential for discussing rules at GCSE level.

In France, discipline can be stricter than in the UK. Les sanctions (punishments) include: le mot dans le carnet (a note in the student's book), la retenue (detention), le rapport d'incident (incident report), l'exclusion temporaire (temporary exclusion). The carnet de correspondance (school planner) is used for communication between school and parents — it must be signed every week.

Exemple

Au collège, il est interdit d'utiliser son téléphone portable. On doit arriver à l'heure et respecter les professeurs. Les sanctions incluent la retenue et l'exclusion. — At collège, it is forbidden to use your mobile phone. You must arrive on time and respect the teachers. Sanctions include detention and exclusion.

L'Uniforme Scolaire: Un Débat Français

Unlike the UK, most French state schools do not have uniforms. Students wear des vêtements de ville (ordinary clothes). However, the debate over l'uniforme scolaire has intensified in France, particularly around la laïcité (secularism). Since 2004, les signes religieux ostensibles (conspicuous religious symbols) — including headscarves, kippahs, and large crosses — are banned in schools under the principle of la laïcité. This is a major cultural and political topic in France.

In 2024, some French schools began experimenting with une tenue commune (a common outfit) — not a full uniform, but a shared dress code such as a white polo and dark trousers. This is intended to reduce social inequalities and tensions. Proponents argue uniforms reduce le jugement sur l'apparence (judgment on appearance); opponents say they restrict la liberté d'expression (freedom of expression).

For the exam, express balanced opinions using contrasting structures: D'un côté, l'uniforme réduit les inégalités, mais d'un autre côté, il limite la liberté d'expression (On one hand, the uniform reduces inequalities, but on the other hand, it limits freedom of expression). Use cependant, tandis que, and en revanche.

Exemple

En France, il n'y a pas d'uniforme dans les écoles publiques, mais les signes religieux ostensibles sont interdits depuis 2004. C'est un débat important lié à la laïcité. — In France, there is no uniform in state schools, but conspicuous religious symbols have been banned since 2004. This is an important debate linked to secularism.

La Laïcité et les Règles dans la Francophonie

The concept of la laïcité (secularism) is fundamental to French identity and education. The 1905 law on the separation of church and state (la loi de séparation des Églises et de l'État) established la laïcité as a core principle. In schools, this means no religious instruction and no conspicuous religious symbols. The charte de la laïcité (charter of secularism) is displayed in every French school.

Other francophone countries approach school rules differently. In Sénégal, which is predominantly Muslim, students wear un uniforme in most schools — usually a white blouse and navy skirt for girls, a white shirt and khaki trousers for boys. Religious schools (les écoles coraniques) operate alongside state schools. In Belgium, there is no national policy on uniforms; each school decides. In Québec, the Charte de la langue française (Bill 101) ensures French is the language of instruction, but there is no uniform requirement in most public schools.

Comparing approaches to school rules across francophone countries demonstrates cultural understanding: Tandis qu'en France, la laïcité interdit les signes religieux à l'école, au Sénégal, les élèves portent un uniforme et les écoles religieuses coexistent avec les écoles publiques.

Exemple

La laïcité est un principe fondamental en France depuis 1905. À l'école, on n'affiche pas de symboles religieux. Au Sénégal par contre, les élèves portent souvent un uniforme scolaire. — Secularism has been a fundamental principle in France since 1905. At school, no religious symbols are displayed. In Sénégal on the other hand, students often wear a school uniform.

Questions Supplémentaires

Q4: Quelles sont les règles les plus importantes dans votre école? Êtes-vous d'accord avec elles? (What are the most important rules in your school? Do you agree with them?)

Q5: Pour ou contre l'uniforme scolaire en France? Donnez des arguments des deux côtés. (For or against school uniforms in France? Give arguments from both sides.)

Réponses Supplémentaires

  1. Dans mon lycée, il est interdit d'utiliser le téléphone portable en classe et il est obligatoire d'arriver à l'heure. On doit respecter les professeurs et travailler sérieusement. Je suis d'accord avec la plupart des règles, cependant je trouve que l'interdiction totale des portables est trop stricte. D'un côté, c'est vrai que les portables peuvent déranger, mais d'un autre côté, on peut en avoir besoin pour les recherches. — In my lycée, it is forbidden to use a mobile phone in class and it is compulsory to arrive on time. You must respect the teachers and work seriously. I agree with most rules, however I find the total ban on phones too strict. On one hand, it's true that phones can be disruptive, but on the other hand, you might need them for research.
  2. D'un côté, l'uniforme scolaire a des avantages. Il réduit les inégalités sociales parce que tout le monde porte la même chose. Il n'y a pas de jugement sur les vêtements et les marques. D'un autre côté, l'uniforme limite la liberté d'expression et il peut être cher pour les familles. En France, la tradition est de ne pas avoir d'uniforme, mais certains lycées testent la tenue commune. Personnellement, je suis contre l'uniforme parce que je préfère choisir mes vêtements, mais je comprends les arguments pour. — On one hand, school uniforms have advantages. They reduce social inequalities because everyone wears the same thing. There is no judgment on clothes and brands. On the other hand, the uniform limits freedom of expression and it can be expensive for families. In France, the tradition is not to have a uniform, but some lycées are testing a common outfit. Personally, I am against uniforms because I prefer to choose my clothes, but I understand the arguments for.
