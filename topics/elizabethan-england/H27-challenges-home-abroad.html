H27: Challenges at Home and Abroad

Foundation Higher All Boards

The Catholic plots that threatened Elizabeth's reign - from the Revolt of the Northern Earls in 1569 to the execution of Mary Queen of Scots in February 1587.

📖 The Catholic Threat

Key Context: After Pope Pius V excommunicated Elizabeth in 1570, Catholic plots to overthrow her intensified. Mary Queen of Scots, Elizabeth's Catholic cousin, was the focal point of every conspiracy - she was the obvious Catholic alternative to Elizabeth. Elizabeth's survival depended on her spy network, led by Francis Walsingham, and her ability to outmanoeuvre the plotters without provoking an international crisis.

Why Catholics Plotted Against Elizabeth

  • The Pope's excommunication (1570) absolved Catholics of their allegiance to Elizabeth
  • Mary Queen of Scots had a strong claim to the English throne (she was Elizabeth's cousin and next in line)
  • Catholic powers - especially Spain and France - wanted to replace Elizabeth with a Catholic monarch
  • English Catholics living in exile on the continent organised resistance and sent missionary priests
  • The Netherlands was in revolt against Spain, and English Catholics hoped to spark a similar uprising

⚔️ The Revolt of the Northern Earls (1569)

Background

  • The Earls of Northumberland (Thomas Percy) and Westmorland (Charles Neville) were powerful Catholic noblemen in the north of England
  • The north was a Catholic stronghold, far from Elizabeth's control in London
  • The Earls were encouraged by Catholic emissaries from the Pope and from Spain
  • They planned to rescue Mary Queen of Scots from captivity at Tutbury Castle and proclaim her queen

The Revolt

  • In November 1569, the Earls gathered a force of approximately 4,500 armed men
  • They marched into Durham and celebrated a Catholic Mass in Durham Cathedral - the first public Catholic service since Mary I's reign
  • They moved south towards Tutbury to rescue Mary Queen of Scots
  • However, Elizabeth moved Mary further south before the rebels could reach her
  • The Earl of Sussex raised a royal army of approximately 7,000 men to confront the rebels
  • The rebel force melted away as support failed to materialise - most gentry refused to join
  • The Earls fled to Scotland; Northumberland was later handed over to the English and executed in 1572
Example: The Aftermath of the Northern Earls' Revolt

After the revolt, Elizabeth took brutal revenge. Over 800 rebels were executed - the largest mass execution in English history. The Earl of Northumberland was handed over by the Scots and beheaded at York on 22 August 1572. The Earl of Westmorland escaped to the Netherlands and died in exile. Elizabeth confiscated the Earls' lands and strengthened her control over the north by appointing more loyal Protestant nobles. The revolt showed that Catholic opposition could be dangerous but also that it lacked widespread popular support.

Significance of the Revolt: The Revolt of the Northern Earls was the only armed Catholic uprising of Elizabeth's reign. It showed that Catholic nobles in the north were willing to use force, but it also demonstrated the limits of their support. Most ordinary people, even Catholics, were unwilling to risk their lives for the cause. The revolt's failure strengthened Elizabeth's position and led to harsher measures against Catholics.

🕵️ The Ridolfi Plot (1571)

Key Features

  • Roberto Ridolfi was an Italian banker and papal agent living in London
  • He plotted to orchestrate a Catholic invasion of England by the Duke of Alva's Spanish army from the Netherlands
  • The plan involved: (1) the Spanish army invading England; (2) English Catholics rising up in support; (3) Mary Queen of Scots marrying the Duke of Norfolk; (4) Elizabeth being overthrown and Mary becoming queen
  • Thomas Howard, the Duke of Norfolk, was the highest-ranking nobleman in England and a Catholic sympathiser

Discovery and Consequences

  • The plot was discovered by William Cecil and Francis Walsingham's intelligence network in 1571
  • Ridolfi was arrested but later released due to lack of evidence - he fled to the continent
  • The Duke of Norfolk was arrested, tried for treason, and executed on 2 June 1572
  • Elizabeth increased surveillance of Catholics and passed harsher laws against recusancy
  • The plot demonstrated the danger of combining domestic Catholic opposition with foreign invasion
Walsingham's Spy Network: The Ridolfi Plot was discovered through the intelligence network of Francis Walsingham, Elizabeth's "spymaster." Walsingham (1532-1590) built an extensive network of agents and informers across England and Europe. He intercepted letters, employed double agents, and used codes and ciphers to gather intelligence. His network was so effective that he often knew of Catholic plots before they were fully formed. Walsingham's espionage was crucial to Elizabeth's survival throughout her reign.

🕵️ The Throckmorton Plot (1583)

Key Features

  • Francis Throckmorton was a young English Catholic who acted as a messenger between English Catholics and Catholic powers abroad
  • The plot planned: (1) a French invasion of England led by the Duke of Guise (Mary Queen of Scots' cousin); (2) a simultaneous uprising of English Catholics; (3) the overthrow of Elizabeth and the restoration of Catholicism under Mary Queen of Scots
  • The plot had the backing of the Pope, Spain, and the Catholic League in France

Discovery and Consequences

  • Walsingham's agents discovered the plot in 1583 by intercepting Throckmorton's correspondence
  • Throckmorton was arrested in November 1583 and tortured on the rack until he confessed
  • He provided details of the conspiracy and named names of Catholic supporters
  • Throckmorton was executed on 10 July 1584
  • The Bond of Association was formed in 1584 - nobles and gentry pledged to defend Elizabeth and pursue anyone who threatened her life
  • Parliament passed the Act for the Queen's Safety (1585), which required Parliament to choose the next monarch if Elizabeth was assassinated
  • Mary Queen of Scots was moved to stricter custody at Tutbury under the surveillance of Sir Amyas Paulet
Example: Walsingham's Methods

To uncover the Throckmorton Plot, Walsingham used a network of spies and informers. He had agents watching the French and Spanish embassies in London. He intercepted Throckmorton's letters to Mary Queen of Scots and the Duke of Guise. When Throckmorton was arrested, Walsingham found a list of Catholic sympathisers and details of invasion plans hidden in his house. After Throckmorton was tortured on the rack, he revealed the full extent of the conspiracy. This was typical of Walsingham's ruthless but effective approach to protecting Elizabeth.

🕵️ The Babington Plot (1586)

Background

  • Anthony Babington was a 24-year-old Catholic gentleman from Derbyshire
  • He became involved in a plot to assassinate Elizabeth and replace her with Mary Queen of Scots
  • The plot was organised with the support of the Spanish and the Catholic priest John Ballard

Walsingham's Trap

  • Walsingham knew of the plot from the start because one of his agents, Gilbert Gifford, had infiltrated the conspiracy
  • Walsingham arranged for Mary's letters to be intercepted and decoded by his secretary, Thomas Phelippes
  • A secret channel was set up: Mary's letters were smuggled out in beer barrels and passed to Walsingham's agents, who copied and decoded them before forwarding them to their intended recipients
  • On 6 July 1586, Babington wrote to Mary outlining the plot to assassinate Elizabeth
  • Mary replied on 17 July 1586, approving the plan and giving her consent for the assassination
  • This was the evidence Walsingham needed - Mary's own written approval of Elizabeth's murder
Walsingham's Trap:
1. Infiltrate the conspiracy (Gilbert Gifford as double agent)
2. Intercept Mary's letters (smuggled in beer barrels)
3. Decode the letters (Thomas Phelippes)
4. Wait for Mary to incriminate herself in writing
5. Use the evidence to justify her execution
Walsingham needed written proof of Mary's involvement to persuade Elizabeth to execute a fellow queen.
Phelippes' Forgery: When Thomas Phelippes decoded Mary's letter of 17 July 1586, he added a postscript in Mary's cipher requesting the names of the conspirators. This was designed to extract more evidence, but the conspirators were arrested before they could respond. The fact that Walsingham's team forged part of the letter has led some historians to question whether the evidence was entirely fair - but Mary's approval of the assassination plot was genuinely in her own hand.

Consequences

  • Babington and 14 co-conspirators were arrested in August 1586
  • Babington and the other men were executed in September 1586 - they were hanged, drawn, and quartered
  • Mary Queen of Scots was put on trial at Fotheringhay Castle in October 1586
  • She was found guilty of treason on 25 October 1586
  • Parliament demanded her execution, but Elizabeth hesitated for months

⚔️ The Execution of Mary Queen of Scots (8 February 1587)

Why Elizabeth Hesitated

  • Executing a fellow monarch was unprecedented and dangerous - it set a precedent that could be used against Elizabeth herself
  • Mary was Elizabeth's cousin (both descended from Henry VII)
  • Elizabeth feared that executing Mary would provoke an invasion by Catholic powers
  • She worried about the reaction of Mary's son, James VI of Scotland, who could become a dangerous enemy
  • She was genuinely uncertain about the morality of executing a queen

The Execution

  • Elizabeth finally signed the death warrant on 1 February 1587
  • She then tried to cancel the warrant, but her secretary, William Davison, had already sent it
  • On 8 February 1587, Mary Queen of Scots was beheaded at Fotheringhay Castle in Northamptonshire
  • Mary faced her death with dignity. She wore a black velvet dress and a scarlet petticoat (the colour of Catholic martyrdom)
  • The executioner required three blows to sever her head
  • When the executioner held up her head, Mary's wig fell off, revealing grey hair - she was 44 years old
Example: Elizabeth's Reaction to Mary's Execution

Elizabeth claimed she had never intended the death warrant to be carried out and blamed her secretary, William Davison, for sending it without her final instruction. She had Davison imprisoned in the Tower of London and fined £10,000. She wrote to Mary's son, James VI of Scotland, expressing her sorrow and denying responsibility. Most historians believe Elizabeth deliberately created ambiguity so she could deny responsibility if necessary, while still ensuring Mary was executed. Her grief may have been genuine, but it was also politically convenient.

Significance of Mary's Execution: Mary's execution removed the figurehead of Catholic opposition, but it also gave Philip II of Spain the justification he needed to launch the Spanish Armada. Philip had claimed to be acting in Mary's interests; now he could claim to be avenging her death. Mary also became a Catholic martyr, and her execution hardened attitudes on both sides. However, the immediate threat of a Catholic coup was ended - there was no longer an obvious Catholic candidate to replace Elizabeth.

📊 Comparing the Four Plots

Plot Year Key Plotters Foreign Support Outcome
Northern Earls' Revolt 1569 Earls of Northumberland and Westmorland Pope, Spain (limited) 800+ rebels executed; Earls fled; Norfolk later executed
Ridolfi Plot 1571 Ridolfi, Duke of Norfolk Spain (Duke of Alva), Pope Norfolk executed; Ridolfi fled; harsher laws against Catholics
Throckmorton Plot 1583 Throckmorton, French Catholics France (Duke of Guise), Spain, Pope Throckmorton executed; Bond of Association formed; Mary moved to stricter custody
Babington Plot 1586 Babington, Ballard Spain, Pope Babington executed; Mary tried and executed February 1587
Escalating Threat: Each plot was more dangerous than the last. The Northern Earls' Revolt was a local uprising; the Ridolfi Plot involved a foreign invasion plan; the Throckmorton Plot had the backing of multiple Catholic powers; the Babington Plot directly targeted Elizabeth's life. The escalation shows why Elizabeth's government became increasingly ruthless in dealing with Catholics - the threat was real and growing.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the Revolt of the Northern Earls in 1569. (4 marks)

Q2: Explain how Walsingham's spy network uncovered the Babington Plot. (4 marks)

Q3: Why did Elizabeth hesitate before signing Mary Queen of Scots' death warrant? (4 marks)

Q4: How serious was the Catholic threat to Elizabeth's reign? (8 marks)

Q5: "Walsingham's spy network was the most important reason Elizabeth survived the Catholic plots." How far do you agree? (16 marks + 4 SPaG)

Q6: Compare the Ridolfi Plot and the Babington Plot. In what ways were they similar and different? (8 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. In November 1569, the Earls of Northumberland (Thomas Percy) and Westmorland (Charles Neville) gathered approximately 4,500 armed men and marched into Durham, where they celebrated a Catholic Mass in the Cathedral. They planned to rescue Mary Queen of Scots and proclaim her queen. However, Elizabeth moved Mary further south, and the royal army of 7,000 men under the Earl of Sussex dispersed the rebellion. Over 800 rebels were executed afterwards.
  2. Walsingham uncovered the Babington Plot by: infiltrating the conspiracy with a double agent (Gilbert Gifford); intercepting Mary's letters, which were smuggled in beer barrels; having his secretary Thomas Phelippes decode the letters using cipher; waiting until Mary wrote back approving the assassination of Elizabeth on 17 July 1586. This written evidence proved Mary's direct involvement in treason and provided the justification for her execution.
  3. Elizabeth hesitated because: executing a fellow monarch was unprecedented and set a dangerous precedent; Mary was her cousin; she feared provoking an invasion by Catholic powers (especially Spain); she worried about the reaction of Mary's son, James VI of Scotland; she was uncertain about the morality of the execution. She delayed for months after the trial verdict before finally signing the death warrant.
  4. The Catholic threat was very serious: there were four major plots between 1569 and 1586, each escalating in ambition; the plots had backing from major Catholic powers (Spain, France, the Pope); Mary Queen of Scots provided a focal point for opposition; the 1570 excommunication made Catholic loyalty incompatible with loyalty to Elizabeth; the Babington Plot directly targeted Elizabeth's life. However, the threat was limited by: lack of popular support among ordinary English people; Walsingham's effective intelligence network; the disunity among Catholic powers; and the failure of every plot to achieve its aims.
  5. A balanced answer should discuss: Walsingham's spy network (essential for uncovering all four plots, especially the Babington Plot where he set a trap); other factors including: Elizabeth's political skill and caution; the loyalty of most English people to the Protestant settlement; the weakness and disunity of Catholic powers; the Bond of Association and parliamentary legislation; the failure of Catholic plots to gain popular support. Argue that Walsingham was essential but not sufficient alone - Elizabeth's survival depended on a combination of intelligence, political skill, and the underlying weakness of the Catholic cause in England.
  6. Similarities: both plots aimed to replace Elizabeth with Mary Queen of Scots; both had foreign Catholic backing; both were discovered by Walsingham's network; both led to executions of the key plotters. Differences: the Ridolfi Plot involved the Duke of Norfolk (the highest-ranking nobleman) and planned a Spanish invasion; the Babington Plot directly targeted Elizabeth's assassination and used Walsingham's deliberate trap to catch Mary in her own handwriting; the Babington Plot had far more serious consequences (Mary's execution).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know all four plots in chronological order: 1569, 1571, 1583, 1586
  • Always use specific dates and named individuals
  • Explain Walsingham's methods in detail - the Babington Plot trap is a very common question
  • For comparison questions, structure around similarities AND differences
  • Link the plots together - show how they escalated and how Elizabeth's response intensified
  • Remember the significance of Mary's execution: it removed the threat but provoked the Armada
  • For evaluation questions, balance the danger of the plots against their failure

📝 Exam Technique

History Exam Tips:
Know the chronological sequence of plots against Elizabeth. For the Armada, explain causes AND reasons for English victory. For poverty, distinguish between attitudes to 'deserving' and 'undeserving' poor. The historic environment question requires linking the site to wider themes.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think elizabeth solved the religious problem. Wrong: Elizabeth solved the religious problem Correct: The Religious Settlement was a compromise that satisfied neither Puritans nor Catholics. Catholic plots continued throughout her reign.

Students often think the spanish armada was defeated entirely by the english. Wrong: The Spanish Armada was defeated entirely by the English Correct: While English tactics (fire ships) were important, the weather played a crucial role. Many Spanish ships were wrecked by storms.

Students often think elizabethan england was a golden age for everyone. Wrong: Elizabethan England was a golden age for everyone Correct: While the elite prospered, many lived in poverty. Enclosure displaced peasants, harvests failed, and the Poor Laws were often harsh.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Explain why Elizabeth faced threats from Catholic plots.

Elizabeth faced threats from Catholic plots because Pope Pius V's excommunication of 1570 absolved Catholics of their allegiance to her, and Mary Queen of Scots provided a legitimate Catholic alternative to the throne. The plots escalated in seriousness throughout the reign, each one more dangerous than the last.

The Revolt of the Northern Earls in November 1569 was the only armed Catholic uprising. The Earls of Northumberland (Thomas Percy) and Westmorland (Charles Neville) gathered approximately 4,500 men, celebrated a Catholic Mass in Durham Cathedral, and planned to rescue Mary Queen of Scots from captivity. Elizabeth moved Mary further south, and the Earl of Sussex raised a royal army of 7,000. The revolt collapsed as support failed to materialise, and over 800 rebels were executed — the largest mass execution in English history.

The Ridolfi Plot of 1571 was more dangerous because it involved foreign invasion. Roberto Ridolfi, an Italian banker and papal agent, plotted to bring the Duke of Alva's Spanish army from the Netherlands to invade England, while English Catholics rose in support and Mary married the Duke of Norfolk. William Cecil and Francis Walsingham's intelligence network uncovered the plot; Norfolk was executed on 2 June 1572. The Throckmorton Plot of 1583 was still more serious: Francis Throckmorton planned a French invasion led by the Duke of Guise, with backing from the Pope, Spain, and the Catholic League. Walsingham intercepted Throckmorton's correspondence, arrested him in November 1583, and had him tortured on the rack; he was executed on 10 July 1584. The plot led to the Bond of Association (1584) and the Act for the Queen's Safety (1585).

The Babington Plot of 1586 was the most dangerous because it directly targeted Elizabeth's life and provided the evidence to execute Mary. Anthony Babington plotted to assassinate Elizabeth with Spanish backing. Walsingham set a trap: his agent Gilbert Gifford infiltrated the conspiracy, and Mary's letters were intercepted and decoded by Thomas Phelippes. When Mary replied on 17 July 1586 approving Elizabeth's assassination, Walsingham had the written proof he needed. Babington and 14 co-conspirators were executed; Mary was tried at Fotheringhay Castle, found guilty on 25 October 1586, and beheaded on 8 February 1587.

In conclusion, Elizabeth faced escalating Catholic threats because the 1570 excommunication made loyalty to the Pope incompatible with loyalty to Elizabeth, and Mary Queen of Scots gave every plot a figurehead. Walsingham's spy network was essential to Elizabeth's survival — without it, the Babington Plot might have succeeded. Mary's execution removed the immediate threat but gave Philip II of Spain the justification to launch the Spanish Armada.
