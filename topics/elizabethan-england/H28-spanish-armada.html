H28: The Spanish Armada

Foundation Higher All Boards

The Spanish Armada of 1588 - why Philip II invaded, the course of the battle, reasons for English victory, and the consequences of this defining moment in Elizabeth's reign.

📖 Causes of the Conflict

Key Context: The Spanish Armada of 1588 was the culmination of decades of growing tension between England and Spain. Philip II of Spain, the most powerful monarch in Europe, was motivated by a combination of religious, political, and economic grievances against Elizabeth I. The execution of Mary Queen of Scots in February 1587 gave Philip the final justification he needed.

Religious Causes

  • Philip II was a devout Catholic who saw it as his duty to defend the Catholic faith across Europe
  • He was outraged by Elizabeth's Protestant Religious Settlement of 1559
  • The Pope's excommunication of Elizabeth in 1570 had called on Catholic rulers to depose her
  • Philip saw the invasion of England as a crusade to restore Catholicism

Political Causes

  • Mary Queen of Scots: Philip had supported the Catholic plots against Elizabeth. After Mary's execution on 8 February 1587, Philip claimed to be avenging her death and restoring Catholicism
  • The Netherlands: Since the 1560s, the Dutch had been in revolt against Spanish rule. Elizabeth had sent money and troops to support the Dutch Protestant rebels (the Treaty of Nonsuch, August 1585, committed England to sending 7,400 troops). This was a direct act of war against Spain
  • English support for Portuguese pretender: When Portugal was annexed by Spain in 1580, Elizabeth supported the claim of Don Antonio, a rival to Philip

Economic Causes

  • Drake's raids: Sir Francis Drake and other English privateers had been attacking Spanish treasure ships and colonies in the Americas since the 1570s. Drake's circumnavigation (1577-80) netted approximately £600,000 in Spanish loot
  • Philip saw Drake as a pirate; Elizabeth knighted him. Drake's raid on Cadiz in April 1587 destroyed 30 Spanish ships and large quantities of supplies - he called it "singeing the King of Spain's beard"
  • English trade was competing with Spanish trade, and English sailors were disrupting Spanish colonial wealth

⚓ Philip II's Invasion Plan

The Strategy

  • Philip planned a two-pronged invasion: the Spanish Armada would sail from Spain to the English Channel, pick up the Duke of Parma's army from the Netherlands, and transport them across to England
  • The Duke of Parma had approximately 30,000 veteran soldiers in the Netherlands, ready to invade
  • The Armada would protect Parma's crossing and then land the invasion force in Kent or Sussex
  • Once ashore, the Spanish army would march on London, overthrow Elizabeth, and restore Catholicism
Philip's Plan:
Armada sails from Spain → Meets Parma's army in the Netherlands → Crosses the Channel together → Lands in England → Marches on London → Overthrows Elizabeth
The plan depended on the Armada and Parma's army meeting at the right time and place.

The Armada Fleet

  • The Armada consisted of approximately 130 ships and carried roughly 30,000 men (19,000 soldiers, 8,000 sailors, and 3,000 volunteers and clergy)
  • It was commanded by the Duke of Medina Sidonia, an experienced administrator but not a naval commander
  • The fleet included 22 galleons (the most powerful warships), 44 armed merchant vessels, and various support ships
  • It carried 2,431 cannons and enough food, ammunition, and supplies for a major campaign
  • Philip had also gathered 49 ships under the Duke of Parma at Dunkirk and Nieuwpoort, ready to cross to England
Weaknesses of the Armada: Despite its size, the Armada had significant weaknesses: (1) the original commander, the Marquis of Santa Cruz, died in February 1588 and was replaced by Medina Sidonia, who had no naval experience; (2) Drake's raid on Cadiz (1587) had destroyed supplies and delayed the fleet; (3) many of the ships were armed merchant vessels, not purpose-built warships; (4) the Spanish ships were designed for boarding and close combat, not long-range gunnery; (5) the plan required perfect coordination between the Armada and Parma's army, which was extremely difficult in an era before radio communication.

⚔️ The Armada Campaign (July-August 1588)

Chronology of the Campaign

  • 28 May 1588: The Armada set sail from Lisbon (then under Spanish control)
  • 19 July 1588: The Armada was first spotted off the Lizard in Cornwall. Beacons were lit across southern England to warn of the invasion. The English fleet under Lord Howard of Effingham put to sea from Plymouth
  • 21 July 1588: The English fleet engaged the Armada in the Channel. The English ships were faster and more manoeuvrable, and their guns had longer range. They harassed the Spanish fleet but could not break its crescent formation
  • 27 July 1588: The Armada anchored at Calais, waiting to rendezvous with Parma's army. However, Parma's troops were not ready - Dutch ships blockaded his ports
  • 28-29 July 1588 (Night of the Fire Ships): The English sent eight burning ships (fire ships) into the anchored Spanish fleet. The Spanish panicked, cut their anchor cables, and scattered. The Armada's tight formation was broken
  • 29 July 1588 (Battle of Gravelines): With the Spanish formation scattered, the English attacked. Five Spanish ships were destroyed and many damaged. The Spanish could not regroup and were driven north-east by the wind
  • July-August 1588: The remnants of the Armada were forced to sail north around Scotland and Ireland to return to Spain, suffering terrible storms and shipwrecks
  • September-October 1588: Approximately 67 ships and 15,000 men were lost during the return voyage around the British Isles
Example: The Fire Ships at Calais (28 July 1588)

When the Armada anchored at Calais, the English saw their opportunity. They loaded eight old ships with tar, pitch, and gunpowder, set them ablaze, and let them drift into the anchored Spanish fleet. The Spanish had been warned about fire ships and had posted lookouts, but the sight of the blazing vessels bearing down on them in the night caused panic. Each captain cut his anchor cables and fled to sea - but in the confusion, many ships collided, and the tight defensive crescent formation that had protected the Armada in the Channel was shattered. The fleet never reformed.

🏆 Reasons for English Victory

English Advantages

1. Better Tactics and Ships: The English ships were smaller, faster, and more manoeuvrable than the Spanish galleons. They could sail closer to the wind and choose their position in battle. English guns had longer range and could fire faster - the English used a "stand-off" tactic, pounding the Spanish from a distance, while the Spanish were designed for close boarding actions. The English fired approximately 2,000 rounds at Gravelines, compared to approximately 750 from the Spanish.
2. Better Commanders: The English were led by Lord Howard of Effingham (Lord High Admiral), with experienced seamen like Sir Francis Drake, Sir John Hawkins, and Martin Frobisher under his command. They understood naval warfare and adapted their tactics to the situation. The Spanish commander, the Duke of Medina Sidonia, had no naval experience and had begged Philip not to appoint him.
3. The Weather: The famous "Protestant Wind" blew the Spanish fleet north after Gravelines and caused devastating storms during the return voyage around Scotland and Ireland. More Spanish ships were destroyed by storms than by English guns. The wind consistently favoured the English, allowing them to keep the weather gauge (upwind position) during the Channel battles.
4. Strategic Errors by Spain: Philip's plan required perfect coordination between the Armada and Parma's army - but this was impossible without modern communications. Parma's troops were blockaded in port by Dutch ships and could not join the Armada. The Armada was forced to wait at Calais, giving the English the chance to use fire ships.

📢 Consequences of the Armada

Short-Term Consequences

  • Approximately 63 Spanish ships were lost (roughly half the fleet) and 15,000-20,000 Spanish lives
  • England lost approximately 8 ships and 100-150 men in battle (though more died later from disease)
  • Elizabeth's victory was celebrated across Protestant Europe - she was hailed as the defender of the Protestant cause
  • Philip II claimed the expedition was defeated by the weather, not the English, and began planning another Armada (which never materialised on the same scale)
  • A thanksgiving service was held at St Paul's Cathedral, and medals were struck with the inscription: "God breathed and they were scattered" (Flavit Jehovah et dissipati sunt)

Long-Term Consequences

  • Elizabeth's prestige: The victory established Elizabeth as one of Europe's most powerful rulers and boosted her popularity at home
  • English naval confidence: The victory gave England confidence in its naval power, laying the foundation for the future British Empire
  • Spanish decline: Although Spain remained powerful for decades, the Armada's defeat marked the beginning of Spain's gradual decline as the dominant European power
  • Protestant confidence: The victory was seen as proof that God favoured the Protestant cause, strengthening Protestantism across Europe
  • Continued war: The war between England and Spain continued until 1604, but England was never seriously threatened with invasion again
Example: Elizabeth's Speech at Tilbury (August 1588)

While the Armada was being defeated at sea, Elizabeth visited her troops at Tilbury camp in Essex. She delivered one of the most famous speeches in English history: "I know I have the body but of a weak and feeble woman, but I have the heart and stomach of a king, and of a king of England too, and think foul scorn that Parma or Spain, or any prince of Europe, should dare to invade the borders of my realm." Whether or not these were her exact words (the speech was recorded differently by different witnesses), it became a powerful symbol of Elizabeth's defiance and leadership.

How Important Was the Armada Victory? The defeat of the Armada was a turning point in European history. It saved England from invasion and preserved Elizabeth's Protestant settlement. It demonstrated that England could compete with the greatest power in Europe. It marked the beginning of English naval dominance, which would eventually lead to the British Empire. However, it did not end the war with Spain, and England still faced Catholic plots and threats in the years that followed.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Give two reasons why Philip II of Spain wanted to invade England. (4 marks)

Q2: Describe the key events of the Armada campaign from 19 July to 29 July 1588. (4 marks)

Q3: Explain why the fire ships were a turning point in the Armada campaign. (4 marks)

Q4: "The weather was the most important reason for England's victory over the Armada." How far do you agree? (8 marks)

Q5: "The defeat of the Spanish Armada was the most important event of Elizabeth's reign." How far do you agree? (16 marks + 4 SPaG)

Q6: Explain the weaknesses of Philip II's invasion plan. (4 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. Two reasons: (1) Religion - Philip was a devout Catholic who wanted to overthrow Elizabeth's Protestant settlement and restore Catholicism in England; (2) English support for the Dutch rebels - Elizabeth sent money and troops to help the Dutch Protestants fighting against Spanish rule in the Netherlands, which was a direct threat to Spanish power. Other reasons include Drake's raids on Spanish ships and Mary's execution.
  2. Key events: 19 July - Armada spotted off the Lizard, beacons lit; 21 July - English fleet engaged the Armada in the Channel, using longer-range guns; 27 July - Armada anchored at Calais to wait for Parma's army; 28 July - English sent fire ships into the anchored Spanish fleet, causing panic and scattering the formation; 29 July - Battle of Gravelines, the English attacked the scattered Spanish ships, destroying five and damaging many more; the wind drove the Armada north-east.
  3. The fire ships were a turning point because they broke the Armada's tight defensive crescent formation, which had protected the fleet during the Channel battles. When the Spanish panicked and cut their anchor cables, they scattered and could not regroup. This made the Armada vulnerable to the English attack at the Battle of Gravelines the next day. Without the fire ships, the Armada might have maintained its formation and successfully linked up with Parma's army.
  4. A balanced answer should discuss: the weather (the "Protestant Wind" scattered the fleet and caused devastating losses on the return voyage - more ships were lost to storms than to English guns); better English ships and tactics (faster, more manoeuvrable, longer-range guns, stand-off tactics); better commanders (Howard, Drake, Hawkins vs the inexperienced Medina Sidonia); Spanish strategic errors (poor coordination with Parma). Argue that the weather was important but the English tactical advantages were equally or more significant.
  5. A balanced answer should discuss: the Armada (it saved England from invasion, boosted Elizabeth's prestige, and established English naval power); other important events such as the Religious Settlement 1559 (provided 45 years of stability), the execution of Mary Queen of Scots (removed the Catholic figurehead), the defeat of the Catholic plots, the exploration and cultural achievements. Argue either that the Armada was the defining moment of the reign or that the Religious Settlement was more important because it had longer-lasting consequences.
  6. Weaknesses of Philip's plan: it required the Armada to meet Parma's army at a specific time and place, which was impossible without modern communications; Parma's troops were blockaded in port by Dutch ships and could not join the Armada; the Spanish commander Medina Sidonia had no naval experience; many Spanish ships were armed merchant vessels, not purpose-built warships; the Spanish were designed for boarding actions but the English used long-range gunnery; there was no fallback plan if the rendezvous failed.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use specific dates: Armada spotted 19 July 1588, fire ships 28 July, Gravelines 29 July
  • Know the numbers: 130 ships, 30,000 men in the Armada; 67 ships, 15,000 men lost
  • Explain the causes in categories: religious, political, economic
  • For "reasons for victory" questions, discuss multiple factors (tactics, ships, commanders, weather, Spanish errors)
  • Remember Elizabeth's Tilbury speech - it's useful for questions about her image and leadership
  • The phrase "God breathed and they were scattered" shows how the victory was interpreted as divine favour
  • Link the Armada to earlier events: the Dutch revolt, Drake's raids, Mary's execution

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think elizabeth solved the religious problem. Wrong: Elizabeth solved the religious problem Correct: The Religious Settlement was a compromise that satisfied neither Puritans nor Catholics. Catholic plots continued throughout her reign.

Students often think the spanish armada was defeated entirely by the english. Wrong: The Spanish Armada was defeated entirely by the English Correct: While English tactics (fire ships) were important, the weather played a crucial role. Many Spanish ships were wrecked by storms.

Students often think elizabethan england was a golden age for everyone. Wrong: Elizabethan England was a golden age for everyone Correct: While the elite prospered, many lived in poverty. Enclosure displaced peasants, harvests failed, and the Poor Laws were often harsh.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Explain why the English defeated the Spanish Armada.

The English defeated the Spanish Armada for a combination of reasons: superior tactics and ship design, effective leadership, and the crucial role of the weather. Philip II of Spain launched the Armada for religious, political, and economic reasons: he wanted to restore Catholicism in England, was outraged by Elizabeth's support for the Dutch Protestant rebels (the Treaty of Nonsuch, August 1585, committed 7,400 English troops), and was angered by Drake's raids on Spanish treasure ships — Drake's circumnavigation (1577-80) had netted approximately £600,000 in Spanish loot. Mary Queen of Scots' execution on 8 February 1587 gave Philip the final justification.

The English had superior ships and tactics. English vessels were smaller, faster, and more manoeuvrable than the Spanish galleons, and their guns had longer range. The English used "stand-off" tactics, pounding the Spanish from a distance, while the Spanish were designed for close boarding actions. At the Battle of Gravelines on 29 July 1588, the English fired approximately 2,000 rounds compared to approximately 750 from the Spanish. English commanders — Lord Howard of Effingham, Sir Francis Drake, Sir John Hawkins, and Martin Frobisher — were experienced seamen who understood naval warfare. The Spanish commander, the Duke of Medina Sidonia, had no naval experience and had begged Philip not to appoint him.

The turning point was the fire ships at Calais on the night of 28 July 1588. When the Armada anchored at Calais to rendezvous with the Duke of Parma's army, the English sent eight burning ships into the anchored fleet. The Spanish panicked, cut their anchor cables, and scattered. The Armada's tight crescent formation was broken and never reformed, making it vulnerable to the English attack at Gravelines the next day. Five Spanish ships were destroyed and many damaged. Furthermore, Parma's troops were blockaded in port by Dutch ships and could not join the Armada — Philip's plan required perfect coordination that was impossible without modern communications.

The weather played a crucial role. The famous "Protestant Wind" blew the scattered Armada north-east after Gravelines, preventing it from regrouping. During the return voyage around Scotland and Ireland, devastating storms destroyed more Spanish ships than English guns. Approximately 67 ships and 15,000-20,000 men were lost. The victory medal inscribed "God breathed and they were scattered" (Flavit Jehovah et dissipati sunt) reflected the belief that divine providence had helped England.

The consequences were significant. Elizabeth's prestige was boosted across Protestant Europe, English naval confidence was established (laying the foundation for the future British Empire), and Spain's gradual decline began. The victory secured Protestantism in England and demonstrated that England could compete with the greatest power in Europe. In conclusion, while English tactics and ship design were crucial — particularly the fire ships that broke the Armada's formation — the weather was equally important in destroying the fleet during its return voyage. Victory depended on both English skill and the "Protestant Wind."
