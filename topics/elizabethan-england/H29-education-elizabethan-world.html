H29: Education and the Elizabethan World

Foundation Higher All Boards

Education for boys, limited opportunities for girls, the rise of the gentry, the flowering of Elizabethan theatre, and Puritan opposition to entertainment.

📖 Education in Elizabethan England

Key Context: The Elizabethan era saw a significant expansion in education, driven by the Renaissance emphasis on learning and the growing need for educated administrators, lawyers, and clergy. However, education was still limited by class and gender: most children received no formal education, and opportunities for girls were extremely restricted.

Who Received Education?

  • Nobility and gentry: boys from wealthy families were educated at home by private tutors or sent to grammar schools and universities
  • Middle class: sons of merchants, professionals, and tradesmen might attend grammar schools if their families could afford the fees
  • Working class: most children of labourers and farmers received no formal education
  • Girls: education was very limited, even for the wealthy - most girls were educated at home in domestic skills

🏫 Education for Boys

Petty Schools (Age 5-7)

  • Also called "dame schools" - small, informal schools usually run by a woman in her home
  • Children learned the basics: reading, writing, and simple arithmetic
  • They used a "hornbook" - a wooden board with a sheet of text covered by transparent horn, showing the alphabet, syllables, and the Lord's Prayer
  • Fees were small but still beyond the reach of the poorest families
  • Discipline was harsh - children were beaten with a birch rod for misbehaviour or slow learning

Grammar Schools (Age 7-14)

  • Grammar schools provided a more advanced education, focusing on Latin and Greek
  • Many were "endowed" schools - funded by wealthy patrons who left money in their wills
  • The curriculum was almost entirely classical: Latin grammar (using Lily's "Grammar"), reading Latin authors (Cicero, Virgil, Ovid), Greek, rhetoric, and logic
  • School hours were long: typically 6am-5pm in summer and 7am-5pm in winter, with a half-day on Thursday and Saturday
  • Holidays were limited to a few weeks per year
  • Discipline was severe: beatings with a birch rod were routine for errors in Latin translation or misbehaviour
Example: A Typical Grammar School Day

A boy at a grammar school would rise at 5am, attend prayers at 6am, and begin lessons in Latin grammar. The morning session (6am-11am) covered Latin translation, vocabulary, and composition. After lunch and recreation (11am-1pm), the afternoon session (1pm-5pm) focused on reading classical authors and practising rhetoric. On Fridays, there was often a "trial" where boys had to recite Latin passages from memory. Errors were punished with the birch. Lessons were conducted entirely in Latin - boys were punished for speaking English in school.

Universities (Age 14+)

  • Elizabethan England had only two universities: Oxford (founded c1096) and Cambridge (founded 1209)
  • University students were typically 14-15 years old when they entered
  • The curriculum focused on the "seven liberal arts": grammar, rhetoric, logic, arithmetic, geometry, music, and astronomy
  • Most students studied for a BA (3-4 years) and then an MA (a further 3 years)
  • Many went on to study theology, law, or medicine at a higher level
  • By the 1580s, Oxford had approximately 1,500 students and Cambridge approximately 1,300
  • Students lived in colleges under strict rules: they wore distinctive gowns, were forbidden to marry, and were subject to curfews
The Purpose of Education: Education in Elizabethan England was not about developing the individual - it was about producing trained men who could serve the state as administrators, diplomats, lawyers, and clergy. A knowledge of Latin was essential for all official and academic communication. The emphasis on classical learning meant that students were immersed in the values and ideas of ancient Rome and Greece, which shaped their worldview and political thinking.

👩 Very Limited Education for Girls

The Expectations of Women

  • Elizabethan society was deeply patriarchal - women were considered inferior to men in almost every respect
  • The prevailing belief was that a woman's proper role was wife, mother, and household manager
  • The Bible was used to justify female subordination: "Wives, submit yourselves unto your own husbands" (Ephesians 5:22)
  • A woman's legal status was determined by her father or husband - she could not own property, enter contracts, or vote
  • Even Elizabeth I, despite being the most powerful woman in England, faced constant pressure to marry and "submit" to a husband

What Education Girls Received

  • Wealthy girls: Might be taught by private tutors at home - reading, writing, French, Italian, music, needlework, and household management. A few exceptional women, like the daughters of Sir Thomas More, received a full humanist education
  • Middle-class girls: Might attend petty school to learn basic reading and writing, but were withdrawn before grammar school age
  • Working-class girls: Virtually no formal education - they learned practical skills from their mothers
  • No university: Women were not admitted to either Oxford or Cambridge

The Irony of Elizabeth's Reign: Elizabeth I was the most educated woman in England and one of the most learned monarchs in European history. She ruled successfully for 45 years, proving that women were capable of intellectual and political leadership. Yet she did little to improve educational opportunities for other women. Her reign is often described as a paradox: a female ruler who upheld patriarchal values and did not challenge the subordination of women.

🎩 The Rise of the Gentry

Who Were the Gentry?

  • The gentry were the class below the nobility - landowners who did not need to work for a living
  • They included knights, esquires, and gentlemen
  • By Elizabeth's reign, the gentry numbered approximately 5,000 families but wielded enormous influence as Justices of the Peace (JPs), Members of Parliament, and local administrators

Why the Gentry Grew in Importance

  • Economic changes: The growth of trade, the dissolution of the monasteries (1530s), and rising land values enriched many gentry families
  • Education: Grammar schools and universities produced educated gentry who could serve as administrators and MPs
  • Political changes: The Tudors relied increasingly on the gentry to govern locally, as JPs handled most legal and administrative matters
  • Social mobility: Successful merchants and lawyers could buy land and join the gentry, making it a growing and dynamic class
The Gentry and Elizabeth: The gentry were crucial supporters of Elizabeth's government. They served as JPs (unpaid local magistrates who maintained law and order), MPs in Parliament, and commissioners for collecting taxes and enforcing the Religious Settlement. In return, Elizabeth gave them status, influence, and the opportunity to profit from their positions. The partnership between the Crown and the gentry was the foundation of Elizabethan government.

🎭 The Elizabethan Theatre

The Rise of Theatre

  • Professional theatre was essentially an Elizabethan invention - before the 1570s, plays were performed by amateur groups or travelling players
  • The first purpose-built theatre in England, The Theatre, was built in Shoreditch, London, in 1576 by James Burbage
  • The most famous theatre, The Globe, was built in Southwark in 1599 by the Lord Chamberlain's Men, Shakespeare's company
  • By 1600, London had at least 8 purpose-built theatres and several smaller "private" theatres
  • Theatres were located outside the City of London's jurisdiction (in Southwark and Shoreditch) because the City authorities disapproved of them

The Globe Theatre

  • The Globe could hold approximately 3,000 spectators
  • "Groundlings" paid 1 penny to stand in the open yard around the stage
  • Seats in the galleries cost 2 pennies; a seat with a cushion cost 3 pennies
  • The stage was a thrust stage that projected into the yard, surrounded by the audience on three sides
  • There was no scenery - Shakespeare's plays used language to create setting ("This castle hath a pleasant seat")
  • Actors were all male - boys played the female roles
Example: William Shakespeare (1564-1616)

William Shakespeare was born in Stratford-upon-Avon in April 1564. He probably attended the local King Edward VI Grammar School, where he would have studied Latin literature. By 1592, he was established in London as an actor and playwright. He wrote approximately 37 plays and 154 sonnets. His plays ranged from comedies (A Midsummer Night's Dream, Twelfth Night) to tragedies (Hamlet, Othello, King Lear, Macbeth) to histories (Henry V, Richard III). He was a shareholder in the Globe Theatre and became a wealthy man. His works were performed before both commoners and Queen Elizabeth herself.

Why Theatre Flourished

  • Peace and prosperity: The relative stability of Elizabeth's reign allowed culture to flourish
  • Growing population: London's population grew from approximately 50,000 in 1550 to over 200,000 by 1600, providing a large audience
  • Renaissance culture: The revival of classical learning inspired new forms of drama and literature
  • Patronage: Theatrical companies were sponsored by noblemen (the Lord Chamberlain's Men, the Lord Admiral's Men)
  • Social mixing: Theatre was one of the few places where all social classes mixed together

📖 Puritan Opposition to Entertainment

Why Puritans Opposed the Theatre

  • Puritans believed that theatre was sinful and immoral - it encouraged lying (actors pretending to be someone else), vanity, and idleness
  • Theatres were associated with crime, prostitution, and public disorder
  • Performances on Sundays violated the Sabbath
  • Cross-dressing (boys playing female roles) was condemned as a violation of the Biblical prohibition: "The woman shall not wear that which pertaineth unto a man" (Deuteronomy 22:5)
  • The crowds attracted by theatres were seen as a source of the plague - when plague struck, theatres were closed by the authorities
Puritan Campaigns Against Theatre: In 1572, the Puritan-influenced London Common Council banned plays within the City of London, forcing theatres to build outside the City's boundaries (in Southwark and Shoreditch). In 1574, the City banned all plays within its jurisdiction. In 1576, the first theatre was built just outside the City walls. Puritans in Parliament repeatedly tried to ban theatre entirely, though they were unsuccessful during Elizabeth's reign. After the Civil War in 1642, the Puritan government finally closed all theatres.

Other Puritan Opposition to Entertainment

  • Puritans objected to many traditional pastimes: bear-baiting, cock-fighting, wrestling, and dancing were all condemned as sinful
  • They especially opposed activities on Sundays, which they believed should be devoted entirely to worship and rest
  • In areas with strong Puritan influence (such as parts of East Anglia and Kent), local magistrates banned Sunday sports and entertainment
  • This brought Puritans into conflict with ordinary people who wanted their traditional recreations
Example: The Book of Sports (1618)

The tension between Puritans and traditional recreations continued after Elizabeth's death. In 1618, James I issued the "Book of Sports," which explicitly permitted certain recreational activities (dancing, archery, leaping) after Sunday church services. Puritans were outraged. When Charles I reissued the declaration in 1633, he ordered it to be read in all churches - Puritan ministers who refused were suspended from their positions. This conflict was one of the long-term causes of the English Civil War (1642-51).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the education a boy would receive at a grammar school in Elizabethan England. (4 marks)

Q2: Explain why education for girls was so limited in Elizabethan England. (4 marks)

Q3: Why did the Elizabethan theatre flourish? (8 marks)

Q4: How far did Puritan opposition threaten the development of Elizabethan theatre? (8 marks)

Q5: "Education was the most important factor in the rise of the gentry." How far do you agree? (16 marks + 4 SPaG)

Q6: Explain the role of the gentry in Elizabethan government. (4 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. At a grammar school, a boy would study Latin grammar (using Lily's Grammar), read classical authors like Cicero and Virgil, study Greek, rhetoric, and logic. School hours were long (6am-5pm), discipline was harsh (beatings with a birch rod), and all teaching was in Latin. Holidays were limited, and boys were punished for speaking English in school.
  2. Education for girls was limited because: Elizabethan society was patriarchal and believed a woman's role was domestic; girls were expected to become wives and mothers, not professionals; the Bible was used to justify female subordination; girls were not admitted to grammar schools or universities; even wealthy girls were taught only reading, writing, music, needlework, and household management; no career required women to be educated.
  3. The Elizabethan theatre flourished because: the relative stability of Elizabeth's reign allowed culture to develop; London's population grew rapidly to over 200,000 by 1600, providing a large audience; the Renaissance inspired new forms of drama; patronage by noblemen supported theatrical companies (the Lord Chamberlain's Men, the Lord Admiral's Men); purpose-built theatres like The Theatre (1576) and The Globe (1599) provided permanent venues; talented playwrights like Shakespeare and Marlowe wrote compelling plays; theatre attracted all social classes.
  4. Puritan opposition threatened theatre by: persuading the London Common Council to ban plays within the City in 1572 and 1574; repeatedly attempting to ban theatre in Parliament; opposing performances on Sundays; condemning cross-dressing (boys playing women); associating theatres with crime and the plague. However, theatre survived because: Elizabeth and her government did not support the Puritan campaign; theatres were built outside the City's jurisdiction; noble patronage protected theatrical companies; the public demand for entertainment was strong; the Puritans were a minority, however vocal.
  5. A balanced answer should discuss: education (grammar schools and universities provided the gentry with the skills to serve as JPs, MPs, and administrators); economic factors (rising land values, the dissolution of the monasteries, and trade enriched gentry families); political factors (the Tudor reliance on the gentry for local government gave them power and influence); social mobility (merchants and lawyers could buy land and join the gentry). Argue that education was important but not the only factor - economic and political changes were equally significant.
  6. The gentry served as: Justices of the Peace (unpaid local magistrates who maintained law and order, handled minor crimes, and enforced the Religious Settlement); Members of Parliament in the House of Commons; commissioners for collecting taxes and mustering troops; sheriffs and other local officials. They were the essential link between Elizabeth's central government and the localities, and without them the Tudor system of government could not have functioned.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Use specific dates: The Theatre 1576, The Globe 1599, Shakespeare born 1564
  • For education questions, distinguish clearly between petty school, grammar school, and university
  • Always highlight the gender divide - education for girls is a very common exam question
  • Link the rise of the gentry to the practical needs of Elizabethan government
  • For theatre questions, explain both why it flourished AND why Puritans opposed it
  • Remember the irony of Elizabeth being an educated female ruler who did not extend opportunities to other women
  • Use the language of analysis: "however," "despite this," "the most important reason was..."

History Exam Tips:
Know the chronological sequence of plots against Elizabeth. For the Armada, explain causes AND reasons for English victory. For poverty, distinguish between attitudes to 'deserving' and 'undeserving' poor. The historic environment question requires linking the site to wider themes.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think elizabeth solved the religious problem. Wrong: Elizabeth solved the religious problem Correct: The Religious Settlement was a compromise that satisfied neither Puritans nor Catholics. Catholic plots continued throughout her reign.

Students often think the spanish armada was defeated entirely by the english. Wrong: The Spanish Armada was defeated entirely by the English Correct: While English tactics (fire ships) were important, the weather played a crucial role. Many Spanish ships were wrecked by storms.

Students often think elizabethan england was a golden age for everyone. Wrong: Elizabethan England was a golden age for everyone Correct: While the elite prospered, many lived in poverty. Enclosure displaced peasants, harvests failed, and the Poor Laws were often harsh.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

8 marks: Describe the features of Elizabethan education.

Elizabethan education was structured in stages and heavily dependent on social class and gender. The first stage was petty school (age 5-7), also called "dame schools," which were small, informal schools run by a woman in her home. Children learned basic reading, writing, and arithmetic using a "hornbook" — a wooden board covered with transparent horn showing the alphabet and the Lord's Prayer. Fees were small but discipline was harsh; children were beaten with a birch rod for misbehaviour.

The second stage was grammar school (age 7-14), which provided a more advanced education focused on Latin and Greek. Many were "endowed" schools funded by wealthy patrons. The curriculum was almost entirely classical: Latin grammar using Lily's "Grammar," reading authors like Cicero and Virgil, Greek, rhetoric, and logic. School hours were long (6am-5pm in summer), and boys were punished for speaking English rather than Latin. By the 1580s, Oxford had approximately 1,500 students and Cambridge approximately 1,300.

Education for girls was extremely limited. Even wealthy girls were taught only reading, writing, music, needlework, and household management at home. Girls were not admitted to grammar schools or universities. The irony of Elizabeth's reign was that the Queen herself was one of the most educated people in England — fluent in Latin, Greek, French, Italian, and Spanish — yet she did little to improve opportunities for other women.

Elizabethan education was also shaped by the rise of the gentry and the flowering of culture. The gentry — approximately 5,000 families — provided JPs, MPs, and local administrators, and grammar schools and universities produced the educated men needed for these roles. The Elizabethan theatre flourished alongside formal education: the first purpose-built theatre, The Theatre, opened in Shoreditch in 1576, and The Globe opened in Southwark in 1599, holding approximately 3,000 spectators. William Shakespeare (1564-1616), who probably attended King Edward VI Grammar School in Stratford, wrote approximately 37 plays performed before both commoners and the Queen. However, Puritans opposed the theatre as sinful and immoral, condemning cross-dressing (boys playing female roles) and successfully banning plays within the City of London in 1572 and 1574.
