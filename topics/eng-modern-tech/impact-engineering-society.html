Impact of Engineering on Society

Social, economic and environmental impacts of engineering industries and infrastructure.

Key Fact: Engineering creates both positive and negative impacts on society, the economy and the environment.
Key Fact: Positive social impacts: improved infrastructure, medical devices, communication technology and housing.
Key Fact: Negative social impacts: noise pollution, traffic congestion, community displacement and digital divide.
Key Fact: Positive economic impacts: job creation, export revenue, technology spin-offs and improved productivity.
Key Fact: Negative economic impacts: job displacement from automation, economic dependency on single industries, cost of pollution clean-up.
Key Fact: Positive environmental impacts: renewable energy systems, efficient transport and pollution control technology.
Key Fact: Negative environmental impacts: resource depletion, pollution, habitat destruction and climate change from fossil fuels.
Key Fact: Sustainable engineering minimises negative impacts while meeting present needs without compromising future generations.
Key Fact: Environmental legislation forces companies to reduce their environmental impact through regulations and standards.
Key Fact: Corporate social responsibility (CSR) means companies voluntarily consider social and environmental effects.
Key Fact: Life cycle assessment evaluates the total environmental impact from raw material through to disposal.
Key Fact: Engineers have an ethical responsibility to design safe products and consider community welfare.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Impact of Engineering on Society, you must know:

  • CSR: Corporate Social Responsibility - a company's voluntary commitment to consider social and environmental impacts.
  • Sustainable engineering: Designing products that meet present needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs.
  • Digital divide: The gap between those who have access to modern technology and those who do not.
  • Environmental legislation: Laws that set standards for pollution, waste, emissions and resource use to protect the environment.
  • Technology spin-off: A secondary product or application derived from technology originally developed for another purpose.
  • Ethical responsibility: The moral duty of engineers to design safe products and consider the welfare of society.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Give two positive and two negative social impacts of building a new motorway.

Q: Explain how engineering can both cause and help solve environmental problems.

Q: What is CSR and why is it important for engineering companies?

Q: Describe how life cycle assessment helps engineers reduce environmental impact.

Q: Explain why engineers have an ethical responsibility to consider safety in their designs.

✅ Answers

  1. Positive: 1) Improved connectivity reducing journey times. 2) Reduced congestion on local roads. Negative: 1) Noise and air pollution affecting nearby communities. 2) Community displacement and habitat destruction.
  2. Engineering causes problems through resource extraction, manufacturing emissions and waste. However, engineering also provides solutions: renewable energy, efficient processes, electric vehicles and recycling infrastructure.
  3. CSR means a company voluntarily considers social and environmental impacts alongside profit. It builds public trust, ensures long-term sustainability and attracts customers who value responsible business.
  4. LCA quantifies all impacts from material extraction through manufacture, use and disposal. By identifying the stage with greatest impact, engineers target improvements effectively.
  5. Engineers create products that can cause harm if they fail. The public trusts that engineered products are safe because they lack expertise to assess risks. Engineers have a moral duty to design with appropriate safety margins and test thoroughly.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • In impact questions, always give BOTH positive AND negative impacts - one-sided answers lose marks.
  • Categorise impacts: social (people), economic (money/jobs), environmental (planet).
  • Use specific examples rather than vague statements.
  • Link CSR and sustainability to commercial benefits, not just ethics.
  • In ethical questions, reference the professional duty of engineers to protect public safety.

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Engineering Exam Tips — Impact of Engineering on Society:
1. For Impact of Engineering on Society questions, use precise design and technology terminology
2. Consider function, aesthetics, ergonomics, sustainability and cost in your answers
3. When evaluating, justify your design decisions with reference to user needs and specifications
4. Show your understanding of Impact of Engineering on Society through both theory and practical application
5. Reference real products and manufacturing processes where relevant

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Engineering always has a positive impact on society. ✓ Engineering creates both positive impacts (infrastructure, medical technology) and negative impacts (pollution, resource depletion).

✗ Environmental legislation only applies to large companies. ✓ Environmental regulations apply to all companies regardless of size.

✗ Sustainability is only about recycling. ✓ Sustainability encompasses the entire life cycle: material choice, energy-efficient manufacture, product longevity AND end-of-life recycling.

✗ Economic growth and environmental protection are always in conflict. ✓ Sustainable engineering can drive economic growth through efficiency savings and new green technologies.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

A company plans to build a large wind farm on agricultural land. Discuss the social, economic and environmental impacts. [8 marks]

The wind farm has significant positive environmental impacts: clean electricity without CO2 emissions, contributing to carbon reduction targets. Positive economic impacts include job creation during construction, ongoing maintenance roles and rental income for landowners. However, opponents raise valid social concerns: visual impact, noise affecting nearby residents and construction disruption. Ecological concerns include bird and bat mortality. Economic concerns include initial subsidy cost and potential property value reduction near turbines. Overall, the long-term environmental benefits outweigh localised impacts, provided careful site selection minimises effects on residents and wildlife.
