Practical Assessment Technique

How to approach the NEA/practical project: research, design, planning, manufacture and evaluation.

Key Fact: The NEA (Non-Exam Assessment) is a practical project that typically contributes 25-40% of the GCSE grade.
Key Fact: AQA Engineering NEA (40%): students produce engineering drawings, a working prototype and a portfolio.
Key Fact: WJEC Eduqas has two NEA units: Manufacturing Engineering Products (Unit 1) and Designing Engineering Products (Unit 2).
Key Fact: The portfolio must show clear progression through research, design, planning, manufacture and evaluation stages.
Key Fact: Research should be relevant and specific: investigate existing products, materials, processes and user needs.
Key Fact: Design development shows how ideas evolve from initial sketches through to the final chosen solution with justification.
Key Fact: Manufacturing plans list operations, tools, materials and safety precautions in the correct sequence.
Key Fact: Quality of manufacture is assessed: accuracy, finish, functionality and adherence to the plan.
Key Fact: Testing and evaluation compare the finished product against the specification; identify improvements.
Key Fact: Annotation on drawings and photographs explains decisions - do not leave the examiner to guess your reasoning.
Key Fact: Time management is critical: allocate sufficient time for each stage and meet all deadlines.
Key Fact: The portfolio should be concise, focused and well-organised - quality of evidence matters more than quantity of pages.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Practical Assessment Technique, you must know:

  • NEA: Non-Exam Assessment - a practical project assessed through a portfolio and manufactured outcome.
  • Portfolio: A structured document presenting evidence of the design and manufacturing process.
  • Annotation: Written notes added to drawings, sketches and photographs explaining design decisions and reasoning.
  • Design development: The process of evolving a design from initial concept sketches through refinement to the final solution.
  • Specification criteria: The measurable requirements from the specification used to evaluate whether the finished product is successful.
  • Manufacturing plan: A detailed document listing every operation, tool, material and safety measure needed to produce the component.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Describe the structure of a strong NEA portfolio.

Q: Why is annotation important in the NEA portfolio?

Q: Explain how to evaluate the finished NEA product against the specification.

Q: What are the most common mistakes in NEA portfolios?

Q: How should you manage your time across the NEA project?

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Context and brief. 2) Research: existing products, materials, processes. 3) Specification: measurable criteria. 4) Design development: initial ideas to final design. 5) Manufacturing plan: operations, tools, materials, safety. 6) Manufacture: photographic evidence. 7) Testing and evaluation: compare against specification.
  2. Annotation explains the reasoning behind design decisions. Without annotation, the examiner cannot understand why decisions were made. Good annotation links each decision to a specification requirement or engineering principle.
  3. Take each specification criterion in turn and test whether the product meets it. Record results with photographic evidence. If any criterion is not met, explain why and suggest improvements.
  4. 1) Too much irrelevant research. 2) Jumping from brief to final design without showing development. 3) Missing manufacturing plan. 4) Poor annotation. 5) Superficial evaluation.
  5. Allocate roughly: 20% to research and specification, 25% to design development, 15% to planning, 30% to manufacture and 10% to evaluation. Set internal deadlines for each stage.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • In NEA questions, emphasise the LINK between stages: research informs specification, which drives design, which guides manufacture, which is evaluated against the specification.
  • Annotation is the most commonly under-used NEA skill - annotate everything.
  • Evaluation must reference specific specification criteria - generic comments gain no marks.
  • Show design DEVELOPMENT: initial ideas should differ from the final design, with clear reasons for changes.
  • Manufacturing plans must be realistic and specific - name actual tools, machines and materials.

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Engineering Exam Tips:
Use precise engineering terminology in all answers. When performing calculations, always show working and include units. For material selection, justify choices by linking properties to requirements. For process questions, explain why a method is suitable for the material, quantity and tolerance. For evaluation questions, consider performance, cost, manufacture and sustainability. AQA 8852 and WJEC Eduqas 5799QA reward answers that demonstrate understanding through specific technical evidence and accurate calculations.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ More pages in the portfolio always means a higher mark. ✓ Quality of evidence and depth of thinking matter far more than quantity; a concise, well-annotated portfolio scores higher.

✗ The NEA is only about making the product. ✓ The NEA assesses the full process: research, design, planning, manufacture AND evaluation. Portfolio evidence is weighted equally with or more than the physical product.

✗ You should choose the easiest product to manufacture for the NEA. ✓ The NEA rewards challenge and skill: a simple product demonstrating limited competence scores lower than an ambitious project showing a range of skills.

✗ Evaluation is just saying whether you liked the product. ✓ Evaluation systematically tests against each specification criterion, identifies what works and what does not, and explains improvements with engineering reasoning.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

A student's NEA project is to design and manufacture a wall-mounted tool holder for a home workshop. Write a suitable specification with at least six measurable criteria. [6 marks]

1) Hold at least 8 hand tools securely. 2) Maximum dimensions: 600mm wide x 300mm high x 100mm deep. 3) Maximum weight: 2 kg. 4) Support a total load of 15 kg when fully loaded. 5) Materials costing no more than 10 pounds total. 6) Made using at least three different manufacturing processes. 7) Surface must be corrosion-resistant for a workshop environment. 8) Mountable on a wall using no more than 4 standard screws. Each criterion is measurable and testable for systematic evaluation.
