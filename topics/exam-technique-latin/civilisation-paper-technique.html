L26: Civilisation Paper Technique

Foundation Higher OCR J282, Eduqas C990QS, AQA 8682

Civilisation Paper Technique

Specific techniques for the GCSE Latin Civilisation paper (OCR Component 06 / Eduqas Component 3), covering source analysis, thematic essays, and the use of prescribed and non-prescribed ancient sources.

Key Fact: OCR J282 Component 06 (Literature and Culture): 50 marks, 1 hour, 25%. Students study two prescribed civilisation themes using ancient source material.
Key Fact: Eduqas C990QS Component 3: Roman Civilisation topics (e.g. Daily Life, Entertainment, Religion, Army). Topics change on a cycle.
Key Fact: The civilisation paper requires knowledge of both prescribed ancient sources AND additional non-prescribed ancient sources that supplement them.
Key Fact: Source-based questions: you must analyse what the ancient source tells us, its limitations, and whether other sources support or contradict it.
Key Fact: Thematic essay questions: structure your answer with an introduction, multiple paragraphs each supported by ancient source evidence, and a conclusion.
Key Fact: Always refer to specific ancient sources by name (e.g. 'Pliny the Younger, Letters 10.33' or 'the Ara Pacis frieze'), not just 'a source says'.
Key Fact: Distinguish between prescribed sources (which you must know in detail) and non-prescribed sources (which you should use to supplement your answer).
Key Fact: Consider the reliability and bias of sources: who wrote it, when, for what audience, and with what purpose? Not all ancient sources are equally trustworthy.
Key Fact: Visual sources (inscriptions, monuments, archaeological evidence) are as important as literary sources and must be studied alongside texts.
Key Fact: Modern scholarly interpretation is NOT required at GCSE — focus on what the ancient sources themselves say, not what modern historians argue.

✅ Answers

  1. Component 06: 50 marks, 1 hour, 25% of GCSE. Students study two prescribed civilisation themes using ancient source material, supplemented by non-prescribed sources.
  2. Prescribed sources are specified by the exam board and must be studied in detail. Non-prescribed sources are additional ancient evidence you should know to supplement and enrich your answers.
  3. Identify what the source tells us, consider who created it and their potential bias, note its limitations (what it doesn't tell us), and compare it with other sources that support or contradict it.
  4. Because marks are awarded for precise source reference. Saying 'a source says' is too vague — you must name the author, work, and where possible the specific passage or item.
  5. Inscriptions (e.g. tombstone epitaphs), monuments (e.g. Ara Pacis, Trajan's Column), archaeological remains (e.g. Pompeii, Hadrian's Wall), coins, and graffiti.
  6. No — at GCSE, focus on what the ancient sources themselves say. Modern scholarship is not required and will not earn marks. Use ancient evidence, not modern opinions.
  7. Introduction stating your thesis, 3-4 paragraphs each making a distinct point supported by named ancient sources, and a conclusion. Use connectives to link your argument.
  8. Who wrote it (their background and bias), when it was written (contemporary vs much later), the intended audience, the author's purpose (e.g. propaganda vs record-keeping), and the type of evidence (archaeological vs literary).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Learn prescribed sources in detail — you will be examined on your specific knowledge of them.
  • Always name the ancient source you are citing: author, work, and reference where possible.
  • Consider source reliability: who created it, when, and for what purpose?
  • Use both literary and visual/archaeological evidence in your answers — this shows broader knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ You can answer civilisation questions from general knowledge about Rome. ✓ Answers must be based on specific ancient source evidence, not general knowledge. Every point must be supported by a named ancient source — marks are awarded for source reference, not for general historical knowledge.

✗ All ancient sources are equally reliable. ✓ Sources vary greatly in reliability depending on who created them, when, and for what purpose. A contemporary inscription is more reliable for factual detail than a much later literary account written for propaganda.

✗ You should discuss what modern historians think about the topic. ✓ At GCSE, you should focus on what the ancient sources themselves say. Modern scholarly debate is not required and will not earn additional marks. Use ancient evidence, not modern interpretation.

✗ Visual and archaeological evidence is less important than literary sources. ✓ Visual sources (inscriptions, monuments, archaeological remains) are equally important and often more reliable than literary sources because they are contemporary and not shaped by an author's agenda. A balanced answer uses both.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how you would approach a civilisation paper question asking 'How useful are ancient sources for understanding Roman entertainment?' (8 marks)

I would structure this as a thematic evaluation with multiple paragraphs, each using a specific named ancient source. First, I would argue that literary sources provide detailed descriptions: Pliny the Younger's Letters describe the Colosseum from an elite spectator's perspective, giving vivid detail about gladiatorial combat and the crowd's reactions. However, I would note Pliny's bias as a wealthy senator — his account reflects elite experience, not that of ordinary spectators. Second, archaeological evidence provides direct, unbiased physical data: the Colosseum itself reveals seating capacity, the hypogeum (underground area), and the amphitheatre's engineering. Inscriptions at Pompeii record gladiators' fight records and crowd favourites, giving a ground-level perspective that literary sources lack. Third, visual sources like the Zliten mosaic from Libya and Trajan's Column show gladiatorial types and combat that corroborate literary descriptions. Coins commemorating the Colosseum's opening and the Ara Pacis's procession scenes illustrate how emperors used entertainment for political messaging. However, sources have limitations: literary accounts are mostly by elite men who did not represent the majority experience; archaeological evidence is fragmentary; and visual sources may idealise or simplify what they depict. The most useful approach combines multiple source types: literary sources for detail and narrative, archaeological evidence for physical facts, and visual sources for contemporary illustration. No single source type is sufficient alone — the value of ancient sources lies in comparing them to build a rounded picture.
