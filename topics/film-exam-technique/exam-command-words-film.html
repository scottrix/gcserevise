Command Words & Question Types

Understanding Eduqas C680QS exam questions

Key Fact: Command words tell you exactly what the examiner expects: 'describe' means give an account, 'explain' means give reasons, 'analyse' means break down and examine, 'evaluate' means make a judgement.
Key Fact: Describe questions require you to identify and outline film language features or production contexts without needing to explain their effect.
Key Fact: Explain questions require you to give reasons why something produces a particular effect or meaning, connecting technique to impact.
Key Fact: Analyse questions require you to break down a sequence or concept into its component parts and examine how they work together to create meaning.
Key Fact: Evaluate questions require you to make a judgement about effectiveness, supported by evidence, weighing strengths against weaknesses or comparing different interpretations.
Key Fact: The Eduqas C680QS exam uses a range of question types from short one-mark identification questions to extended 15-mark analytical essays.
Key Fact: Short-answer questions test recall and identification: name a technique, identify a feature or state a production fact accurately and concisely.
Key Fact: Medium-answer questions typically require explanation of how a film language element creates meaning, connecting description to analysis in a few sentences.
Key Fact: Extended questions demand sustained argument with multiple examples, clear structure and a conclusion that directly answers the question set.
Key Fact: Reading the question carefully is essential: many students lose marks by answering a different question from the one actually asked.
Key Fact: The mark scheme rewards precise film language terminology, specific examples and clear connections between technique and meaning.
Key Fact: Timing is critical: allocate roughly one minute per mark and ensure extended answers receive sufficient time for planning and structuring.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Command Words & Question Types, you must know:

  • Command word: The specific verb in an exam question that indicates what type of response is required, such as describe, explain, analyse or evaluate.
  • Describe: A command word requiring you to give an account of something, identifying and outlining its features without explaining why or how.
  • Explain: A command word requiring you to give reasons, showing how or why something produces a particular effect or meaning.
  • Analyse: A command word requiring you to break something into its component parts and examine how they work together to create meaning or effect.
  • Evaluate: A command word requiring you to make a judgement about effectiveness or quality, supported by evidence and reasoning.
  • Mark scheme: The document that sets out how marks are awarded for each question, specifying the criteria and levels of response expected by examiners.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the difference between a 'describe' question and an 'explain' question in the Eduqas Film Studies exam?

Q: How should you approach an 'analyse' question in the film studies exam?

Q: What does an 'evaluate' question require that an 'analyse' question does not?

Q: Why do students frequently lose marks on short-answer questions in the film studies exam?

Q: How should you manage your time in the Eduqas Film Studies exam to maximise marks?

✅ Answers

  1. A describe question asks you to identify and outline what you see or know, such as naming a camera technique or stating a production context. An explain question goes further by requiring you to give reasons how or why that technique or context creates a particular effect or meaning. For example, describing a low-angle shot means stating what it is, while explaining it means showing how it makes a character appear powerful and why the filmmaker chose this. Many students lose marks by describing when the question asks them to explain, failing to connect observation to meaning.
  2. An analyse question requires you to break down a sequence or concept into its component parts and examine how they work together to create meaning. Start by identifying the key film language elements present, then examine how each contributes to meaning, and finally show how these elements combine and interact. Use specific examples from the film to support each point. Avoid simply listing techniques; instead, demonstrate how they function together within the sequence. Structure your response with clear paragraphs, each focusing on a different element or layer of meaning, building towards an overall interpretation.
  3. An evaluate question requires a judgement about effectiveness, quality or significance, going beyond analysis to weigh the relative strengths and weaknesses of something. While analysis breaks down how meaning is created, evaluation assesses how successfully or effectively it is created. For example, analysis might show how cinematography and sound create tension, while evaluation would judge whether this tension is effective and why, or compare different interpretations of the same sequence. Evaluation requires you to take a position supported by evidence, considering alternative viewpoints before reaching a supported conclusion.
  4. Students lose marks on short-answer questions through imprecise terminology, such as saying 'the camera moves' instead of 'the camera tracks left'. They may give vague or partial answers that do not match what the mark scheme specifically requires, or write too much and waste time that is needed for higher-mark questions. Failing to read the question carefully leads to answering a slightly different question from the one set. Some students provide explanations when only identification is required, or provide descriptions when reasons are needed. Accuracy is essential: a close-up is not the same as an extreme close-up, and a pan is not the same as a tilt.
  5. Allocate roughly one minute per mark as a general guide: a 5-mark question deserves about five minutes, while a 15-mark essay deserves at least fifteen minutes. Read all questions first and plan your time before starting. Attempt every question, as even a brief answer can gain marks that are impossible to recover from a blank response. For extended answers, spend two to three minutes planning your structure and key points before writing. Leave time to check your answers, ensuring you have used precise terminology and answered the actual question set. Prioritise questions where you are confident but do not neglect lower-mark questions that offer easier marks.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always identify the command word first and plan your response around exactly what it asks you to do.
  • For 'evaluate' questions, make sure you reach a clear judgement and do not just analyse without concluding.
  • Use precise film language terminology throughout: examiner mark schemes reward correct technical vocabulary.
  • In extended answers, structure your response with a clear introduction, focused paragraphs and a conclusion that answers the question.
  • Check that every paragraph actually addresses the question set, not just the general topic area.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Describing a technique when the question asks you to explain its effect or meaning. ✓ Identify the command word and ensure your response goes beyond description: 'explain' requires reasons, 'analyse' requires examination of parts, 'evaluate' requires judgement.

✗ Writing a general essay about a film without addressing the specific question asked. ✓ Every paragraph must directly answer the question; read it carefully and refer back to it as you write to stay focused.

✗ Using vague language like 'the camera moves' or 'the lighting is dark' instead of precise terminology. ✓ Use specific terms: 'the camera tracks right' and 'low-key lighting with harsh shadows' demonstrate the knowledge examiners are assessing.

✗ Spending too long on low-mark questions and rushing or omitting high-mark extended answers. ✓ Follow the one-minute-per-mark rule and attempt every question, allocating proportionate time to each based on its mark value.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How should you approach a 15-mark 'evaluate' question in the Eduqas Film Studies exam?

A 15-mark evaluate question requires a structured, sustained argument that reaches a supported judgement about effectiveness. Begin by reading the question carefully and identifying exactly what you are being asked to evaluate. Spend two to three minutes planning: note your key points, the specific examples you will use and your overall judgement. Open with a clear statement that establishes your position in response to the question. Develop your argument through focused paragraphs, each making a distinct point supported by specific evidence from the film. For each point, identify a technique or element, explain how it creates meaning and evaluate its effectiveness, considering both strengths and potential limitations. Use precise film language terminology throughout, as the mark scheme rewards technical accuracy. Address alternative interpretations where relevant, showing that you can weigh different readings before reaching your conclusion. Ensure your conclusion directly answers the question, summarising your judgement and the key reasons for it. Allocate approximately fifteen minutes for the answer, allowing time for planning and checking. The strongest responses demonstrate detailed knowledge, precise analysis and confident evaluation, structured clearly and focused consistently on the question set.
