F1: Cinematography

Eduqas C680QS

Camera shots, angles, movement, composition and lighting

Cinematography

Camera shots, angles, movement, composition and lighting

Key Fact: Cinematography encompasses all visual choices made with the camera to create meaning and emotion in film.
Key Fact: Camera shots are defined by distance: extreme long shot establishes setting, long shot shows full body, medium shot frames waist-up, close-up fills the frame with face or object, extreme close-up isolates a tiny detail.
Key Fact: Camera angles communicate power and status: low angles make subjects appear powerful and dominant, high angles make them seem vulnerable or small, eye-level angles feel neutral and objective.
Key Fact: Dutch or canted angles create unease, disorientation and psychological instability, commonly used in horror and thriller genres.
Key Fact: Camera movement includes pans, tilts, tracking shots, dolly shots, crane shots, handheld and steadicam, each producing distinct emotional and narrative effects.
Key Fact: Tracking and dolly shots follow subjects through space, creating audience immersion and involvement in the on-screen action.
Key Fact: Handheld camera produces shaky, unstable imagery that conveys immediacy, realism, chaos or documentary-style authenticity.
Key Fact: Composition uses the rule of thirds, leading lines, depth and symmetry to arrange visual elements within the frame and guide the audience's eye.
Key Fact: Deep focus keeps foreground and background in sharp focus simultaneously, allowing layered meaning and giving the audience choice about where to look.
Key Fact: Shallow focus isolates a subject against a blurred background, directing attention and creating intimacy or concealment.
Key Fact: Lighting creates mood and atmosphere: high-key lighting is bright and even for comedies and safety, low-key lighting creates strong shadows for drama and threat.
Key Fact: Three-point lighting uses key, fill and back lights to shape the subject, while practical lighting comes from visible in-scene sources for realism.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Cinematography, you must know:

  • Close-up: A shot framing a character's face or a specific object at close range, emphasising emotion and detail.
  • Low-angle shot: A shot taken from below eye level looking up at a subject, making them appear powerful, dominant or threatening.
  • Deep focus: A technique where everything in the frame from foreground to background remains in sharp focus simultaneously.
  • Chiaroscuro: A lighting style with strong contrasts between light and dark, creating dramatic shadows and a tense atmosphere.
  • Tracking shot: A shot where the camera moves physically through space on tracks, following or paralleling a subject's movement.
  • Rule of thirds: A compositional guideline dividing the frame into nine equal sections using two horizontal and two vertical lines, placing key elements at intersections.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How does a low-angle shot influence the audience's perception of a character?

Q: Explain the difference between high-key and low-key lighting and their typical narrative effects.

Q: Why might a filmmaker choose shallow focus rather than deep focus in a particular scene?

Q: What is the 180-degree rule and why is it important in cinematography?

Q: How can camera movement be used to create meaning rather than simply following action?

✅ Answers

  1. A low-angle shot positions the camera below eye level looking up at the subject, making them appear powerful, dominant or threatening. This angle encourages the audience to feel subordinate to or in awe of the character. For example, a villain filmed from a low angle may seem more menacing and imposing, while a hero may appear heroic and commanding.
  2. High-key lighting is bright, even and minimises shadows, creating an optimistic, safe or comedic tone often used in rom-coms and sitcoms. Low-key lighting creates strong contrasts between light and shadow, producing a dark, moody or mysterious atmosphere typical of film noir, horror and thrillers. The choice fundamentally shapes the audience's emotional engagement and genre expectations.
  3. Shallow focus isolates a subject by blurring the foreground or background, directing the audience's attention to a specific element such as a character's emotional reaction or a key object. Deep focus keeps everything sharp, allowing viewers to scan the entire frame and notice relationships between elements at different depths. A filmmaker might use shallow focus to create intimacy or conceal information, while deep focus reveals complexity within the mise-en-scene.
  4. The 180-degree rule states that the camera should stay on one side of an imaginary line drawn between two interacting characters. Crossing this line reverses the screen direction, causing spatial confusion where characters appear to swap positions. Maintaining the rule ensures consistent left-right orientation so the audience can follow the geography of the scene during shot-reverse-shot editing.
  5. Camera movement can convey psychological and narrative meaning beyond simply tracking a subject. A slow push-in can build tension or intimacy; a handheld camera can suggest documentary realism, chaos or instability; a crane rising above a scene can signal transcendence or a character's loss of control. Each movement choice shapes how the audience reads a moment emotionally and thematically.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always link cinematography choices to meaning — never just identify a technique, explain its effect on the audience.
  • Use precise terminology: say 'low-angle shot' not 'shot from below', and 'shallow focus' not 'blurred background'.
  • Compare cinematography across scenes or films to show how different choices create different meanings.
  • When writing about lighting, consider whether it is motivated by an in-film source or unmotivated and purely atmospheric.
  • Think about how cinematography works with other film language elements — a close-up is more powerful when combined with diegetic silence.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Describing the shot type without explaining its effect on meaning or the audience. ✓ Always connect the shot type to its purpose: 'The close-up emphasises the character's fear, drawing the audience into their emotional state.'

✗ Confusing camera angles — e.g. saying a high-angle shot makes a character look powerful. ✓ A high-angle shot looks down on a subject making them appear weak or vulnerable; a low-angle shot looks up making them appear powerful.

✗ Saying 'the camera moves' without specifying the type of movement (pan, tilt, track, dolly). ✓ Use precise movement terms: 'The camera tracks left alongside the character' rather than 'the camera moves.'

✗ Discussing lighting only in terms of brightness, ignoring shadow, contrast and colour temperature. ✓ Analyse lighting quality: 'Low-key lighting with harsh shadows creates a threatening atmosphere' rather than 'the lighting is dark.'

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How does the cinematography in the opening sequence of your chosen film create meaning for the audience?

In the opening sequence, cinematography creates meaning through a combination of shot types, angles, movement and lighting that position the audience in relation to the protagonist. The extreme long shot establishes the isolated setting, with the small figure dwarfed by the vast landscape, conveying vulnerability and insignificance. A high-angle shot looks down on the character as they approach, making them appear small and powerless against their environment. Medium close-ups with shallow focus isolate the character's anxious expression against a blurred background, intensifying unease. Handheld camera during the interior sequence creates instability that mirrors the protagonist's disorientation and makes the audience feel the same uncertainty. Low-key lighting with a single practical light source casts deep shadows across the character's face, the chiaroscuro effect suggesting a divided or hidden nature. A Dutch angle at the moment of revelation tilts the frame to express psychological disturbance. A slow tracking shot moves towards the character in the closing moments, gradually increasing proximity and discomfort. Each choice contributes to cumulative meaning: the protagonist is trapped, vulnerable and psychologically unravelled, and the audience experiences this through deliberate camera manipulation.
