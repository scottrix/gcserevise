F2: Mise-en-Scène

Eduqas C680QS

Setting, props, costume, makeup, staging and performance

Mise-en-Scène

Setting, props, costume, makeup, staging and performance

Key Fact: Mise-en-scene is a French term meaning 'everything placed in the frame' and includes setting, props, costume, makeup, lighting and the positioning and performance of actors.
Key Fact: Setting establishes time period, location, social context and atmosphere — it grounds the narrative and can function as a character itself.
Key Fact: Props carry symbolic meaning and can signal character traits, narrative development or thematic concerns; a motif is a recurring prop that gains significance through repetition.
Key Fact: Costume communicates identity, status, group belonging and character transformation across the film.
Key Fact: Makeup and hair design contribute to character identity, period authenticity and genre conventions such as monstrous appearance in horror.
Key Fact: Staging or blocking refers to the physical positioning and movement of actors within the frame, revealing relationships, power dynamics and emotional states.
Key Fact: Performance encompasses facial expression, body language, gesture and vocal delivery, shaping how characters are read by the audience.
Key Fact: Naturalistic performance aims for realism and authenticity, while stylised performance uses exaggerated or theatrical techniques for effect.
Key Fact: Colour within mise-en-scene is a powerful signifier — warm tones suggest comfort or danger, cool tones suggest detachment or melancholy.
Key Fact: Iconography refers to recurring visual motifs and objects that signal genre, theme or character type within a film.
Key Fact: Changes in mise-en-scene across a film can trace character development, shifting power dynamics or thematic evolution.
Key Fact: Mise-en-scene operates in combination with cinematography, editing and sound to construct layered and cumulative meaning.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Mise-en-Scène, you must know:

  • Mise-en-scene: A French term meaning 'putting on stage'; refers to everything visible within the frame including setting, props, costume, makeup, lighting and performance.
  • Iconography: The visual symbols and recurring motifs associated with a particular genre, character or theme — e.g. the cowboy hat in Westerns.
  • Motif: A recurring element in a film — an object, colour, sound or image — that develops thematic meaning through repetition.
  • Blocking: The precise physical arrangement and movement of actors within a scene, revealing relationships and power dynamics through spatial positioning.
  • Set design: The art of designing and constructing the physical environment in which a film's action takes place.
  • Costume code: The system by which costume choices communicate meaning about character identity, social status, group membership or emotional state.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How does setting function as more than just a backdrop in film?

Q: Explain how costume can be used to show character development across a film.

Q: What is the significance of iconography in genre film?

Q: How can the staging of actors within a scene reveal power dynamics?

Q: Why is it important to consider what is absent from the mise-en-scene as well as what is present?

✅ Answers

  1. Setting can operate as a character in its own right, shaping the narrative and the audience's emotional response. A cramped, cluttered interior may reflect a character's mental state, while a vast, empty landscape can convey isolation or freedom. Historical settings anchor the film in a specific social context, influencing how characters behave and what conflicts arise. Genre is often defined by setting — the dark alley in noir, the spaceship in sci-fi — making it integral to audience expectations and meaning.
  2. Costume changes can trace a character's arc visually. At the start, a character may wear drab, constricting clothes reflecting repression or low status. As they gain confidence or power, costume may become bolder in colour, more fitted or more expressive. Conversely, a character's fall from grace might be marked by deterioration — torn, stained or ill-fitting clothing. Each costume choice is a visual shorthand that the audience reads intuitively, making costume a powerful narrative and thematic tool.
  3. Iconography provides a visual shorthand that audiences recognise instantly, setting expectations and creating pleasure through familiarity. In horror, iconography includes dark houses, knives, masks and shadows; in sci-fi, it includes futuristic technology, spaceships and dystopian cityscapes. These elements encode genre conventions, signal narrative possibilities and allow filmmakers to either satisfy or subvert audience expectations.
  4. Physical positioning communicates hierarchy and relationships. A character placed higher in the frame or physically above others conveys dominance; one isolated at the edge of the frame suggests marginalisation. Characters placed close together may signal intimacy or alliance, while distance between them can indicate conflict or estrangement. Blocking that positions a character centrally draws the eye, making them the focus of attention.
  5. What is deliberately excluded from the frame can be as meaningful as what is included. An empty chair may signify loss or absence; a character's bare, impersonal room may reveal emotional emptiness. Excluding certain objects or colours can create unease or highlight what a character lacks. Filmmakers make deliberate choices about every element in the frame, so considering absences can reveal thematic concerns about deprivation, loss or repression that presence alone cannot convey.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Don't just list mise-en-scene elements — explain what each one communicates to the audience.
  • Use specific examples from the film: name the prop, describe the costume, identify the colour change.
  • Show how mise-en-scene changes across scenes to track character or thematic development.
  • Connect mise-en-scene to genre: how do setting, costume and props signal the film's genre conventions?
  • Remember that mise-en-scene works with cinematography — a prop's meaning changes depending on how it is framed.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Listing mise-en-scene elements without explaining what they mean or why they are significant. ✓ Always analyse: 'The peeling wallpaper and dim lighting suggest decay and neglect, reflecting the family's decline.'

✗ Confusing mise-en-scene with cinematography — e.g. calling a camera angle part of mise-en-scene. ✓ Mise-en-scene covers what is IN the frame; cinematography covers HOW the frame captures it. A prop is mise-en-scene; a close-up of it is cinematography.

✗ Discussing costume only in terms of what characters wear, ignoring colour, condition and change. ✓ Analyse costume details: 'Her transition from muted grey to vivid red signals her growing confidence and shift from passive to active.'

✗ Treating performance as separate from mise-en-scene rather than an integrated element. ✓ Integrate performance with other elements: 'The character's rigid posture combined with the sterile, symmetrical set design creates an unsettling atmosphere of control.'

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How does mise-en-scene construct meaning in a key sequence from your chosen film?

The mise-en-scene creates meaning through a carefully constructed combination of setting, props, costume, makeup and performance that establishes a world of social division and personal desperation. The setting is a cramped, dimly lit apartment with peeling wallpaper and minimal furniture, immediately communicating poverty and neglect. The worn fabric of the sofa and the empty fridge function as props that reinforce the character's economic hardship without dialogue. The protagonist's faded, oversized coat and scuffed shoes contrast sharply with the crisp, tailored suit of the visitor, visually encoding the power imbalance between them. Makeup enhances this contrast: the protagonist's pallid complexion and dark circles suggest exhaustion, while the visitor appears well-groomed and healthy. The staging places the visitor standing in the centre of the room looking down at the protagonist who sits hunched on the edge of the sofa, their positions literalising the hierarchy between them. A recurring motif — a child's drawing pinned to the wall — appears in the background, its bright colours the only vibrant element in the muted palette, symbolising hope and the stakes of the encounter. As the scene progresses, the protagonist gradually straightens their posture, a subtle shift in performance that signals growing resolve. The mise-en-scene thus operates on multiple levels: setting and props establish context, costume and makeup visualise power dynamics, staging and performance dramatise the emotional encounter, and the motif connects the immediate scene to the wider narrative.
