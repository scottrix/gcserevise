Horror Genre

Horror conventions, sub-genres, audience pleasures and key films

Key Fact: Horror is a genre designed to elicit fear, dread and disgust, confronting audiences with the transgressive, the unknown and the repressed.
Key Fact: Core horror conventions include isolation, the monstrous Other, transgression of boundaries, the return of the repressed and the struggle for survival.
Key Fact: Horror sub-genres include slasher, supernatural, psychological, body horror, found footage, folk horror and gothic horror.
Key Fact: Carol Clover's 'final girl' theory identifies the last surviving female character in slasher films as the figure with whom the audience ultimately identifies.
Key Fact: Julia Kristeva's concept of the abject — that which disturbs identity and bodily boundaries — is central to understanding body horror.
Key Fact: Freud's concept of the uncanny — the familiar made strange — explains the power of doppelgangers, dolls and haunted domestic spaces.
Key Fact: Audience pleasures in horror include adrenaline rush, catharsis, the safe exploration of taboo subjects and the intellectual pleasure of decoding meaning.
Key Fact: Horror iconography includes masks, knives, dark houses, isolated locations, blood and religious imagery.
Key Fact: The monster in horror often functions as a metaphor for social anxieties — disease, otherness, sexuality, technology or social change.
Key Fact: Key horror films include Nosferatu, Psycho, The Exorcist, Halloween, The Shining and Get Out, each redefining the genre.
Key Fact: Horror often features a narrative structure of normality, transgression, escalation and resolution or ambiguous survival.
Key Fact: The genre's relationship with censorship and controversy reflects its power to disturb, making it culturally significant beyond entertainment.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Horror Genre, you must know:

  • The Final Girl: Carol Clover's term for the last surviving character in a slasher film, typically female, who defeats the killer and embodies both vulnerability and resourcefulness.
  • The abject: Julia Kristeva's concept for that which disturbs identity and bodily boundaries — blood, decay, corpses — provoking disgust by threatening the clean self.
  • The uncanny: Freud's concept for the unsettling feeling when something familiar becomes strangely disturbing — doppelgangers, dolls, haunted homes.
  • Transgression: The violation of boundaries — moral, physical, social or psychological — that is central to horror's power to disturb and confront audiences with the forbidden.
  • Slasher film: A horror sub-genre featuring a killer who murders a group of victims one by one, typically using bladed weapons, with the final survivor confronting the killer.
  • Folk horror: A horror sub-genre set in rural or isolated communities where ancient beliefs, rituals and landscapes generate a sense of pagan dread — e.g. The Wicker Man.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How does Carol Clover's 'final girl' theory help us understand the slasher sub-genre?

Q: What is the abject and why is it central to understanding body horror?

Q: How does the monster in horror function as a metaphor for social anxieties?

Q: What audience pleasures does the horror genre offer, and why do audiences seek them out?

Q: How has the horror genre evolved to reflect changing cultural anxieties?

✅ Answers

  1. Clover's theory identifies the pattern where the last surviving character — typically a young woman — defeats the killer and survives. This figure embodies both traditionally feminine qualities (vulnerability, empathy) and masculine ones (resourcefulness, physical courage), allowing audiences of all genders to identify with her. Her survival is earned through moral purity and active resistance, distinguishing her from earlier victims. The theory reveals that slasher films, despite apparent misogyny, ultimately centre female experience and agency.
  2. The abject, as defined by Kristeva, refers to that which disturbs identity and bodily boundaries — bodily fluids, decay, corpses, open wounds. These provoke horror because they remind us of the body's fragility and mortality. In body horror, the abject is central because the genre's project is to violate bodily integrity — bodies mutate, erupt and disintegrate. Films like The Fly make the abject visceral, forcing audiences to confront the body as unstable and permeable. The horror is the collapse of the boundary between self and not-self.
  3. Horror monsters externalise fears that society struggles to articulate directly. Zombies represent mindless conformity or consumerism; vampires embody fears of sexuality, disease or exploitation; aliens stand for immigration or racial otherness. The monster gives form to diffuse anxieties, making them concrete and confrontable. Godzilla embodies nuclear terror; Frankenstein's monster embodies fear of scientific overreach. The monster's defeat temporarily contains the anxiety, but its power lies in reminding audiences that the real-world source of the fear persists.
  4. Horror offers multiple pleasures: the adrenaline rush of controlled fear in a safe environment; catharsis where repressed anxieties are released; intellectual pleasure of decoding meaning — reading the monster as metaphor; transgressive pleasure of safely experiencing the taboo; and social pleasure where communal viewing amplifies emotional response. Audiences seek horror not despite its discomfort but because of it: the genre offers emotional intensity and cognitive engagement that few other genres provide.
  5. Horror evolves because it is fundamentally responsive to its cultural moment. 1950s Cold War horror reflected communist paranoia; 1970s horror reflected post-Vietnam disillusionment; 1980s slashers reflected Reagan-era moral panic; 1990s self-aware horror reflected media saturation; 2010s elevated horror reflects identity politics, generational trauma and social inequality. Each era's horror externalises its specific anxieties, making the genre a barometer of cultural fear. This evolution demonstrates horror's unique capacity to make invisible anxieties terrifyingly visible.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always connect horror conventions to their emotional or psychological effect on the audience.
  • Name specific theorists when relevant — Clover for the final girl, Kristeva for the abject, Freud for the uncanny.
  • Identify which sub-genre your film belongs to and how it uses or challenges that sub-genre's conventions.
  • Discuss the monster as metaphor — what real-world anxiety does it embody?
  • Consider the film's cultural context: when was it made, and what fears were current in society at that time?

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Saying horror is 'scary' without analysing what specific fears the genre exploits and how. ✓ Identify the fear: 'The film exploits the fear of bodily violation, using body horror conventions to make the audience confront the fragility of the human body.'

✗ Describing the final girl without engaging with Clover's theory or explaining the figure's significance. ✓ Apply theory: 'The final girl, as Clover describes, is the audience's identificatory figure — her survival rewards moral purity and active resistance.'

✗ Treating the monster literally without considering its metaphorical or allegorical function. ✓ Read metaphorically: 'The vampire functions as a metaphor for sexual predation and the fear of contamination, reflecting Victorian anxieties about sexuality and disease.'

✗ Generalising about 'horror' without distinguishing between sub-genres that have very different conventions. ✓ Be specific: 'As a folk horror film, it draws on conventions of rural isolation and pagan dread rather than the urban slashing of the slasher sub-genre.'

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How does your chosen horror film use genre conventions to explore a social or cultural anxiety?

The film uses horror conventions to expose the anxiety of a specific social issue that mainstream cinema often avoids. The film deploys classic horror iconography: the isolated house, the unsettling behaviour of hosts, the smiling faces that conceal threat, the basement as a space of imprisonment. These conventions immediately position the audience within horror territory, but the filmmaker reinvents them: the monster is not a masked killer but a social system that disguises exploitation as acceptance. The convention of the final survivor is reconfigured: survival depends not on moral purity but on resistance to a system designed to consume identity. The film's horror is rooted in the uncanny — the familiar made strange — as everyday social pleasantries become menacing. Contrapuntal sound, with cheerful chatter over scenes of objectification, creates the dissonance that characterises the critique. Through horror conventions, the film makes visible an anxiety that society prefers to keep hidden, demonstrating that horror is not escapist but uniquely capable of confronting audiences with truths they would rather avoid.
