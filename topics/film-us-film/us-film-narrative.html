US Film Narrative

Hollywood narrative: three-act structure, classical Hollywood and alternatives

Key Fact: Classical Hollywood narrative is the dominant storytelling model in US cinema, characterised by cause-and-effect logic, goal-oriented protagonists, narrative closure and invisible filmmaking style.
Key Fact: The three-act structure divides the narrative into setup (establishing characters and conflict), confrontation (complications and obstacles) and resolution (climax and closure).
Key Fact: Cause-and-effect logic means that events in the narrative are driven by character decisions and actions rather than coincidence, creating a sense of narrative necessity and coherence.
Key Fact: The protagonist in classical Hollywood is typically an active, goal-driven individual whose desires define the narrative trajectory and whose actions propel the plot forward.
Key Fact: Narrative closure resolves the central conflict and answers the story's key questions, providing the audience with a satisfying sense of completion and emotional resolution.
Key Fact: Binary oppositions — such as good versus evil, order versus chaos, individual versus society — provide the structural tension that drives the narrative forward.
Key Fact: Tzvetan Todorov's narrative theory proposes a cycle of equilibrium, disruption and new equilibrium, which maps closely onto many Hollywood film structures.
Key Fact: Vladimir Propp's character functions — hero, villain, donor, helper, princess, dispatcher and false hero — can be identified across many Hollywood narratives.
Key Fact: Roland Barthes' narrative codes — enigma, action, symbolic, referential and cultural — provide tools for analysing how Hollywood narratives create and resolve meaning.
Key Fact: Alternative Hollywood narratives challenge classical conventions through non-linear timelines, ambiguous endings, multiple perspectives or open narratives that resist closure.
Key Fact: Independent US films often employ alternative narrative structures — fragmented chronology, unreliable narrators, unresolved plots — to create complexity and challenge audience expectations.
Key Fact: Understanding narrative structure is essential because it reveals how films position audiences, create emotional engagement and communicate ideological meaning through the logic of the story.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For US Film Narrative, you must know:

  • Classical Hollywood narrative: The dominant storytelling model in US cinema, characterised by cause-and-effect logic, goal-oriented protagonists, linear chronology and narrative closure with an invisible filmmaking style.
  • Three-act structure: A narrative framework dividing the story into setup, confrontation and resolution, providing a clear trajectory from conflict to closure that structures most Hollywood screenplays.
  • Narrative closure: The resolution of a film's central conflicts and questions at the narrative's conclusion, providing the audience with a sense of completion and emotional satisfaction.
  • Binary opposition: A structuralist concept identifying paired opposing forces — good/evil, order/chaos, nature/culture — that generate narrative tension and drive the plot towards resolution.
  • Open narrative: A narrative structure that deliberately avoids full resolution, leaving key questions unanswered and inviting the audience to interpret meaning rather than receive closure.
  • Enigma code: Barthes' term for the narrative device that creates mystery and suspense by posing questions that the audience is compelled to see answered, propelling the story forward.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How does the three-act structure shape audience engagement in a Hollywood film?

Q: Explain how Todorov's theory of equilibrium applies to a Hollywood film.

Q: What are the key differences between classical and alternative Hollywood narratives?

Q: How can Propp's character functions be applied to a Hollywood film?

Q: Why might a filmmaker choose an open narrative rather than classical closure?

✅ Answers

  1. The three-act structure creates a predictable but satisfying emotional trajectory. The setup establishes characters and stakes, creating investment; the confrontation raises obstacles and complications, building tension and suspense; the resolution delivers climax and closure, providing emotional payoff. This structure manages audience expectations by promising a satisfying arc and delivering it, which is why it remains the dominant Hollywood narrative model despite its familiarity.
  2. Todorov proposes that narratives follow a cycle: equilibrium (a state of balance), disruption (an event that disturbs the balance), recognition (acknowledgement of the disruption), attempt to repair (efforts to restore order) and new equilibrium (a restored but changed balance). Most Hollywood films follow this pattern: the opening establishes normality, the inciting incident disrupts it, the protagonist spends the middle fighting to restore order, and the ending achieves a new stability. The new equilibrium differs from the original, reflecting the character's transformation.
  3. Classical Hollywood narratives are linear, cause-driven and goal-oriented with clear resolution. Alternative narratives disrupt these conventions: they may use non-linear timelines, ambiguous or open endings, passive protagonists, unresolved enigmas or multiple unreliable perspectives. Classical narratives aim to immerse the audience in a coherent fictional world; alternative narratives foreground their own construction, demanding active interpretation and often resisting the ideological comfort of closure.
  4. Propp identified recurring character roles in folk narratives that map onto Hollywood films: the hero drives the narrative by pursuing a goal; the villain creates conflict by opposing the hero; the donor provides crucial assistance or information; the helper supports the hero's quest; the princess represents the reward or goal; the dispatcher sends the hero on the mission. Identifying these functions reveals how Hollywood narratives distribute roles to create conflict, motivation and resolution through established archetypal patterns.
  5. An open narrative deliberately avoids resolving all conflicts and questions, leaving the audience with ambiguity that demands interpretation. A filmmaker might choose this to reflect the complexity of real life where problems are not neatly solved, to challenge ideological assumptions embedded in classical closure, or to create a more lasting impact by forcing the audience to continue thinking about the film after it ends. Open narratives resist the comfort of certainty, treating audiences as active meaning-makers rather than passive recipients.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Apply narrative theory specifically to your set film — do not discuss theory in the abstract without concrete examples.
  • Identify where your film follows and where it deviates from classical Hollywood conventions — most films do both.
  • Use precise theoretical terminology: 'enigma code', 'binary opposition', 'new equilibrium' rather than vague descriptions.
  • Explain how narrative structure creates meaning, not just how it organises events — structure itself is ideological.
  • When discussing alternative narratives, explain what the deviation from classical norms achieves rather than simply noting that it is different.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Describing the plot without analysing the narrative structure or how it creates meaning. ✓ Move from plot to structure: 'The film follows a three-act structure where the protagonist's goal drives cause-and-effect logic, but the open ending refuses classical closure, forcing the audience to confront the film's moral ambiguity.'

✗ Applying Todorov or Propp mechanically without explaining what the theory reveals about the film. ✓ Use theory analytically: 'The film reaches a new equilibrium that is fundamentally different from the opening state, revealing that the disruption has permanently transformed the protagonist's worldview.'

✗ Confusing narrative structure with plot — listing events rather than analysing how they are organised. ✓ Distinguish clearly: 'The non-linear structure reorganises the chronological plot, forcing the audience to piece together cause and effect, which mirrors the protagonist's fragmented memory and creates active engagement.'

✗ Saying a film 'subverts' classical narrative without specifying which conventions are challenged and how. ✓ Be precise: 'The film subverts classical closure by ending on an enigma — the protagonist's fate is unresolved — which denies the audience the ideological reassurance that resolution typically provides.'

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How does your chosen US film use narrative structure to create meaning for the audience?

The film uses narrative structure deliberately to shape the audience's emotional and intellectual engagement, both following and departing from classical Hollywood conventions to create a layered viewing experience. At the macro level, the film adheres to a three-act structure: the setup establishes the protagonist within a specific social context and introduces the central conflict; the confrontation presents escalating obstacles that test the protagonist's resolve; the resolution delivers a climactic confrontation followed by a new equilibrium. This structure provides the satisfying arc that audiences expect, ensuring emotional investment. However, within this classical framework, the film introduces significant deviations. The cause-and-effect logic that typically drives Hollywood narrative is complicated by an unreliable protagonist whose motivations are gradually revealed to be more ambiguous than initially presented. Enigma codes are established in the opening sequence — questions about the protagonist's past — that are not fully resolved, resisting the classical imperative of closure. Todorov's model is useful but incomplete: the new equilibrium achieved at the end is unstable and provisional, suggesting that the disruption has not been fully contained. The film also employs binary oppositions — truth versus deception, individual versus institution — but refuses to resolve them neatly, instead allowing the tensions to persist. This partial subversion of classical narrative structure creates meaning precisely through the gap between audience expectation and narrative delivery: the audience anticipates resolution but receives ambiguity, and this discomfort becomes the film's most powerful statement about the impossibility of simple answers in a complex world.
