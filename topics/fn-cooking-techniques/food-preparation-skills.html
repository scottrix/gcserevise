Food Preparation Skills

Essential food preparation skills including knife skills, weighing, measuring, peeling, grating, and safe and efficient use of kitchen equipment.

Key Fact: Mise en place means 'everything in its place' — preparing and organising all ingredients and equipment before cooking begins, ensuring efficient and safe food preparation.
Key Fact: The bridge grip is used to hold food securely when cutting: form a bridge with thumb and fingers over the food, guiding the knife underneath. Used for cutting foods in half.
Key Fact: The claw grip is used for slicing and chopping: curl fingers inward like a claw, with fingertips behind the knuckles, guiding the knife while keeping fingers safe.
Key Fact: Knife types and uses: chef's knife (general chopping), paring knife (small precision work), bread knife (serrated for bread), filleting knife (flexible for fish), carving knife (slicing cooked meat).
Key Fact: Weighing methods: digital scales (most accurate), balance scales, measuring spoons (level for dry, heaped for some ingredients), measuring jugs for liquids.
Key Fact: Standard measuring: 1 teaspoon = 5ml, 1 tablespoon = 15ml, 1 cup = 240ml; use level measures unless recipe specifies 'heaped' or 'rounded'.
Key Fact: Preparation techniques: peeling (vegetable peeler or blanching — briefly boiling to loosen skins), grating (box grater: coarse, fine, slicing, grating-zester sides), juicing, zesting.
Key Fact: Equipment safety: always carry knives with the blade pointing down, cut away from your body, use a stable chopping board (place a damp cloth underneath to prevent slipping), and keep knives sharp (dull knives require more force and slip more easily).
Key Fact: Washing and preparing vegetables: wash under running water, peel if necessary, trim and remove inedible parts, cut to uniform size for even cooking.
Key Fact: Accuracy in measurement is critical: too much raising agent causes a cake to collapse; too little liquid makes pastry tough; inaccurate ratios affect the success of most recipes.

✅ Answers

  1. Mise en place means 'everything in its place' — preparing and organising all ingredients and equipment before cooking. It ensures efficient, safe, and stress-free food preparation, preventing mistakes like missing ingredients.
  2. The bridge grip involves forming a bridge with your thumb and fingers over the food, creating a tunnel for the knife to pass under. It is used for cutting food in half safely, such as halving an onion or potato.
  3. The claw grip involves curling your fingertips inward like a claw, with fingertips behind the knuckles, resting on the food to guide the knife blade. It protects fingertips from the blade while providing control for slicing and chopping.
  4. Sharp knives cut more easily with less force, giving better control and reducing the risk of the knife slipping and causing injury. Dull knives require more pressure and are more likely to slip.
  5. Place a damp cloth or paper towel underneath the chopping board to create friction and keep it stable during food preparation.
  6. A level measure means the ingredient is flat and level with the top of the spoon (scraped with a straight edge). A heaped measure means as much as naturally sits on the spoon without pressing down. Recipes default to level unless stated otherwise.
  7. Coarse grating (cheese, vegetables), fine grating (hard cheese, chocolate, citrus zest), slicing (cucumber, carrot), and grating-zester side (nutmeg, ginger, fine zest) (identify all four).
  8. Uniform size ensures even cooking — smaller pieces cook faster than larger ones, so mixed sizes would result in some pieces overcooked and others undercooked.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Demonstrate knowledge of specific knife grips (bridge and claw) — these are frequently tested practical skills.
  • When asked about weighing and measuring, emphasise accuracy and explain what happens if measurements are wrong.
  • Safety questions require specific practices, not vague answers: name the grip, describe the technique, explain the precaution.
  • Use French culinary terms where relevant (mise en place) to show technical knowledge.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ You hold food with your flat hand when chopping. ✓ You should use the claw grip: curl your fingers inward with fingertips behind the knuckles, guiding the knife safely. A flat hand exposes fingertips to the blade.

✗ A dull knife is safer than a sharp knife. ✓ A sharp knife is safer because it requires less force, gives better control, and is less likely to slip. Dull knives need more pressure and are more likely to slip and cause injury.

✗ It doesn't matter how accurately you measure ingredients. ✓ Accuracy is critical: too much raising agent causes a cake to sink, too little liquid makes dough tough, and wrong ratios can cause a recipe to fail entirely.

✗ You can use the same chopping board for raw meat and vegetables. ✓ Separate colour-coded boards must be used: raw meat on a red board, vegetables on a green or white board, to prevent cross-contamination.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain the importance of mise en place and describe two safe knife techniques used in food preparation. (6 marks)

Mise en place (French for 'everything in its place') is the practice of preparing and organising all ingredients and equipment before cooking begins. This includes reading the recipe, weighing and measuring all ingredients, preparing vegetables (washing, peeling, chopping), and laying out all tools and equipment. Mise en place is essential because it prevents mistakes (such as discovering a missing ingredient mid-cooking), reduces stress, ensures efficient time management, and allows the cook to focus on technique rather than searching for items. Two safe knife techniques are the bridge grip and the claw grip. The bridge grip is used for cutting food in half: the hand forms a bridge over the food with thumb on one side and fingers on the other, creating a tunnel for the knife to pass safely underneath. This keeps fingers away from the blade while holding the food securely. The claw grip is used for slicing and chopping: fingers are curled inward like a claw with fingertips tucked behind the knuckles, resting on the food to guide the knife blade up and down. The blade rides against the knuckles, ensuring fingers remain protected while the non-dominant hand controls the size of the cut. Both techniques are fundamental to safe knife work and are essential skills in any food preparation context.
