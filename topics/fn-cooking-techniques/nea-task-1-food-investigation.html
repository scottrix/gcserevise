NEA Task 1: Food Investigation

Understanding the requirements of NEA Task 1: a food investigation assessing students' understanding of the working characteristics and functional properties of ingredients.

Key Fact: NEA Task 1 is a food investigation worth 15% of the total GCSE grade (AQA 8585). It is completed in controlled conditions over approximately 10 hours.
Key Fact: The investigation assesses students' understanding of the working characteristics, functional properties, and chemical processes of ingredients.
Key Fact: Task 1 involves: researching the given brief, conducting practical experiments, recording results, and writing a scientific report (1,500–2,000 words plus supporting evidence).
Key Fact: A typical Task 1 brief might ask you to investigate how different raising agents affect the volume and texture of a sponge cake, or how different fats affect pastry shortness.
Key Fact: The investigation must include: a hypothesis or prediction, controlled variables, a method, results table, analysis, and a conclusion linked to food science.
Key Fact: Controlled variables are essential: to test the effect of one ingredient, all other variables (temperature, quantities, timing, equipment) must be kept constant.
Key Fact: Results should be recorded systematically: tables for quantitative data (measurements), photographs for visual evidence, and sensory evaluation for taste/texture.
Key Fact: The report must link findings to food science: e.g. 'bicarbonate of soda produced more CO2 than baking powder because it reacts with both heat and acid, creating a larger volume'.
Key Fact: Sensory evaluation uses rating scales (e.g. 1–5 for sweetness, texture, appearance) to objectively compare test results.
Key Fact: The conclusion must directly answer the research question, explain the science behind the results, and suggest practical applications (e.g. which ingredient works best and why).

✅ Answers

  1. 15% of the total GCSE grade. It is completed in controlled conditions over approximately 10 hours.
  2. To assess students' understanding of the working characteristics, functional properties, and chemical processes of ingredients through a scientific investigation.
  3. A controlled variable is a factor kept constant during experiments (e.g. oven temperature, ingredient quantities, cooking time). It is essential so that any differences in results can be attributed to the independent variable being tested, not other factors.
  4. Research, hypothesis, method (including controlled variables), results table, sensory evaluation, photographic evidence, analysis linked to food science, conclusion, and practical applications.
  5. The independent variable is the factor you change (e.g. type of raising agent). The dependent variable is the outcome you measure (e.g. cake volume, texture).
  6. To objectively compare the taste, texture, appearance, and aroma of test products using rating scales (e.g. 1–5), providing evidence beyond just visual or quantitative data.
  7. 'Investigate how different types of fat (butter, margarine, and vegetable oil) affect the shortness and texture of shortcrust pastry.' or 'Investigate the effect of different raising agents on the volume of a sponge cake.'
  8. Because Task 1 assesses understanding of functional properties and chemical processes. The conclusion must explain WHY results occurred using scientific knowledge (e.g. 'butter produced shorter pastry because its fat coats flour proteins, preventing gluten formation').

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Structure your investigation report clearly: Introduction, Research, Hypothesis, Method, Results, Analysis, Conclusion.
  • Always name your variables: independent (what you change), dependent (what you measure), controlled (what you keep the same).
  • Use food science terminology in your analysis: say 'denaturation of proteins' not 'the egg went hard', 'gelatinisation of starch' not 'the sauce thickened'.
  • Photograph every stage of your investigation — visual evidence is essential supporting evidence.

📝 Exam Technique

Food Preparation & Nutrition Exam Tips — NEA Task 1: Food Investigation:
1. For NEA Task 1: Food Investigation questions, show every step of your working clearly — method marks count even if the final answer is wrong
2. Check your answer makes sense in context (estimation, units, reasonableness)
3. Use correct mathematical notation and state formulae before substituting values
4. If a NEA Task 1: Food Investigation question asks you to 'prove' or 'show', write a logical chain of reasoning with a conclusion line
5. For problem-solving, identify the topic first, then recall the relevant method

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Task 1 is a cooking practical with no written work. ✓ Task 1 is primarily a scientific investigation with a written report (1,500–2,000 words). Practical experiments provide evidence, but the report demonstrating understanding of food science is what is assessed.

✗ You don't need to control variables in a food investigation. ✓ Controlling variables is essential. If you change more than one factor between tests, you cannot know which factor caused the observed difference in results. Only the independent variable should change.

✗ The conclusion just describes what happened. ✓ The conclusion must explain results using food science. Don't just say 'the cake with bicarbonate of soda was larger' — explain WHY: 'bicarbonate of soda produced more CO2 because it releases gas when heated AND when combined with acid, creating greater volume through more extensive aeration'.

✗ Task 1 is the same as Task 2. ✓ Task 1 is a food INVESTIGATION (scientific experiment on ingredient properties, 15%). Task 2 is a food PREPARATION task (planning and cooking a menu for a brief, 35%). They assess different skills.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

You are investigating how different raising agents affect the volume and texture of a sponge cake. Describe how you would conduct this investigation, including variables and how you would ensure reliable results. (8 marks)

I would investigate how three raising agents — baking powder, bicarbonate of soda, and self-raising flour — affect sponge cake volume and texture. The independent variable is the type of raising agent. The dependent variables are cake volume (measured in cm³ by displacement or height) and texture (assessed by sensory evaluation). Controlled variables include: 50g each of flour, sugar, butter, and one egg; oven temperature 180°C; baking time 20 minutes; same mixing method (creaming); same cake tin size and material; same cooling time before measurement. I would make three cakes, changing only the raising agent: Cake A uses baking powder (1 teaspoon), Cake B uses bicarbonate of soda (half teaspoon, with an acidic ingredient like lemon juice), Cake C uses self-raising flour (replacing plain flour). After baking and cooling, I would measure the height and volume of each cake, photograph cross-sections, and conduct a sensory evaluation with at least three testers rating texture, lightness, and overall acceptability on a 1–5 scale. Results would be recorded in a table comparing raising agent, height, volume, and sensory scores. I would repeat the experiment once to verify reliability. In my analysis, I would link results to food science: baking powder contains both an alkali and acid that react with moisture and heat to produce CO2; bicarbonate of soda requires an external acid to react; self-raising flour contains a measured amount of baking powder. The conclusion would identify which raising agent produced the best volume and texture and explain the chemical reasons, demonstrating understanding of aeration and the role of raising agents.
