NEA Task 2: Food Preparation

Understanding the requirements of NEA Task 2: a food preparation assessment where students plan, prepare, cook, and present a menu of three dishes for a specific context and brief.

Key Fact: NEA Task 2 is a food preparation assessment worth 35% of the total GCSE grade (AQA 8585). It is completed in controlled conditions over approximately 20 hours.
Key Fact: Task 2 requires students to plan, prepare, cook, and present a menu of three dishes (plus accompaniments) in response to a set brief, typically within a 3-hour practical session.
Key Fact: The brief specifies a context and target group, e.g. 'Plan and prepare a menu for a teenager following a vegetarian diet' or 'Produce a menu for a family on a low budget'.
Key Fact: Task 2 portfolio must include: research into the brief, analysis of the task, menu planning and justification, nutritional analysis, time plan, costings, practical skills evidence, and evaluation.
Key Fact: High-scoring menus demonstrate a wide range of skills: at least one complex skill per dish (e.g. making pastry from scratch, filleting fish, bread-making, sauce-making from a roux).
Key Fact: The time plan (production schedule) is critical: it lists every task in chronological order with timings, oven temperatures, and key checkpoints for the 3-hour session.
Key Fact: Nutritional analysis of the full menu must be included, comparing it against the Reference Intake (RI) and explaining how the menu meets the nutritional needs of the target group.
Key Fact: Costing the menu demonstrates understanding of budget considerations; each dish should be costed per portion and the total menu cost presented.
Key Fact: Photographic evidence of every stage and the final presentation is essential; dishes should demonstrate skilful, attractive presentation.
Key Fact: The evaluation must reflect on: how well the menu met the brief, the nutritional balance, the range of skills demonstrated, time management, and what could be improved.

✅ Answers

  1. 35% of the total GCSE grade. It is completed in controlled conditions over approximately 20 hours, including a 3-hour practical cooking session.
  2. Research and analysis of the brief, menu planning with justification, nutritional analysis, costing per portion, a detailed time plan (production schedule), photographic evidence, and a comprehensive evaluation.
  3. A detailed chronological plan of all tasks during the 3-hour practical session, including timings, oven temperatures, and checkpoints. It ensures efficient time management, prevents dishes being ready at the wrong time, and allows the student to demonstrate organisation.
  4. A complex skill is an advanced cooking technique such as making pastry from scratch, filleting fish, bread-making, or making a sauce from a roux. Including complex skills demonstrates higher-level practical ability and is required for the highest marks.
  5. Justification explains why each dish was chosen: how it meets the brief, suits the target group's nutritional needs, demonstrates a range of skills, and fits within the time and budget constraints. This shows analytical thinking rather than random dish selection.
  6. A breakdown of the energy, macronutrients, and micronutrients in the full menu, compared against the Reference Intake (RI). It should show how the menu meets the specific nutritional needs of the target group (e.g. iron for vegetarians, calcium for teenagers).
  7. How well the menu met the brief, the nutritional balance achieved, the range and complexity of skills demonstrated, the effectiveness of the time plan, the quality of presentation, sensory outcomes, and what could be improved with reasons.
  8. It provides visual proof of the practical work, showing the progression of each dish from preparation through cooking to final presentation. It demonstrates skill levels, hygiene practices, and the quality of the finished products.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Plan a menu that shows the WIDEST range of skills possible — include at least one complex skill per dish.
  • Your time plan must be realistic: allow time for preparation, cooking, washing up between dishes, and final presentation.
  • Always justify dish choices by linking them back to the brief: nutritional needs, target group, budget, skills demonstrated.
  • The evaluation should be honest and reflective — identifying what went wrong and how you would fix it earns more marks than claiming everything was perfect.

📝 Exam Technique

Food Preparation & Nutrition Exam Tips:
Always use specific food science terminology (e.g. denaturation, gelatinisation, coagulation). Support answers with practical examples from cooking. For evaluation questions, consider nutritional, sensory, and sustainability factors. Reference the Eatwell Guide and nutritional data where relevant. Show understanding of how functional properties of ingredients affect cooking outcomes.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Task 2 only requires cooking three dishes — the written portfolio is optional. ✓ The written portfolio is essential and assessed. It includes research, menu justification, nutritional analysis, costing, time plan, and evaluation. The practical session provides evidence, but the portfolio demonstrates understanding.

✗ You should choose the dishes you most enjoy cooking. ✓ Dishes should be chosen to meet the brief, demonstrate a wide range of skills, and suit the target group's nutritional needs. Personal preference is irrelevant — the brief and mark scheme must drive your choices.

✗ The time plan is just a list of recipes. ✓ The time plan (production schedule) is a detailed chronological plan of ALL tasks across the 3-hour session, including timings, temperatures, dish preparation order, cleaning checkpoints, and final presentation. It must show how all three dishes will be ready simultaneously.

✗ Presentation doesn't matter as long as the food tastes good. ✓ Presentation is assessed as part of the practical skills. Dishes should be attractively plated, with appropriate garnishes, clean plates, and thoughtful arrangement. Poor presentation limits the marks achievable.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

You have been set the following NEA Task 2 brief: 'Plan and prepare three dishes suitable for a vegetarian teenager, ensuring the menu is nutritionally balanced.' Explain how you would approach this task, including your choice of dishes with justification, key skills, and how you would ensure nutritional balance. (8 marks)

I would begin by researching the nutritional needs of vegetarian teenagers, who require adequate protein (for growth), iron (to replace that normally obtained from red meat), calcium (for bone development), vitamin B12 (from eggs and dairy), and vitamin C (to enhance iron absorption from plant sources). My three-dish menu would be: Starter — roasted red pepper and goat's cheese tart with a homemade shortcrust pastry case (complex skill: pastry-making from scratch; provides protein and calcium from goat's cheese, vitamin C from peppers). Main course — chickpea and spinach curry with basmati rice and homemade naan bread (complex skills: bread-making and spice blending; provides iron from spinach and chickpeas, protein from chickpeas, complex carbohydrates from rice and bread). Dessert — mango and pistachio yoghurt panna cotta with a berry coulis (skill: gelatine setting and coulis-making; provides calcium from yoghurt, vitamins from fruit, and healthy fats from pistachios). This menu is nutritionally balanced because it provides protein from multiple sources (goat's cheese, chickpeas, yoghurt), iron from spinach and chickpeas, calcium from cheese and yoghurt, and vitamin C from peppers and berries to enhance iron absorption. The dishes demonstrate a wide range of complex skills (pastry-making, bread-making, gelatine work) and diverse cooking methods (baking, simmering, setting). For the time plan, I would prepare pastry first (it needs chilling), then start the naan dough (it needs proving), cook the curry, blind-bake the tart case, cook the naan, assemble and bake the tart, and finish the panna cotta presentation last. The portfolio would include nutritional analysis comparing the full menu against Reference Intake for a teenager, costings per portion, and a detailed evaluation of how well the menu meets the brief.
