British & International Cuisine

Exploring traditional British dishes, international cuisines, and how multicultural influences have shaped the modern British diet.

Key Fact: Traditional British dishes include: roast beef and Yorkshire pudding, fish and chips, shepherd's pie, full English breakfast, Sunday roast, and trifle.
Key Fact: British cuisine has been significantly influenced by immigration and global trade: Indian, Chinese, Italian, Caribbean, and Middle Eastern foods are now part of everyday British eating.
Key Fact: Chicken tikka masala is often described as a British national dish, reflecting the fusion of Indian cooking with British tastes.
Key Fact: Key international cuisines and their staples: Italian (pasta, olive oil, tomatoes), Indian (rice, spices, lentils, ghee), Chinese (rice, soy sauce, stir-frying), Mexican (tortillas, beans, chillies), Thai (rice, coconut, lemongrass).
Key Fact: Multiculturalism has changed British food habits: increased availability of world ingredients in supermarkets, more diverse restaurant options, and fusion cuisine.
Key Fact: Historical influences on British food include: Roman (imported vegetables and herbs), Viking and Norman (farming and dairy), Tudor (sugar and spices from trade routes), and post-war Caribbean and South Asian immigration.
Key Fact: Regional British specialities include: Cornish pasty, haggis (Scotland), Welsh rarebit, Lancashire hotpot, and Bakewell tart.
Key Fact: International cuisine introduces new cooking techniques: stir-frying (Chinese), slow-cooking tagines (Moroccan), tandoori (Indian), pasta-making (Italian).
Key Fact: Understanding international cuisine is important for food choice because it expands the range of flavours, ingredients, and cooking methods available.
Key Fact: Cultural appropriation vs appreciation: learning about and respectfully cooking dishes from other cultures broadens food choice, but awareness of cultural significance is important.

✅ Answers

  1. Roast beef and Yorkshire pudding, fish and chips, shepherd's pie, full English breakfast, Sunday roast, trifle, Cornish pasty (any three).
  2. Multiculturalism has introduced new ingredients, recipes, and cooking techniques from around the world, expanded supermarket ranges, created diverse restaurant options, and led to fusion cuisine. Chicken tikka masala is a prime example of this fusion.
  3. Rice, lentils (dahl), spices (cumin, coriander, turmeric), ghee, and flatbreads (naan, chapatti).
  4. The Tudor spice trade introduced cinnamon, pepper, and nutmeg to Britain. Sugar from Caribbean plantations became affordable in the 18th century. Tea from China and India became the national drink.
  5. Fusion cuisine combines elements from different culinary traditions. Examples: chicken tikka masala (Indian spices with British cream-based sauce), sushi burrito (Japanese-Mexican), ramen burger (Japanese-American).
  6. Cornish pasty (Cornwall), haggis (Scotland), Welsh rarebit (Wales), Lancashire hotpot (Lancashire), Bakewell tart (Derbyshire) (any one).
  7. Stir-frying: fast cooking at high heat in a wok, which preserves nutrients, colour, and texture while using little oil.
  8. It broadens understanding of ingredients, cooking techniques, and cultural food practices; supports adapting recipes for different dietary needs; and reflects the multicultural reality of modern British food choice.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When asked about British cuisine, give specific named dishes, not just vague descriptions.
  • Link multiculturalism to specific cuisines and how they changed British eating habits (e.g. Indian cuisine led to the popularity of curry).
  • Use the term 'fusion cuisine' when describing dishes that combine culinary traditions.
  • For higher marks, explain the historical reasons behind food influences (immigration, trade, empire).

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ British food has always been diverse and multicultural. ✓ Traditional British food was relatively simple and meat-and-two-veg based. Multicultural diversity came through immigration, particularly post-WW2 from South Asia and the Caribbean.

✗ Chicken tikka masala is a traditional Indian dish. ✓ Chicken tikka masala was created in Britain, adapting Indian tandoori chicken with a British-style creamy tomato sauce. It is a British-Indian fusion dish.

✗ All international cuisines use the same cooking methods. ✓ Different cuisines use distinct techniques: Chinese stir-frying, Italian pasta-boiling and slow ragu cooking, Indian tandoori and tempering of spices, Moroccan slow-cooked tagines.

✗ Stir-frying is unhealthy because it uses a lot of oil. ✓ Stir-frying uses relatively little oil because of the high heat and fast cooking time, and it retains more nutrients than boiling because vegetables are cooked briefly.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how multicultural influences have changed the British diet, giving specific examples of cuisines and dishes. (6 marks)

Multicultural influences have transformed the British diet from its traditional meat-and-two-veg foundations into a diverse food culture. Post-WW2 immigration from South Asia, the Caribbean, China, and Italy brought new ingredients, cooking techniques, and flavour profiles. Indian cuisine had the greatest impact: spices like cumin, turmeric, and garam masala are now everyday store-cupboard ingredients, and curry houses are a staple of British high streets. Chicken tikka masala — a British-Indian fusion dish combining tandoori chicken with a creamy tomato sauce — is often cited as a national dish. Italian cuisine introduced pasta and pizza as everyday meals, replacing some traditional British staples. Chinese cuisine popularised stir-frying, soy sauce, and rice as a staple carbohydrate. Caribbean cuisine brought ingredients like plantain, jerk seasoning, and rice and peas. Supermarkets now stock world ingredients, and ready meals reflect this diversity. These influences have expanded British food choice, introduced healthier cooking methods like stir-frying, and created fusion cuisine that combines elements from multiple traditions. The British diet today is defined by this multicultural heritage rather than solely by traditional dishes.
