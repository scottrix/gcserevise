FN15: Factors Affecting Food Choice

Foundation Higher AQA 8585, OCR J309, WJEC C560QS

Exploring the wide range of factors that influence what people choose to eat, including social, economic, cultural, religious, ethical, and psychological factors.

Factors Affecting Food Choice

Exploring the wide range of factors that influence what people choose to eat, including social, economic, cultural, religious, ethical, and psychological factors.

Key Fact: Food choice is influenced by multiple interacting factors: social, economic, cultural, religious, ethical, psychological, and health-related.
Key Fact: Social factors include: family meals and traditions, peer pressure, media and advertising, and social status (e.g. eating out as a social activity).
Key Fact: Economic factors include: income level, food prices, availability of affordable fresh produce, and access to shops (food deserts).
Key Fact: Cultural factors: different cultures have distinct food traditions, staple foods, flavour preferences, and cooking methods (e.g. rice in Asian cuisine, pasta in Italian cuisine).
Key Fact: Religious factors: Halal and Haram in Islam, Kosher in Judaism, beef avoidance in Hinduism, vegetarianism in some Buddhist traditions.
Key Fact: Ethical factors: animal welfare concerns, fair trade, sustainability, food miles, and environmental impact of food production.
Key Fact: Health factors: medical conditions (diabetes, coeliac disease), allergies, weight management, and following dietary advice (e.g. 5 A Day).
Key Fact: Psychological factors: comfort eating, food neophobia (fear of new foods), emotional connections to food, and sensory preferences (taste, texture, appearance).
Key Fact: Availability and seasonality affect what foods are accessible; imported foods have expanded choice but raised sustainability concerns.
Key Fact: Technological factors: microwave ovens, ready meals, online shopping, and food delivery apps have changed how and what people eat.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Name four categories of factors that influence food choice.

Q: What is the difference between Halal and Haram foods in Islam?

Q: What is a food desert?

Q: How does food advertising influence food choice?

Q: Explain what food neophobia is and who it commonly affects.

Q: Give two examples of how religion influences food choice.

Q: What are food miles and why do they affect food choice?

Q: How do economic factors limit food choice for low-income families?

✅ Answers

  1. Social, economic, cultural, religious, ethical, health, psychological, or availability factors (any four).
  2. Halal foods are permitted under Islamic law (e.g. meat slaughtered in the name of Allah). Haram foods are forbidden, including pork, alcohol, and meat not slaughtered according to Halal rules.
  3. An area where people have limited access to affordable, fresh, and nutritious food, often because there are few shops or they are difficult to reach.
  4. Advertising uses persuasive techniques (attractive images, health claims, celebrity endorsement) to make products appealing, often targeting children and promoting processed or high-sugar foods.
  5. Food neophobia is the fear or reluctance to try new foods. It commonly affects young children but can persist into adulthood, limiting dietary variety.
  6. Islam: Muslims eat only Halal meat and avoid pork and alcohol. Hinduism: Many Hindus avoid beef and some are vegetarian. Judaism: Jewish people follow Kosher laws, not eating pork or shellfish and keeping meat and dairy separate (any two).
  7. Food miles are the distance food travels from production to consumer. High food miles mean greater carbon emissions, so ethically-minded consumers may choose locally produced food to reduce environmental impact.
  8. Low-income families may be unable to afford fresh produce, rely on cheaper processed foods that are often less nutritious, live in food deserts with limited access to shops, and have less time or equipment for cooking from scratch.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When asked about factors affecting food choice, categorise your answer (social, economic, cultural, etc.) rather than giving a random list.
  • Always explain HOW a factor influences choice, not just NAME it — e.g. 'Religious factors influence choice because Muslims will only eat Halal meat' not just 'religion'.
  • For higher marks, show how factors interact — e.g. economic and cultural factors combine when low income restricts access to traditional ingredients.
  • Use real examples to illustrate each factor, especially for cultural and religious dietary laws.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ People choose food based only on taste and preference. ✓ Food choice is influenced by many interacting factors including social, economic, cultural, religious, ethical, health, and psychological factors — taste is just one of many.

✗ All people from the same culture eat the same foods. ✓ Within any culture, food choices vary based on individual preferences, income, region, family traditions, and degree of religious observance.

✗ Fair trade means food is more nutritious. ✓ Fair trade ensures producers in developing countries receive a fair price and work in acceptable conditions — it is an ethical standard, not a nutritional one.

✗ Food miles are the only measure of a food's environmental impact. ✓ Food miles are just one factor; production methods (e.g. heated greenhouses), water usage, and packaging also contribute to environmental impact.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Discuss how social, economic, and cultural factors influence the food choices people make. (8 marks)

Social factors influence food choice through family traditions (e.g. Sunday roast as a family meal), peer pressure (teenagers choosing trendy or convenience foods), and media influence (advertising and social media promoting certain products). Social occasions such as celebrations and festivals also determine what foods are eaten. Economic factors significantly affect choice: people on low incomes may be unable to afford fresh fruit, vegetables, and quality proteins, and instead rely on cheaper processed foods that are often higher in fat, sugar, and salt. Living in a food desert — where affordable fresh food is hard to access — further limits choice. Cooking skills, time, and kitchen equipment also vary with income. Cultural factors shape food choice through established food traditions and staple foods: rice and soy sauce are staples in many Asian cultures, while pasta and olive oil are central to Italian cuisine. Cultural norms also determine flavour preferences, cooking methods, and meal structures. These factors interact: for example, a family from an Asian cultural background on a low income may find traditional ingredients expensive or unavailable locally, forcing them to adapt their diet. Understanding these factors is essential for nutritionists and food policymakers when promoting healthy eating.
