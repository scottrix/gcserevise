Special Diets & Food Intolerance

Understanding special diets for medical, religious, ethical, and lifestyle reasons, including food allergies, intolerances, and how to adapt recipes.

Key Fact: A food allergy is an immune system reaction to a specific food protein; symptoms can be severe and include anaphylaxis (a life-threatening reaction).
Key Fact: A food intolerance is a non-immune adverse reaction to food; symptoms are usually less severe and include bloating, diarrhoea, and stomach cramps.
Key Fact: Coeliac disease is an autoimmune condition where gluten damages the small intestine lining, preventing nutrient absorption. Sufferers must follow a strict gluten-free diet.
Key Fact: Lactose intolerance is the inability to digest lactose (milk sugar) due to a lack of the enzyme lactase; symptoms include bloating and diarrhoea after consuming dairy.
Key Fact: Common food allergies include: peanuts, tree nuts, milk, eggs, fish, shellfish, soy, wheat, celery, and sesame.
Key Fact: Vegetarians do not eat meat, poultry, or fish; types include lacto-ovo (eat dairy and eggs), lacto (eat dairy), and ovo (eat eggs).
Key Fact: Vegans exclude all animal products including meat, dairy, eggs, honey, and often avoid animal-derived additives like gelatine and rennet.
Key Fact: Religious diets: Muslims follow Halal, Jews follow Kosher, Hindus often avoid beef and many are vegetarian, Sikhs may avoid Halal meat.
Key Fact: Medical diets include: diabetic (controlled sugar and carbohydrate intake), low-sodium (for high blood pressure), low-fat (for heart disease), and high-fibre (for bowel health).
Key Fact: When adapting recipes for special diets, substitutions must maintain nutritional balance: e.g. replacing wheat flour with gluten-free blends, using plant milk instead of dairy, or using eggs alternatives like aquafaba or flaxseed.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the key difference between a food allergy and a food intolerance?

Q: What is coeliac disease and what diet must sufferers follow?

Q: Why does lactose intolerance cause symptoms after consuming dairy?

Q: What is the difference between a lacto-ovo vegetarian and a vegan?

Q: Name three common food allergies.

Q: What is anaphylaxis and how is it treated?

Q: Suggest a substitution for wheat flour in a recipe for someone with coeliac disease.

Q: Why must vegans ensure they get enough vitamin B12, iron, and calcium?

✅ Answers

  1. A food allergy involves the immune system and can cause severe, life-threatening reactions (anaphylaxis). A food intolerance does not involve the immune system and typically causes less severe digestive symptoms.
  2. Coeliac disease is an autoimmune condition where gluten damages the lining of the small intestine, reducing nutrient absorption. Sufferers must follow a strict gluten-free diet, avoiding wheat, barley, and rye.
  3. People with lactose intolerance lack sufficient lactase enzyme to break down lactose (milk sugar). Undigested lactose ferments in the gut, causing bloating, diarrhoea, and stomach cramps.
  4. A lacto-ovo vegetarian avoids meat, poultry, and fish but eats dairy products and eggs. A vegan excludes all animal products including dairy, eggs, honey, and animal-derived ingredients.
  5. Peanuts, tree nuts, milk, eggs, fish, shellfish, soy, wheat, celery, sesame (any three).
  6. Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction causing swelling of the airways, difficulty breathing, and a drop in blood pressure. It is treated with an adrenaline auto-injector (EpiPen).
  7. Gluten-free flour blends (rice flour, potato starch, tapioca flour, xanthan gum to replace gluten's binding properties).
  8. Vitamin B12 is found almost exclusively in animal products. Plant iron (non-haem) is less well absorbed than animal iron (haem). Dairy is a major calcium source, so vegans need fortified plant milks, tofu, or leafy greens.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always distinguish between allergy (immune, potentially fatal) and intolerance (non-immune, usually digestive) — this is a very common question.
  • When adapting recipes, name the specific substitute ingredient AND explain why it works (e.g. 'gluten-free flour blend plus xanthan gum to replace the binding properties of gluten').
  • For vegan diets, always mention the key nutrients at risk: B12, iron, calcium, and omega-3 fatty acids.
  • Learn the symptoms of coeliac disease, lactose intolerance, and anaphylaxis — they are frequently tested.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Coeliac disease is a food allergy to gluten. ✓ Coeliac disease is an autoimmune condition, not an allergy. In coeliac disease, gluten triggers the immune system to attack the body's own small intestine lining. In a food allergy, the immune system attacks the food protein itself.

✗ Lactose intolerance means you cannot eat any dairy at all. ✓ Many people with lactose intolerance can tolerate small amounts of lactose or can consume lactose-free dairy products, hard cheeses (which contain very little lactose), and fermented products like yoghurt.

✗ Vegetarians and vegans can easily get all their nutrients from plants. ✓ Well-planned plant-based diets can meet nutritional needs, but vegans especially must ensure adequate intake of vitamin B12 (only reliably from fortified foods or supplements), iron, calcium, and omega-3 fatty acids.

✗ A food allergy always causes immediate, visible symptoms. ✓ Some allergic reactions can be delayed by several hours. Also, the severity of reactions can vary; some are mild (tingling, hives) while others are severe (anaphylaxis).

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain the difference between a food allergy and a food intolerance. Describe how you would adapt a cheese and pasta bake for someone with coeliac disease and someone who is lactose intolerant. (8 marks)

A food allergy involves the immune system reacting to a specific food protein. Reactions can be immediate and severe, including swelling, difficulty breathing, and anaphylaxis (a life-threatening condition treated with adrenaline). Even trace amounts of the allergen can trigger a reaction. A food intolerance does not involve the immune system; it is a difficulty digesting certain foods, causing symptoms such as bloating, stomach cramps, and diarrhoea. Intolerances are generally less dangerous but can significantly affect quality of life. To adapt a cheese and pasta bake for someone with coeliac disease, I would replace wheat-based pasta with gluten-free pasta (made from rice, corn, or quinoa flour) and use a gluten-free flour blend to thicken the cheese sauce, adding xanthan gum to replace gluten's binding properties. I would also check that all other ingredients (stock, mustard, breadcrumb topping) are gluten-free, as even small amounts of gluten can cause damage. For someone with lactose intolerance, I would replace the cow's milk in the sauce with lactose-free milk or a plant-based alternative such as oat milk, and substitute the cheese with lactose-free cheese or a small amount of mature hard cheese (which contains minimal lactose). These adaptations ensure the dish is safe for both conditions while maintaining similar flavour and texture.
