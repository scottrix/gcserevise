Food Poisoning & Food-Borne Illness

Identifying the main types of food poisoning bacteria, their sources, symptoms, and how to prevent food-borne illness through safe food handling.

Key Fact: Food poisoning is illness caused by eating food contaminated with harmful bacteria, viruses, or toxins.
Key Fact: The main food poisoning bacteria include: Salmonella, E. coli (especially O157), Campylobacter, Staphylococcus aureus, and Clostridium perfringens.
Key Fact: Salmonella is commonly found in raw poultry and eggs; symptoms include diarrhoea, vomiting, fever, and abdominal cramps.
Key Fact: E. coli O157 is found in undercooked minced beef and unpasteurised milk; it can cause severe kidney damage (haemolytic uraemic syndrome).
Key Fact: Campylobacter is the most common cause of bacterial food poisoning in the UK, often from undercooked poultry.
Key Fact: Staphylococcus aureus produces toxins in food; it is carried on skin and in nasal passages and transfers to food through poor hand hygiene.
Key Fact: Clostridium perfringens forms heat-resistant spores; it thrives in food left at room temperature (e.g. large batches of cooked meat).
Key Fact: Norovirus is a virus (not a bacterium) causing 'winter vomiting bug'; it spreads through contaminated food and poor hygiene.
Key Fact: Food poisoning can be caused by infection (eating live bacteria) or intoxication (eating pre-formed toxins in food).
Key Fact: Cross-contamination is the transfer of harmful bacteria from one food (usually raw) to another, and is the most common cause of food poisoning.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Name the most common bacterial cause of food poisoning in the UK.

Q: What is the difference between food poisoning by infection and by intoxication?

Q: Which bacterium produces toxins in food and is carried on human skin?

Q: What is cross-contamination and why is it dangerous?

Q: Name two sources of E. coli O157 and a serious complication it can cause.

Q: Why is Clostridium perfringens particularly difficult to eliminate through cooking?

Q: Give three ways to prevent cross-contamination.

Q: What is norovirus and how is it commonly spread?

✅ Answers

  1. Campylobacter, often found in undercooked poultry.
  2. Infection: caused by eating food containing live bacteria that multiply in the gut. Intoxication: caused by eating food containing toxins already produced by bacteria before the food is eaten.
  3. Staphylococcus aureus. It is carried on skin and in nasal passages, and can transfer to food through poor hand hygiene.
  4. Cross-contamination is the transfer of harmful bacteria from one food (usually raw meat/poultry) to ready-to-eat food. It is the most common cause of food poisoning.
  5. Sources: undercooked minced beef and unpasteurised milk. Complication: haemolytic uraemic syndrome (HUS), which can cause kidney failure.
  6. It forms heat-resistant spores that can survive normal cooking temperatures. If food is then left at room temperature, the spores germinate and the bacteria multiply rapidly.
  7. Use separate chopping boards for raw and cooked food, wash hands thoroughly after handling raw meat, store raw meat below ready-to-eat food in the fridge, and clean and disinfect surfaces between tasks (any three).
  8. Norovirus is a virus (the 'winter vomiting bug') spread through contaminated food, water, surfaces, or poor personal hygiene, especially by food handlers who are ill.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Learn the specific sources, symptoms, and prevention methods for each key bacterium — examiners often ask you to name a bacterium linked to a specific food.
  • Always distinguish between infection and intoxication when explaining how food poisoning occurs.
  • When discussing prevention, use the '4 Cs': Cross-contamination, Cleaning, Chilling, and Cooking.
  • Use precise names: 'Campylobacter' not 'campylobacter bacteria', 'E. coli O157' not just 'E. coli'.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ All food poisoning is caused by bacteria. ✓ Food poisoning can also be caused by viruses (e.g. norovirus), chemical contaminants, or naturally occurring toxins in some plants and fish.

✗ Cooking food always makes it safe to eat. ✓ Cooking kills most bacteria, but if bacteria have already produced toxins in the food (e.g. Staphylococcus aureus), those toxins may not be destroyed by cooking.

✗ E. coli is always harmful. ✓ Most strains of E. coli are harmless and live naturally in the human gut. E. coli O157 is the dangerous strain that produces Shiga toxins.

✗ Food poisoning only happens in restaurants. ✓ Many food poisoning cases are caused by poor food hygiene in the home, such as cross-contamination, inadequate cooking, and improper storage.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe two food poisoning bacteria, their sources, and explain how cross-contamination can be prevented. (6 marks)

Salmonella is commonly found in raw poultry, raw eggs, and unpasteurised milk. Symptoms include diarrhoea, vomiting, fever, and abdominal cramps. E. coli O157 is found in undercooked minced beef, unpasteurised milk, and contaminated water; it can cause severe bloody diarrhoea and potentially life-threatening kidney damage (haemolytic uraemic syndrome). Cross-contamination — the transfer of harmful bacteria from raw food to ready-to-eat food — is the most common cause of food poisoning and can be prevented in several ways. First, use separate colour-coded chopping boards and utensils for raw meat and ready-to-eat foods. Second, always wash hands thoroughly with soap and warm water before and after handling raw meat. Third, store raw meat on the bottom shelf of the fridge so that juices cannot drip onto other foods. Fourth, clean and disinfect all work surfaces between tasks. These measures break the chain of contamination and significantly reduce the risk of food-borne illness.
