Food Safety Legislation & HACCP

Understanding food safety legislation, the role of Environmental Health Officers, and the HACCP system for identifying and controlling food safety hazards.

Key Fact: The Food Safety Act 1990 is the key UK legislation: it requires food businesses to ensure food is safe, not harmful to health, and labelled accurately.
Key Fact: Under the Food Safety Act, it is an offence to sell food that is unfit for human consumption, or to describe food falsely (e.g. mislabelling).
Key Fact: Environmental Health Officers (EHOs) enforce food safety law: they inspect premises, take food samples, issue improvement notices, and can close businesses that pose a health risk.
Key Fact: EHOs inspect food businesses unannounced and assess hygiene based on: food handling, physical condition of premises, and confidence in management.
Key Fact: The Food Hygiene Rating Scheme (FHRS) gives businesses a rating from 0 (urgent improvement) to 5 (very good), displayed as a sticker.
Key Fact: HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points) is a systematic method for identifying and controlling food safety hazards.
Key Fact: The 7 principles of HACCP are: 1. Conduct a hazard analysis, 2. Identify CCPs, 3. Set critical limits, 4. Monitor CCPs, 5. Take corrective action, 6. Verify the system, 7. Keep records.
Key Fact: A hazard is anything that could cause harm: biological (bacteria), chemical (cleaning products), or physical (glass, bone, packaging).
Key Fact: A Critical Control Point (CCP) is a step where control is essential to prevent or eliminate a food safety hazard (e.g. cooking, chilling).
Key Fact: The due diligence defence allows a business to prove they took all reasonable precautions to avoid committing an offence under food safety law.

✅ Answers

  1. Food must be safe to eat, not harmful to health, and labelled and described accurately (not misleadingly).
  2. EHOs enforce food safety law by inspecting food premises (often unannounced), taking food samples, issuing improvement notices, and closing businesses that pose an immediate health risk.
  3. Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points.
  4. Biological (e.g. Salmonella bacteria), chemical (e.g. cleaning fluid contamination), physical (e.g. glass, bone, or packaging in food).
  5. A step in the food process where control is essential to prevent, eliminate, or reduce a food safety hazard to an acceptable level (e.g. cooking to 75°C, chilling below 5°C).
  6. 1. Conduct hazard analysis, 2. Identify CCPs, 3. Set critical limits, 4. Monitor CCPs, 5. Establish corrective actions, 6. Verify the system works, 7. Keep documentation and records.
  7. A scheme where EHOs rate food businesses from 0 (urgent improvement necessary) to 5 (very good) based on hygiene, structure, and management. Ratings are displayed publicly.
  8. A business can defend itself against a food safety charge by proving it took all reasonable precautions and exercised due diligence to avoid the offence — for example, having HACCP records, staff training logs, and supplier checks.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Learn the 7 HACCP principles in order — examiners often ask you to list or apply them.
  • Always give three types of hazard (biological, chemical, physical) with specific examples when discussing HACCP.
  • When asked about legislation, name the specific Act (Food Safety Act 1990) and state its key requirements.
  • For due diligence, explain it is a legal defence that requires documented evidence of all reasonable precautions.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ HACCP stands for Health And Catering Control Plan. ✓ HACCP stands for Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points — it is a risk management system, not a general health plan.

✗ A hazard and a risk are the same thing. ✓ A hazard is something with the potential to cause harm (e.g. raw chicken). A risk is the likelihood that the harm will actually occur (e.g. high risk if cross-contamination is not controlled).

✗ The Food Safety Act only applies to restaurants and food manufacturers. ✓ The Food Safety Act 1990 applies to all food businesses, including school canteens, market stalls, food banks, and any organisation that prepares or sells food.

✗ EHOs must give notice before inspecting a food business. ✓ EHOs can inspect food premises at any reasonable time without prior notice, which is essential to see true working practices.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how the HACCP system helps food businesses ensure food safety. Include the types of hazard and at least three HACCP principles. (6 marks)

HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points) is a systematic, preventive approach to food safety that helps businesses identify and control hazards before they cause harm. There are three types of hazard: biological (e.g. pathogenic bacteria such as Salmonella), chemical (e.g. cleaning products contaminating food), and physical (e.g. glass, bone, or metal fragments). The HACCP system works through seven principles. First, a hazard analysis is conducted at every stage of the food process to identify what could go wrong and how. Second, Critical Control Points (CCPs) are identified — these are the steps where control is essential to prevent or eliminate a hazard, such as cooking or chilling. Third, critical limits are set for each CCP, for example a minimum core cooking temperature of 75°C or a fridge temperature of 0–5°C. Fourth, monitoring procedures ensure CCPs are consistently met, such as using a food thermometer for each cooked dish and recording fridge temperatures daily. If monitoring reveals a CCP has not been met, corrective action must be taken — for example, if food has not reached 75°C, it must be returned to the oven until it does. The system also requires verification (checking the HACCP plan works) and record-keeping (documented evidence for due diligence defence). This structured approach makes food safety proactive rather than reactive.
