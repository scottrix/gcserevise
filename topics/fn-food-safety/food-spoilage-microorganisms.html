Food Spoilage & Microorganisms

Understanding how microorganisms cause food spoilage, the conditions they need to grow, and how to prevent spoilage in food storage and preparation.

Key Fact: Food spoilage is caused by microorganisms (bacteria, yeasts, moulds) breaking down food.
Key Fact: Bacteria are the most common cause of food spoilage and food-borne illness.
Key Fact: Yeasts cause fermentation, producing alcohol and carbon dioxide — useful in bread-making but spoilage in other foods.
Key Fact: Moulds grow as visible fuzzy patches and can produce harmful mycotoxins.
Key Fact: The conditions microorganisms need to grow are summarised by FATTOM: Food, Acidity, Temperature, Time, Oxygen, Moisture.
Key Fact: The danger zone for bacterial growth is 5°C to 63°C; bacteria multiply fastest at around 37°C.
Key Fact: Signs of spoilage include: unpleasant smell, discolouration, slime, visible mould, gas bubbles, off-taste.
Key Fact: Enzymatic browning and oxidation are chemical changes (not microbial) that also spoil food appearance and quality.
Key Fact: Freezing at −18°C stops microbial growth but does not kill bacteria; they reactivate when thawed.
Key Fact: Pasteurisation heats food to 72°C for 15 seconds, killing most harmful bacteria without destroying flavour.

✅ Answers

  1. Food, Acidity, Temperature, Time, Oxygen, Moisture — the six conditions microorganisms need to grow.
  2. 5°C to 63°C. Bacteria multiply fastest at around 37°C (body temperature).
  3. Unpleasant smell, discolouration, slime, visible mould, gas bubbles, or off-taste (any three).
  4. It heats food to 72°C for 15 seconds, killing most harmful bacteria without significantly changing the food's flavour or nutritional value.
  5. Freezing stops bacterial growth by making water unavailable, but bacteria survive in a dormant state and can reactivate when the food is thawed.
  6. Spoilage makes food unpalatable (bad smell, taste, appearance) but may not always cause illness. Food poisoning is caused by consuming harmful bacteria or their toxins and causes illness.
  7. Yeasts ferment sugars, producing alcohol and carbon dioxide gas. This causes food to smell yeasty, taste sour, or develop gas bubbles.
  8. Mycotoxins are harmful chemical substances produced by moulds. They can cause illness even if the mould is removed.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to FATTOM when explaining how to prevent microbial growth — examiners look for this acronym.
  • Distinguish clearly between spoilage (food becomes unpalatable) and food poisoning (food causes illness) — they are not the same.
  • When asked about storage, always state the specific temperature: fridge at 0–5°C, freezer at −18°C or below.
  • Use scientific vocabulary: say 'microorganisms' or 'pathogenic bacteria' rather than 'germs' or 'bugs'.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Freezing kills all bacteria in food. ✓ Freezing stops bacterial growth but does not kill them; bacteria survive in a dormant state and can multiply again when thawed.

✗ All bacteria cause food spoilage. ✓ Many bacteria are harmless or even beneficial (e.g. in yoghurt production). Only some bacteria cause spoilage or food poisoning.

✗ If food looks and smells fine, it is safe to eat. ✓ Pathogenic bacteria may not produce any visible signs of spoilage — food can look and smell fine but still cause food poisoning.

✗ Mouldy food is safe to eat if you cut the mould off. ✓ Moulds can produce mycotoxins that spread through the food. Soft foods with mould should be discarded entirely.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain the conditions that microorganisms need to grow and how understanding these conditions helps prevent food spoilage. (6 marks)

Microorganisms need six conditions to grow, remembered by the acronym FATTOM: Food (nutrients as an energy source), Acidity (most bacteria prefer pH 6.6–7.5; acidic conditions below pH 4.5 inhibit growth), Temperature (the danger zone of 5–63°C promotes rapid growth, with fastest reproduction at 37°C), Time (bacteria can double every 20 minutes in ideal conditions), Oxygen (aerobic bacteria need oxygen, though some are anaerobic), and Moisture (bacteria require water to survive and reproduce). Understanding these conditions allows us to control food safety: refrigeration below 5°C slows growth, cooking above 63°C kills most bacteria, reducing moisture through drying or adding sugar/salt prevents microbial activity, and acidic preservation (pickling in vinegar) creates an unfavourable pH. Limiting time in the danger zone — by cooling food quickly and not leaving it at room temperature for more than 90 minutes — is also critical. Each control targets one or more FATTOM conditions to prevent spoilage and food-borne illness.
