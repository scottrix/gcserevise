Diet-Related Health Issues

Diet-related health conditions: obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, tooth decay, and anaemia. Causes, prevention, and the role of diet in managing these conditions.

Key Fact: Obesity: BMI over 30. Affects 28% of UK adults and rising in children. Caused by sustained positive energy balance (calories in exceed calories out). Increases risk of type 2 diabetes, heart disease, stroke, and certain cancers.
Key Fact: Type 2 diabetes: the body becomes resistant to insulin or doesn't produce enough. Strongly linked to obesity, physical inactivity, and diets high in sugar and refined carbohydrates. Management includes weight loss, exercise, and reduced sugar intake.
Key Fact: Cardiovascular disease (CVD): includes heart disease and stroke. Risk factors include high blood pressure, high LDL cholesterol, smoking, obesity, and physical inactivity. Diet influences: excess saturated fat raises LDL cholesterol; excess salt raises blood pressure; fruit and vegetables provide antioxidants.
Key Fact: Tooth decay (dental caries): caused by bacteria fermenting sugars to produce acid that erodes tooth enamel. Frequency of sugar consumption matters more than total amount. Prevention: reduce sugar frequency, brush twice daily with fluoride toothpaste, visit the dentist.
Key Fact: Iron-deficiency anaemia: most common nutritional deficiency globally. Particularly affects women (menstrual losses), teenagers (rapid growth), and vegetarians (non-haem iron is poorly absorbed). Symptoms: fatigue, pallor, breathlessness, poor concentration.
Key Fact: Osteoporosis: weakened bones prone to fracture. Linked to inadequate calcium and vitamin D intake, particularly in post-menopausal women. Prevention: adequate calcium, vitamin D, and weight-bearing exercise throughout life.
Key Fact: Bowel cancer: linked to low fibre intake, high red and processed meat consumption, obesity, and alcohol. The UK has one of the highest rates in Europe. Increasing fibre to 30g/day and reducing processed meat are key dietary strategies.
Key Fact: Food poverty: inability to afford or access nutritious food. Affects diet quality — cheaper foods are often energy-dense but nutrient-poor, contributing to both obesity and micronutrient deficiencies in deprived communities.
Key Fact: The Sugar Reduction Programme: PHE challenged the food industry to reduce sugar in key product categories by 20% by 2020. The Soft Drinks Industry Levy ('sugar tax') reduced sugar in drinks by approximately 29%.
Key Fact: The National Diet and Nutrition Survey (NDNS) monitors UK dietary habits and nutritional status, providing data for public health policy.
Key Fact: Socioeconomic factors: diet quality is strongly linked to income and education. Healthier diets cost more per calorie than unhealthy ones, creating inequality in health outcomes.
Key Fact: Prevention strategies: education, food labelling (traffic light system), reformulation, taxation, advertising restrictions, and improving access to healthy foods in deprived areas.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Explain the link between obesity and type 2 diabetes.

Q: How does diet contribute to cardiovascular disease?

Q: Evaluate the effectiveness of the sugar tax in improving public health.

✅ Answers

  1. Excess body fat, particularly visceral fat, causes cells to become resistant to insulin. The pancreas produces more insulin to compensate, but eventually cannot keep up, leading to elevated blood glucose levels (type 2 diabetes). Weight loss of 5-10% can significantly improve insulin sensitivity and in some cases reverse early type 2 diabetes.
  2. Excess saturated fat raises LDL cholesterol, which deposits in arteries forming plaques (atherosclerosis), restricting blood flow. Excess salt raises blood pressure, damaging artery walls. Insufficient fruit and vegetables means fewer antioxidants that protect artery walls. High sugar intake contributes to obesity and triglyceride levels. Reducing saturated fat, salt, and sugar while increasing fruit, vegetables, and oily fish reduces CVD risk.
  3. The Soft Drinks Industry Levy led to a 29% reduction in sugar in soft drinks as manufacturers reformulated to avoid the tax. This reduces calorie and sugar intake, potentially reducing obesity and tooth decay. However, it only covers drinks (not food), some consumers switched to cheaper untaxed brands, and the impact on overall dietary sugar intake is modest compared to total sugar consumption. A comprehensive approach including education, labelling, and wider reformulation is needed.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Obesity is simply caused by eating too much. ✓ Obesity results from a sustained positive energy balance, but the causes are complex: genetic predisposition, socioeconomic factors, food environment, psychological factors, and physical inactivity all contribute. Simplifying to 'eating too much' ignores the systemic factors.

✗ Only overweight people get type 2 diabetes. ✓ While obesity is the primary risk factor, type 2 diabetes can also occur in normal-weight individuals, particularly those with high visceral fat or genetic predisposition. BMI is not the only indicator of metabolic health.

✗ Tooth decay is only caused by eating sweets. ✓ Any fermentable carbohydrate (including bread, fruit, and dried fruit) can contribute to tooth decay. The frequency of sugar exposure matters more than the total amount — snacking on sugary foods throughout the day is more harmful than consuming the same amount in one sitting.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the role of diet in preventing cardiovascular disease. [12 marks]

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) is the UK's second leading cause of death, and diet plays a significant role in both increasing and reducing risk. The primary dietary risk factor is excessive saturated fat intake, which raises LDL ('bad') cholesterol. LDL cholesterol deposits in artery walls, forming plaques that narrow arteries (atherosclerosis), potentially leading to heart attacks and strokes. Replacing saturated fats with unsaturated fats (olive oil, oily fish) can lower LDL and raise HDL ('good') cholesterol, reducing CVD risk. Excess salt intake (UK average 8.4g/day vs recommended 6g/day) raises blood pressure, another major CVD risk factor. Processed foods contribute approximately 75% of dietary salt. Reducing salt intake by 1.6g/day could prevent approximately 5,000 deaths annually in the UK. Insufficient fruit and vegetable consumption means fewer protective antioxidants and less potassium (which helps lower blood pressure). The UK 5-a-day target is met by only about 30% of adults. Oily fish provides omega-3 fatty acids that reduce inflammation, lower triglycerides, and reduce blood clotting tendency. The recommended two portions per week (including one oily) is rarely met. However, diet alone cannot prevent CVD — physical inactivity, smoking, and genetic factors also contribute significantly. A comprehensive prevention strategy must address all modifiable risk factors. Overall, dietary modification is one of the most effective strategies for reducing CVD risk, but it must be part of a broader lifestyle approach for maximum impact.
