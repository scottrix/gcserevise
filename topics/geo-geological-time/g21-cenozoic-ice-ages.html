Cenozoic Era & Ice Ages

The Cenozoic Era: evolution of mammals, climate change, the ice ages, and the formation of the British landscape.

Key Fact: The Cenozoic Era (66 Ma to present) is the 'Age of Mammals' — mammals diversified after the dinosaurs went extinct
Key Fact: The Quaternary Period (2.6 Ma to present) includes the Pleistocene (repeated glaciations) and the Holocene (last 11,700 years, current interglacial)
Key Fact: During the Pleistocene, there were multiple glacial (cold) and interglacial (warm) cycles driven by Milankovitch orbital variations
Key Fact: The last glacial maximum was ~20,000 years ago; ice sheets covered much of Britain down to the Midlands
Key Fact: Glaciation shaped the British landscape: U-shaped valleys, corries, drumlins, erratics, till deposits and moraines
Key Fact: Sea level fell ~120 m during glaciations (water stored in ice sheets) and rose again during interglacials, separating Britain from the continent

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Cenozoic Era & Ice Ages, you must know:

  • Quaternary: The geological period from 2.6 million years ago to the present, characterised by repeated glaciations
  • Pleistocene: The epoch of the Quaternary marked by repeated glacial-interglacial cycles, from 2.6 Ma to 11,700 years ago
  • Holocene: The current interglacial epoch, from 11,700 years ago to the present
  • Erratic: A rock fragment transported by a glacier and deposited on different bedrock, sometimes hundreds of kilometres from its origin
  • Drumlin: An elongated, smooth hill of till shaped by glacial ice flow, with a steep upstream end and gentle downstream tail

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Why is the Cenozoic called the 'Age of Mammals'?

Q: What caused the repeated glacial-interglacial cycles of the Pleistocene?

Q: How did glaciation shape the British landscape?

Q: What is an erratic and what does it tell us?

Q: Why did sea level change during the ice ages?

✅ Answers

  1. After the K-Pg extinction eliminated the dinosaurs (~66 Ma), mammals rapidly diversified to fill the vacant ecological niches. They evolved from small, nocturnal creatures into the wide variety of forms we see today, including whales, horses, primates and elephants.
  2. Milankovitch cycles: variations in Earth's orbital parameters (eccentricity of orbit, tilt of axis, and precession of equinoxes) that alter the distribution and intensity of solar radiation received at different latitudes over tens of thousands of years.
  3. Glaciers carved U-shaped valleys (e.g. Lake District), corries (cwms) on mountainsides, and arêtes. They deposited till (boulder clay) as moraines and drumlins. Erratics were carried far from their source. Meltwater channels and eskers were formed by glacial rivers. Glacial lakes deposited varved clays.
  4. A rock fragment transported by a glacier and deposited on different bedrock from its origin. Erratics indicate the direction of ice flow (tracing them to their source outcrop) and the maximum extent of ice sheets.
  5. During glacial periods, water was stored on land as ice sheets, causing sea level to fall by ~120 m. During interglacials, the ice melted and returned to the oceans, raising sea level. This is eustatic sea level change. Britain was connected to Europe by a land bridge during low sea levels.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Cenozoic = Age of Mammals (after dinosaurs died out at K-Pg boundary)
  • Pleistocene = repeated glaciations; Holocene = current warm period
  • Milankovitch cycles drive glacial-interglacial cycles
  • British landscape: U-valleys, drumlins, erratics, till all indicate glaciation

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ There was only one ice age ✓ There were multiple glacial-interglacial cycles during the Pleistocene; the 'ice age' was a series of cold and warm periods, not a single cold spell

✗ Britain was entirely covered by ice during the last glaciation ✓ Ice covered Britain roughly from the Midlands northward; southern England remained unglaciated but had periglacial conditions (permafrost, freeze-thaw)

✗ Sea level rise during interglacials was caused by thermal expansion alone ✓ Sea level rose mainly because water stored in continental ice sheets returned to the oceans; thermal expansion of seawater was a secondary contribution

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe the key events of the Cenozoic Era and explain how glaciation shaped the British landscape. [6 marks]

The Cenozoic Era (66 Ma to present) began after the K-Pg extinction eliminated the dinosaurs. Mammals rapidly diversified to fill vacant niches, making this the 'Age of Mammals'. The Paleogene and Neogene periods saw the evolution of modern mammal groups, the rise of grasslands, and the formation of the Alps and Himalayas through continental collision. The Quaternary (2.6 Ma to present) was characterised by repeated glacial-interglacial cycles driven by Milankovitch orbital variations. During glacial maxima, ice sheets up to 3 km thick covered northern Britain; during interglacials, conditions were similar to or warmer than today. The last glacial maximum was ~20,000 years ago. Glaciation profoundly shaped the British landscape. Ice carved U-shaped valleys (e.g. Wasdale in the Lake District), corries on mountainsides, and arêtes between valleys. Glacial erosion produced dramatic mountain scenery. Deposition left till (unsorted boulder clay) as ground moraine, drumlins (streamlined hills aligned with ice flow), and terminal moraines marking ice limits. Erratics (e.g. the Norber Erratics in Yorkshire) were transported far from their source, proving ice-flow direction. Meltwater carved channels and deposited eskers and outwash sands. Periglacial processes in southern England produced head deposits and patterned ground. Current sea level is ~120 m higher than at the last glacial maximum, submerging the land bridge that once connected Britain to continental Europe.
