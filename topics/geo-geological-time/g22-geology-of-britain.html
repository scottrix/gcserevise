Geology of Britain

The geological history of Britain, key rock units, and how plate tectonics shaped the British Isles over geological time.

Key Fact: Britain's geology is remarkably diverse for its size, with rocks ranging from the Archean (Lewisian Gneiss, ~3 billion years) to recent Holocene deposits
Key Fact: The Scottish Highlands contain the oldest rocks: Lewisian Gneiss (Precambrian), overlain by Torridon Sandstone and Moine Schists
Key Fact: The Lake District has Ordovician and Silurian volcanic and sedimentary rocks, deformed during the Caledonian orogeny
Key Fact: The Pennines are a Carboniferous limestone and millstone grit anticline, formed by the Variscan orogeny
Key Fact: The Jurassic Coast (Dorset) shows a continuous Mesozoic sequence from Triassic to Cretaceous with abundant fossils
Key Fact: Southern England has Cretaceous chalk (White Cliffs of Dover) overlying older rocks, gently folded into the Weald anticline

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Geology of Britain, you must know:

  • Caledonian orogeny: The mountain-building event (~490-390 Ma) caused by the collision of Laurentia, Avalonia and Baltica, forming the Scottish Highlands and Welsh mountains
  • Variscan orogeny: The mountain-building event (~380-280 Ma) caused by the collision of continents forming Pangaea, affecting SW England and parts of the Pennines
  • Lewisian Gneiss: The oldest rocks in Britain (~3 Ga), found in northwest Scotland; heavily metamorphosed Precambrian basement rocks
  • Chalk: A soft, white, fine-grained Cretaceous limestone made of coccolith plates; forms the White Cliffs of Dover and the South Downs
  • Jurassic Coast: A UNESCO World Heritage Site in Dorset showing continuous Mesozoic rock exposure from Triassic through Jurassic to Cretaceous

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What are the oldest rocks in Britain and where are they found?

Q: What caused the Caledonian orogeny and what rocks did it produce?

Q: Why is the Jurassic Coast important for geology?

Q: How were the Pennines formed?

Q: What formed the White Cliffs of Dover?

✅ Answers

  1. The Lewisian Gneiss, approximately 3 billion years old, found in northwest Scotland. They are Precambrian metamorphic basement rocks that have been through multiple deformation events.
  2. The collision of the continental plates Laurentia (Scotland), Avalonia (England) and Baltica (Scandinavia) ~490-390 Ma. It produced deformed metamorphic rocks in the Scottish Highlands (Moine Schists) and Welsh mountains, with granitic intrusions (e.g. Cairngorms).
  3. It exposes a nearly continuous sequence of Mesozoic rocks from the Triassic through the Jurassic to the Cretaceous, with abundant fossils including ammonites, belemnites and marine reptiles. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site for its geological significance.
  4. The Pennines are a large anticline formed mainly from Carboniferous limestone and millstone grit. They were uplifted and folded during the Variscan orogeny when plates collided to form Pangaea. The limestone was deposited in warm shallow tropical seas.
  5. Cretaceous chalk, a soft white limestone formed from billions of coccolith (calcareous algae) shells deposited in a warm, shallow tropical sea. The chalk was later uplifted and exposed by erosion of the overlying rocks and coastal wave action.

  • Caledonian orogeny = formed Scottish Highlands and Welsh mountains

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ All British rocks are the same age ✓ Britain has rocks spanning nearly 3 billion years, from Archean Lewisian Gneiss to Holocene deposits; its geology is remarkably diverse for such a small area

✗ The Pennines are igneous mountains ✓ The Pennines are made of sedimentary rocks (Carboniferous limestone and millstone grit) folded into an anticline; they are not igneous in origin

✗ Chalk is made of coral ✓ Chalk is made of microscopic calcite plates (coccoliths) from marine algae (coccolithophores), not coral. Coral limestone is different and forms reef structures

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe the geological history of Britain and explain how plate tectonics shaped its geology. [6 marks]

Britain's geology spans nearly 3 billion years. The oldest rocks are the Lewisian Gneiss (~3 Ga) in NW Scotland, Precambrian metamorphic basement. Through the early Palaeozoic, Britain was divided: Scotland was part of Laurentia, separated from southern Britain (Avalonia) by the Iapetus Ocean. The Caledonian orogeny (~490-390 Ma) closed this ocean as the plates collided, deforming rocks in the Scottish Highlands (Moine Schists) and Welsh mountains, with granitic intrusions (Cairngorms). During the Silurian and Devonian, marine sediments and Old Red Sandstone were deposited. The Carboniferous saw tropical seas depositing thick limestone (Pennines, Peak District) and swamp forests forming coal measures. The Variscan orogeny (~380-280 Ma) folded these rocks as Pangaea assembled, affecting SW England (Cornwall granites) and the Pennines. In the Mesozoic, Britain was mostly under warm seas: the Triassic saw desert conditions (New Red Sandstone); the Jurassic had marine clays and limestones with abundant ammonites (Jurassic Coast); the Cretaceous saw chalk deposition (White Cliffs of Dover). The Cenozoic brought uplift, erosion and the Alpine orogeny's distant effects (gentle folding of southern England, e.g. the Weald anticline). Finally, Pleistocene glaciation carved valleys and deposited till across much of northern Britain.
