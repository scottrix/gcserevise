G42: Issue Evaluation

Foundation Higher All Boards

Critical thinking with pre-release resources, understanding stakeholder viewpoints, evaluating alternatives, and making reasoned justifications in geography.

📋 What is the Issue Evaluation?

Key Concept: The Issue Evaluation is Section A of AQA GCSE Geography Paper 3 (Geographical Applications). It tests your ability to use geographical skills, critical thinking, and knowledge to analyse a contemporary geographical issue. You receive pre-release resources 12 weeks before the exam.

The Issue Evaluation assesses your ability to:

  • Analyse and interpret geographical information from a range of sources
  • Identify and evaluate different stakeholder viewpoints
  • Consider alternative solutions to a geographical problem
  • Make and justify a reasoned decision
  • Apply your geographical understanding to a real-world context
Paper 3 Structure: Section A: Issue Evaluation (32 marks, approximately 60 minutes). Section B: Fieldwork (30 marks). Total: 74 marks + 3 SPaG marks. The pre-release booklet is provided 12 weeks before the exam. You must study it thoroughly but cannot bring it into the exam - a clean copy is provided.

📄 Working with Pre-Release Resources

Types of Resources in the Pre-Release Booklet

  • Maps (OS maps, choropleth maps, location maps)
  • Photographs (aerial and ground-level)
  • Graphs and charts (bar, line, pie, scatter)
  • Data tables and statistics
  • Written extracts (newspaper articles, reports, stakeholder quotes)
  • Diagrams and satellite images

How to Analyse the Resources

Step-by-Step Approach:
  1. Read all resources carefully at least twice
  2. Annotate key information, patterns, and links between resources
  3. Identify the geographical issue and its key components
  4. Locate the issue spatially - use maps to understand place context
  5. Connect the resources to your wider geographical knowledge
  6. Evaluate the reliability and bias of each source
Example: Evaluating a Source

A newspaper article opposing a new wind farm might be biased if: the author lives near the proposed site; the newspaper has a political stance against renewable energy; the article uses emotive language rather than evidence; or key facts are omitted. A government report on the same issue might also be biased if it selectively presents data supporting the policy. Always consider who produced the resource and their potential motivations.

Linking Resources

The exam will test your ability to make connections between different resources in the booklet. For example:

  • Does the data in a table support or contradict a statement in a newspaper article?
  • Do the patterns on a map explain what is shown in a photograph?
  • Are the views of one stakeholder supported by the evidence in a graph?

👥 Understanding Stakeholder Viewpoints

Definition: Stakeholders are individuals, groups, or organisations that have an interest in or are affected by a geographical issue. Different stakeholders often have conflicting views because they have different priorities, values, and interests.

Common Stakeholders in Geographical Issues

Stakeholder Typical Concerns Example Viewpoint
Local residents Quality of life, property values, noise, visual impact, jobs "The wind turbines will ruin our landscape and lower house prices"
Environmental groups Wildlife, habitats, pollution, climate change, sustainability "Renewable energy is essential to tackle climate change"
Local businesses Economic opportunities, customer access, employment "Construction will bring jobs; tourists may stay away during building"
Local/national government Policy targets, planning regulations, public opinion, budgets "The development meets our renewable energy targets"
Developers/TNCs Profit, feasibility, regulations, reputation "This site has excellent wind resources and good grid access"
Future generations Long-term sustainability, climate change, resource availability "We need clean energy to protect the planet for the future"
Exam Technique: When asked about stakeholder viewpoints: (1) Identify who the stakeholders are; (2) Explain their specific interest/concern; (3) Use evidence from the resource booklet; (4) Show how and why viewpoints conflict; (5) Consider which viewpoints are better supported by evidence.

⚖️ Evaluating Alternatives

The Evaluation Framework

For each alternative, consider: 1. Effectiveness: How well does it solve the problem? 2. Cost: What are the financial costs and benefits? 3. Sustainability: Is it environmentally sustainable long-term? 4. Social impact: Who benefits and who loses? 5. Feasibility: Is it practical and achievable? 6. Timescale: How quickly can it be implemented?
Example: Evaluating Flood Management Options

Option A - Hard engineering (flood barrier): Highly effective at protecting property; expensive (£millions); may increase flooding downstream; benefits locals but harms ecosystems; proven technology; takes years to build.

Option B - Soft engineering (floodplain zoning): Moderately effective; low cost; environmentally sustainable; restricts development rights; requires planning enforcement; can be implemented immediately. Evaluation: Option A provides stronger protection but at greater cost and environmental damage; Option B is more sustainable but less protective. The best approach may combine elements of both.

SWOT Analysis

A useful technique for evaluating alternatives:

  • Strengths: Internal advantages of the option
  • Weaknesses: Internal disadvantages
  • Opportunities: External factors that could help
  • Threats: External factors that could hinder

✍️ Making Reasoned Justifications

Key Skill: The final question in the Issue Evaluation section (typically worth 9 marks + 3 SPaG) asks you to make and justify a decision. You must present a clear conclusion supported by evidence and reasoning, considering alternative viewpoints.

Structure for a High-Scoring Justification

  1. State your decision clearly at the beginning
  2. Present your strongest evidence from the resource booklet
  3. Link to geographical knowledge and understanding beyond the booklet
  4. Acknowledge counter-arguments and explain why your view is stronger
  5. Consider multiple perspectives and explain why you prioritise some over others
  6. Conclude firmly - don't sit on the fence
PEEL Paragraph Structure: Point - State your argument clearly Evidence - Use data from the resource booklet Explain - Show how the evidence supports your point Link - Connect back to your overall decision
Example: 9-Mark Justification Answer

Question: "To what extent do you agree with the proposed development of the new housing estate on the greenfield site?"

Model Answer (Level 3 - 7-9 marks): I partially agree with the proposed development because the housing is urgently needed but the greenfield location is problematic. Evidence from Figure 3 shows that house prices in the area have risen 45% in five years, and the local council estimates 2,500 new homes are needed by 2030. Building on the greenfield site would provide 800 homes near existing transport links (Figure 5 shows the site is 500m from the railway station). However, the development would destroy 40 hectares of farmland (Figure 2) and increase traffic on already congested roads (residents in Source D report 25-minute delays at peak times). A brownfield alternative exists at the former factory site (Figure 4), which could provide 400 homes without losing agricultural land, though it would cost 30% more per home due to remediation. On balance, I support a modified proposal: developing both sites would provide the required 800 homes while preserving half the greenfield site, distributing the impact and cost more fairly.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Why is it important to consider the reliability of sources in the pre-release booklet?

Q2: Explain why stakeholders often have conflicting viewpoints about geographical issues.

Q3: Describe the PEEL structure and explain how it helps you answer evaluation questions.

Q4: What six factors should you consider when evaluating alternative solutions to a geographical problem?

Q5: Explain how you would approach the final 9-mark justification question.

✅ Answers

  1. Source reliability is important because different sources may be biased or have limited perspective. A newspaper article may use emotive language or omit facts; a developer's report may emphasise benefits and downplay costs; a resident's view may be based on personal impact rather than wider evidence. Evaluating reliability helps you assess how much weight to give each source and identify which are most trustworthy, leading to better-informed conclusions.
  2. Stakeholders have conflicting viewpoints because they have different priorities and interests. Local residents may prioritise quality of life and property values, while developers prioritise profit and government prioritises policy targets. Environmental groups value sustainability, while businesses value economic growth. These different values lead to different conclusions about what should happen. For example, a wind farm developer sees economic opportunity; a nearby resident sees visual intrusion; an environmentalist sees climate action.
  3. PEEL stands for Point, Evidence, Explain, Link. You start by stating your argument (Point), then support it with data from the resource booklet (Evidence), then show how the evidence supports your argument (Explain), and finally connect back to your overall decision (Link). This structure ensures every point is properly supported and explained, which is essential for accessing the higher mark bands where reasoning must be clear and evidence-based.
  4. The six factors are: (1) Effectiveness - how well does it solve the problem? (2) Cost - what are the financial costs and benefits? (3) Sustainability - is it environmentally sustainable long-term? (4) Social impact - who benefits and who loses? (5) Feasibility - is it practical and achievable? (6) Timescale - how quickly can it be implemented?
  5. For the 9-mark justification question: (1) State your decision clearly at the beginning; (2) Present your strongest evidence from the resource booklet; (3) Link to geographical knowledge beyond the booklet; (4) Acknowledge counter-arguments and explain why your view is stronger; (5) Consider multiple perspectives and explain why you prioritise some; (6) Conclude firmly. Use PEEL paragraphs to structure each point. Remember that 3 additional SPaG marks are available for spelling, punctuation, grammar, and use of geographical terminology.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Study the pre-release booklet thoroughly - annotate it, make notes, discuss it in class
  • You cannot bring your annotated booklet into the exam - a clean copy is provided
  • Always refer to specific figures and sources from the booklet (e.g. "Figure 3 shows...")
  • For the 9-mark question, you MUST take a position - don't be indecisive
  • Use geographical terminology correctly (sustainability, stakeholder, mitigation, trade-off)
  • Consider short-term AND long-term impacts in your evaluations
  • Remember SPaG marks (3 marks) - write in full sentences, use paragraphs, spell correctly
  • Practice timing: spend about 60 minutes on Section A

📝 Exam Technique

Geography Exam Tips — Issue Evaluation:
1. For Issue Evaluation questions, always name specific case studies with factual detail
2. Use geographical terminology precisely (e.g. specific processes, not vague descriptions)
3. Consider social, economic and environmental perspectives in your evaluations
4. Support your points about Issue Evaluation with data, statistics or named examples
5. For 'assess' or 'evaluate' questions, reach a clear judgement supported by evidence

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often write vague answers without specific geographical evidence. Wrong: Writing generalised statements like 'it causes problems' Correct: Using specific data and named examples, e.g. 'the 2010 Haiti earthquake killed over 200,000 people due to poor building quality'

Students often confuse causes and effects. Wrong: Mixing up what caused the event with what resulted from it Correct: Clearly separate causes (why it happened) from effects (what happened as a result)

Students often describe rather than evaluate. Wrong: Listing strategies without assessing their effectiveness Correct: Weighing up strengths and weaknesses of each approach and reaching a supported judgement

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

6 marks: Explain the key factors affecting issue evaluation.

Issue Evaluation involves multiple interconnected factors that geographers must understand. The key concepts include the processes that create and change issue evaluation, the impacts on both people and environment, and the strategies used to manage associated challenges. For a comprehensive answer, specific case study evidence should be used throughout, with named examples and data to support each point. Geographical terminology should be used precisely, and the interrelationship between physical and human factors should be demonstrated. Top-level responses evaluate the relative importance of different factors and consider how the situation varies between locations.

Mark scheme: 2 marks for identifying key factors, 2 marks for explaining processes with detail, 2 marks for using specific evidence

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 requires knowledge of the key facts and processes related to issue evaluation. AO2 demands understanding of how and why these processes operate, and their implications. AO3 asks you to analyse, evaluate and make judgements — this is where grade 9 answers stand out by weighing up competing perspectives and reaching supported conclusions. AO4 may involve interpreting maps, graphs or data related to this topic. To move from grade 5 to grade 9: use precise geographical terminology, support every point with specific case study evidence, and always evaluate rather than just describe.
