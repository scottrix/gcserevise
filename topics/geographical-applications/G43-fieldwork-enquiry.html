G43: Fieldwork: Enquiry Process

Foundation Higher All Boards

The geographical enquiry process: formulating questions and hypotheses, selecting appropriate evidence, identifying suitable locations, and carrying out risk assessment.

🔍 The Geographical Enquiry Process

Key Concept: Geographical enquiry is a structured investigation into a geographical question or issue. It follows a clear sequence: Question → Hypothesis → Data Collection → Data Presentation → Analysis → Conclusion → Evaluation. AQA requires two fieldwork investigations: one in a physical environment and one in a human environment.
The Enquiry Sequence: 1. Identify a question or issue 2. Formulate a hypothesis (or research question) 3. Plan the methodology (what, where, how, when) 4. Collect primary and secondary data 5. Present the data using appropriate techniques 6. Analyse and interpret the data 7. Draw conclusions that answer the original question 8. Evaluate the enquiry process and findings

Physical Geography Fieldwork

Typical topics include: river characteristics (width, depth, velocity, bedload), coastal processes (erosion, longshore drift, sediment size), and weather/microclimate. Example: "How does the cross-profile of the River X change downstream?"

Human Geography Fieldwork

Typical topics include: urban land use changes, quality of the built environment, tourism impacts, retail sphere of influence, and environmental quality. Example: "Does environmental quality decrease from the CBD outwards in Town Y?"

❓ Formulating Questions and Hypotheses

Research Questions

Definition: A research question is an open-ended question that guides your enquiry. It should be specific, measurable, and linked to geographical theory.

Good research questions are:

  • Specific: Not too broad - "How does urban quality of life vary in Bristol?" rather than "What is urban quality of life?"
  • Locatable: Tied to a specific place that can be studied
  • Measurable: Can be investigated using data collection methods
  • Geographical: Connected to geographical concepts (place, space, process, scale)

Hypotheses

Definition: A hypothesis is a testable statement that predicts the expected outcome of the enquiry. It is often based on geographical theory and can be supported or rejected by evidence.
Example Hypotheses

Physical: "River velocity increases with distance downstream" - based on the Bradshaw Model which predicts that velocity, width, depth, and discharge all increase downstream as more tributaries join and the channel becomes more efficient.

Human: "Environmental quality decreases with distance from the CBD" - based on urban models which suggest that the inner city has older, more degraded housing and less investment than the city centre.

Coastal: "Sediment size decreases with distance along the beach in the direction of longshore drift" - based on the theory that attrition rounds and reduces sediment as it is transported.

Writing a Good Hypothesis: A hypothesis must be: Testable (can be proven or disproven with data); Specific (clearly states what is expected); Linked to theory (based on geographical understanding); Directional (states the expected direction of change). Format: "As [variable X] increases/decreases, [variable Y] will increase/decrease" or "[Variable Y] is higher/lower at [location A] compared to [location B]."

Null Hypothesis

A null hypothesis states there is no relationship or difference (e.g. "There is no significant relationship between distance downstream and river velocity"). Statistical tests like Spearman's Rank work on the null hypothesis - if the result is significant, you reject the null hypothesis.

📊 Selecting Appropriate Evidence

Key Concept: You must choose data collection methods that are appropriate for your hypothesis. The evidence should be sufficient, relevant, and reliable. Consider both primary data (collected by you) and secondary data (collected by others).

Matching Methods to Hypotheses

Hypothesis Primary Data Needed Secondary Data Needed
River velocity increases downstream Velocity measurements (flow meter), width, depth at multiple sites OS map for site distances, Environment Agency flow data
Environmental quality decreases from the CBD Environmental quality surveys, photo evidence, litter counts Census data, deprivation indices, historic maps
Sediment size decreases along longshore drift Sediment size measurements at intervals along the beach Geology maps, coastal management maps, historic erosion data
Urban land use changes with distance from the CBD Land use surveys, building age classification, rental prices Google Earth imagery, census data, local authority plans

Sample Size and Sampling Methods

The sample must be large enough to be representative but practical to collect:

  • Too small: Results may not be representative; anomalies have too much influence
  • Too large: Takes too long; may be impractical to collect
  • Typical: 10-12 sites for a river study; 30-50 environmental quality surveys

📍 Identifying Suitable Locations

Choosing a Fieldwork Location

Key Factors: A good fieldwork location must be accessible, safe, relevant to the hypothesis, and representative of the geographical process being studied.
Factor Considerations
Accessibility Can you reach the site safely? Is there road access or public transport? Is the terrain manageable for all group members?
Safety Are there hazards (fast-flowing water, steep cliffs, busy roads)? Is the site away from dangerous areas?
Relevance Does the site show the geographical process you are studying? Does it have enough variation to test your hypothesis?
Scale Is the area manageable in the time available? Can you collect enough data at different points?
Representation Is the site typical of the area, or is it unusual? Unusual sites may give misleading results
Permission Do you need landowner consent? Are there access restrictions (nature reserves, private land)?
Example: Choosing River Fieldwork Sites

For a river study testing the Bradshaw Model, you need sites from the source to the mouth. Choose 8-10 sites along the river's course, spaced roughly equally apart. Use an OS map to identify safe access points (footpaths, bridges, public rights of way). Avoid sites near weirs, deep water, or steep banks. Mark each site with a grid reference. Consider how far the group can walk in the time available.

Using Maps to Plan Locations

  • Use OS maps to identify river courses, access points, and contours
  • Use Google Earth/Street View for a virtual recce before visiting
  • Mark sampling points on a map before fieldwork
  • Record six-figure grid references for each site

⚠️ Risk Assessment

Definition: A risk assessment identifies potential hazards in the fieldwork environment, assesses the likelihood and severity of harm, and describes measures to reduce or eliminate risks.
Environment Hazard Risk Level Prevention/Mitigation
River Drowning / deep water High Never enter water above knee height; wear buoyancy aids; work in pairs; avoid sites near weirs
River Slippery banks Medium Wear appropriate footwear (wellingtons/gripping boots); avoid steep banks; use walking aids
RiverWeil's disease (bacteria in water) Medium Cover cuts with waterproof plasters; wash hands before eating; avoid swallowing river water
Coastal Rising tide / being cut off High Check tide times; identify escape routes; set a turnaround time; never work below cliffs
Coastal Falling rocks / cliff collapse High Stay away from cliff base; wear hard hats near cliffs; check for warning signs
Urban Traffic Medium Work on pavements; use pedestrian crossings; wear high-visibility vests; brief on road safety
Urban Personal safety / harassment Medium Work in groups of 3+; stay in public areas; carry a mobile phone; have a meeting point
All Extreme weather Medium Check forecast; wear appropriate clothing; have a wet-weather alternative plan; carry first aid kit
Risk Assessment Structure: 1. Identify the hazard (what could cause harm?) 2. Assess who is at risk and how 3. Evaluate the risk level (high/medium/low) 4. Describe measures to eliminate or reduce the risk 5. Plan what to do if an incident occurs (emergency procedures)

General Safety Precautions

  • Always work in groups (minimum pairs, ideally groups of 3-4)
  • Carry a fully charged mobile phone
  • Inform someone of your route and expected return time
  • Carry a basic first aid kit
  • Wear appropriate clothing and footwear for the conditions
  • Have a clear emergency plan with contact numbers
  • Check weather forecasts and have a contingency plan

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between a research question and a hypothesis.

Q2: Write a suitable hypothesis for a study investigating how river characteristics change downstream.

Q3: Describe three factors you would consider when choosing a fieldwork location.

Q4: Identify two hazards of river fieldwork and explain how you would minimise the risk from each.

Q5: Explain why it is important to use both primary and secondary data in a geographical enquiry.

✅ Answers

  1. A research question is an open-ended question that guides the enquiry (e.g. "How does environmental quality vary across the city?"). A hypothesis is a testable statement that predicts the expected outcome (e.g. "Environmental quality decreases with distance from the CBD"). A hypothesis is more specific and directional, allowing you to clearly support or reject it with evidence, whereas a research question invites broader exploration.
  2. "River velocity, width, and depth all increase with distance downstream from the source" - based on the Bradshaw Model which predicts these changes as the river is joined by more tributaries and the channel becomes more efficient through erosion and deposition processes.
  3. Factor 1: Accessibility - the site must be reachable safely by the group, with road or public transport access and manageable terrain. Factor 2: Relevance - the site must show the geographical process being studied (e.g. a river must have enough variation downstream to test the hypothesis). Factor 3: Safety - the site must be free from unacceptable hazards, such as fast-flowing water or unstable cliffs.
  4. Hazard 1: Deep water / drowning - minimise by never entering water above knee height, wearing buoyancy aids, working in pairs, and avoiding sites near weirs or waterfalls. Hazard 2: Slippery river banks - minimise by wearing appropriate footwear with good grip, avoiding steep or muddy banks, and using a walking pole for stability.
  5. Primary data is collected firsthand by the researcher, ensuring it is specific to the study location and up-to-date. Secondary data provides wider context, historical comparison, and larger datasets that would be impossible to collect personally (e.g. census data, Environment Agency records). Using both allows triangulation - checking that findings from one source are supported by another, increasing the reliability and validity of conclusions.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • You must know your own fieldwork in detail - the examiner will ask about YOUR specific investigation
  • Be able to state your hypothesis clearly and explain the geographical theory behind it
  • Know exactly where your fieldwork took place (named locations, grid references)
  • Be prepared to explain and justify your data collection methods
  • Risk assessment is a common exam question - know specific hazards and mitigations for your environment
  • Remember you need TWO fieldwork investigations: one physical, one human
  • Practice explaining why each method was chosen - link it to your hypothesis

📝 Exam Technique

Geography Exam Tips:
The issue evaluation paper tests synoptic thinking — use your knowledge from across the course. Always consider multiple stakeholder viewpoints. Reach a justified conclusion supported by evidence from the resources. Practice with past pre-release materials.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often write vague answers without specific geographical evidence. Wrong: Writing generalised statements like 'it causes problems' Correct: Using specific data and named examples, e.g. 'the 2010 Haiti earthquake killed over 200,000 people due to poor building quality'

Students often confuse causes and effects. Wrong: Mixing up what caused the event with what resulted from it Correct: Clearly separate causes (why it happened) from effects (what happened as a result)

Students often describe rather than evaluate. Wrong: Listing strategies without assessing their effectiveness Correct: Weighing up strengths and weaknesses of each approach and reaching a supported judgement

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

6 marks: Explain the key factors affecting fieldwork: enquiry process.

Fieldwork: Enquiry Process involves multiple interconnected factors that geographers must understand. The key concepts include the processes that create and change fieldwork: enquiry process, the impacts on both people and environment, and the strategies used to manage associated challenges. For a comprehensive answer, specific case study evidence should be used throughout, with named examples and data to support each point. Geographical terminology should be used precisely, and the interrelationship between physical and human factors should be demonstrated. Top-level responses evaluate the relative importance of different factors and consider how the situation varies between locations.

Mark scheme: 2 marks for identifying key factors, 2 marks for explaining processes with detail, 2 marks for using specific evidence

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 requires knowledge of the key facts and processes related to fieldwork: enquiry process. AO2 demands understanding of how and why these processes operate, and their implications. AO3 asks you to analyse, evaluate and make judgements — this is where grade 9 answers stand out by weighing up competing perspectives and reaching supported conclusions. AO4 may involve interpreting maps, graphs or data related to this topic. To move from grade 5 to grade 9: use precise geographical terminology, support every point with specific case study evidence, and always evaluate rather than just describe.
