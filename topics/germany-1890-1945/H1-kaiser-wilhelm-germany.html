H1: Kaiser Wilhelm and Germany

Foundation Higher All Boards

The Kaiser's role, constitutional government, Prussian influence, Weltpolitik, Navy Laws, and domestic opposition in Imperial Germany.

👑 Kaiser Wilhelm II

Key Fact: Kaiser Wilhelm II became German Emperor (Kaiser) in June 1888 aged 29, following the death of his father Frederick III who had ruled for only 99 days. Wilhelm would reign until his abdication in November 1918.

Wilhelm II believed deeply in the principle of personal monarchy. He saw himself as chosen by God to rule Germany and insisted on making all key decisions himself. In 1892 he declared: "There is only one person who is master in this Empire and I am not going to tolerate any other."

Wilhelm's Personality and Style of Rule

  • He was impulsive and unpredictable, often making dramatic speeches without consulting ministers
  • In 1908, the Daily Telegraph Affair embarrassed Germany when Wilhelm gave an interview suggesting he wanted an alliance with Britain but the British were anti-German - his own ministers were not consulted
  • He had a withered left arm from birth, which made him determined to appear strong and military
  • He loved military uniforms and ceremony - he typically wore uniform on 350 days of the year
  • He interfered constantly in foreign policy, dismissing Bismarck's careful diplomacy
Example: The Daily Telegraph Affair 1908

Wilhelm gave an interview to the Daily Telegraph claiming that Germans "hated" the British and that he had personally warned France against a pro-Boer position during the Boer War. The interview caused an international scandal. Reichstag members demanded limits on the Kaiser's power to speak publicly, but Wilhelm simply replaced the Chancellor, Prince von Bülow, with Theobald von Bethmann-Hollweg in 1909.

⚖️ The German Constitution

Key Fact: Germany was a federal state with 25 states in 1871 (27 after 1912), each retaining its own ruler, government and laws. The Prussian King was automatically the German Kaiser.

The German Constitution of 1871 created a system that appeared democratic but gave enormous power to the Kaiser and the elite:

Body Role Power
The Kaiser Head of state, commander-in-chief Appointed/dismissed the Chancellor, could dissolve the Reichstag, controlled foreign policy and the armed forces
The Chancellor Head of government Responsible only to the Kaiser, not the Reichstag; could ignore the Reichstag entirely
The Bundesrat Upper house (Federal Council) 58 members from state governments; could veto legislation; Prussia had 17 of 58 votes (effective veto)
The Reichstag Lower house (elected parliament) Elected by all men over 25; could approve or reject laws and set the budget; but the Kaiser could dissolve it at any time
Key Weakness of the Constitution:
The Chancellor was NOT responsible to the Reichstag. He answered only to the Kaiser. This meant the elected parliament could not remove an unpopular Chancellor, and the government did not need majority support in the Reichstag to stay in power.

Democratic Features vs Autocratic Features

Democratic Features Autocratic Features
Universal male suffrage (all men over 25 could vote) Secret ballot introduced but influence of landlords remained
Reichstag elected every 3 years (5 years from 1888) Kaiser could dissolve the Reichstag at will
Reichstag had to approve laws and budgets Bundesrat (dominated by Prussia) could veto any law
Multiple political parties could compete Chancellor and ministers not answerable to Reichstag

🏰 Prussian Influence

Key Fact: Prussia was the largest and most powerful state in Germany. It covered 60% of German territory and contained 65% of the population. The King of Prussia was always the Kaiser.

Prussia dominated Germany in several crucial ways:

  • Military dominance: The Prussian Army became the German Army; Prussia had 17 votes in the 58-seat Bundesrat, enough to block any constitutional change
  • Junkers: The Prussian aristocratic landowning class (Junkers) held enormous political influence. They dominated the officer corps of the army and the civil service
  • Three-class franchise: In Prussia itself, the voting system was deeply undemocratic. The three-class system divided voters by the amount of tax they paid - the wealthiest 4% could choose a third of the electors, effectively controlling elections
  • Militaristic culture: Prussia had built Germany through "blood and iron" under Bismarck. Military values permeated German society - the army was seen as the "school of the nation"
Example: The Zabern Affair 1913

In the Alsatian town of Zabern, a German lieutenant insulted local civilians, calling them "Wackes" (a derogatory term). When locals protested, the army arrested 28 people without legal authority. The Reichstag passed a vote of no confidence in the Chancellor Bethmann-Hollweg by 293 to 54, but the Chancellor simply ignored it and the Kaiser backed the army. This showed that the constitution gave the elected parliament no real power over the military elite.

🌍 Weltpolitik

Key Fact: Weltpolitik ("world policy") was Wilhelm II's aggressive foreign policy aiming to make Germany a major world power with colonies, a large navy, and international influence. It replaced Bismarck's Realpolitik ("practical politics") which had focused on maintaining peace and stability in Europe.

Wilhelm dismissed Bismarck in 1890 and pursued an entirely different approach:

Bismarck's Realpolitik Wilhelm's Weltpolitik
Keep France isolated Challenge Britain for global dominance
Avoid colonial adventures Acquire overseas colonies and a "place in the sun"
Maintain friendship with Russia Let the Reinsurance Treaty with Russia lapse in 1890
Keep on good terms with Britain Build a rival navy, alienating Britain
Stability and alliances in Europe Aggressive "gunboat diplomacy"

Key Events of Weltpolitik

  • 1896 Kruger Telegram: Wilhelm sent a telegram congratulating the Boer leader Paul Kruger on repelling the British Jameson Raid - this outraged Britain
  • 1898-1914 Navy Laws: Massive naval expansion to challenge British supremacy at sea
  • 1905 and 1911 Morocco Crises: Wilhelm intervened in Morocco, a French sphere of influence, to test the Entente Cordiale - both times Germany was humiliated and isolated
  • 1907 "Blank Cheque": German support for Austria-Hungary during the Bosnian Crisis
Consequences of Weltpolitik:
1. Alienated Britain, which joined the Triple Entente with France and Russia in 1907
2. Pushed Russia towards France (Franco-Russian Alliance 1892)
3. Failed to gain significant colonies - Germany acquired only modest territories in Africa and the Pacific
4. Created the alliance system that would drag Europe into war in 1914
5. Cost enormous sums that could have been spent on domestic reform

⚓ The Navy Laws

Key Fact: Admiral von Tirpitz and Wilhelm II pushed through a series of Navy Laws (1898, 1900, 1906, 1908, 1912) to build a fleet capable of challenging the Royal Navy. The aim was to make Germany a world power and force Britain to respect German interests.

The Navy Laws in Detail

Navy Law Key Provisions Cost
First Navy Law 1898 7 battleships, 14 large cruisers over 7 years 408 million marks
Second Navy Law 1900 Doubled the fleet: 38 battleships, 20 large cruisers by 1920 Massively increased budget
1906 Amendment Added 6 large cruisers; accelerated building Further costs added
1908 Amendment Reduced lifetime of ships from 25 to 20 years, speeding replacement Enormous ongoing expense
1912 Amendment 3 more battleships added Rising naval budget

The Navy Laws had enormous consequences:

  • British response: Britain launched HMS Dreadnought in 1906, making all existing battleships obsolete, and began a naval arms race. By 1914 Britain had 29 dreadnoughts to Germany's 17
  • Financial strain: Naval spending rose from 207 million marks in 1897 to 455 million marks by 1914. The army also demanded more funding, creating a budget crisis
  • Political consequences: The Naval Laws were Tirpitz's "Risk Theory" - build a fleet so large that attacking Germany would be too costly even for Britain. Instead, it pushed Britain into the Triple Entente
  • Social impact: The cost of the naval arms race meant higher taxes and less money for social reform, creating domestic tension
Example: The "Risk Theory"

Tirpitz argued that Germany did not need a navy larger than Britain's. Instead, it needed a navy large enough that any British attack would be so costly that Britain would not risk it. This was the "Risk Theory." In practice, it failed because Britain simply built more ships and formed alliances with France and Russia to counter the German threat.

✊ Domestic Opposition

Key Fact: The Social Democratic Party (SPD) was the main opposition to the Kaiser's regime. By 1912, the SPD was the largest party in the Reichstag with 110 seats (34.8% of the vote), yet the government continued to ignore its demands for reform.

The Social Democratic Party (SPD)

  • Founded in 1875 from a merger of two socialist groups
  • Bismarck had tried to suppress socialism through the Anti-Socialist Laws (1878-1890), but the party continued to grow
  • The SPD believed in democratic reform, workers' rights, and eventually the overthrow of capitalism
  • By 1914, the SPD had over 1 million members and was linked to trade unions with 2.5 million members
  • Despite being the largest Reichstag party, it had no influence on government because the Chancellor was appointed by the Kaiser, not the Reichstag

Other Opposition Groups

Group Demands Success
SPD (Social Democrats) Democratic reform, better working conditions, state socialism Largest party by 1912 but excluded from government
Centre Party (Zentrum) Protect Catholic interests, democratic reform Major party but compromised with the system
Progressive Liberals Constitutional reform, more power for the Reichstag Small party, limited impact
Trade Unions Better pay, conditions, and working hours 2.5 million members by 1914; won some concessions
Why the Opposition Failed:
1. The constitution gave the Reichstag no power over the Chancellor
2. The Kaiser could dissolve the Reichstag and call new elections
3. The three-class franchise in Prussia meant the SPD could never win power there
4. The army and civil service were loyal to the Kaiser, not the parliament
5. The SPD was divided between revolutionaries and reformists

🏥 Social Reforms Under Wilhelm II

Despite opposing socialism, the German government introduced some of the most advanced social welfare measures in Europe:

  • 1883 Health Insurance Act: Workers contributed 2/3 and employers 1/3; provided free medical treatment and sick pay for up to 13 weeks
  • 1884 Accident Insurance Act: Employers funded insurance against workplace injury
  • 1889 Old Age and Disability Insurance: Workers, employers and the state all contributed; provided pensions at age 70
  • 1911 National Insurance Code: Consolidated existing insurance laws
  • Sammlungspolitik: From 1897, the government tried to build a "concentration of forces" - an alliance of industrialists, Junkers and conservative parties to isolate the SPD
Key Fact: Bismarck introduced social welfare legislation in the 1880s specifically to undermine support for the SPD. However, the measures were too limited to satisfy workers and the SPD continued to grow. By 1912, despite 30 years of social welfare, the SPD was the largest party in the Reichstag.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two ways in which the German Constitution was undemocratic. (4 marks)

Q2: How far did Prussian influence dominate Imperial Germany? Explain your answer. (12 marks)

Q3: What was Weltpolitik and why did Wilhelm II pursue it instead of Bismarck's Realpolitik? (8 marks)

Q4: Explain why the Navy Laws were significant for Germany's foreign relations. (8 marks)

Q5: "The SPD was the biggest threat to the Kaiser's regime." How far do you agree? (16 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. The Chancellor was responsible only to the Kaiser, not the elected Reichstag, meaning parliament could not hold the government to account. The Prussian three-class voting system gave the wealthy minority control over elections, and Prussia had 17 of 58 Bundesrat votes, enough to veto any constitutional change.
  2. Prussia dominated through: the Kaiser was always the King of Prussia; Prussia had 17 of 58 Bundesrat votes (enough to block changes); the Junker class controlled the army and civil service; the three-class franchise in Prussia was deeply undemocratic. However, other states retained their own governments and the Reichstag was elected by universal male suffrage across all of Germany, giving some democratic check on Prussian power.
  3. Weltpolitik was Wilhelm's policy of making Germany a world power through colonial expansion and naval build-up. He pursued it because he believed Germany deserved a "place in the sun," wanted to challenge British naval supremacy, and saw Bismarck's careful diplomacy as too cautious. Bismarck's Realpolitik had focused on peace and isolation of France; Wilhelm wanted global prestige.
  4. The Navy Laws provoked a naval arms race with Britain, pushing Britain towards the Triple Entente with France and Russia (1907). They created financial strain, requiring higher taxes and diverting funds from social reform. The Morocco Crises (1905, 1911) showed Germany's aggressive diplomacy isolated it further. The Laws made Britain see Germany as a direct threat rather than a potential ally.
  5. The SPD was a significant threat as the largest Reichstag party by 1912 (110 seats), representing working-class demands for reform. However, the constitution limited the SPD's effectiveness: the Chancellor was appointed by the Kaiser, the Bundesrat could veto laws, and the army remained loyal to the Kaiser. Other threats included the Centre Party and progressive liberals. The Kaiser's regime survived because the constitution was designed to resist democratic pressure, not because the SPD was weak.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use specific dates: Wilhelm became Kaiser in June 1888; the first Navy Law was 1898; the SPD won 110 seats in 1912
  • When evaluating the constitution, explain both democratic and autocratic features - don't just list one side
  • For "how far" questions, always give a clear judgement at the end
  • Link Weltpolitik to consequences: it was a CAUSE of Britain joining the Triple Entente
  • The Zabern Affair (1913) is excellent evidence for showing the weakness of the Reichstag
  • Remember: the SPD being the largest party proved the constitution was undemocratic - they had no power despite winning elections

📝 Exam Technique

History Exam Tips:
Always use specific dates and statistics. For 'explain why' questions, identify multiple causes and link them. For consequence questions, distinguish between political, economic and social impacts. Know the chronological narrative from Kaiser to Fuhrer.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally. Wrong: Hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally Correct: People with savings and fixed incomes suffered most, but those with debts or land actually benefited. Foreign tourists could buy goods very cheaply.

Students often think the nazis won a majority in a free election. Wrong: The Nazis won a majority in a free election Correct: The Nazis never won over 50% in a free election — their best result was 37.3% in July 1932. Hitler became Chancellor through backroom politics, not a majority vote.

Students often think all germans supported the nazis. Wrong: All Germans supported the Nazis Correct: There was significant opposition including the White Rose group, Edelweiss Pirates, and the 20 July Plot. Many Germans were passive rather than active supporters.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

4 marks: Describe two features of the Kaiser's Germany.

Feature 1: Constitutional monarchy. Germany was a constitutional monarchy in which the Kaiser held supreme power. Wilhelm II appointed and dismissed the Chancellor, who was responsible only to the Kaiser, not the elected Reichstag. The Kaiser controlled the armed forces and foreign policy, and could dissolve the Reichstag at any time. The Reichstag, elected by universal male suffrage over 25, could approve laws and budgets but could not remove the Chancellor. The Bundesrat (upper house), dominated by Prussia's 17 of 58 votes, could veto any legislation. This meant that while Germany appeared democratic, real power lay with the Kaiser and the elite.

Feature 2: Prussian militarism. Prussia dominated Germany, covering 60% of territory and 65% of the population. The Prussian aristocratic landowning class, the Junkers, held enormous influence over the officer corps of the army and the civil service. The Prussian three-class franchise gave the wealthiest 4% of voters control over a third of electors, making the system deeply undemocratic. Military values permeated society — the army was seen as the "school of the nation." The Navy Laws (1898–1912), pushed by Admiral von Tirpitz, expanded the fleet to challenge Britain and implement Weltpolitik, Wilhelm's aggressive "world policy" aiming for colonial expansion and a "place in the sun." Naval spending rose from 207 million marks in 1897 to 455 million by 1914.

Mark scheme: 1-2 marks per feature described, with specific detail for full marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AQA History tests three AOs: AO1 (Knowledge, 35%) requires recall of specific facts, dates and details about Kaiser Wilhelm and Germany; AO2 (Explanation, 35%) requires explaining causes, consequences and changes using detailed knowledge; AO3 (Sources and Interpretations, 30%) requires analysing provenance, content and usefulness of sources, and evaluating different historical interpretations. For grade 9, you need precise knowledge (specific dates, statistics and named individuals), sophisticated explanation showing how factors interrelate, and nuanced source analysis considering purpose, audience and context.
