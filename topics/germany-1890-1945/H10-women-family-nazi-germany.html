H10: Women and the Family in Nazi Germany

Foundation Higher All Boards

The Three Ks, marriage loans, the Mother's Cross, Nazi policies on women's employment, and how the war changed women's roles.

👩 The Nazi View of Women

Key Fact: The Nazi ideal for women was summed up as the "Three Ks" - Kinder, Küche, Kirche (Children, Kitchen, Church). Hitler believed that a woman's proper place was in the home, producing Aryan children and supporting her husband. He said: "The woman is the eternal companion of the man, and her world is the family."

Nazi Beliefs About Women

  • Motherhood was the highest duty: Women's primary role was to bear healthy Aryan children for the Reich. The Nazis saw motherhood as a national service, comparable to military service for men
  • Women should not work: Professional women were encouraged to give up their careers and return to the home. The Nazis believed working women took jobs from men and neglected their family duties
  • Women should be physically fit: To produce healthy children, women needed to be strong and athletic - but NOT competitive (that was for men)
  • Women should be subservient: Women should obey their husbands and the state. They should not involve themselves in politics or decision-making
  • Racial purity: Women must only have children with Aryan men. Relationships with Jews or other "inferior" races were forbidden
The Nazi Ideal Woman:
• Married with many children (4+ was the aim)
• Not in professional employment
• Physically fit but not competitive
• Not interested in politics or higher education
• Devoted to her husband, children, and the Nazi state
• Active in community work (but not leadership)

💰 Marriage Loans and Policies

Key Fact: In June 1933, the Law for the Encouragement of Marriage offered marriage loans of 1,000 marks to young couples if the wife gave up her job. For each child born, 25% of the loan was cancelled. If a couple had four children, the entire loan was wiped out.

Example: The Mother's Cross

The Mother's Cross was awarded annually on 12 August (Hitler's mother's birthday). Gold crosses were given to mothers of 8+ children, silver for 6+, and bronze for 4+. Recipients received privileges: they were saluted by the Hitler Youth, given priority in shops, and seated in the best seats at public events. The Mother's Cross was the Nazi equivalent of the Iron Cross for soldiers - making motherhood a form of national service. About 3 million women received the Mother's Cross by 1939.

🚫 Removing Women from Work

Key Fact: The Nazis introduced several policies to push women out of professional employment. In 1933, women in the civil service, medicine, and teaching were dismissed or encouraged to resign. By 1936, women were barred from legal professions and from being judges. The number of women at university was limited to 10% of enrolments.

Key Restrictions

  • 1933: Women dismissed from senior civil service positions; married women doctors and civil servants encouraged to leave
  • 1934: Women banned from being prosecutors or judges; restricted from standing as candidates in elections
  • 1936: Women barred from receiving research grants or scholarships for academic work
  • University restrictions: The proportion of female students was limited to 10% of total enrolments (down from 17% in 1932)
  • Marriage loans: The requirement that women give up work to receive a loan directly removed women from the workforce

Impact on Women's Employment

Year Women in Employment (millions) Context
1933 11.5 When Hitler became Chancellor
1936 11.6 Slight increase despite policies to remove women

1943 14.8 War economy desperate for workers; total mobilisation
Key Fact: Despite Nazi ideology, the number of women in employment actually INCREASED during the 1930s. By 1937, there were more women in work than when Hitler came to power. The reality was that Germany's economic recovery and rearmament programme needed workers, and many families needed a second income. The gap between Nazi ideology and economic reality was significant.

📊 The Birth Rate

Key Fact: The Nazi birth rate policy had mixed results. The birth rate rose from 14.7 per 1,000 in 1933 to 19.0 per 1,000 in 1939 - a significant increase. However, it never returned to Weimar levels (23.2 per 1,000 in 1920), and by 1939 it was already starting to decline again.

Why Did the Birth Rate Rise?

The birth rate increase was partly due to Nazi policies (marriage loans, Mother's Cross, improved maternity care) but also reflected broader trends: the economic recovery meant more couples could afford to marry and have children; the post-depression period naturally saw a rebound in births; and improved economic confidence encouraged family formation. It is difficult to separate the impact of Nazi policies from the general economic recovery. By 1939, the birth rate was already declining despite continued Nazi incentives.

💀 Sterilisation and Racial Policy

Key Fact: The Law for the Prevention of Hereditarily Diseased Offspring (July 1933) allowed the forced sterilisation of women with supposed "hereditary illnesses" including schizophrenia, epilepsy, "feeble-mindedness," and alcoholism. By 1939, approximately 320,000 people had been forcibly sterilised - the majority were women.
  • The law was one of the first anti-human rights measures of the Nazi regime
  • Hereditary Health Courts decided who should be sterilised - 320,000 cases decided by 1939
  • Women who had abortions or were considered "asocial" could be sent to concentration camps
  • German women who had relationships with foreign workers or prisoners of war could be publicly humiliated (shaved heads) or sent to camps
  • Jewish women and other "racially impure" women were actively prevented from having children - the opposite policy to that applied to Aryan women

⚔️ The Impact of War on Women

Key Fact: The outbreak of war in September 1939 dramatically changed women's roles. With millions of men conscripted into the military, women were needed to keep the economy running. By 1943, 14.8 million women were in employment - a 33% increase from 1933. The Nazis had to abandon their ideology and mobilise women for work.

Women in the War Economy

  • Despite initial reluctance, the Nazis were forced to mobilise women for war work
  • Women worked in factories, farms, offices, and hospitals - replacing men who had been called up
  • By 1943, 52% of the German workforce was female
  • From 1943, women were conscripted for war work under the Total War decree
  • However, Germany never fully mobilised its female workforce as effectively as Britain, where 80% of married women were in war work by 1943

Women's Experiences During the War

Aspect Impact on Women
Employment More women worked than ever before, but many did so reluctantly and faced long hours
Rationing Women managed households on reduced rations; food shortages worsened from 1943
Bombing Allied bombing from 1942-1945 destroyed homes; women and children were main civilian casualties
Family separation Men away at war; children evacuated to the countryside; families divided
Refugees From 1944, millions of women and children fled the advancing Soviet army
Example: The Contradiction of Nazi Women's Policy

The war exposed the fundamental contradiction in Nazi women's policy. For six years, the Nazis had told women their place was in the home, then suddenly demanded they work in factories. Many women resented this reversal. Hitler was reluctant to conscript women for war work even when the military demanded it - he feared the social consequences of taking women from their homes. By the time Total War mobilisation was ordered in 1943, it was too late to match Allied production levels.

🤔 How Far Did Nazi Policy Benefit Women?

Key Judgement: Nazi policy benefited some women (married mothers who accepted their prescribed role) but severely disadvantaged others (professional women, unmarried women, and "racially impure" women). The Nazis gave with one hand (marriage loans, Mother's Cross) and took with the other (careers, education, political rights, bodily autonomy). The war showed that Nazi ideology was incompatible with economic reality - when the state needed women to work, it abandoned its own principles.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two ways the Nazis encouraged women to have more children. (4 marks)

Q2: How far did Nazi policies improve the lives of German women? (8 marks)

Q3: "The Nazis failed to control women's employment." How far do you agree? (12 marks)

Q4: Explain how the war changed women's roles in Nazi Germany. (8 marks)

Q5: "Nazi policies towards women were contradictory." How far do you agree? (16 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. Marriage loans (June 1933): offered 1,000 marks to couples if the wife gave up work; 25% of the loan was cancelled for each child born, so four children meant the entire loan was wiped out. The Mother's Cross (August 1938): awarded to mothers who had many children - bronze for 4+, silver for 6+, gold for 8+ children - giving social status and privileges to mothers.
  2. Nazi policies improved life for some women: marriage loans provided financial help, the Mother's Cross gave recognition to mothers, and family allowances supported larger families. However, many women lost professional careers and educational opportunities; women were excluded from politics and decision-making; abortion was banned and contraception restricted; and 320,000 women were forcibly sterilised. The policies only benefited women who accepted the Nazi ideal of motherhood and domesticity. Professional women, single women, and "racially impure" women suffered under Nazi rule.
  3. The Nazis did fail to control women's employment in practice. Despite policies to remove women from work (dismissal from civil service, marriage loans requiring women to leave jobs), the number of women in employment actually increased from 11.5 million (1933) to 12.7 million (1939). Economic recovery and rearmament created demand for labour that could not be met by men alone. By 1937, there were more women in work than in 1933. The war made this failure complete - by 1943, 14.8 million women were employed. However, the Nazis did succeed in reducing women in professional and senior positions, and they controlled the narrative about women's "proper" role. Their failure was in economic reality, not in propaganda.
  4. The war dramatically changed women's roles. With millions of men conscripted, women were needed in factories, farms, and offices. By 1943, 52% of the workforce was female. From 1943, women were conscripted for war work. The Nazis had to abandon their ideology that women belonged in the home. Women also faced new challenges: managing households on reduced rations, surviving Allied bombing, and coping with family separation. By 1944-45, millions of women became refugees fleeing the Soviet advance. The war showed that Nazi ideology was incompatible with economic necessity.
  5. Nazi policies were deeply contradictory. The Nazis preached that women should stay at home (the Three Ks), yet the economy needed women to work. They dismissed women from professions, yet by 1939 more women were employed than in 1933. They promoted motherhood, yet the birth rate never returned to Weimar levels and was declining by 1939. They honoured mothers with the Mother's Cross, yet forced 320,000 "unfit" women to be sterilised. The biggest contradiction was during the war: after years of telling women to stay home, the Nazis suddenly demanded they work in factories. These contradictions showed that Nazi ideology (women in the home) clashed with economic reality (the state needed women workers). The ideology was never abandoned in principle but was repeatedly violated in practice.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • The Three Ks (Kinder, Küche, Kirche) is the starting point for any answer about Nazi women's policy
  • Know the specific policies: Marriage Loans (June 1933), Mother's Cross (August 1938), 10% university limit
  • The employment statistics show the CONTRADICTION in Nazi policy - more women worked in 1939 than 1933
  • Always distinguish between Nazi IDEOLOGY (women at home) and REALITY (women needed at work)
  • The birth rate rose but never matched Weimar levels and was falling by 1939 - Nazi policies were not fully successful
  • Forced sterilisation of 320,000 women is crucial evidence that Nazi policy was coercive, not just encouraging
  • Link the war to the failure of Nazi ideology - the state needed women workers and abandoned its own principles

📝 Exam Technique

History Exam Tips:
Always use specific dates and statistics. For 'explain why' questions, identify multiple causes and link them. For consequence questions, distinguish between political, economic and social impacts. Know the chronological narrative from Kaiser to Fuhrer.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally. Wrong: Hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally Correct: People with savings and fixed incomes suffered most, but those with debts or land actually benefited. Foreign tourists could buy goods very cheaply.

Students often think the nazis won a majority in a free election. Wrong: The Nazis won a majority in a free election Correct: The Nazis never won over 50% in a free election — their best result was 37.3% in July 1932. Hitler became Chancellor through backroom politics, not a majority vote.

Students often think all germans supported the nazis. Wrong: All Germans supported the Nazis Correct: There was significant opposition including the White Rose group, Edelweiss Pirates, and the 20 July Plot. Many Germans were passive rather than active supporters.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

8 marks: Explain how the Nazis changed women's roles.

The Nazis promoted the ideal of the "Three Ks" — Kinder, Küche, Kirche (Children, Kitchen, Church). Hitler declared that a woman's proper place was in the home, producing Aryan children and supporting her husband. The Law for the Encouragement of Marriage (June 1933) offered loans of 1,000 marks to young couples if the wife gave up her job; 25% of the loan was cancelled for each child born, so four children wiped out the entire debt. The Mother's Cross (Mutterkreuz), introduced in August 1938, awarded bronze for 4+ children, silver for 6+, and gold for 8+; about 3 million women received the cross by 1939. Women were dismissed from the civil service, barred from legal professions by 1936, and university enrolment was limited to 10% (down from 17% in 1932). Abortion for Aryan women was banned and contraception restricted.

However, Nazi ideology clashed with economic reality. Despite policies to remove women from work, the number of women in employment actually increased from 11.5 million (1933) to 12.7 million (1939). Germany's rearmament programme needed workers, and many families needed a second income. The birth rate rose from 14.7 per 1,000 (1933) to 19.0 per 1,000 (1939) but never returned to Weimar levels and was already declining by 1939. Forced sterilisation of 320,000 "unfit" women under the Law for the Prevention of Hereditarily Diseased Offspring (July 1933) showed the coercive side of Nazi policy.

The war exposed the fundamental contradiction. After years of telling women to stay home, the Nazis suddenly needed them in factories. By 1943, 14.8 million women were employed and 52% of the workforce was female. From 1943, women were conscripted for war work under the Total War decree. The Nazis gave with one hand (marriage loans, Mother's Cross) and took with the other (careers, education, political rights, bodily autonomy). In conclusion, Nazi policy changed women's roles ideologically but could not override economic necessity — the gap between propaganda and reality was significant.

Mark scheme: 2 marks per explained aspect with specific dates and statistics, recognition of contradictions for top level
