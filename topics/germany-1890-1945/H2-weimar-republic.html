H2: The Weimar Republic

Foundation Higher All Boards

The 1918 revolution, the Weimar Constitution, challenges from the left and right, and political violence in the early Republic.

🔄 The Revolution of 1918

Key Fact: In October 1918, Germany was facing defeat in World War I. The Kaiser's government transformed into a parliamentary democracy to secure better peace terms. However, the revolution went further than intended when sailors mutinied at Kiel on 3 November 1918.

Events of the Revolution

  • October 1918: Prince Max of Baden became Chancellor, introducing parliamentary government to negotiate with President Wilson of the USA
  • 3 November 1918: Sailors at Kiel mutinied rather than sail out for a final battle against the British Navy
  • 9 November 1918: Workers' and soldiers' councils (soviets) took control of major cities. The Kaiser abdicated and fled to Holland. Philipp Scheidemann (SPD) declared a democratic republic from the Reichstag balcony
  • 10 November 1918: The Council of People's Representatives formed - a temporary government of SPD and USPD (Independent Socialists)
  • 11 November 1918: Germany signed the Armistice, ending World War I
  • January 1919: Elections held for a National Assembly; the Assembly met in Weimar (not Berlin, which was too unstable) to draft a new constitution
Example: The "Stab-in-the-Back" Myth

Right-wing opponents of the Republic claimed that Germany had not been defeated militarily but had been "stabbed in the back" (Dolchstoss) by politicians who signed the Armistice. This myth was dangerous because it undermined the Republic's legitimacy from the start and was exploited by the Nazis. In reality, the German Army was on the verge of collapse - General Ludendorff himself had demanded an armistice in September 1918.

📜 The Weimar Constitution

Key Fact: The Weimar Constitution, approved on 11 August 1919, created the most democratic system Germany had ever known - but it contained crucial weaknesses that opponents exploited.

Democratic Features

Feature Detail
Universal suffrage All men AND women over 20 could vote (previously only men over 25)
Proportional representation Seats in the Reichstag allocated proportionally to votes - every 60,000 votes = 1 seat
President Elected by the people every 7 years; chose the Chancellor
Referendums The people could initiate referendums on key issues
Bill of Rights Freedom of speech, religion, and equal rights guaranteed

Crucial Weaknesses

Article 48 - The "Emergency Decree" Clause:
The President could rule by decree in an emergency, bypassing the Reichstag entirely. This was intended for genuine crises but was used increasingly often. Hitler would later use Article 48 to destroy democracy from within.
  • Proportional representation: Meant that no single party ever won a majority. By 1930, 28 different parties won seats. Coalition governments were always needed but frequently collapsed, leading to repeated elections (11 elections between 1919-1933)
  • Article 48: Gave the President dictatorial powers in an "emergency" - but the constitution never defined what constituted an emergency
  • Presidential power: The President chose the Chancellor and could dissolve the Reichstag. After 1930, Chancellor Brüning governed almost entirely using Article 48, setting a dangerous precedent
  • State governments: Individual states retained their own governments, creating tensions with the central government
Key Fact: Friedrich Ebert, leader of the SPD, became the first President of the Weimar Republic in February 1919. He governed using Article 48 136 times during his presidency (1919-1925), often to deal with political crises - setting a dangerous precedent for future use.

⚔️ The Spartacist Uprising (January 1919)

Key Fact: In January 1919, the Communist Spartacist League (led by Rosa Luxemburg and Karl Liebknecht) attempted to overthrow the Weimar government in Berlin. The government used the Freikorps (right-wing ex-soldiers) to crush the uprising. Luxemburg and Liebknecht were murdered on 15 January 1919.

Causes of the Uprising

  • The Communist Spartacists (KPD, formed December 1918) wanted a Soviet-style revolution in Germany
  • They opposed the SPD-led government, which they saw as too moderate and capitalist
  • Workers' councils in Berlin were radicalised and demanding more revolutionary change
  • The government had dismissed the left-wing police chief of Berlin, Emil Eichhorn, on 4 January 1919

Events

  • 5 January 1919: Spartacists seized the Berlin newspaper district and government buildings
  • 6 January 1919: 100,000 workers demonstrated in Berlin
  • The Spartacists failed to seize key communication and transport centres
  • Ebert called in the Freikorps - 3,000 heavily armed ex-soldiers
  • The Freikorps crushed the uprising over several days of fierce fighting
  • 15 January 1919: Luxemburg and Liebknecht were arrested and murdered by Freikorps officers; Luxemburg's body was thrown into the Landwehr Canal
Significance of the Spartacist Uprising

The crushing of the Spartacists created a permanent rift between the SPD and the KPD (Communists). The left was permanently divided - the Communists never forgave the SPD for using the Freikorps to murder Luxemburg and Liebknecht. This division meant the left could never unite against the Nazi threat in the early 1930s. The SPD became known to Communists as "social fascists."

🛡️ The Kapp Putsch (March 1920)

Key Fact: In March 1920, right-wing Freikorps units under Wolfgang Kapp attempted to seize control of Berlin. The army (Reichswehr) refused to fire on the Freikorps. The government survived only through a general strike called by trade unions, which paralysed Berlin and forced Kapp to flee.

Causes

  • The Treaty of Versailles (signed June 1919) required Germany to reduce its army to 100,000 men
  • The government ordered the disbandment of the Freikorps in March 1920
  • Freikorps units (about 12,000 men under General von Lüttwitz) refused to disband and marched on Berlin
  • The Reichswehr refused to fire on the Freikorps - General Seeckt declared "Reichswehr does not fire on Reichswehr"

Events

  • 13 March 1920: Kapp and Lüttwitz seized Berlin; the government fled to Dresden and then Stuttgart
  • The trade unions called a general strike, bringing Berlin to a standstill - no transport, no electricity, no water
  • 17 March 1920: Kapp fled to Sweden after just 4 days; the Putsch collapsed
  • 22 March 1920: The government returned to Berlin
Key Lesson from the Kapp Putsch:
1. The Weimar government could not rely on the army to defend it from right-wing threats
2. It depended on the working class and trade unions to survive
3. Right-wing conspirators were treated leniently - only one conspirator was jailed, and Kapp died of illness before he could be prosecuted. This contrasted sharply with the brutal treatment of left-wing revolutionaries.

🔫 Political Violence 1919-1923

The early Weimar Republic was plagued by political violence from both left and right:

Event Date Left or Right Outcome
Spartacist Uprising January 1919 Left (Communist) Crushed by Freikorps; Luxemburg and Liebknecht murdered
Bavarian Soviet Republic April-May 1919 Left (Communist) Crushed by Freikorps; over 1,000 killed
Kapp Putsch March 1920 Right (Freikorps) Defeated by general strike; conspirators barely punished
Ruhr Uprising March-April 1920 Left (Communist) 50,000 Freikorps sent; up to 1,000 workers killed
Assassination of Erzberger August 1921 Right Financier who signed Armistice murdered; killers given light sentences
Assassination of Rathenau June 1922 Right Jewish Foreign Minister murdered by right-wing extremists
Munich Putsch November 1923 Right (Nazi) Hitler's failed uprising; 16 Nazis and 4 police killed
Key Fact: Between 1919 and 1922, right-wing extremists committed 354 political murders in Germany. Left-wing extremists committed 22. However, right-wing assassins received an average sentence of 4 months, while left-wing assassins received an average of 15 years. This showed the justice system was biased towards the right and against the Republic.

📊 The Early Republic: Challenges Summary

Challenge Detail Impact on the Republic
Treaty of Versailles Signed June 1919; War Guilt, reparations, military cuts, territory lost Republic blamed for "Diktat"; undermined from the start
Left-wing opposition Spartacists 1919, Bavarian Soviet 1919, Ruhr Uprising 1920 SPD-KPD split prevented left-wing unity against fascism
Right-wing opposition Kapp Putsch 1920, assassinations, Munich Putsch 1923 Army and judiciary sympathetic to right; Republic could not trust its own institutions
Economic crisis Hyperinflation 1923, Ruhr occupation Middle class savings destroyed; faith in Republic shaken
Constitutional weaknesses Article 48, PR voting system Allowed Hitler to destroy democracy legally in 1933

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe two weaknesses of the Weimar Constitution. (4 marks)

Q2: Explain why the Spartacist Uprising failed. (8 marks)

Q3: How far was the Weimar Republic threatened by right-wing opposition in the years 1919-1923? (12 marks)

Q4: "The Weimar Constitution was doomed from the start." How far do you agree? (16 marks)

Q5: Explain why the Kapp Putsch was significant for the Weimar Republic. (8 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. Article 48 allowed the President to rule by decree in an emergency, but "emergency" was never defined, so it could be abused. Proportional representation meant that no single party could win a majority, leading to unstable coalition governments that frequently collapsed.
  2. The Spartacist Uprising failed because: it lacked popular support - most Berlin workers did not actively join the revolt; the Spartacists failed to seize key infrastructure like communications and transport; the government called in the Freikorps who were heavily armed and ruthless; the SPD had control of the state machinery and could mobilise force against the Communists. Luxemburg and Liebknecht were also poor strategists who hesitated at crucial moments.
  3. Right-wing threats were very serious: the Kapp Putsch briefly seized Berlin in March 1920; right-wing extremists murdered Erzberger (1921) and Rathenau (1922); the Munich Putsch in 1923 showed the growing Nazi threat. The army was unreliable - it refused to fire on the Freikorps during the Kapp Putsch. The judiciary was biased, giving light sentences to right-wing killers. However, left-wing threats were also serious (Spartacists, Bavarian Soviet Republic), and the Republic survived both. The Republic's biggest weakness was that its own institutions (army, courts, civil service) were often sympathetic to its right-wing opponents.
  4. The Constitution had significant flaws: Article 48 gave the President emergency powers that Hitler exploited; PR produced fragmented parliaments and weak coalitions; the President had too much power over the Chancellor. However, the Constitution was not inherently doomed - it functioned reasonably well during the Stresemann era (1924-1929). The real problems were external: the Versailles Treaty undermined the Republic's legitimacy, economic crises destroyed confidence, and the Republic's own institutions (army, judiciary) were hostile. The Constitution was a contributing factor but not the sole cause of the Republic's failure.
  5. The Kapp Putsch was significant because: it showed the Weimar government could not rely on the army to defend democracy (Reichswehr refused to fire on Freikorps); it demonstrated that the Republic depended on the working class (general strike saved the government); it revealed the biased justice system - right-wing conspirators were barely punished; and it set a pattern where right-wing threats to the Republic were treated leniently while left-wing revolts were crushed with maximum force.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • For questions about the Constitution, always discuss both strengths AND weaknesses - examiners want balance
  • Use precise dates: Kaiser abdicated 9 November 1918; Constitution approved 11 August 1919; Spartacist Uprising January 1919; Kapp Putsch March 1920
  • When comparing left and right threats, use the statistics: 354 right-wing murders vs 22 left-wing; average sentences 4 months vs 15 years
  • Article 48 is the single most important constitutional weakness - make sure you can explain exactly how it was used and abused
  • The "stab-in-the-back" myth is crucial for explaining why the Republic lacked legitimacy from the start
  • Always link political events to consequences: the Spartacist Uprising led to the SPD-KPD split, which later prevented anti-Nazi unity

📝 Exam Technique

History Exam Tips — The Weimar Republic:
1. For The Weimar Republic, include specific factual detail: dates, names, events and statistics where relevant
2. For source questions, analyse provenance (who, when, why, audience) before using the content
3. In evaluation questions, discuss multiple factors and weigh their relative significance
4. Always link back to the question focus — don't just narrate what happened with The Weimar Republic
5. Use phrases like 'the most significant factor was... because...' to show analytical judgement

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally. Wrong: Hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally Correct: People with savings and fixed incomes suffered most, but those with debts or land actually benefited. Foreign tourists could buy goods very cheaply.

Students often think the nazis won a majority in a free election. Wrong: The Nazis won a majority in a free election Correct: The Nazis never won over 50% in a free election — their best result was 37.3% in July 1932. Hitler became Chancellor through backroom politics, not a majority vote.

Students often think all germans supported the nazis. Wrong: All Germans supported the Nazis Correct: There was significant opposition including the White Rose group, Edelweiss Pirates, and the 20 July Plot. Many Germans were passive rather than active supporters.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Explain why the Weimar Republic faced opposition from both left and right.

The Weimar Republic faced opposition from the left because communists wanted a Soviet-style revolution. In January 1919, the Spartacist League, led by Rosa Luxemburg and Karl Liebknecht, seized government buildings in Berlin and called for a general strike. Ebert used the Freikorps to crush the uprising; Luxemburg and Liebknecht were murdered on 15 January 1919. This permanently divided the left — the KPD never forgave the SPD for using right-wing Freikorps to kill communist leaders, preventing anti-Nazi unity in the 1930s.

The Republic faced opposition from the right because nationalists, the army, and the judiciary despised democracy and blamed the Republic for the "Diktat" of Versailles. In March 1920, the Kapp Putsch saw Freikorps units under Wolfgang Kapp seize Berlin. The Reichswehr refused to fire on the Freikorps — General Seeckt declared "Reichswehr does not fire on Reichswehr." The government survived only through a general strike by trade unions. Right-wing conspirators were treated leniently: between 1919 and 1922, right-wing extremists committed 354 political murders but received an average sentence of just 4 months, compared to 15 years for left-wing assassins.

The Constitution itself enabled opposition. Article 48 allowed the President to rule by decree in an undefined "emergency" — Ebert used it 136 times during his presidency (1919–1925), setting a dangerous precedent that Hitler later exploited to destroy democracy from within. In conclusion, the Republic faced determined enemies on both sides, but the right-wing threat was more dangerous because the Republic's own institutions — the army, the courts, and the civil service — were sympathetic to the right and hostile to democracy.

Mark scheme: 3-4 marks per explained factor with specific evidence, conclusion for top level
