H3: Hyperinflation and the Ruhr Crisis

Foundation Higher All Boards

The French occupation of the Ruhr, passive resistance, hyperinflation of 1923, impact on different social groups, and the beginnings of recovery.

🏭 The Ruhr Crisis 1923

Key Fact: In January 1923, French and Belgian troops occupied the Ruhr, Germany's industrial heartland, after Germany defaulted on reparations payments. The German government responded with a policy of passive resistance.

Background: Reparations

  • The Treaty of Versailles (1919) set reparations at £6.6 billion (132 billion gold marks) to be paid in annual instalments
  • In 1922, Germany requested a payment holiday, claiming economic hardship
  • The French, under Prime Minister Raymond Poincaré, refused and demanded payment in full
  • When Germany defaulted on a timber delivery in December 1922 and a coal delivery in January 1923, France sent troops

Events of the Occupation

  • 11 January 1923: 60,000 French and Belgian troops occupied the Ruhr
  • The aim was to take resources (coal, iron, steel) directly as payment in kind
  • Chancellor Wilhelm Cuno declared a policy of passive resistance - workers would not cooperate with the French
  • The government paid the wages of striking workers, continuing their salaries from Berlin
  • The French responded with force: 132 workers were killed and thousands expelled from their homes
  • Albert Leo Schlageter, a Freikorps member who carried out sabotage against the French, was executed on 26 May 1923 and became a right-wing martyr
Example: French Methods in the Ruhr

The French used harsh methods to break passive resistance. Workers who refused to cooperate were expelled from the Ruhr with their families. The French set up a customs barrier between the Ruhr and the rest of Germany. Food supplies were disrupted and hunger became widespread. Over 150,000 people were expelled from the occupied zone. The French also separated the Rhineland from the rest of Germany, issuing their own currency (the Rentenmark) in the occupied zone.

💰 Hyperinflation 1923

Key Fact: By November 1923, the German mark had become virtually worthless. At the peak of hyperinflation, 4.2 trillion marks equalled $1 USD. A loaf of bread that cost 1 mark in 1919 cost 200,000 million marks by November 1923. Workers were paid twice daily and given time off to spend their wages before they lost further value.

Causes of Hyperinflation

The Inflation Chain:
1. Germany defaulted on reparations (1922) →
2. France occupied the Ruhr (Jan 1923) →
3. Government ordered passive resistance →
4. Government printed money to pay striking workers →
5. More money chased fewer goods →
6. Prices rose →
7. Government printed even more money to cover rising costs →
8. Prices rose even faster →
9. Hyperinflation spiral

Key Fact: The mark had been losing value since the war - Germany had funded WWI by printing money rather than raising taxes, doubling the money supply between 1914-1918. The Ruhr crisis accelerated an existing problem into a catastrophe.

👥 Impact on Different Groups

Winners from Hyperinflation

Group Why They Benefited
Borrowers / Debtors Loans could be repaid with worthless money. A mortgage of 100,000 marks could be paid off with a few eggs' worth of cash
Industrialists Factory owners could pay workers in worthless marks while selling goods for stable foreign currency. Hugo Stinnes became one of Germany's richest men
Foreign visitors British and American visitors could live luxuriously on very little money
Farmers Agricultural prices rose with inflation; food retained real value when money was worthless

Losers from Hyperinflation

Group Why They Suffered
Savers Life savings became worthless. 800 million marks in savings could buy a cup of coffee
Pensioners Fixed pensions became completely worthless overnight
Middle class Salary earners and professionals on fixed incomes were devastated. Their social status and financial security were destroyed
Small business owners Shops could not restock goods because prices rose faster than they could sell items
People on fixed incomes Anyone with a set wage or salary found it worthless within days or hours
Example: The Human Cost

A Berlin woman carried a laundry basket full of notes to buy a few groceries. When she put the basket down for a moment, a thief stole the basket - but left the worthless money behind. Pensioners who had saved carefully for decades found their life savings could not buy a loaf of bread. A university professor's salary of 24,000 marks per year in 1913 would not even buy a newspaper by late 1923. Some people resorted to burning banknotes as fuel because they were cheaper than coal.

🧮 The Social and Political Impact

Key Fact: Hyperinflation destroyed the confidence of the German middle class in the Weimar Republic. Many who had been reasonably prosperous were reduced to poverty, and they blamed the democratic government for their suffering. This anger would later fuel support for extremist parties like the Nazis.

Political Consequences

  • Munich Putsch (November 1923): Hitler and the Nazis attempted to seize power in Bavaria, exploiting the chaos of hyperinflation. The Putsch failed but gave Hitler national publicity
  • Hamburg Communist Uprising (October 1923): Communists seized control of Hamburg for several days; 100 people were killed
  • Separatist movements: Rhineland and Palatinate separatists attempted to break away from Germany with French encouragement
  • Gustav Stresemann became Chancellor in August 1923: He ended passive resistance in September 1923, recognising that continuing would destroy the economy completely

Psychological Impact

  • The middle class never fully trusted the Republic again - they associated democracy with economic chaos
  • Many Germans blamed the Republic rather than the war or France for their suffering
  • The experience created a culture of "never again" - but different groups drew opposite conclusions (some supported democracy, others extremism)
  • Women who had to barter and scavenge for food lost faith in the state's ability to protect them

📈 Recovery Under Stresemann

Key Fact: Gustav Stresemann became Chancellor in August 1923 and Foreign Secretary from 1924 until his death in October 1929. He was the key figure in Germany's economic and diplomatic recovery.

Stresemann's Recovery Measures

Measure Date Impact
Ended passive resistance in the Ruhr September 1923 Removed the need to print money to pay strikers; angered right-wing nationalists
Introduced the Rentenmark November 1923 New currency backed by land and industry; 1 Rentenmark = 1 trillion old marks; stabilised prices
Dawes Plan August 1924 Reduced annual reparations; American loans to German industry; French troops left the Ruhr
Replaced Rentenmark with Reichsmark August 1924 Permanent stable currency backed by gold
How the Rentenmark Worked:
The Rentenmark was backed by mortgage on land and industrial assets (not gold, which Germany lacked). The Rentenbank issued the new currency and strictly limited the amount in circulation. People were willing to accept it because they trusted the real assets behind it. The exchange rate was set at 4.2 Rentenmarks = $1, the same as the pre-war mark. The psychological trick was crucial: people BELIEVED the new currency would hold its value, so it did.
Example: The "Miracle of the Rentenmark"

When the Rentenmark was introduced on 20 November 1923, prices stabilised almost overnight. Shops began displaying prices again. People who had been hoarding goods started selling them. Within weeks, the economic chaos subsided. However, those who had lost their savings could never recover them - the Rentenmark only prevented future inflation; it could not undo the damage already done.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain why France occupied the Ruhr in January 1923. (4 marks)

Q2: How far did hyperinflation affect all groups in German society equally? (8 marks)

Q3: "Hyperinflation was the worst crisis the Weimar Republic faced in the years 1919-1923." How far do you agree? (12 marks)

Q4: Explain how Stresemann solved the hyperinflation crisis. (8 marks)

Q5: Why did the German government pursue a policy of passive resistance during the Ruhr Crisis? What were the consequences? (12 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. France occupied the Ruhr because Germany defaulted on reparations payments - specifically failing to deliver timber (December 1922) and coal (January 1923). French Prime Minister Poincaré was determined to enforce the Treaty of Versailles and believed taking resources directly was the only way to ensure payment.
  2. Hyperinflation did NOT affect all groups equally. Borrowers benefited because they could repay debts with worthless money. Industrialists who sold goods for foreign currency profited. Farmers benefited because food retained real value. However, savers, pensioners, the middle class on fixed incomes, and small businesses were devastated. Life savings became worthless overnight. The impact was deeply unequal - it destroyed the middle class while some wealthy industrialists grew richer.
  3. Hyperinflation was an extremely serious crisis because it destroyed the savings of the middle class, caused widespread starvation, and shattered confidence in the Republic. It also triggered the Munich Putsch. However, the political violence of 1919-1923 (Spartacists, Kapp Putsch, assassinations) was also a severe threat. The Treaty of Versailles undermined the Republic from the start. The Ruhr Crisis was the immediate trigger but hyperinflation was its economic consequence. Overall, hyperinflation was probably the worst crisis because it destroyed the economic foundation of the Republic and alienated the middle class permanently.
  4. Stresemann ended passive resistance in September 1923, stopping the need to print money to pay Ruhr strikers. He introduced the Rentenmark in November 1923, replacing the worthless mark at a rate of 1 Rentenmark = 1 trillion old marks, backed by land and industrial assets. He negotiated the Dawes Plan (August 1924), which reduced reparations and brought American loans. He replaced the temporary Rentenmark with the Reichsmark in August 1924. These measures restored confidence and stabilised the economy, though those who had lost savings could never recover them.
  5. The government pursued passive resistance to show Germany would not accept French demands passively, to maintain national dignity, and because it could not afford to pay reparations. However, the consequences were catastrophic: the government printed money to pay striking workers, triggering hyperinflation; the economy collapsed; the middle class was destroyed; political extremism increased (Munich Putsch, Hamburg Uprising); and Stresemann eventually had to abandon the policy in September 1923, accepting that it was economically unsustainable.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use specific figures: 4.2 trillion marks = $1; 60,000 French troops; 132 workers killed
  • For impact questions, structure your answer by GROUP (winners/losers or class by class)
  • The key chain of causation is: Default → Ruhr occupation → Passive resistance → Print money → Hyperinflation
  • Remember that hyperinflation had begun BEFORE 1923 - WWI spending and reparations had already weakened the mark
  • The Rentenmark was backed by LAND and INDUSTRY, not gold - this is a common exam question
  • When evaluating Stresemann, note that the recovery was built on American loans, which were fragile
  • Link hyperinflation to political consequences: it pushed the middle class towards extremism and destroyed trust in democracy

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally. Wrong: Hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally Correct: People with savings and fixed incomes suffered most, but those with debts or land actually benefited. Foreign tourists could buy goods very cheaply.

Students often think the nazis won a majority in a free election. Wrong: The Nazis won a majority in a free election Correct: The Nazis never won over 50% in a free election — their best result was 37.3% in July 1932. Hitler became Chancellor through backroom politics, not a majority vote.

Students often think all germans supported the nazis. Wrong: All Germans supported the Nazis Correct: There was significant opposition including the White Rose group, Edelweiss Pirates, and the 20 July Plot. Many Germans were passive rather than active supporters.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

8 marks: Explain the effects of hyperinflation in 1923.

Hyperinflation devastated savers and those on fixed incomes. By November 1923, 4.2 trillion marks equalled $1 USD. A loaf of bread that cost 1 mark in 1919 cost 200,000 million marks by November 1923. Pensioners' fixed incomes became worthless overnight; a university professor's salary of 24,000 marks per year would not buy a newspaper. Some people resorted to burning banknotes as fuel because they were cheaper than coal. The middle class — Germany's natural defenders of democracy — never fully trusted the Republic again, associating democratic government with economic chaos.

However, borrowers benefited enormously. Loans could be repaid with worthless money — a mortgage of 100,000 marks could be paid off with a few eggs' worth of cash. Industrialists like Hugo Stinnes who sold goods for stable foreign currency grew rich. Farmers benefited because food retained real value. The psychological impact was profound: the experience created a culture of "never again," but different groups drew opposite conclusions — some supported democracy, others turned to extremism, fueling the Munich Putsch (November 1923) and the Hamburg Communist Uprising (October 1923).

Recovery came under Stresemann, who ended passive resistance in September 1923 and introduced the Rentenmark in November 1923, backed by land and industrial assets at a rate of 1 Rentenmark = 1 trillion old marks. Prices stabilised almost overnight. However, those who had lost their savings could never recover them — the Rentenmark prevented future inflation but could not undo the damage already done.

Mark scheme: 2 marks per explained effect with specific evidence, range of impacts for top level
