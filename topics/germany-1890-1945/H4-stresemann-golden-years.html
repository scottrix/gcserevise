H4: The Golden Years: Stresemann

Foundation Higher All Boards

Stresemann's economic and diplomatic recovery, the Dawes and Young Plans, Locarno Pact, League of Nations, and the cultural flowering of Weimar Germany.

🏛️ Gustav Stresemann

Key Fact: Gustav Stresemann served as Chancellor for just 102 days (August-November 1923) but remained Foreign Secretary from August 1923 until his death on 3 October 1929. He was the dominant figure in German politics during the "Golden Years" and was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 1926 alongside French Foreign Minister Aristide Briand.

Stresemann was a pragmatist from the moderate right (DVP - German People's Party). He believed Germany could only recover by cooperating with the international community, even if this meant accepting the Treaty of Versailles in practice while opposing it in principle. He described his approach as "fulfilment policy" - meeting international obligations to prove the Versailles settlement was unworkable and negotiate better terms.

Stresemann's Key Achievements

Achievement Date Significance
Ended passive resistance in the Ruhr September 1923 Stopped the need to print money; began economic stabilisation
Introduced the Rentenmark November 1923 Ended hyperinflation; restored confidence in German currency
Dawes Plan August 1924 Reduced reparations; brought American investment; French left the Ruhr
Locarno Pact October 1925 Guaranteed western borders; improved relations with France

Young Plan August 1929 Reduced total reparations from £6.6bn to £2bn; extended payment period to 59 years

💰 Economic Recovery

The Dawes Plan (August 1924)

Key Fact: The Dawes Plan, negotiated by American banker Charles Dawes, restructured Germany's reparations payments. Annual payments were reduced from £50 million to a starting figure of £10 million, rising to £25 million over 5 years. Crucially, it also arranged American loans of 800 million gold marks to Germany.
  • Reparations were set at a level Germany could actually afford
  • American banks lent Germany 25 billion marks between 1924-1929
  • German industry used these loans to modernise factories and infrastructure
  • The French and Belgians withdrew from the Ruhr in August 1925
  • The Reichsbank was reorganised under Allied supervision
  • Industrial output grew: by 1928, Germany produced the same as all the British Empire combined
Was the Dawes Plan a Success?
YES: Economic recovery began; French troops left the Ruhr; industry modernised; employment rose; confidence returned
NO: Germany became dependent on American loans; if the loans stopped, the economy would collapse; reparations were still being paid; nationalists opposed accepting the Versailles system

The Young Plan (August 1929)

  • Negotiated by American banker Owen Young
  • Reduced total reparations from 132 billion marks (£6.6 billion) to 37 billion marks (£2 billion)
  • Extended payment period to 59 years (until 1988)
  • Set up the Bank for International Settlements to manage payments
  • Foreign troops were to evacuate the Rhineland by June 1930 (5 years early)
  • However, right-wing opposition campaigned against it - the DNVP organised a referendum in December 1929 that gained 5.6 million votes but fell short of the 21 million needed to force change

🌍 Diplomatic Recovery

The Locarno Pact (October 1925)

Key Fact: The Locarno Pact was signed on 16 October 1925 in Locarno, Switzerland. Germany, France and Belgium agreed to guarantee their mutual western borders as set by Versailles. This was hailed as the beginning of a new era of peace - the "Locarno Spring" or "Locarno Honeymoon."

The Pact had several components:

  • Rhine Pact: Germany, France and Belgium agreed that their western borders (including the demilitarised Rhineland) were final. Britain and Italy acted as guarantors
  • Eastern Treaties: Germany signed arbitration treaties with Poland and Czechoslovakia but did NOT guarantee eastern borders - this left the door open for future territorial revision
  • France's alliances: France signed mutual assistance treaties with Poland and Czechoslovakia as insurance against German aggression in the east
Significance of Locarno

Locarno was significant because: (1) Germany was treated as an equal negotiating partner for the first time since Versailles; (2) it suggested Germany accepted the Versailles settlement in the west; (3) it paved the way for Germany joining the League of Nations; (4) it created a period of improved international relations. However, Germany's refusal to guarantee eastern borders meant Locarno did not provide complete security - Stresemann specifically left open the possibility of revising Germany's eastern borders (recovering territory lost to Poland).

Germany and the League of Nations (September 1926)

  • Germany joined the League of Nations in September 1926 as a permanent member of the Council
  • This gave Germany equal status with Britain and France in international affairs
  • It showed that the policy of "fulfilment" was working - by cooperating, Germany gained respect and influence
  • However, Germany was still unhappy about the disarmament clauses of Versailles - it was the only major power not allowed to rearm

The Kellogg-Briand Pact (August 1928)

  • 65 nations signed a pact to renounce war as an instrument of national policy
  • Germany was one of the first signatories, demonstrating its commitment to peace
  • However, the Pact had no enforcement mechanism and was essentially a statement of good intentions

🎭 Cultural Developments

Key Fact: The Weimar Republic (1919-1933) was one of the most creative periods in German cultural history. Berlin became one of the most exciting and cosmopolitan cities in Europe, known for its art, cinema, architecture, and nightlife.

Art and Design

Movement Key Details
Bauhaus Founded by Walter Gropius in Weimar in 1919; combined art, craft and technology; famous for functional, minimalist design; moved to Dessau in 1925; closed by Nazis in 1933
Expressionism Artists like Ernst Barlach and Käthe Kollwitz explored themes of suffering, war and social injustice; emotionally intense and often critical of traditional values
New Objectivity (Neue Sachlichkeit) Artists like Otto Dix and George Grosz painted harsh, realistic images of Weimar society - wounded veterans, prostitutes, corrupt businessmen

Cinema

  • Fritz Lang's "Metropolis" (1927): Groundbreaking science fiction film about a futuristic city divided between workers and elites; the most expensive silent film ever made
  • "The Cabinet of Dr Caligari" (1920): A horror film that used distorted sets to reflect psychological states; pioneered German Expressionist cinema
  • "The Blue Angel" (1930): Starring Marlene Dietrich, it was one of Germany's first successful sound films
  • By 1929, Germany was producing over 100 feature films per year
  • UFA studios in Berlin was one of the largest in Europe

Literature and Theatre

  • Erich Maria Remarque's "All Quiet on the Western Front" (1929): The most famous anti-war novel of the 20th century; sold 2.5 million copies in 18 months; the Nazis later burned it
  • Bertolt Brecht: Revolutionary playwright who developed "epic theatre" - plays like "The Threepenny Opera" (1928) challenged capitalist society
  • Thomas Mann: Won the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1929

Nightlife and "Degeneracy"

  • Berlin became famous for its cabaret, jazz clubs and liberal attitudes
  • Homosexuality was tolerated in a way that was unusual for the era
  • Women had new freedoms: they could vote, work in professions, and dress fashionably
  • Conservative Germans saw this as moral decay - the Nazis would later condemn it as "cultural Bolshevism"
Example: The "New Woman" of Weimar

Weimar Germany saw the emergence of the "Neue Frau" (New Woman) - independent, employed, fashionably dressed, with bobbed hair. Women made up 36% of the workforce by 1925. However, this image was mainly a big-city phenomenon; most German women in rural areas and small towns lived traditional lives. The "New Woman" was also controversial - conservatives saw her as a threat to traditional family values.

⚠️ How Golden Were the Golden Years?

Evidence It Was "Golden" Evidence It Was NOT "Golden"
Economy recovered: industrial output surpassed 1913 levels by 1928 Recovery depended on American loans - 25 billion marks borrowed between 1924-1929
Unemployment fell: from 2 million in 1926 to 1.4 million in 1928 Unemployment never fell below 1 million; agricultural workers suffered throughout
International respect: Locarno, League of Nations, Kellogg-Briand Pact Extremist parties still active: Nazis got 2.6% in 1928; Communists growing
Cultural flowering: Bauhaus, cinema, literature Cultural divide: conservatives hated "decadent" Weimar culture
Welfare state: improved housing, unemployment insurance (1927) Coalition governments still unstable; 6 different Chancellors 1924-1929
Big business and industrialists prospered Farmers were in debt; small shops lost ground to department stores
Key Judgement: The "Golden Years" were golden mainly for the urban middle class and big business. Farmers, agricultural workers, and those on fixed incomes continued to struggle. The recovery was built on fragile foundations - American loans that could be withdrawn at any time. When the Wall Street Crash happened in October 1929, just a month after Stresemann's death, the entire recovery collapsed.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two ways Stresemann improved Germany's international position. (4 marks)

Q2: How far did the Dawes Plan solve Germany's economic problems? (8 marks)

Q3: "The Locarno Pact was Stresemann's greatest achievement." How far do you agree? (12 marks)

Q4: Explain why Weimar culture was controversial. (8 marks)

Q5: "The years 1924-1929 were truly golden for the Weimar Republic." How far do you agree? (16 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. Through the Locarno Pact (October 1925), which improved relations with France and Britain by guaranteeing western borders, and by joining the League of Nations (September 1926) as a permanent Council member, which restored Germany to equal status in international affairs. Both achievements showed that the policy of "fulfilment" was working.
  2. The Dawes Plan helped by reducing annual reparations from £50m to a starting £10m, arranging 800 million gold marks in American loans, and leading to French withdrawal from the Ruhr. Industrial output recovered and employment rose. However, it did not solve all problems: Germany became dependent on American loans; reparations were still being paid; unemployment never fell below 1 million; and farmers continued to struggle. The Plan addressed symptoms but not the underlying fragility of depending on foreign credit.
  3. The Locarno Pact was a great achievement: it normalised relations with France, guaranteed western borders, led to Germany joining the League of Nations, and created the "Locarno Spring" of international goodwill. However, the Dawes Plan was arguably equally important because it provided the economic foundation for recovery. The Young Plan (1929) actually reduced total reparations. Locarno's weakness was that Germany refused to guarantee eastern borders, leaving the door open for future revision. Overall, Locarno was important diplomatically but the economic measures were equally crucial.
  4. Weimar culture was controversial because it challenged traditional values. Bauhaus and New Objectivity art were seen as ugly and "un-German" by conservatives. Cabaret, jazz and liberal attitudes towards sexuality offended traditional moral values. The "New Woman" threatened the traditional family model. Remarque's anti-war novel offended nationalists. Conservatives called it "cultural Bolshevism." The Nazis later condemned it as "degenerate art." The cultural divide between progressive cities and conservative rural areas showed that Weimar society was deeply split.
  5. The years 1924-1929 were golden in some respects: the economy recovered, Germany regained international respect, unemployment fell, and culture flourished. However, the recovery was fragile: it depended on American loans; unemployment never fell below 1 million; farmers suffered throughout; coalition governments remained unstable; extremist parties (Nazis 2.6% in 1928, Communists growing) never disappeared; and the cultural flowering divided rather than united society. The death of Stresemann in October 1929 and the Wall Street Crash a month later showed how shallow the recovery was. The "Golden Years" were golden for urban middle classes and big business, but not for all Germans.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know the exact dates: Dawes Plan August 1924, Locarno October 1925, League of Nations September 1926, Young Plan August 1929
  • For "how far" questions about the Golden Years, always give BOTH sides before making a judgement
  • Stresemann's death (3 October 1929) and the Wall Street Crash (29 October 1929) happened in the same month - this is a crucial turning point
  • The key criticism of the recovery is DEPENDENCE ON AMERICAN LOANS - 25 billion marks borrowed 1924-1929
  • When discussing culture, always link it to political division - conservatives hated Weimar culture
  • Remember that Stresemann was Foreign Secretary, not Chancellor, for most of this period (only Chancellor for 102 days)
  • The Locarno Pact did NOT guarantee eastern borders - Stresemann deliberately left this open

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally. Wrong: Hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally Correct: People with savings and fixed incomes suffered most, but those with debts or land actually benefited. Foreign tourists could buy goods very cheaply.

Students often think the nazis won a majority in a free election. Wrong: The Nazis won a majority in a free election Correct: The Nazis never won over 50% in a free election — their best result was 37.3% in July 1932. Hitler became Chancellor through backroom politics, not a majority vote.

Students often think all germans supported the nazis. Wrong: All Germans supported the Nazis Correct: There was significant opposition including the White Rose group, Edelweiss Pirates, and the 20 July Plot. Many Germans were passive rather than active supporters.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: How far did Stresemann restore stability 1924-29?

Stresemann restored significant economic stability. As Chancellor in August 1923, he ended passive resistance in the Ruhr and introduced the Rentenmark (November 1923), replacing the worthless mark at 1 Rentenmark = 1 trillion old marks, backed by land and industrial assets. The Dawes Plan (August 1924), negotiated by American banker Charles Dawes, reduced annual reparations from £50 million to a starting £10 million and arranged 800 million gold marks in American loans. French and Belgian troops withdrew from the Ruhr in August 1925. Industrial output recovered, and by 1928 Germany produced the same as all the British Empire combined.

Diplomatically, Stresemann achieved remarkable results. The Locarno Pact (October 1925) guaranteed Germany's western borders and created the "Locarno Spring" of international goodwill. Germany joined the League of Nations in September 1926 as a permanent Council member, restoring equal status. The Young Plan (August 1929) reduced total reparations from £6.6 billion to £2 billion and extended the payment period to 59 years. Stresemann was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 1926 alongside French Foreign Minister Aristide Briand.

However, the recovery had critical weaknesses. Germany borrowed 25 billion marks from American banks between 1924 and 1929 — the entire recovery was dependent on US loans that could be withdrawn at any time. Unemployment never fell below 1 million; farmers were in debt throughout. Coalition governments remained unstable, with 6 different Chancellors between 1924 and 1929. Extremist parties persisted — the Nazis won 2.6% in 1928. When the Wall Street Crash struck in October 1929, just a month after Stresemann's death on 3 October, the entire recovery collapsed. In conclusion, Stresemann restored stability in the short term, but the foundations were fragile — the "Golden Years" were golden only for the urban middle class, and built on American credit rather than genuine strength.

Mark scheme: 3-4 marks per explained factor with specific evidence, balanced judgement for top level
