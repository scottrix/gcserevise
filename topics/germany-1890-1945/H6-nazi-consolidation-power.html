H6: Nazi Consolidation of Power

Foundation Higher All Boards

The Reichstag Fire, Enabling Act, banning of parties and trade unions, Night of the Long Knives, and the creation of the Fuhrer state.

🔥 The Reichstag Fire (27 February 1933)

Key Fact: On 27 February 1933, the Reichstag building in Berlin was set on fire. Marinus van der Lubbe, a Dutch Communist, was found at the scene and arrested. Hitler used the fire to persuade Hindenburg to issue the Decree for the Protection of the People and State on 28 February 1933, which suspended all civil liberties.

Events

  • 27 February 1933: The Reichstag was set ablaze; van der Lubbe was arrested inside the building
  • 28 February 1933: Hindenburg signed the Decree for the Protection of the People and State (the "Reichstag Fire Decree")
  • Hitler claimed the fire was the start of a Communist revolution
  • 4,000 Communists were arrested in the following days, including KPD leader Ernst Thälmann
  • Van der Lubbe was executed in January 1934; there is still debate about whether he acted alone

The Reichstag Fire Decree

What the Decree Suspended:
1. Freedom of speech
2. Freedom of the press
3. Freedom of assembly
4. Privacy of post and telephone
5. Habeas corpus (right to a fair trial)
This gave the Nazis legal authority to arrest anyone without trial, ban opposition meetings, and censor newspapers.
Significance of the Reichstag Fire

The Reichstag Fire was crucial because it allowed Hitler to: (1) arrest 4,000 Communists, removing the KPD as an electoral threat; (2) campaign for the March 1933 election in an atmosphere of fear, with SA violence now legally sanctioned; (3) establish the precedent of ruling by decree rather than through parliament. Whether the Nazis started the fire themselves remains debated by historians, but its political usefulness to Hitler is beyond question.

🗳️ The March 1933 Election

Key Fact: In the March 1933 election, the Nazis won 43.9% of the vote (288 seats). This was not a majority, but combined with the DNVP's 8% (52 seats), Hitler had a majority in the Reichstag. The election was held in an atmosphere of terror - SA thugs patrolled the streets, opposition meetings were banned, and the Communist Party was effectively illegal.
  • The KPD won 81 seats but these were all annulled - Communist deputies were arrested or in hiding
  • SPD leader Otto Wels was the only person to speak against the Enabling Act in the Reichstag
  • The SA violently disrupted opposition campaigning; 69 people were killed in political violence during the campaign
  • Goebbels controlled radio broadcasting, giving the Nazis a huge media advantage
  • Despite all these advantages, the Nazis still only won 43.9% - showing that a majority of Germans had NOT freely chosen Nazism

📜 The Enabling Act (23 March 1933)

Key Fact: The Enabling Act (Law to Remedy the Distress of the People and the Reich), passed on 23 March 1933, gave Hitler the power to make laws without the Reichstag for four years. This effectively made Hitler a legal dictator. It required a two-thirds majority, which Hitler achieved by arresting Communists and intimidating the remaining deputies.

How Hitler Passed the Enabling Act

  • Communist deputies were prevented from taking their seats (arrested under the Reichstag Fire Decree)
  • SA troops surrounded the Kroll Opera House (where the Reichstag was meeting) and intimidated deputies as they entered
  • The Centre Party was persuaded to support the Act after Hitler promised to respect Catholic rights (a promise he later broke)
  • Only the SPD voted against (94 votes); 444 voted in favour
  • The Act needed a two-thirds majority: with Communists excluded, this was achieved
What the Enabling Act Allowed:
Hitler could make laws WITHOUT the Reichstag for 4 years. These laws could even deviate from the Constitution. Only the President's veto remained - but Hindenburg was old and compliant. The Act was renewed in 1938 and 1943. This single law destroyed the Weimar Constitution and created the legal framework for the dictatorship.
Example: SPD Opposition

SPD leader Otto Wels was the only deputy to speak against the Enabling Act. He declared: "You can take our lives and our freedom, but you cannot take our honour." The entire SPD group voted against the Act. Within months, the SPD was banned, its assets seized, and its leaders arrested or forced into exile. Wels himself fled to Paris. This was the last free vote in the Reichstag until 1949.

🚫 Banning Parties and Trade Unions

The Elimination of Opposition (March-July 1933)

Date Action Effect
March 1933 KPD effectively banned (deputies arrested) Communist opposition eliminated
2 May 1933 Trade unions abolished; leaders arrested Workers' organisations destroyed; replaced by German Labour Front (DAF)
10 May 1933 SPD offices seized; assets confiscated Social Democratic opposition ended
22 June 1933 SPD formally banned Last major left-wing party gone
June-July 1933 Other parties "voluntarily" dissolved (DVP, DDP, BVP, DNVP) Only the Nazi Party remained
14 July 1933 Law Against the Formation of New Parties Nazi Party declared the only legal party in Germany
Key Fact: On 14 July 1933, the Law Against the Formation of New Parties made the NSDAP the only legal political party in Germany. Attempting to form or maintain another party became a criminal offence. Germany was now a one-party state.

🔪 The Night of the Long Knives (29-30 June 1934)

Key Fact: On 29-30 June 1934, Hitler ordered the SS to murder the leadership of the SA, including its head Ernst Röhm. At least 85 people were killed, including former Chancellor von Schleicher and other conservative critics. The SA's power was broken and the SS (under Himmler) became the dominant force in Nazi terror.

Causes

  • The SA threat: By 1934, the SA had grown to over 3 million members. Röhm wanted the SA to absorb the army and become the new German military. The army (only 100,000 strong) was alarmed
  • Röhm's ambitions: Röhm wanted a "second revolution" - after political revolution, a social revolution with the SA as the core of a people's army
  • Army support: Hitler needed the army's support to become President when Hindenburg died. The army would only support him if he destroyed the SA
  • SS rivalry: Himmler and Heydrich (SS leaders) wanted to eliminate the SA as a rival power base
  • Rumours: Röhm's homosexuality was used as propaganda to discredit him and the SA

Events

  • Hitler flew to Munich and personally arrested Röhm and other SA leaders at a hotel in Bad Wiessee
  • The SS execution squads killed SA leaders across Germany over 2-3 days
  • Röhm was offered the chance to commit suicide; when he refused, he was shot
  • Other victims included: Gregor Strasser (Nazi left-winger), General von Schleicher (former Chancellor), Gustav von Kahr (who had blocked the 1923 Munich Putsch)
  • On 3 July 1934, the cabinet retroactively legalised the murders as "necessary for the defence of the state"
Significance of the Night of the Long Knives

The Night of the Long Knives was significant because: (1) it destroyed the SA as a political force, removing any threat of a "second revolution"; (2) it secured the army's support for Hitler; (3) it showed that Hitler was willing to murder even old comrades to maintain power; (4) it established the SS as the main instrument of Nazi terror; (5) it demonstrated that the rule of law had been completely replaced by the Fuhrer's will - the cabinet retroactively legalised murder. After this event, no one in Germany dared challenge Hitler.

👑 The Fuhrer State

Hindenburg's Death and the Final Step (August 1934)

  • 2 August 1934: President Hindenburg died aged 86
  • Within hours, Hitler announced that the offices of President and Chancellor would be combined
  • He took the title "Fuhrer und Reichskanzler" (Leader and Chancellor)
  • Every soldier in the army was required to swear a personal oath of loyalty to Hitler - not to the state or the constitution, but to Hitler personally
  • A plebiscite on 19 August 1934 gave Hitler 90% approval - though the vote was not free or fair
Key Fact: By August 1934, Hitler had achieved total power: the Reichstag was powerless; all other parties were banned; trade unions were abolished; the SA was neutered; the army had sworn personal loyalty; and he combined the roles of President and Chancellor. Germany was now a totalitarian dictatorship.

Timeline to Total Power

Date Event Power Gained
30 Jan 1933 Appointed Chancellor Control of government

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain how the Reichstag Fire helped Hitler consolidate power. (4 marks)

Q2: How important was the Enabling Act in the establishment of the Nazi dictatorship? (8 marks)

Q3: "The Night of the Long Knives was the most important event in Hitler's consolidation of power." How far do you agree? (12 marks)

Q4: Explain why Hitler was able to ban all political parties by July 1933. (8 marks)

Q5: "Hitler consolidated power through legal means rather than violence." How far do you agree? (16 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. The Reichstag Fire (27 February 1933) allowed Hitler to persuade Hindenburg to issue the Reichstag Fire Decree (28 February), which suspended civil liberties including freedom of speech, assembly, and the press. This gave the Nazis legal authority to arrest 4,000 Communists, ban opposition meetings, and use SA violence freely. It created an atmosphere of fear before the March 1933 election, helping the Nazis win 43.9% of the vote.
  2. The Enabling Act (23 March 1933) was critically important because it gave Hitler the power to make laws without the Reichstag for four years. This single law destroyed the Weimar Constitution and created the legal basis for dictatorship. Without it, Hitler could not have banned parties, abolished trade unions, or created the one-party state. However, it was not sufficient on its own - the Reichstag Fire Decree had already suspended civil liberties, and the Night of the Long Knives was needed to eliminate internal threats. The Enabling Act was the legal cornerstone of the dictatorship but required other measures to make it complete.
  3. The Night of the Long Knives was very important because: it destroyed the SA as a rival power base; it secured the army's support for Hitler (crucial for becoming Fuhrer); it eliminated potential opposition within the Nazi movement itself; and it showed that Hitler ruled by terror. However, other events were equally or more important: the Reichstag Fire gave Hitler emergency powers; the Enabling Act gave him legal dictatorship; and the banning of parties eliminated all organised opposition. The Night of the Long Knives was essential for securing the army's backing, but without the Enabling Act, Hitler could not have consolidated power at all.
  4. Hitler was able to ban all parties by July 1933 because: the Reichstag Fire Decree (28 February) suspended civil liberties, allowing the arrest of opponents; the Enabling Act (23 March) gave Hitler the power to make laws without the Reichstag; the KPD was effectively destroyed first (March 1933); trade unions were abolished on 2 May, removing workers' ability to organise; the SPD was formally banned on 22 June; and remaining parties "voluntarily" dissolved under pressure. The Law Against New Parties (14 July) completed the process. Each step was made possible by the powers gained from the previous one.
  5. Hitler used a combination of legal and violent methods. Legal methods included: the Reichstag Fire Decree (signed by Hindenburg); the Enabling Act (passed by the Reichstag); laws banning parties and trade unions; and the plebiscite of August 1934. These gave the dictatorship a veneer of legality. However, violence was equally important: the SA terrorised voters and opponents; the Reichstag Fire was exploited to justify emergency powers; the Night of the Long Knives used murder to eliminate threats; and the constant threat of arrest by the SS/Gestapo kept the population compliant. Hitler's genius was using legal methods to authorise violence - the Decree and Enabling Act made SA and SS violence state-sanctioned. Both were essential; neither alone would have been sufficient.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know the exact sequence: Fire (27 Feb) → Decree (28 Feb) → Election (5 Mar) → Enabling Act (23 Mar) → Unions banned (2 May) → Parties banned (14 Jul) → Night of Long Knives (30 Jun) → Fuhrer (2 Aug)
  • The Enabling Act was passed using LEGAL methods (vote in the Reichstag) but under ILLEGAL conditions (Communists excluded, SA intimidation)
  • The Night of the Long Knives was RETROACTIVELY legalised - this shows the complete absence of rule of law
  • Always distinguish between consolidation of power (1933-34) and the RISE to power (1929-33) - these are different topics
  • The army oath (2 August 1934) was crucial - it meant the army was bound to Hitler personally, not to the state
  • The Nazis never won a majority in a free election - Hitler consolidated power by destroying democracy from within

📝 Exam Technique

History Exam Tips:
Always use specific dates and statistics. For 'explain why' questions, identify multiple causes and link them. For consequence questions, distinguish between political, economic and social impacts. Know the chronological narrative from Kaiser to Fuhrer.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally. Wrong: Hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally Correct: People with savings and fixed incomes suffered most, but those with debts or land actually benefited. Foreign tourists could buy goods very cheaply.

Students often think the nazis won a majority in a free election. Wrong: The Nazis won a majority in a free election Correct: The Nazis never won over 50% in a free election — their best result was 37.3% in July 1932. Hitler became Chancellor through backroom politics, not a majority vote.

Students often think all germans supported the nazis. Wrong: All Germans supported the Nazis Correct: There was significant opposition including the White Rose group, Edelweiss Pirates, and the 20 July Plot. Many Germans were passive rather than active supporters.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

8 marks: Explain how Hitler consolidated power Jan 1933-Aug 1934.

Hitler consolidated power through a sequence of events that systematically destroyed democracy. On 27 February 1933, the Reichstag Fire gave Hitler the pretext to persuade Hindenburg to sign the Reichstag Fire Decree (28 February), which suspended all civil liberties — freedom of speech, press, assembly, and habeas corpus. This allowed the arrest of 4,000 Communists and legalised SA violence before the March 1933 election, where the Nazis won 43.9% of the vote.

The Enabling Act (23 March 1933) was the legal cornerstone of the dictatorship. It gave Hitler the power to make laws without the Reichstag for four years. Hitler passed it by arresting Communist deputies and using SA intimidation at the Kroll Opera House; only the SPD voted against (94 votes). The Act destroyed the Weimar Constitution. By 14 July 1933, the Law Against the Formation of New Parties made the NSDAP the only legal party — Germany was a one-party state.

The Night of the Long Knives (29-30 June 1934) removed internal threats. Hitler ordered the SS to murder SA leader Ernst Röhm and at least 85 others, including former Chancellor von Schleicher. This secured the army's support — Röhm had wanted the SA to absorb the army. On 2 August 1934, when Hindenburg died, Hitler combined the offices of President and Chancellor, taking the title "Fuhrer und Reichskanzler." Every soldier swore a personal oath of loyalty to Hitler. By August 1934, Germany was a totalitarian dictatorship: the Reichstag was powerless, all parties banned, the SA neutered, and the army bound to Hitler personally.

Mark scheme: 2 marks per explained step with specific dates and evidence, clear narrative for top level
