H7: Nazi Economic Policies

Foundation Higher All Boards

How the Nazis reduced unemployment from 6 million to under 500,000 through the RAD, public works, rearmament, and the German Labour Front programmes.

📉 The Unemployment Problem

Key Fact: When Hitler became Chancellor in January 1933, over 6 million Germans were unemployed - nearly 30% of the workforce. By 1939, official unemployment was under 500,000. The Nazis claimed to have solved the unemployment crisis, but the reality was more complex.

🛤️ Public Works Programmes

Key Fact: The Nazis launched massive public works programmes, most famously the autobahn (motorway) network. Between 1933 and 1938, over 3,000 km of autobahn were built. However, the autobahn programme was originally proposed by the Weimar government - the Nazis simply took credit for it.

The Autobahns

  • Hitler appointed Fritz Todt as Inspector General for German Roadways in 1933
  • The first autobahn (Frankfurt-Darmstadt) was opened in May 1935
  • By 1938, over 3,000 km had been completed, employing up to 150,000 workers directly
  • Thousands more were employed in supplying materials - cement, steel, machinery
  • Propaganda heavily promoted the autobahns as a symbol of the "New Germany"
  • However, most Germans did not own cars - the motorways were also designed for military transport

Other Public Works

  • Housing: Over 1.4 million homes were built between 1933-1937
  • Reichsarbeitsdienst (RAD): The National Labour Service employed men on land reclamation, drainage, and forestry projects
  • Dams and canals: Major infrastructure projects like the Harz Mountains dam system
  • Olympic buildings: The 1936 Berlin Olympics stimulated construction - the Olympic Stadium, swimming pool, and athletes' village
Example: The Autobahn and Propaganda

The autobahns were presented as Hitler's personal achievement. Posters showed Hitler breaking ground for new motorways. Films celebrated the engineering. However, the programme employed far fewer workers than the Nazis claimed - at its peak, only about 150,000 worked on the autobahns in a country of 40 million workers. The real economic driver was rearmament, not road-building.

🏭 Rearmament

Key Fact: Rearmament was the single biggest factor in reducing unemployment. Government spending on arms rose from 3.5 billion marks in 1933 to 30 billion marks by 1939. By 1939, 40% of government expenditure was on the military.

Key Rearmament Steps

  • March 1935: Hitler publicly announced German rearmament, violating the Treaty of Versailles. Conscription introduced - army expanded to 550,000 immediately
  • 1935-1939: The army grew from 100,000 to 1.4 million men. The Luftwaffe (air force) was created from nothing - by 1939, it had 8,000 aircraft
  • Krupp and IG Farben: Major industrial companies received massive government contracts for weapons, chemicals, and military equipment
  • Steel production: Rose from 5.6 million tonnes (1933) to 22.7 million tonnes (1939)
  • The Four Year Plan (1936): Göring was put in charge of making Germany ready for war within 4 years - achieving autarky (self-sufficiency) and expanding arms production
The Real Reason Unemployment Fell:
Rearmament was far more important than public works in reducing unemployment. By 1939, over 50% of all government spending went to the military. Hundreds of thousands of young men were removed from the workforce by conscription. Arms factories employed millions. The autobahns were propaganda; rearmament was reality.

⛏️ The Reichsarbeitsdienst (RAD)

Key Fact: The National Labour Service (RAD) was made compulsory for all men aged 18-25 in June 1935. They had to serve 6 months doing manual labour - building roads, draining marshes, planting forests, and working on farms. They wore military-style uniforms and lived in camps.

The RAD in Practice

  • Workers were paid just 25 marks per week (well below normal wages)
  • They wore military uniforms and were subject to military discipline
  • The work was physically demanding - drainage, forestry, land reclamation
  • By 1935, over 500,000 young men were in the RAD at any one time
  • The RAD removed young men from unemployment statistics and prepared them for military discipline
  • From 1938, women were also required to do a "Pflichtjahr" (duty year) of farm or domestic work
Example: How the RAD Manipulated Statistics

The RAD helped reduce the unemployment figures in two ways: (1) the young men in the RAD were counted as employed, not unemployed; (2) by removing young men from the labour market, there were fewer people competing for jobs. This was not genuine employment - it was a transitional stage between unemployment and military service. The RAD was essentially pre-military training dressed up as public service.

💪 Strength Through Joy (KdF)

Key Fact: The Kraft durch Freude (KdF) programme was set up in November 1933 as part of the German Labour Front (DAF). It provided leisure activities for workers - cruises, holidays, trips to the theatre, and sport. By 1938, over 10 million people had taken KdF holidays.

KdF Activities

Activity Detail
KdF cruises Subsidised cruise holidays to Norway, Italy, and Madeira; prices as low as 62 marks for a week's cruise. In 1938, 180,000 went on KdF cruises
KdF holidays Package holidays within Germany; walking trips and ski trips at affordable prices
KdF car The Volkswagen ("people's car") was promised at 990 marks - workers paid 5 marks per week into a savings scheme. However, no cars were actually delivered to savers before the war; the factory was converted to military production
Cultural activities Theatre trips, concerts, and cinema visits at reduced prices; 43 million KdF theatre tickets sold by 1939

Example: The KdF Cruise Ship

The KdF operated its own cruise ship, the "Wilhelm Gustloff," which took workers on Mediterranean and Scandinavian cruises. Workers from different classes were treated equally on board - no first class or second class. This was deliberately designed to show that the Nazi state valued workers and broke down class barriers. However, critics noted that the cruises were heavily supervised and served as propaganda opportunities.

🌸 Beauty of Labour (Schönheit der Arbeit)

Key Fact: The Beauty of Labour division of the DAF was established in 1934 to improve working conditions. It pressured employers to provide better facilities: washrooms, canteens, rest areas, and sports facilities. Between 1936-1939, it claimed to have improved 60,000 workplaces.
  • The SdA was not voluntary for employers - it could name and shame companies that refused to comply
  • It provided new canteens, washrooms, and sports facilities in factories
  • It organised factory beauty competitions and "Clean Factory" campaigns
  • However, it did NOT address real workers' concerns: wages, hours, or the right to strike
  • Trade unions had been abolished in May 1933 - workers could not collectively bargain
  • Workers could not complain about pay or conditions - the DAF represented the state, not the workers

Key Fact: Real wages (adjusted for inflation) were only slightly higher in 1939 than in 1928 - and in some industries, they were lower. Workers also lost basic rights: they could not strike, could not change jobs without government permission (labour books from 1935), and had no independent representation. The German Labour Front (DAF) was a state organisation, not a workers' union.
The Nazi Economic Miracle - Myth vs Reality:
MYTH: Hitler solved unemployment and made Germany prosperous
REALITY: Unemployment was reduced primarily by rearmament and removing people from statistics. Workers lost rights and saw limited real wage improvement. The economy was built on war preparation and unsustainable government debt. By 1939, Germany had a balance of payments crisis and shortages of raw materials.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two ways the Nazis reduced unemployment. (4 marks)

Q2: How important was rearmament in reducing German unemployment? (8 marks)

Q3: "Nazi economic policies benefited German workers." How far do you agree? (12 marks)

Q4: Explain the purpose and impact of the Strength Through Joy programme. (8 marks)

Q5: "The Nazis created an economic miracle." How far do you agree? (16 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. Rearmament: conscription from March 1935 removed young men from the workforce, and arms factories employed millions. The RAD (National Labour Service): made compulsory in June 1935, it placed young men in work camps doing manual labour, removing them from unemployment figures.
  2. Rearmament was the most important factor in reducing unemployment. Government spending on arms rose from 3.5 billion marks (1933) to 30 billion marks (1939). Conscription removed hundreds of thousands of young men from the workforce. Arms factories employed millions directly and in supply industries. By 1939, 40% of government spending was military. However, other factors also contributed: public works (autobahns) created some jobs; the RAD removed young men from statistics; and the removal of Jews and women from the workforce also lowered official figures. Rearmament was the single biggest factor but it was not the only one.
  3. Nazi economic policies benefited workers in some ways: unemployment fell dramatically; KdF provided affordable leisure; Beauty of Labour improved conditions; and the sense of national recovery gave hope and pride. However, workers also lost fundamental rights: trade unions were abolished; the right to strike was removed; wages remained low in real terms; working hours increased from 43 to 49 per week; labour books restricted job changes; and the economy was geared towards war, not welfare. The KdF car scheme took workers' savings without delivering cars. Overall, workers gained employment but lost freedom and representation - the benefits were real but limited, while the losses were fundamental.
  4. The Strength Through Joy (KdF) programme provided subsidised leisure activities for workers: cruises (180,000 participants in 1938), package holidays, theatre tickets (43 million sold by 1939), and sports. Its purpose was to improve worker satisfaction and loyalty to the regime, and to show that the Nazi state valued workers. However, it also served as propaganda - cruises were supervised and promoted Nazi ideology. The KdF car (Volkswagen) scheme took savings from workers but delivered no cars before the war. KdF improved workers' leisure but did not address their real concerns about wages, hours, and rights.
  5. The Nazis did NOT create an economic miracle. Unemployment fell from 6 million to under 500,000, but this was achieved through: rearmament (the biggest factor), removing people from statistics (women, Jews, political opponents), compulsory RAD service, and conscription. Workers lost rights - no unions, no strikes, restricted job mobility. Real wages barely improved. The economy was built on unsustainable military spending; by 1939, Germany faced a balance of payments crisis and raw material shortages. The KdF and Beauty of Labour were propaganda designed to mask the reality of workers' lost rights. The apparent miracle was achieved by preparing for war, not by creating genuine prosperity.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always question the unemployment statistics - they were manipulated by excluding women, Jews, and political opponents
  • Rearmament was MORE important than public works (like autobahns) in reducing unemployment
  • The KdF car (Volkswagen) is a great example of Nazi broken promises - workers saved but never received cars
  • For "did workers benefit" questions, always balance benefits (jobs, leisure) against losses (rights, unions, wages)
  • Remember: the DAF was NOT a trade union - it was a state organisation that represented the regime, not the workers
  • Working hours INCREASED under the Nazis (43 to 49 per week) - workers traded freedom for employment
  • The Four Year Plan (1936) under Göring aimed at autarky (self-sufficiency) - this is important for showing the economy was being prepared for war

📝 Exam Technique

History Exam Tips:
Always use specific dates and statistics. For 'explain why' questions, identify multiple causes and link them. For consequence questions, distinguish between political, economic and social impacts. Know the chronological narrative from Kaiser to Fuhrer.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally. Wrong: Hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally Correct: People with savings and fixed incomes suffered most, but those with debts or land actually benefited. Foreign tourists could buy goods very cheaply.

Students often think the nazis won a majority in a free election. Wrong: The Nazis won a majority in a free election Correct: The Nazis never won over 50% in a free election — their best result was 37.3% in July 1932. Hitler became Chancellor through backroom politics, not a majority vote.

Students often think all germans supported the nazis. Wrong: All Germans supported the Nazis Correct: There was significant opposition including the White Rose group, Edelweiss Pirates, and the 20 July Plot. Many Germans were passive rather than active supporters.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: How far did the Nazis improve workers' lives?

The Nazis did improve workers' lives in some respects. Unemployment fell dramatically from 6 million in January 1933 to under 500,000 by 1939. The Strength Through Joy (KdF) programme, set up in November 1933, provided subsidised leisure: 180,000 workers went on KdF cruises in 1938, 43 million theatre tickets were sold by 1939, and 7 million participated in KdF sports. The Beauty of Labour (SdA) division, established in 1934, pressured employers to improve conditions — it claimed to have improved 60,000 workplaces between 1936 and 1939, providing new canteens, washrooms, and sports facilities. The autobahn programme employed up to 150,000 workers directly, and over 1.4 million homes were built between 1933 and 1937.

However, workers lost fundamental rights and saw limited real wage improvement. Trade unions were abolished on 2 May 1933; the right to strike was removed; the German Labour Front (DAF) was a state organisation, not a workers' union. Workers could not change jobs without government permission — labour books were introduced in 1935. The average working week rose from 43 hours (1933) to 49 hours (1939). Real wages, adjusted for inflation, were only slightly higher in 1939 than in 1928. The KdF car (Volkswagen) scheme was a scam: workers paid 5 marks per week into a savings scheme, but no cars were delivered before the war — the factory was converted to military production. Unemployment figures were manipulated: women, Jews, and political opponents were removed from the statistics.

In conclusion, the Nazis gave workers employment and leisure but took away their freedom and representation. The improvements were real but limited; the losses were fundamental. Unemployment fell primarily through rearmament — government spending on arms rose from 3.5 billion marks (1933) to 30 billion marks (1939) — not through genuine economic miracle. Workers traded rights for jobs and propaganda for prosperity.

Mark scheme: 3-4 marks per explained factor with specific evidence, balanced judgement for top level
