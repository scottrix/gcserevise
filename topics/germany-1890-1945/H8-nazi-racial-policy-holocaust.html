H8: Nazi Racial Policy and the Holocaust

Foundation Higher All Boards

Nazi racial ideology, the persecution of the Jews from boycott to genocide, the Wannsee Conference, the Final Solution, death toll, and resistance.

🧬 Nazi Racial Ideology

Key Fact: The Nazis believed in a racial hierarchy with the "Aryan" race (tall, blonde, blue-eyed Germans and Nordics) at the top as the "master race" (Herrenvolk). At the bottom were "sub-humans" (Untermenschen) - Jews, Roma, Slavs, and black people. Hitler wrote in Mein Kampf (1925): "The Jew is and remains the eternal enemy of the Aryan."

Key Elements of Nazi Racial Belief

  • Aryan supremacy: Germans were the purest Aryans, destined to rule. Other Aryans included Scandinavians, Dutch, and English
  • Anti-Semitism: Jews were seen as an alien race conspiring to destroy Aryan civilisation. Hitler blamed them for Germany's defeat in WWI, the Weimar Republic, communism, and capitalism
  • Social Darwinism: Races were in a struggle for survival; the strong must dominate or be destroyed by the weak
  • Lebensraum: The Aryan race needed "living space" in eastern Europe, which required the removal of Slavic peoples
  • Eugenics: The German race must be "purified" by removing the "unfit" - the disabled, mentally ill, and genetically "impure"

1941-42: Mass shootings and death camps →
1942-45: The Final Solution

🏪 Early Persecution (1933-1935)

Key Fact: Persecution of Jews began immediately after Hitler became Chancellor. On 1 April 1933, the Nazis organised a nationwide boycott of Jewish shops and businesses. SA men stood outside Jewish shops with signs reading "Germans! Defend yourselves! Don't buy from Jews!"

Key Anti-Jewish Measures 1933-1935

Date Measure Impact
1 April 1933 National boycott of Jewish shops SA intimidation; many shops damaged; some Germans ignored the boycott
7 April 1933 Civil Service Law Jews expelled from government jobs, teaching, and the judiciary
10 May 1933 Book burnings Books by Jewish and "degenerate" authors burned publicly
July 1933 Law for Revocation of Citizenship Naturalised Jews from Eastern Europe stripped of German citizenship
September 1933 Reich Chamber of Culture Jews excluded from arts, music, literature, and journalism
1934 Increasing local bans Jews banned from parks, swimming pools, public transport in some areas
Example: The 1933 Boycott

The April 1933 boycott was not a complete success. Some Germans continued to shop at Jewish stores, and foreign press coverage was negative. However, it marked the beginning of systematic persecution and signalled to the Jewish community that the Nazi state was their enemy. Within weeks, the Civil Service Law removed Jews from their professions, causing immediate economic hardship for the 500,000 Jews living in Germany (0.77% of the population).

⚖️ The Nuremberg Laws (September 1935)

Key Fact: At the Nuremberg Rally on 15 September 1935, Hitler announced two laws that fundamentally changed the legal status of German Jews: the Reich Citizenship Law and the Law for the Protection of German Blood and Honour. These laws stripped Jews of citizenship and banned marriages between Jews and Germans.

The Two Laws

Law Key Provisions
Reich Citizenship Law Jews were no longer German citizens - they became "state subjects" (Staatsangehörige) with no political rights. Only those of "German or related blood" could be citizens
Law for the Protection of German Blood and Honour Banned marriages and sexual relationships between Jews and Germans. Forbidden to employ German women under 45 in Jewish households

Who Was Defined as Jewish?

  • Full Jew: Anyone with 3 or more Jewish grandparents, or who practised Judaism
  • Mischling (half-caste) 1st degree: Two Jewish grandparents - treated as Jewish if practising Judaism or married to a Jew
  • Mischling 2nd degree: One Jewish grandparent - subject to some restrictions but not the full force of persecution
Key Fact: The Nuremberg Laws were a turning point because they made anti-Semitism legal and official. Before 1935, persecution had been through SA violence and local regulations; after 1935, it was enshrined in national law. This gave persecution the authority of the state and made it systematic.

💔 Kristallnacht (9-10 November 1938)

Key Fact: On the night of 9-10 November 1938, Nazi mobs attacked Jewish property across Germany in a organised pogrom called Kristallnacht ("Night of Broken Glass"). 191 synagogues were set on fire, over 7,000 Jewish shops were destroyed, 91 Jews were killed, and 20,000-30,000 Jewish men were arrested and sent to concentration camps.

Trigger

  • On 7 November 1938, a Polish Jewish teenager, Herschel Grynszpan, shot and killed a German diplomat, Ernst vom Rath, in Paris
  • Grynszpan was protesting the deportation of his parents (and 12,000 other Polish Jews) from Germany to Poland
  • Goebbels used the assassination as a pretext to organise a "spontaneous" pogrom
  • Hitler authorised Goebbels to let the attacks proceed

Events

  • SA and SS men in civilian clothes attacked Jewish property across Germany
  • 191 synagogues were burned; fire brigades were ordered to protect neighbouring buildings, not the synagogues
  • Over 7,000 Jewish businesses had windows smashed and contents looted
  • 91 Jews were murdered; hundreds more injured; many committed suicide
  • 20,000-30,000 Jewish men were arrested and sent to Dachau, Buchenwald, and Sachsenhausen concentration camps

Aftermath

  • The Jewish community was fined 1 billion reichsmarks for "provoking" the violence
  • Jews had to pay for all repairs to their own property; insurance claims were confiscated by the state
  • Jews were excluded from schools, theatres, cinemas, and public spaces
  • The events of Kristallnacht convinced many Jews that they had no future in Germany - emigration accelerated
Significance of Kristallnacht

Kristallnacht marked the shift from legal persecution to open violence. After 1938, it was clear that the Nazi state would not just discriminate against Jews but would actively destroy them. Many ordinary Germans were shocked by the violence, but few spoke out. Those who did were threatened into silence. The event showed that there were no limits to what the Nazi state would do to Jews. After Kristallnacht, the remaining Jews in Germany were desperate to emigrate, but other countries were reluctant to accept them.

🏚️ Ghettos (1939-1941)

Key Fact: After Germany invaded Poland in September 1939, 2 million more Jews came under Nazi control. The Nazis forced Polish Jews into sealed, overcrowded areas called ghettos. The largest was the Warsaw Ghetto, where over 400,000 Jews were confined in an area of just 3.4 square km.

Conditions in the Ghettos

  • Starvation: Rations were deliberately set below survival levels - in Warsaw, official rations provided just 200 calories per day (compared to 2,600 for Germans)
  • Disease: Overcrowding and lack of sanitation caused typhus and tuberculosis epidemics. In the Warsaw Ghetto, 5,000 people died per month by 1941
  • Overcrowding: In Warsaw, 400,000 people in 3.4 sq km - an average of 7 people per room
  • Child smuggling: Children smuggled food through gaps in the walls; if caught, they were shot
  • Jewish councils (Judenräte): The Nazis forced Jewish leaders to administer the ghettos, creating moral dilemmas about cooperation

📋 The Wannsee Conference (20 January 1942)

Key Fact: On 20 January 1942, 15 senior Nazi officials met at a villa in Wannsee, a suburb of Berlin, to coordinate the "Final Solution to the Jewish Question." The conference was chaired by Reinhard Heydrich and organised by Adolf Eichmann. It formalised the systematic genocide of European Jews.

What Was Decided

  • The "Final Solution" would involve the extermination of all 11 million Jews in Europe
  • Jews would be transported to extermination camps in occupied Poland
  • The SS would manage the logistics; Eichmann would coordinate transport
  • Definition of "Jew" was clarified for occupied territories
  • Some Jews would be worked to death first (the "annihilation through labour" programme)
Example: The Wannsee Protocol

The minutes of the Wannsee Conference (the "Wannsee Protocol") used bureaucratic, euphemistic language to describe mass murder. Jews would be "evacuated to the East" and "treated accordingly." The conference was not about whether to kill the Jews - that decision had already been made. It was about logistics: how to coordinate the transport and extermination efficiently across all of occupied Europe.

💀 The Final Solution

Key Fact: The Final Solution (Endlösung) was the systematic, industrialised murder of European Jews. Six extermination camps were built in occupied Poland: Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec, Majdanek, and Chelmno. By the end of 1944, approximately 6 million Jews had been murdered.

The Death Camps

Camp Operation Period Estimated Deaths
Auschwitz-Birkenau 1940-1945 1.1 million (mostly Jews)
Treblinka July 1942-Nov 1943 800,000-900,000
Belzec March-December 1942 600,000
Sobibor April 1942-Oct 1943 250,000
Chelmno Dec 1941-Jan 1945 152,000-340,000
Majdanek 1941-1944 78,000

The Process of Extermination

  • Deportation: Jews were transported by rail from across Europe in cattle trucks - journeys could take days without food or water
  • Selection: At Auschwitz, Dr Josef Mengele selected those fit to work (about 10-30%) from those to be killed immediately (children, elderly, mothers with children, the sick)
  • Gassing: Victims were told they were going to "showers"; Zyklon B poison gas pellets were dropped into the chambers. Death took 15-20 minutes
  • Cremation: Bodies were burned in crematoria or open pits
  • Property: Victims' possessions were sorted and sent to Germany - hair was used for mattress stuffing, gold teeth were extracted
The Escalation of Persecution:
Boycotts (1933) → Legal discrimination (Nuremberg Laws 1935) → Violent pogroms (Kristallnacht 1938) → Forced emigration → Ghettos (1939-1941) → Mass shootings by Einsatzgruppen (1941) → Extermination camps (1942-1945)

Each step was a radicalisation of the previous one. The shift from persecution to genocide was gradual but relentless.

✊ Resistance

The White Rose Group

  • Led by Hans and Sophie Scholl, students at Munich University
  • They distributed anti-Nazi leaflets in 1942-1943 calling on Germans to resist
  • On 18 February 1943, Sophie Scholl was spotted distributing leaflets by a janitor who reported them
  • Hans and Sophie Scholl and Christoph Probst were executed by guillotine on 22 February 1943
  • Other members were arrested and executed later

The July Bomb Plot (20 July 1944)

  • Led by Colonel Claus von Stauffenberg, a German army officer
  • Stauffenberg planted a bomb in Hitler's headquarters at Rastenburg, East Prussia
  • The bomb exploded on 20 July 1944 but Hitler survived - the blast was cushioned by a table leg
  • Stauffenberg and other conspirators were arrested and executed that night - some were hanged with piano wire
  • About 200 people were executed in the aftermath; the plot was the closest anyone came to killing Hitler

Other Resistance

  • Warsaw Ghetto Uprising (April-May 1943): Jewish fighters armed with smuggled weapons resisted deportation for 27 days before the SS destroyed the ghetto
  • Sobibor Uprising (October 1943): Prisoners killed 11 SS guards and 300 escaped; about 50 survived the war
  • Auschwitz Sonderkommando (October 1944): Prisoners who worked in the crematoria destroyed Crematorium IV and escaped briefly
  • Individual rescue efforts: Oskar Schindler saved over 1,200 Jews by employing them in his factory; Raoul Wallenberg saved thousands of Hungarian Jews
Key Fact: The death toll of the Holocaust was approximately 6 million Jews - two-thirds of all European Jews. Other victims of Nazi racial policy included 200,000-500,000 Roma (Gypsies), 200,000 disabled people (under the T4 "euthanasia" programme), and millions of Slavic civilians and Soviet prisoners of war.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain how the Nuremberg Laws affected the lives of German Jews. (4 marks)

Q2: Why was Kristallnacht a significant turning point? (8 marks)

Q3: "The persecution of the Jews escalated gradually between 1933 and 1945." How far do you agree? (12 marks)

Q4: Explain the purpose and decisions of the Wannsee Conference. (8 marks)

Q5: "Resistance to Nazi racial policy was impossible." How far do you agree? (16 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. The Nuremberg Laws (September 1935) stripped Jews of German citizenship, reducing them to "state subjects" with no political rights. They banned marriages and sexual relationships between Jews and Germans, and forbade Jewish households from employing German women under 45. The laws made anti-Semitism official state policy, moving persecution from SA violence to systematic legal discrimination.
  2. Kristallnacht (9-10 November 1938) was a turning point because it marked the shift from legal discrimination to open, organised violence. 191 synagogues were burned, over 7,000 Jewish businesses destroyed, 91 Jews killed, and 20,000-30,000 men sent to concentration camps. The Jewish community was fined 1 billion reichsmarks for the damage. It showed that there were no limits to Nazi persecution and convinced many Jews they had no future in Germany. After Kristallnacht, emigration accelerated and the remaining Jews in Germany lived in terror.
  3. The persecution did escalate gradually, following a clear pattern: boycotts (1933) → legal discrimination (Nuremberg Laws 1935) → violent pogroms (Kristallnacht 1938) → ghettos (1939-1941) → mass shootings (Einsatzgruppen 1941) → extermination camps (1942-1945). Each stage was a radicalisation of the previous one. However, the escalation was not always smooth - there were leaps forward, particularly with Kristallnacht and the decision for the Final Solution after the invasion of the USSR. The war was crucial: it provided cover for genocide and brought millions more Jews under Nazi control. The escalation was gradual in method but accelerated dramatically by circumstances, especially the war.
  4. The Wannsee Conference (20 January 1942) was called by Reinhard Heydrich to coordinate the "Final Solution to the Jewish Question." The 15 officials present agreed that all 11 million European Jews would be transported to extermination camps in Poland. Eichmann would organise transport logistics. Some would be worked to death first ("annihilation through labour"). The conference did not decide to kill the Jews - that decision had already been taken. It was a bureaucratic meeting to organise the logistics of genocide efficiently across all occupied territories.
  5. Resistance was extremely difficult but not impossible. The White Rose group distributed anti-Nazi leaflets; the July 1944 bomb plot nearly killed Hitler; Jews in the Warsaw Ghetto fought the SS for 27 days; prisoners at Sobibor and Auschwitz led uprisings; individuals like Oskar Schindler and Raoul Wallenberg saved thousands. However, resistance was severely limited by: the terror apparatus (Gestapo, SS); the difficulty of organising in a police state; the fact that most Germans supported or acquiesced to Nazi policies; and the overwhelming military power of the state. All known resistance attempts failed to stop the genocide. Resistance was heroic but largely ineffective in changing outcomes.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always trace the ESCALATION: boycott → laws → violence → ghettos → genocide. Examiners want to see you understand the progression
  • Use specific dates: Nuremberg Laws 15 September 1935, Kristallnacht 9-10 November 1938, Wannsee 20 January 1942
  • The death toll is approximately 6 million Jews - always include this figure
  • Kristallnacht was organised by Goebbels, not "spontaneous" - the SA and SS were in civilian clothes to maintain the pretence
  • For resistance questions, acknowledge the extreme difficulty but give specific examples of those who tried
  • The Wannsee Conference was about LOGISTICS, not decision-making - the genocide had already begun
  • Remember that other groups were also persecuted: Roma, disabled, Slavs, homosexuals

📝 Exam Technique

History Exam Tips:
Always use specific dates and statistics. For 'explain why' questions, identify multiple causes and link them. For consequence questions, distinguish between political, economic and social impacts. Know the chronological narrative from Kaiser to Fuhrer.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally. Wrong: Hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally Correct: People with savings and fixed incomes suffered most, but those with debts or land actually benefited. Foreign tourists could buy goods very cheaply.

Students often think the nazis won a majority in a free election. Wrong: The Nazis won a majority in a free election Correct: The Nazis never won over 50% in a free election — their best result was 37.3% in July 1932. Hitler became Chancellor through backroom politics, not a majority vote.

Students often think all germans supported the nazis. Wrong: All Germans supported the Nazis Correct: There was significant opposition including the White Rose group, Edelweiss Pirates, and the 20 July Plot. Many Germans were passive rather than active supporters.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Explain how persecution of Jews escalated 1933-45.

Persecution escalated through clearly defined stages, each more radical than the last. In 1933, the regime began with boycotts: on 1 April 1933, the Nazis organised a nationwide boycott of Jewish shops, with SA men standing outside with signs reading "Germans! Defend yourselves! Don't buy from Jews!" The Civil Service Law of 7 April 1933 expelled Jews from government jobs, teaching, and the judiciary. This was persecution through exclusion and economic ruin.

The Nuremberg Laws (15 September 1935) made anti-Semitism official state policy. The Reich Citizenship Law stripped Jews of German citizenship, reducing them to "state subjects" with no political rights. The Law for the Protection of German Blood and Honour banned marriages and sexual relationships between Jews and Germans. This moved persecution from SA violence to systematic legal discrimination. Kristallnacht (9-10 November 1938) marked the shift from legal persecution to open violence: 191 synagogues were burned, over 7,000 Jewish businesses destroyed, 91 Jews murdered, and 20,000-30,000 men sent to concentration camps. Goebbels organised the pogrom after Herschel Grynszpan shot diplomat Ernst vom Rath in Paris on 7 November 1938. The Jewish community was fined 1 billion reichsmarks for the damage.

The war brought genocide. After invading Poland (September 1939), the Nazis forced Jews into sealed, overcrowded ghettos — the Warsaw Ghetto confined over 400,000 people in just 3.4 square km, with rations of just 200 calories per day. The Wannsee Conference (20 January 1942), chaired by Reinhard Heydrich with Adolf Eichmann coordinating logistics, formalised the "Final Solution": the extermination of all 11 million European Jews. Six death camps were built in occupied Poland. At Auschwitz-Birkenau, Dr Josef Mengele selected those fit to work from those to be gassed immediately; Zyklon B pellets killed victims in 15-20 minutes. By 1945, approximately 6 million Jews had been murdered — two-thirds of all European Jews. In conclusion, the escalation was gradual in method but relentless in direction: from boycott to legal discrimination to violence to ghettoisation to industrialised genocide.

Mark scheme: 3-4 marks per explained stage with specific dates, named individuals and statistics, conclusion for top level
