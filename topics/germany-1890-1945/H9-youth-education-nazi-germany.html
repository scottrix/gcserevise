H9: Youth and Education in Nazi Germany

Foundation Higher All Boards

The Hitler Youth, League of German Maidens, Nazi control of schools and curriculum, and youth resistance movements.

🏕️ The Hitler Youth (Hitlerjugend)

Key Fact: The Hitler Youth was made compulsory by law on 1 December 1936. By 1939, over 8.7 million young people (over 90% of eligible youth) were members. The organisation aimed to indoctrinate young Germans with Nazi ideology and prepare boys for military service.

Structure of the Hitler Youth

Age Group Boys' Organisation Activities
6-10 Pimpfen (Little Fellows) Marching, camping, outdoor activities
10-13 Jungvolk (Young Folk) Military training, map reading, physical fitness
14-18 Hitler Youth (Hitlerjugend) Weapons training, field exercises, political indoctrination, marching

What Did the Hitler Youth Do?

  • Physical training: Cross-country runs, boxing, climbing, swimming, and competitive sports - fitness for military service was the priority
  • Military skills: Map reading, weapons training, marching in formation, field exercises that simulated combat
  • Political indoctrination: Learning about Nazi racial theory, the "stab-in-the-back" myth, anti-Semitism, and the greatness of the Fuhrer
  • Camping and outdoor life: Summer camps involved hiking, singing Nazi songs around campfires, and building comradeship
  • Community service: Collecting money for Winterhilfe (winter relief), helping with harvests, distributing propaganda
Example: A Typical Hitler Youth Week

Members typically attended meetings 3-4 evenings per week plus weekends. A typical schedule included: Monday - political education; Wednesday - sports and physical training; Friday - marching and drill; Saturday afternoon - outdoor activities or community service; Sunday - full-day hike or field exercise. This intensive schedule left little time for schoolwork, family, or independent activities - which was deliberate. The Hitler Youth replaced family and school as the primary influence on young people.

👧 League of German Maidens (BDM)

Key Fact: The Bund Deutscher Mädel (BDM) was the girls' equivalent of the Hitler Youth. By 1939, it had over 2 million members. The BDM focused on physical fitness, domestic skills, and motherhood training - preparing girls to be healthy mothers for the Aryan race.

Structure of the BDM

  • Physical fitness, camping, hiking, first aid
    • Age Group Organisation Activities
    10-14 Jungmädel (Young Girls)
    14-17 Bund Deutscher Mädel Domestic skills, health and hygiene, motherhood training, nursing
    17-21 Confidence (Glaube und Schönheit) Advanced training in culture, marriage preparation, political education

    What Did the BDM Do?

    • Physical fitness: Gymnastics, swimming, hiking, and sports - to produce healthy mothers
    • Domestic skills: Cooking, sewing, cleaning, childcare - training for their future role as housewives
    • Political education: Nazi racial theory, anti-Semitism, the importance of motherhood for the Reich
    • Community service: Helping with harvests, collecting money, volunteering in hospitals and nurseries
    • "Faith and Beauty": For 17-21 year olds - cultural activities, marriage preparation, and training in "feminine virtues"
    Key Fact: The BDM emphasised that a woman's highest duty was to bear healthy Aryan children. Members were taught that education was less important than physical fitness and motherhood. The BDM motto was: "Be faithful, be pure, be German." Members who became pregnant out of wedlock were not punished - the Nazis valued Aryan births regardless of marital status.

    📚 Nazi Control of Education

    Key Fact: The Nazis took complete control of the education system. All teachers had to join the Nazi Teachers' League (NSLB) - by 1936, 97% had joined. The curriculum was rewritten to reflect Nazi ideology, and textbooks were replaced with racially "correct" versions.

    Control of Teachers

    • All teachers had to join the Nazi Teachers' League (NSLB) or lose their jobs
    • Teachers attended special training camps (4-week courses) to learn Nazi ideology
    • Jewish teachers were dismissed in April 1933 under the Civil Service Law
    • Teachers who criticised the regime were reported by students and dismissed or arrested
    • By 1936, 97% of teachers had joined the NSLB

    The Nazi Curriculum

    Subject Nazi Emphasis
    History Treaty of Versailles as injustice; "stab-in-the-back" myth; German greatness; the Nazi revolution; anti-Semitism
    Biology Racial theory; Aryan supremacy; Social Darwinism; classification of races; dangers of "racial pollution"
    Geography Lebensraum (living space); German territory lost at Versailles; land and soil as the basis for national strength
    Maths Military applications: trajectory of bombs, calculations for armaments, solving problems involving "racial purity" statistics
    PE Boxing, cross-country running, military-style exercises; 15% of timetable for boys; medical inspections to assess racial "fitness"
    German Reading Nazi-approved literature; essays on the Fuhrer and the Fatherland; propaganda analysis as "appreciation"
    Science Military applications; chemistry of weapons; physics of ballistics
    Example: Nazi Textbooks

    A maths textbook asked students to calculate: "The cost of maintaining a hereditarily ill person is 5.50 RM per day. How much does it cost to support 3 hereditarily ill people for one year?" A biology textbook included diagrams comparing Aryan and "inferior" skulls. A history textbook described the Treaty of Versailles as "the greatest act of robbery in the history of the world." These examples show how every subject was used to promote Nazi ideology.

    🏫 Elite Schools

    The Nazis created special schools to train the future leaders of the Third Reich:

    School Type Purpose Entry Requirements
    Adolf Hitler Schools (AHS) Train future Nazi Party leaders; 12-year programme Selected from Hitler Youth at 12; rigorous physical and political tests
    Napolas (National Political Education Institutes) Train future army officers and administrators Sons of military families and Nazi officials; emphasis on discipline and sport
    Ordensburgen (Order Castles) Train the very top elite of the Nazi leadership Graduates of AHS; 3-year programme at medieval-style castles

    🎸 Youth Resistance

    Key Fact: Not all young people accepted Nazi indoctrination. Several youth groups resisted Nazi control, including the Edelweiss Pirates, Swing Youth, and the White Rose group. These groups represented different forms of opposition, from non-conformity to active resistance.

    The Edelweiss Pirates (Edelweisspiraten)

    • Working-class youth who rejected the Hitler Youth's discipline and militarism
    • They wore edelweiss flower badges or pins as a symbol of resistance
    • Groups formed in Cologne, Düsseldorf, Essen and other cities
    • Activities included: camping trips (forbidden by the Nazis), singing banned songs, fighting Hitler Youth patrols, writing anti-Nazi graffiti
    • In 1944, the Gestapo hanged 12 Edelweiss Pirates in Cologne publicly as a warning
    • The Pirates were not political revolutionaries - they mainly wanted freedom from Nazi control

    The Swing Youth (Swingjugend)

    • Middle-class teenagers in cities like Hamburg who loved American jazz and swing music
    • They held illegal dance parties, wore British-style clothing (long hair, checked jackets), and welcomed Jewish friends
    • The Nazis saw them as degenerate and racially tainted - jazz was condemned as "Negro music"
    • In 1941, Himmler ordered Swing Youth leaders to be sent to concentration camps
    • They were not political - their resistance was cultural, rejecting Nazi conformity

    The White Rose Group

    • University students in Munich who distributed anti-Nazi leaflets in 1942-1943
    • Led by Hans and Sophie Scholl, Christoph Probst, and other students
    • Their leaflets called on Germans to resist the Nazi regime and condemned the genocide
    • On 18 February 1943, Sophie Scholl was caught distributing leaflets at Munich University
    • Hans, Sophie and Christoph were executed by guillotine on 22 February 1943
    Group Background Type of Resistance Outcome
    Edelweiss Pirates Working class Non-conformity; anti-Hitler Youth; some sabotage 12 hanged in Cologne 1944; members sent to camps
    Swing Youth Middle class Cultural rebellion; American music and fashion Leaders sent to concentration camps 1941
    White Rose University students Active political resistance; distributing anti-Nazi leaflets Leaders executed February 1943
    Example: The Edelweiss Pirates in Cologne

    In Cologne, the Edelweiss Pirates grew increasingly bold. They beat up Hitler Youth members, distributed Allied propaganda leaflets, and helped army deserters. In November 1944, the Gestapo arrested 40 Pirates in Cologne. Twelve were publicly hanged without trial - their bodies were left hanging as a warning. The youngest was just 16 years old. This showed both the courage of those who resisted and the brutality of the Nazi response to any opposition.

    🤔 How Effective Was Nazi Indoctrination?

    Evidence of Success Evidence of Failure
    Over 90% of youth joined the Hitler Youth by 1939 Membership was compulsory from 1936 - joining was not voluntary
    Many young Germans genuinely admired Hitler Truancy rates increased: in 1936, 1 million truancies; in 1938, 3 million
    The Hitler Youth produced committed soldiers Youth groups like Edelweiss Pirates and Swing Youth resisted
    School curriculum successfully promoted Nazi ideology Some students mocked Nazi teachers behind their backs
    University purges removed opposition White Rose showed educated youth could resist
    BDM successfully promoted motherhood values Some girls resented the emphasis on domesticity and restrictions
    Key Judgement: The Nazi youth programme was partially successful. Many young people were genuinely indoctrinated and became committed Nazis. The 12th SS Panzer Division "Hitlerjugend," formed from Hitler Youth members, fought fiercely in 1944. However, compulsion was necessary because not all youth wanted to join. Truancy rates increased, and resistance groups showed that some young people rejected Nazi values entirely. The programme was most effective on younger children who knew no other system.

    ❓ Practice Questions

    Q1: Explain two ways the Nazis controlled education. (4 marks)

    Q2: How far did the Hitler Youth succeed in indoctrinating young Germans? (8 marks)

    Q3: "Youth resistance in Nazi Germany was ineffective." How far do you agree? (12 marks)

    Q4: Explain the differences between the Hitler Youth and the League of German Maidens. (8 marks)

    Q5: "The Nazis were more successful in controlling young people through schools than through youth organisations." How far do you agree? (16 marks)

    ✅ Answers

    1. Teachers were forced to join the Nazi Teachers' League (97% by 1936) or lose their jobs, ensuring only ideologically reliable teachers remained. The curriculum was rewritten to promote Nazi ideology - biology taught racial theory, history taught the "stab-in-the-back" myth, and PE became 15% of the timetable to build military fitness.
    2. The Hitler Youth partially succeeded. Over 90% of eligible youth were members by 1939, and many were genuinely committed to Nazi ideals. The 12th SS Panzer Division was formed from Hitler Youth members. However, membership was compulsory from 1936, meaning many joined because they had to, not because they wanted to. Truancy rates increased from 1 million (1936) to 3 million (1938). Resistance groups like the Edelweiss Pirates showed some youth actively rejected Nazism. The programme was most effective on those too young to remember alternatives.
    3. Youth resistance was limited in its impact but showed that Nazi control was never total. The Edelweiss Pirates rejected Hitler Youth discipline and some engaged in sabotage, but they were not a political movement and were brutally suppressed (12 hanged in Cologne 1944). The Swing Youth was cultural resistance, not political. The White Rose was the most serious resistance but was quickly crushed - Sophie and Hans Scholl were executed in February 1943. All resistance failed to change Nazi policy significantly. However, the existence of resistance proved that not all young people accepted indoctrination, and the White Rose leaflets provided evidence of moral courage that inspired post-war Germans.
    4. The Hitler Youth prepared boys for military service: physical training, weapons drill, marching, and political indoctrination about German greatness. The BDM prepared girls for motherhood: domestic skills, health and hygiene, childcare training, and physical fitness to produce healthy Aryan children. Both organisations promoted Nazi ideology and racial theory, but their practical activities were entirely different - boys were trained to fight, girls were trained to breed. The BDM had no military training equivalent; the Hitler Youth had no domestic skills equivalent.
    5. Schools and youth organisations were both important but worked in different ways. Schools had a captive audience (all children had to attend) and could embed Nazi ideology across every subject - racial theory in biology, Versailles injustice in history, military maths. However, school was limited to lesson time. The Hitler Youth controlled leisure time - evenings, weekends, and holidays - providing a total immersion in Nazi culture. The youth organisations were arguably more effective because they created emotional loyalty through comradeship, camping, and a sense of belonging. However, increasing truancy from Hitler Youth meetings showed its limits. Schools could compel attendance more easily but produced intellectual conformity rather than passionate commitment. Both were necessary for the Nazis' total control of youth.

    🎯 Exam Tips

    • Hitler Youth became COMPULSORY in December 1936 - this is crucial for evaluating its "success"
    • Know the truancy statistics: 1 million (1936) rising to 3 million (1938) - evidence that enthusiasm was limited
    • Distinguish between different types of youth resistance: Edelweiss Pirates (working class, non-conformist), Swing Youth (middle class, cultural), White Rose (educated, political)
    • The BDM focused on motherhood and domestic skills - this links to the wider topic of women in Nazi Germany
    • For effectiveness questions, always consider BOTH voluntary support and compulsory membership
    • The 12th SS Panzer Division is good evidence that some youth were genuinely committed - they fought fanatically
    • Nazi textbooks (maths calculating costs of "hereditarily ill") are excellent specific examples for how education was corrupted

    ⚠️ Common Errors

    Watch Out!

    Students often think hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally. Wrong: Hyperinflation meant everyone suffered equally Correct: People with savings and fixed incomes suffered most, but those with debts or land actually benefited. Foreign tourists could buy goods very cheaply.

    Students often think the nazis won a majority in a free election. Wrong: The Nazis won a majority in a free election Correct: The Nazis never won over 50% in a free election — their best result was 37.3% in July 1932. Hitler became Chancellor through backroom politics, not a majority vote.

    Students often think all germans supported the nazis. Wrong: All Germans supported the Nazis Correct: There was significant opposition including the White Rose group, Edelweiss Pirates, and the 20 July Plot. Many Germans were passive rather than active supporters.

    ✍️ Model Answer

    Full-Mark Response

    8 marks: Explain how the Nazis controlled young people.

    The Nazis controlled young people through the Hitler Youth and the education system. The Hitler Youth was made compulsory by law on 1 December 1936; by 1939, over 8.7 million young people — over 90% of eligible youth — were members. Boys aged 10-13 joined the Jungvolk for military training, map reading, and physical fitness; those aged 14-18 joined the Hitler Youth itself for weapons training, field exercises, and political indoctrination. Members typically attended meetings 3-4 evenings per week plus weekends, deliberately replacing family and school as the primary influence on young people. The League of German Maidens (BDM), with over 2 million members by 1939, trained girls in domestic skills and motherhood rather than military skills.

    The Nazi curriculum was rewritten to reflect ideology. All teachers had to join the Nazi Teachers' League (97% by 1936) or lose their jobs. Biology taught racial theory and Aryan supremacy; history taught the "stab-in-the-back" myth and Versailles injustice; PE became 15% of the timetable for boys to build military fitness. Maths textbooks asked students to calculate the cost of maintaining the "hereditarily ill." Jewish teachers were dismissed in April 1933 under the Civil Service Law.

    However, control was never total. Truancy rates rose from 1 million (1936) to 3 million (1938), suggesting enthusiasm was limited despite compulsory membership. The Edelweiss Pirates — working-class youth who rejected Hitler Youth discipline, wore edelweiss badges, sang banned songs, and fought Hitler Youth patrols — showed active resistance. In November 1944, the Gestapo publicly hanged 12 Edelweiss Pirates in Cologne as a warning. The White Rose group, led by Hans and Sophie Scholl at Munich University, distributed anti-Nazi leaflets in 1942-43 before being executed by guillotine on 22 February 1943. In conclusion, the Nazis controlled the majority through compulsion and indoctrination, but a significant minority resisted.

    Mark scheme: 2 marks per explained method with specific evidence, recognition of limitations for top level
