FR27: Tenses: Present, Perfect and Imperfect

Foundation Higher All Boards

Forming and using the present, perfect (passé composé) and imperfect tenses; when to use each

The Present Tense

The present tense in French is used for:

  • Actions happening now: Je mange = I am eating / I eat
  • Habits and routines: Je joue au tennis le samedi = I play tennis on Saturdays
  • General truths: L'eau bout à 100 degrés = Water boils at 100 degrees
  • Near future with time expressions: Je pars demain = I leave tomorrow
Key Fact: Regular -er verbs drop -er and add: -e, -es, -e, -ons, -ez, -ent. Regular -ir verbs (like choisir) drop -ir and add: -is, -is, -it, -issons, -issez, -issent. Irregular verbs (être, avoir, aller, faire) must be memorised.

The Perfect Tense (Passé Composé)

The perfect tense describes completed past actions. It is formed with an auxiliary verb + past participle.

AuxiliaryWhen UsedExample
avoirMost verbsJ'ai mangé = I ate / I have eaten
êtreMovement and reflexive verbs (DR MRS VANDERTRAMP)Je suis allé(e) = I went / I have gone
Exam Tip: With être, the past participle must agree with the subject in gender and number: allé → allée (feminine), allés (masculine plural), allées (feminine plural). With avoir, the past participle usually does NOT agree — unless there is a preceding direct object.

The Imperfect Tense (L'Imparfait)

The imperfect tense is used for:

  • Ongoing past actions: Je regardais la télé = I was watching TV
  • Habits in the past: Je jouais au foot tous les jours = I used to play football every day
  • Descriptions in the past: Il faisait beau = The weather was nice

Formation: Take the nous form of the present tense, drop -ons, and add: -ais, -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez, -aient.

Key Fact: The only irregular imperfect stem is être → ét- (j'étais, tu étais, il était, nous étions, vous étiez, ils étaient).

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Conjugate the verb "manger" in the present, perfect and imperfect tenses for "je".

Q: When would you use the perfect tense vs the imperfect tense? Give an example of each.

✅ Answers

  1. Present: je mange. Perfect: j'ai mangé. Imperfect: je mangeais.
  2. Perfect = completed single action in the past: "J'ai vu un film hier" (I saw a film yesterday). Imperfect = ongoing or habitual past: "Je regardais la télé quand le téléphone a sonné" (I was watching TV when the phone rang). Use both together: imperfect sets the scene, perfect interrupts it.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Use a range of tenses (present, past, future) to access higher marks
  • Include opinions with reasons (je pense que... parce que...)
  • Check adjective agreement (gender and number) before finishing
  • Use complex structures: negation (ne...pas, ne...jamais), pronouns (le, la, les), conjunctions (cependant, donc, en plus)
  • Include cultural references to French-speaking countries
  • For writing: plan your response, check verb conjugations, re-read for errors

📝 Exam Technique

French Exam Tips:
For grammar questions: Show you understand the rules by applying them accurately in your writing and speaking. Use a range of tenses (at least 3) to access higher marks. Check agreement (gender, number, subject-verb). Include complex structures (negation, pronouns, subordinating conjunctions). Accuracy in grammar is worth up to 40% of writing and speaking marks.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often forget to make adjectives agree. Wrong: Les filles sont petit. Correct: Les filles sont petites (feminine plural — add -es). Students also confuse the imperfect and perfect tenses. Wrong: Quand j'étais jeune, j'ai habité à Paris (perfect = single event). Correct: Quand j'étais jeune, j'habitais à Paris (imperfect = ongoing/habitual past).

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Writing task: Write about forming and using the present, perfect (passé composé) and imperfect tenses; when to use each.

A grade 9 answer will: use at least three tenses accurately; include complex structures (subordinate clauses, pronouns, negation); show precise grammar (agreement, conjugation, spelling); express and justify opinions; include cultural knowledge; use a wide range of vocabulary; be well-organised with connecting words (cependant, en revanche, de plus, en conclusion).

Mark scheme: Content (relevance and detail) + Quality of language (accuracy and range) = total marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE French tests four equally-weighted AOs: AO1 Listening (25%) — understand spoken French across all three themes; AO2 Reading (25%) — understand written French including translation; AO3 Speaking (25%) — communicate effectively in conversation and role-play; AO4 Writing (25%) — produce accurate French including translation into French. For grade 9, use a wide range of vocabulary and complex structures, demonstrate consistent accuracy across all tenses, and include cultural knowledge. The difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the range and accuracy of language — not just what you say but how precisely you say it.
