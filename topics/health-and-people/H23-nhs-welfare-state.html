H23: The NHS and the Welfare State

Foundation Higher All Boards

How the Beveridge Report of 1942 identified the Five Giant Evils and led to the creation of the NHS on 5 July 1948 and the foundations of the modern welfare state.

📖 The Road to the Welfare State

Key Context: The Liberal reforms of 1906-1914 had established the principle that the state should help protect citizens from poverty, but the system was incomplete and patchy. Two world wars and the Great Depression exposed the inadequacies of the existing system. The Beveridge Report of 1942 provided a comprehensive vision for a welfare state that would protect every citizen "from the cradle to the grave."

Problems Before the Welfare State

  • Healthcare was not free - many people could not afford to see a doctor or go to hospital
  • Insurance schemes only covered workers, not their families
  • Hospitals were a mixture of voluntary (charity), municipal (local authority), and private hospitals with no coordination
  • The Great Depression of the 1930s caused mass unemployment - in 1932, over 2.7 million people were unemployed
  • The Means Test (1931) forced the unemployed to sell possessions before receiving help, causing humiliation and resentment
  • There was no comprehensive system of social security
Example: Healthcare Before the NHS

Before 1948, healthcare in Britain was a chaotic mixture. Voluntary hospitals (funded by charity) and municipal hospitals (run by local authorities) operated independently with no coordination. Only about 21 million workers (and not their families) were covered by National Health Insurance. Everyone else had to pay for medical treatment. A GP consultation could cost 5 shillings (a significant sum for a working-class family). Many people simply could not afford treatment and suffered in silence or turned to unqualified practitioners. Dental care and glasses were expensive luxuries that most working-class people went without.

📋 The Beveridge Report (1942)

William Beveridge

  • William Beveridge (1879-1963) was a civil servant, economist, and Director of the London School of Economics
  • In 1941, the wartime coalition government commissioned him to review the existing social insurance schemes
  • He far exceeded his brief, producing a comprehensive plan for social reform
  • He published "Social Insurance and Allied Services" (the Beveridge Report) on 1 December 1942
The Five Giant Evils: Beveridge identified five "giant evils" that needed to be defeated to rebuild Britain after the war:
  • Want (poverty) - to be defeated by comprehensive social insurance
  • Disease (ill health) - to be defeated by a national health service
  • Ignorance (lack of education) - to be defeated by improved education
  • Squalor (poor housing) - to be defeated by better housing and town planning
  • Idleness (unemployment) - to be defeated by government commitment to full employment

Key Principles of the Beveridge Report

  • Comprehensive: The system should cover all citizens, not just workers
  • Universal: Everyone should contribute and everyone should benefit
  • Compulsory: All working people had to pay national insurance contributions
  • Adequate: Benefits should be enough to live on, not a bare minimum
  • From cradle to grave: Protection should cover every stage of life
Beveridge's Framework:
Flat-rate contributions from all workers + Flat-rate benefits for all in need = Security "from cradle to grave"
The state would act as a safety net, catching anyone who fell into difficulty.
Example: The Popularity of the Beveridge Report

The Beveridge Report was an instant sensation. It sold over 600,000 copies, making it one of the best-selling government publications in history. A Mass-Observation survey found that 90% of people knew about the report. The public embraced Beveridge's vision because the war had created a sense of shared sacrifice - people felt that a better, fairer society should be the reward for their suffering. A famous wartime poster read: "Your Britain - Fight for It Now." The report gave people hope that a better future was possible.

🗳️ The 1945 Labour Government

The 1945 Election

  • On 5 July 1945, the British public voted in the first general election since 1935
  • Against most predictions, the Labour Party won a landslide victory with 393 seats against the Conservatives' 213
  • Clement Attlee became Prime Minister, leading a government determined to implement the Beveridge Report
  • The Labour manifesto promised to create "a socialist commonwealth of Great Britain" with full employment, free healthcare, and social security
Why Labour Won in 1945: Several factors contributed to Labour's victory: (1) the war had shown that government could organise society effectively (the Beveridge Report was popular); (2) the British people wanted a better society as a reward for wartime sacrifice; (3) Labour was associated with the Beveridge Report and the promise of a "New Jerusalem"; (4) Churchill, although popular as a wartime leader, was seen as less committed to social reform; (5) soldiers' votes favoured Labour, which promised a "land fit for heroes."

Labour's Reforms 1945-1951

  • National Insurance Act 1946: Implemented Beveridge's plan for comprehensive social insurance, covering unemployment, sickness, maternity, widowhood, and old age. Everyone of working age had to pay a weekly contribution.
  • National Assistance Act 1948: Provided a safety net for those not covered by National Insurance, effectively abolishing the Poor Law
  • National Health Service Act 1946: Established the NHS (launched 5 July 1948)
  • Education Act 1944: (Passed by the wartime coalition) Raised the school leaving age to 15 and introduced free secondary education for all
  • New Towns Act 1946: Authorised the building of new towns to address housing shortages
  • Housing programmes: Between 1945 and 1951, Labour built over 1 million new homes

🏥 The NHS Established (5 July 1948)

Aneurin Bevan

  • Aneurin "Nye" Bevan (1897-1960) was the Minister of Health in the Labour government and the architect of the NHS
  • He was a Welsh miner's son who had personally experienced the effects of poverty and lack of healthcare
  • He believed that healthcare should be free at the point of use and available to all regardless of wealth
  • He described his vision: "No society can legitimately call itself civilised if a sick person is denied medical aid because of lack of means"

The Principles of the NHS

  • Free at the point of use: No one should have to pay for medical treatment when they needed it
  • Available to all: Every citizen was entitled to NHS care, regardless of income
  • Comprehensive: The NHS covered all medical services: GP consultations, hospital treatment, dental care, and eye care
  • Funded by taxation: The NHS was paid for through general taxation, not by individual insurance contributions
  • Nationalised: All hospitals (voluntary and municipal) were brought under state control
NHS Core Principles (1948):
1. Free at the point of use
2. Available to all based on need, not ability to pay
3. Funded from general taxation
4. Comprehensive coverage for all medical needs
These principles remain the foundation of the NHS today.
Example: The First Day of the NHS (5 July 1948)

On 5 July 1948, the NHS came into operation across Britain. Over 2,700 hospitals were nationalised overnight. Patients who had previously been unable to afford treatment flooded into surgeries and hospitals. In the first year alone, 8.5 million people had their teeth treated and 5 million received free glasses - many for the first time in their lives. One GP reported seeing 100 patients on the first morning. Aneurin Bevan symbolically launched the NHS at Park Hospital in Davyhulme, Manchester (now Trafford General Hospital), declaring: "We are going to 'nationalise' the whole of the hospital service."

⚔️ Opposition to the NHS

The British Medical Association (BMA)

The most significant opposition came from doctors, organised through the British Medical Association:

  • Many doctors feared they would become "civil servants" and lose their professional independence
  • They objected to being forced to join the NHS - they wanted the right to opt out
  • They were concerned about their pay and working conditions under the new system
  • Some doctors worried that the NHS would lead to "socialised medicine" and lower standards
  • The BMA voted against the NHS proposals in 1946 and 1947, with 90% of doctors opposing Bevan's plans
How Bevan Overcame Opposition: Bevan used a combination of negotiation and compromise. He allowed consultants to keep their private practices and private beds in NHS hospitals. He offered GPs a "capitation fee" (payment per patient registered) rather than a fixed salary, preserving their independence. He gave doctors the right to appeal to an independent tribunal. He famously declared that he had "stuffed their mouths with gold" to win the consultants' support. By 1948, 90% of doctors had voluntarily registered with the NHS.

Other Opposition

  • Conservative Party: Many Conservatives opposed the NHS as too expensive and socialistic, though they did not reverse it when they returned to power in 1951
  • Local authorities: Some resented losing control of municipal hospitals to regional health boards
  • Voluntary hospitals: Some charitable hospitals objected to being nationalised
  • Cost concerns: The NHS cost £248 million in its first year - £140 million more than budgeted
Source of Opposition Reason Bevan's Response
BMA / Doctors Feared loss of independence and income Allowed private practice; capitation fees for GPs; independent tribunals
Conservatives Objected to cost and state control Legislation passed using Labour's large majority
Local authorities Resented losing municipal hospitals Created regional health boards instead
Treasury Worried about the cost Introduced prescription charges in 1951 (Bevan resigned in protest)

📊 Impact of the NHS and Welfare State

The Impact of the NHS

  • For the first time, every citizen could access free medical treatment regardless of income
  • In the first year: 8.5 million dental treatments, 5 million pairs of glasses, and 187 million GP consultations
  • Life expectancy increased: from 66 years for men in 1948 to 75 years by 1980
  • Infant mortality fell dramatically: from 34 per 1,000 live births in 1948 to 10 per 1,000 by 1980
  • Deaths from tuberculosis fell from 25,000 in 1948 to under 3,000 by 1960 due to free treatment
  • The NHS became one of Britain's most popular and cherished institutions

The Impact of the Welfare State

  • The combined effect of National Insurance, the NHS, free education, and housing programmes transformed living standards
  • Poverty was significantly reduced, though not eliminated
  • The system provided security that previous generations could not have imagined
  • However, the cost was enormous and continued to rise
  • The welfare state became a permanent feature of British life, supported by all major political parties
The NHS as a Turning Point: The creation of the NHS in 1948 was arguably the most important event in the history of British healthcare. It represented a fundamental shift: healthcare was now a right of citizenship, not a commodity to be bought and sold. The principle that medical treatment should be based on need, not the ability to pay, was revolutionary and has remained the NHS's core value for over 75 years.

⚠️ Challenges of the Welfare State

  • Cost: The NHS cost far more than expected - Bevan had underestimated demand. In the first year, it spent £248 million instead of the budgeted £108 million
  • Prescription charges: Introduced in 1951 to help cover costs, causing Aneurin Bevan to resign from the government in protest
  • Rising expectations: As medical technology advanced, the NHS faced growing demand for expensive new treatments
  • An ageing population: More people living longer meant increasing costs for pensions and healthcare
  • Economic constraints: Post-war Britain was virtually bankrupt - the government had to borrow heavily to fund the welfare state
  • The American loan (1946): Britain borrowed $3.75 billion from the USA to help rebuild the economy
Example: Bevan's Resignation (April 1951)

In April 1951, Hugh Gaitskell (the new Chancellor of the Exchequer) introduced prescription charges of one shilling and a 50% increase in dental charges to help fund the Korean War and the NHS. Bevan saw this as a betrayal of the principle of free healthcare and resigned from the government on 12 April 1951, along with Harold Wilson. Bevan argued that the charges were "the first step" towards dismantling the NHS. His resignation highlighted the enduring tension between the ideal of free healthcare and the reality of limited resources.

  2. A balanced answer should discuss: the role of WWII (shared sacrifice, the Beveridge Report, the 1945 Labour landslide, demonstrated government could organise effectively); the role of earlier developments (the Liberal reforms, the 1875 Public Health Act, the work of Booth and Rowntree); the role of individuals (Beveridge, Bevan, Attlee); the role of economic and social conditions (the Great Depression, the Means Test). Argue that the war was a crucial catalyst but that the foundations had been laid by earlier reformers and the welfare state was the product of decades of progress, not just the war.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use specific dates: Beveridge Report December 1942, Labour election July 1945, NHS launched 5 July 1948
  • Know the Five Giant Evils by heart: Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor, Idleness
  • Explain Bevan's compromises with doctors in detail - this is a very common question
  • Link the NHS to earlier developments: the 1875 Public Health Act, the Liberal reforms, the Beveridge Report
  • Use statistics: 600,000 copies of Beveridge, £248 million first-year cost, 8.5 million dental treatments
  • For evaluation questions, consider whether the welfare state was truly "from cradle to grave"
  • Remember Bevan's resignation over prescription charges in 1951 - it shows the tension between ideals and costs

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think medieval medicine was entirely backward. Wrong: Medieval medicine was entirely backward Correct: Medieval doctors used rational observation (urine analysis, pulse checking) alongside religious approaches. Monasteries preserved medical texts and some hospitals provided genuine care.

Students often think one individual caused all medical progress. Wrong: One individual caused all medical progress Correct: While individuals like Pasteur were crucial, their discoveries depended on other factors: technology, government support, and the work of predecessors.

Students often think progress in medicine has been steady and continuous. Wrong: Progress in medicine has been steady and continuous Correct: Medical progress was not a straight line — there were periods of regression, and some ideas took centuries to be accepted.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Explain the impact of the NHS on health care in Britain.

The NHS had a transformative impact on health care in Britain. The Beveridge Report of December 1942 identified the "Five Giant Evils" — Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor, and Idleness — and sold over 600,000 copies, creating public demand for a comprehensive welfare state. The 1945 Labour landslide gave Clement Attlee's government the mandate to implement Beveridge's vision, and Aneurin Bevan, as Minister of Health, became the architect of the NHS.

The NHS was launched on 5 July 1948, when over 2,700 hospitals were nationalised overnight. Its core principles — free at the point of use, available to all based on need not ability to pay, and funded from general taxation — were revolutionary. Before 1948, only about 21 million workers (not their families) were covered by National Health Insurance, and a GP consultation could cost 5 shillings — a significant sum for a working-class family. In the NHS's first year, 8.5 million people received dental treatment, 5 million received free glasses, and there were 187 million GP consultations. Many people received medical care for the first time in their lives.

Bevan overcame significant opposition from the British Medical Association (BMA), which voted against the NHS proposals in 1946 and 1947, with 90% of doctors initially opposing. Bevan won them over by allowing consultants to keep private practices and private beds in NHS hospitals, offering GPs capitation fees rather than fixed salaries, and giving doctors the right to appeal to independent tribunals. He famously declared he had "stuffed their mouths with gold." By 1948, 90% of doctors had voluntarily registered with the NHS.

The NHS's long-term impact has been profound. Life expectancy for men rose from 66 years in 1948 to 75 by 1980; infant mortality fell from 34 per 1,000 live births to 10 per 1,000 in the same period; deaths from tuberculosis fell from 25,000 to under 3,000 by 1960. The principle that health care is a right of citizenship, not a commodity, has endured for over 75 years.

However, the NHS has faced persistent funding challenges. It cost £248 million in its first year — £140 million more than budgeted — because Bevan had underestimated demand. Prescription charges were introduced in 1951, causing Bevan to resign from the government in protest. Rising costs from an ageing population, expensive new technologies, and increasing patient expectations continue to create pressure. In conclusion, the NHS transformed access to health care in Britain, replacing a chaotic and unfair system with universal free provision, but the tension between unlimited demand and limited resources has remained a challenge since its very first year.
