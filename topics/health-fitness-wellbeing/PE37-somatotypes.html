PE37: Somatotypes

Foundation Higher All Boards Paper 2

Understanding the three somatotypes - endomorph, mesomorph and ectomorph - and identifying which body types are best suited to specific sports and positions.

🧬 What are Somatotypes?

Somatotypes: A system of classifying body types based on physical characteristics. There are three main somatotypes: endomorph, mesomorph and ectomorph. Most people are a blend of all three, with one type being dominant.

The somatotype system was developed by psychologist William Sheldon in the 1940s. Each somatotype is characterised by specific physical features that make certain body types more suited to particular sports and activities.

The Three Somatotypes:
ENDOMORPH ——— MESOMORPH ——— ECTOMORPH
Fat & wide       Muscular & athletic       Lean & thin
Most people fall somewhere between these extremes, with one dominant type.

🔴 Endomorph

Endomorph: A somatotype characterised by a high proportion of body fat, a wide waist and hips, and a rounded, soft body shape. Endomorphs tend to store fat easily and find it difficult to lose weight.

Physical Characteristics

  • High body fat percentage - soft, round body shape
  • Wide waist and hips (apple or pear shape)
  • Shorter limbs relative to trunk size
  • Large joint circumference
  • Slow metabolism - gains fat easily, loses it slowly
  • Less muscle definition visible under body fat
  • Wide bone structure

Suitable Sports

  • Sumo wrestling: The extra mass provides a low centre of gravity and makes it harder for opponents to move or topple the wrestler
  • Rugby prop forward: Body mass provides stability in the scrum and helps with the pushing force required
  • Weightlifting (super-heavyweight): Greater body mass can support heavier lifts and provide a stable base
  • Shot put: Mass provides momentum and stability for the throwing action
Sporting Example

A sumo wrestler is a classic endomorph. Their large body mass and low centre of gravity make them incredibly difficult to push out of the ring. The extra weight provides stability and momentum. Similarly, a rugby prop forward uses their bulk to anchor the scrum and provide a powerful pushing force against the opposition pack.

🟢 Mesomorph

Mesomorph: A somatotype characterised by a muscular, athletic build with broad shoulders, narrow waist and low body fat. Mesomorphs gain muscle easily and have a naturally strong, powerful physique.

Physical Characteristics

  • Muscular, well-proportioned body with visible muscle definition
  • Broad shoulders and narrow waist (V-shape or athletic shape)
  • Low to moderate body fat percentage
  • Strong bone structure and well-developed musculature
  • Fast metabolism - gains muscle easily, responds well to training
  • Efficient at both gaining muscle and losing fat
  • Good posture and physical presence

Suitable Sports

  • Sprinting: Muscular power and fast-twitch muscle fibres produce explosive speed
  • Gymnastics: Strength-to-weight ratio and muscular control are essential
  • Swimming: Broad shoulders and powerful upper body muscles generate force through the water
  • Boxing (middleweight): Muscular build provides punching power and endurance
  • Decathlon: All-round athletic ability across multiple events
  • Rowing: Powerful legs, back and arms drive the boat forward
Sporting Example

Usain Bolt, despite his height, has a mesomorphic build with powerful leg muscles and broad shoulders that generate the explosive force needed for sprinting. A gymnast like Simone Biles shows a compact mesomorphic build with exceptional strength-to-weight ratio, enabling powerful vaults and complex tumbling passes.

🔵 Ectomorph

Ectomorph: A somatotype characterised by a lean, thin build with low body fat and low muscle mass. Ectomorphs are typically tall and slender with narrow shoulders and a fast metabolism.

Physical Characteristics

  • Lean and thin with low body fat
  • Narrow shoulders, chest and hips
  • Long, thin limbs
  • Low muscle mass - finds it hard to gain muscle
  • Fast metabolism - burns calories quickly, difficult to gain weight
  • Fine bone structure
  • Often tall with a linear appearance

Suitable Sports

  • Marathon running: Low body weight reduces energy cost of running; light frame is efficient over long distances
  • High jump: Tall, lean build with long legs provides leverage and a high centre of gravity
  • Long-distance cycling: Light body weight is crucial for climbing hills; efficient power-to-weight ratio
  • Basketball: Height and long limbs provide reach and stride advantage
  • Horse racing (jockey): Very low body weight is essential for the sport
  • Triathlon: Lean build is efficient across swimming, cycling and running
Sporting Example

Mo Farah is a classic ectomorph - lean and lightweight, ideal for distance running where carrying excess weight would increase the energy cost of every stride. A high jumper like Mutaz Barshim uses their tall, lean build and long legs to clear the bar, where the ectomorphic advantage of long levers and low body weight is critical.

🔄 Most Athletes Are a Blend

Key Principle: Very few people are purely one somatotype. Most individuals - and most athletes - are a blend of all three, with one type being dominant. The blend determines which sports they are best suited to.
Blended Somatotype Examples

Rugby winger: Mesomorph-dominant with some ectomorph (muscular but lean and fast) - needs strength for tackles but speed for running.

Rugby lock forward: Mesomorph-ectomorph blend (tall and muscular) - height for lineouts, strength for scrummaging.

Swimmer (freestyle): Mesomorph with ectomorph traits (broad shoulders, long lean limbs) - power from mesomorph, reach and efficiency from ectomorph.

Footballer (midfielder): Balanced mesomorph-ectomorph (lean and athletic) - needs endurance and speed without carrying excess bulk.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the three somatotypes and give a sporting example for each.

Q2: Explain why an endomorph is well-suited to being a rugby prop forward.

Q3: Why is an ectomorph the ideal somatotype for a marathon runner?

Q4: A basketball player is tall, lean and muscular. Which two somatotypes are blended and why?

Q5: Explain why most athletes are not purely one somatotype. Use a specific sporting example.

Q6: A swimmer has broad shoulders, powerful arms and low body fat. Name their dominant somatotype and explain why this body type is advantageous for swimming.

✅ Answers

  1. Endomorph: high body fat, round and soft body shape, wide waist and hips. Example: sumo wrestler or rugby prop forward. Mesomorph: muscular, athletic build, broad shoulders and narrow waist, low body fat. Example: sprinter or gymnast. Ectomorph: lean and thin, low body fat and muscle mass, narrow shoulders and long limbs. Example: marathon runner or high jumper.
  2. An endomorph is well-suited to being a rugby prop forward because their large body mass provides stability in the scrum - they are harder to push backwards. Their wide build and heavy frame anchor the scrum, allowing them to generate a powerful pushing force against the opposition pack. The extra weight provides a low centre of gravity, making them difficult to move or tackle. Without sufficient body mass, a prop would be pushed back in the scrum, undermining the entire forward pack.
  3. An ectomorph is ideal for marathon running because their low body weight reduces the energy cost of every stride - carrying less mass over 26.2 miles requires significantly less energy than a heavier build. Their light frame means less impact force on joints, reducing injury risk over the high mileage of training and racing. The lean build is more efficient for thermoregulation (heat dissipation) during prolonged exercise. Their long limbs can provide an efficient stride length without the burden of excess muscle or fat weight.
  4. A basketball player blends ectomorph and mesomorph somatotypes. The ectomorph component provides height, long limbs and a lean build - essential for reaching the basket, covering the court quickly and having a stride advantage. The mesomorph component provides muscular power for jumping, sprinting and the physical contact of defending and rebounding. Pure ectomorphs would lack the strength for the physical demands, while pure mesomorphs might not have the height and reach advantages.
  5. Most athletes are not purely one somatotype because different sports require a combination of physical characteristics that no single somatotype provides alone. For example, a rugby winger needs the muscular power of a mesomorph for tackling and acceleration, combined with the lean, lightweight qualities of an ectomorph for sprinting speed. A pure mesomorph would be too heavy for the speed required, while a pure ectomorph would lack the strength for tackles. The mesomorph-ectomorph blend creates the ideal build: strong enough for contact but lean enough for pace.
  6. The dominant somatotype is mesomorph. This body type is advantageous for swimming because: broad shoulders provide a powerful lever for the arm pull through the water, generating greater propulsive force; low body fat means less drag and resistance in the water, allowing faster movement; well-developed musculature (particularly in the upper body and core) produces the sustained power needed for all four swimming strokes; the mesomorph's efficient metabolism supports the high training volumes required for competitive swimming.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know all three somatotypes with their key physical characteristics
  • Give a specific sport AND position example for each somatotype
  • Remember: most athletes are a BLEND - always mention the dominant type plus others
  • Always explain WHY a somatotype is suited to a sport, not just that it is
  • Endomorph = fat/wide, Mesomorph = muscular/athletic, Ectomorph = lean/thin
  • When comparing sports positions, explain how the somatotype blend varies

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often describe somatotypes as categories rather than points on a continuum. Wrong: "A person is either an endomorph, mesomorph or ectomorph." Correct: "Most people are a blend of all three somatotypes, with one being dominant. An individual's somatotype is positioned on a continuum between the three extremes."

Students often list characteristics without explaining why they suit a sport. Wrong: "A mesomorph is good at sprinting because they are muscular." Correct: "A mesomorph is suited to sprinting because their muscular build and high proportion of fast-twitch muscle fibres generate explosive power, while their low body fat means less mass to accelerate, resulting in greater sprint speed."

4. Link your understanding of Somatotypes to real-world contexts where possible
5. For longer answers, plan your response to address all parts of the question

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think you need to be slim to be fit. Wrong: You need to be slim to be fit Correct: Fitness depends on the ability to meet demands placed on you. Sumo wrestlers, rugby forwards and powerlifters may have high body fat but are extremely fit for their sport.

Students often think drinking water during exercise is enough to stay hydrated. Wrong: Drinking water during exercise is enough to stay hydrated Correct: For prolonged exercise (over 60 minutes), electrolyte replacement may also be needed. Drinking too much plain water can cause hyponatraemia (dangerously low sodium).

Students often think obesity is just about eating too much. Wrong: Obesity is just about eating too much Correct: Obesity has multiple causes: genetics, socio-economic factors, sedentary lifestyle, medical conditions, and mental health. It is not simply a matter of willpower or overeating.
