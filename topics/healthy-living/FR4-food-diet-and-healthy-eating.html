🥗 Food, Diet and Healthy Eating

Meals, food vocabulary, healthy eating; partitive articles; opinions on diet and lifestyle

Key Fact: This topic covers key vocabulary and structures for food, diet and healthy eating. You need to know the topic vocabulary, be able to use it in all four skills (listening, speaking, reading, writing), and express opinions with reasons.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Structures

For Food, Diet and Healthy Eating, you must know:

  • Topic-specific vocabulary (nouns, verbs, adjectives)
  • Key grammar structures for this topic area
  • How to express and justify opinions
  • How to describe past, present and future related to this topic
  • Cultural knowledge about French-speaking countries and communities

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Write 4 sentences in French about food, diet and healthy eating.

Q2: Write a paragraph (40-60 words) giving your opinion on food, diet and healthy eating.

Q3: Translate into French: "I think food, diet and healthy eating is important because..."

✅ Answers

  1. Four sentences using topic vocabulary, with correct adjective agreement, articles and verb conjugation. Include at least two different tenses for Higher tier.
  2. A structured paragraph with: introduction (à mon avis...), opinion with reason (je pense que... parce que...), a different viewpoint (cependant...), and a conclusion (en conclusion...). Use connecting words and varied vocabulary.
  3. "Je pense que [topic] est important(e) parce que..." — ensure adjective agreement (important vs importante depending on noun gender), correct verb form, and give a specific reason.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Use a range of tenses (present, past, future) to access higher marks
  • Include opinions with reasons (je pense que... parce que...)
  • Check adjective agreement (gender and number) before finishing
  • Use complex structures: negation (ne...pas, ne...jamais), pronouns (le, la, les), conjunctions (cependant, donc, en plus)
  • Include cultural references to French-speaking countries
  • For writing: plan your response, check verb conjugations, re-read for errors

📝 Exam Technique

French Exam Tips:
For healthy living questions: Use partitive articles correctly (du, de la, des — some). Distinguish between faire de + sport and jouer à + sport. Use il faut / il ne faut pas for advice. Include the imperative for health advice. Use a range of opinions with justifications. Cover both positive and negative health habits.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often misuse faire de and jouer à. Wrong: Je joue du tennis / je fais à le foot Correct: Je joue au tennis (= jouer à + le sport) / je fais du foot (= faire de + le sport). Students also forget partitive articles change to de/d' after negation. Wrong: Je ne mange pas du porc. Correct: Je ne mange pas de porc.

Notes Détaillées

Les Aliments et les Partitifs

Food vocabulary is central to the healthy living topic. You must master the partitive articles: du (some masculine), de la (some feminine), de l' (before vowel), des (some plural). These translate as "some" or "any" in English and are used for unspecified quantities: Je mange du fromage (I eat some cheese), Elle boit de l'eau (She drinks some water). After negation, partitives become de/d': Je ne mange pas de viande (I don't eat meat) — this is a very common GCSE error.

French meal structure is culturally significant. Le petit-déjeuner (breakfast) is typically light — du pain with du beurre and de la confiture, or des croissants, with du café or du chocolat chaud. Le déjeuner (lunch) is the main meal, traditionally four courses: l'entrée (starter), le plat principal (main course), le fromage (cheese course), le dessert. Le dîner (dinner) is lighter. This structure differs from British eating habits.

For a balanced diet, use vocabulary like: les protéines (proteins), les glucides (carbohydrates), les lipides (fats), les vitamines (vitamins), les minéraux (minerals), les fibres (fibre). Express dietary advice with il faut (one must) and il ne faut pas (one must not): Il faut manger cinq fruits et légumes par jour.

Exemple

Pour être en bonne santé, il faut manger des fruits et des légumes tous les jours. Il ne faut pas manger trop de sucre ni trop de graisses. — To be healthy, you must eat fruits and vegetables every day. You must not eat too much sugar or too much fat.

Le Régime Français et la Gastronomie

The French régime alimentaire (diet) is famous worldwide. In 2010, UNESCO added le repas gastronomique des Français to its list of Intangible Cultural Heritage. This recognises the importance of food in French culture — meals are social events, not just fuel. The traditional French diet includes plenty of fresh vegetables, cheese (over 1,000 varieties), bread, and wine (in moderation). The paradoxe français refers to the observation that French people have relatively low rates of heart disease despite a diet rich in cheese and butter.

However, modern eating habits are changing. La malbouffe (junk food) and fast food chains have become more popular, especially among young people. L'obésité (obesity) is rising, though France's rate remains lower than the UK's. The French government has taken action: la taxe soda (soda tax) was introduced in 2012, and school canteens must follow strict nutritional guidelines — for example, le menu végétarien (vegetarian menu) must be offered at least once per week since 2019.

In francophone Africa, the diet is quite different. In Sénégal, le thiéboudienne (rice with fish and vegetables) is the national dish. In Côte d'Ivoire, l'attiéké (cassava couscous) with grilled fish is a staple. These dishes are naturally balanced and reflect local agriculture.

Exemple

Le repas gastronomique des Français est classé par l'UNESCO. Il comprend l'entrée, le plat principal, le fromage et le dessert. C'est un moment social très important. — The French gastronomic meal is classified by UNESCO. It includes the starter, the main course, the cheese and the dessert. It is a very important social moment.

Conseils pour une Alimentation Saine

Giving health advice in French requires the imperative and il faut/il ne faut pas. The imperative forms: Mangez (Eat — vous form), Bois (Drink — tu form), Évitez (Avoid). For negative imperatives: Ne mangez pas trop de sucre. The il faut construction is impersonal and works for general advice: Il faut boire beaucoup d'eau (One must drink a lot of water).

Use the conditional to give gentle advice: On devrait manger plus de légumes (One should eat more vegetables). Il vaudrait mieux éviter la malbouffe (It would be better to avoid junk food). These structures show language sophistication for Higher tier.

The French government's Programme National Nutrition Santé (PNNS) recommends: five fruits and vegetables per day, regular physical activity, limiting salt, sugar and saturated fats, and drinking water. The Nutri-Score system (A to E labels on food packaging) helps consumers make healthier choices — a good cultural point to mention.

Exemple

Il faut manger cinq portions de fruits et légumes par jour, boire beaucoup d'eau et faire du sport régulièrement. On devrait éviter la malbouffe et limiter les aliments transformés. — You must eat five portions of fruit and vegetables per day, drink plenty of water and do sport regularly. You should avoid junk food and limit processed foods.

Questions Supplémentaires

Q4: Qu'est-ce que vous mangez normalement dans une journée? Est-ce que votre régime est équilibré? (What do you normally eat in a day? Is your diet balanced?)

Q5: Comparez les habitudes alimentaires en France et au Royaume-Uni. (Compare eating habits in France and the UK.)

Réponses Supplémentaires

  1. Normalement, pour le petit-déjeuner, je mange du pain avec du beurre et de la confiture, et je bois du jus d'orange. À midi, je prends un sandwich à la cantine, mais ce n'est pas très sain. Le soir, ma mère prépare un repas équilibré avec des légumes, des protéines et du riz. Je pense que mon régime n'est pas assez équilibré parce que je mange trop de malbouffe. Je devrais manger plus de fruits et moins de chips. — Normally, for breakfast, I eat bread with butter and jam, and I drink orange juice. At noon, I have a sandwich in the canteen, but it's not very healthy. In the evening, my mother prepares a balanced meal with vegetables, protein and rice. I think my diet is not balanced enough because I eat too much junk food. I should eat more fruit and fewer crisps.
  2. En France, le repas est un moment social important. On prend le temps de manger, avec quatre plats au déjeuner. En Grande-Bretagne, les gens mangent souvent plus vite et le dîner est le repas principal. Cependant, la malbouffe devient plus populaire en France, surtout chez les jeunes. Tandis qu'au Royaume-Uni, le petit-déjeuner est souvent un repas chaud, en France, on mange plutôt du pain et des croissants. — In France, the meal is an important social moment. People take the time to eat, with four courses at lunch. In Great Britain, people often eat faster and dinner is the main meal. However, junk food is becoming more popular in France, especially among young people. Whereas in the UK, breakfast is often a hot meal, in France, people eat bread and croissants instead.
