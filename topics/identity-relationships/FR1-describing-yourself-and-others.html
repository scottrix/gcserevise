Key Fact: This topic covers key vocabulary and structures for describing yourself and others. You need to know the topic vocabulary, be able to use it in all four skills (listening, speaking, reading, writing), and express opinions with reasons.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Write 4 sentences in French about describing yourself and others.

Q2: Write a paragraph (40-60 words) giving your opinion on describing yourself and others.

Q3: Translate into French: "I think describing yourself and others is important because..."

✅ Answers

  1. Four sentences using topic vocabulary, with correct adjective agreement, articles and verb conjugation. Include at least two different tenses for Higher tier.
  2. A structured paragraph with: introduction (à mon avis...), opinion with reason (je pense que... parce que...), a different viewpoint (cependant...), and a conclusion (en conclusion...). Use connecting words and varied vocabulary.
  3. "Je pense que [topic] est important(e) parce que..." — ensure adjective agreement (important vs importante depending on noun gender), correct verb form, and give a specific reason.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Use a range of tenses (present, past, future) to access higher marks
  • Include opinions with reasons (je pense que... parce que...)
  • Check adjective agreement (gender and number) before finishing
  • Use complex structures: negation (ne...pas, ne...jamais), pronouns (le, la, les), conjunctions (cependant, donc, en plus)
  • Include cultural references to French-speaking countries
  • For writing: plan your response, check verb conjugations, re-read for errors

📝 Exam Technique

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often forget adjective agreement. Wrong: Elle est intelligent. Correct: Elle est intelligente. (Add -e for feminine.) Students also confuse mon/ma/mes. Wrong: Ma père Correct: Mon père (use mon before masculine nouns starting with a vowel for euphony).

Notes Détaillées

Les Adjectifs et l'Accord

In GCSE French, describing yourself and others requires a solid grasp of adjective agreement. French adjectives must agree in gender (masculine/feminine) and number (singular/plural) with the noun they describe. For most regular adjectives, you add -e for feminine and -s for plural: grand → grande → grands → grandes. However, many common adjectives are irregular — beau/belle, nouveau/nouvelle, vieux/vieille, blanc/blanche, and adjectives ending in -eux change to -euse (heureux → heureuse).

Position matters too: most adjectives follow the noun (une voiture rouge), but the BANGS adjectives (Beauty, Age, Number, Goodness, Size) come before: un beau jardin, une petite fille, un jeune homme. Getting this wrong is a common GCSE error. Remember that some adjectives change meaning depending on position: un grand homme (a great man) vs un homme grand (a tall man).

For Higher tier, use intensified adjectives to show range: très (very), assez (quite), vraiment (really), plutôt (rather). You can also use comparative structures: plus ... que (more... than), moins ... que (less... than), aussi ... que (as... as).

Exemple

Elle est grande, intelligente et très créative. Elle a les yeux bleus et les cheveux bruns. — She is tall, intelligent and very creative. She has blue eyes and brown hair.

Se Présenter et Décrire Quelqu'un

When introducing yourself in a GCSE speaking or writing exam, structure your response logically: start with basic information (Je m'appelle..., J'ai... ans, J'habite à...), then describe your appearance (Je suis grand(e), J'ai les cheveux...), your personality (Je suis sociable, gentil(le)), and finish with an opinion or extra detail. The reflexive verb se présenter (to introduce oneself) uses je me présente — reflexive verbs are a key GCSE grammar point.

To describe someone else, use il/elle est + adjective or il/elle a + feature. For a third person you know well, add the person's name and relationship: Mon meilleur ami s'appelle Thomas. Il est très drôle et généreux. Use possessive adjectives correctly: mon/ma/mes (my), son/sa/ses (his/her), notre/nos (our). Note: use mon/son before feminine nouns starting with a vowel or mute h (mon amie, not ma amie).

Cultural note: in France, greetings are important. When meeting someone, French people often do la bise — kissing on both cheeks. The number of kisses varies by region: two in Paris, three in the south, sometimes four in rural areas. In formal situations, shake hands (serrer la main).

Exemple

Je me présente — je m'appelle Léa, j'ai quinze ans et j'habite à Lyon. Je suis assez grande, j'ai les cheveux noirs et les yeux verts. Je suis timide mais aussi créative. — Let me introduce myself — my name is Léa, I'm fifteen and I live in Lyon. I'm quite tall, I have black hair and green eyes. I'm shy but also creative.

Les Descriptions Physiques et le Caractère en Profondeur

For top marks, go beyond basic descriptions. Use compound adjectives and nuanced vocabulary: instead of just grand, try de taille moyenne (medium height) or assez petit(e) (rather short). For hair: les cheveux bouclés (curly), les cheveux raides (straight), les cheveux frisés (wavy), les cheveux tressés (braided). For personality, aim for less common adjectives: loyal(e) (loyal), têtu(e) (stubborn), ambitieux/ambitieuse (ambitious), patient(e) (patient), sensible (sensitive).

In francophone cultures, names often reflect heritage. In Sénégal, you might meet someone called Amadou or Fatou; in Québec, Étienne or Geneviève; in Belgium, names like Jean-Pierre are common. Being aware of francophone naming conventions shows cultural knowledge that examiners value.

Use connectors to link your descriptions: et (and), mais (but), cependant (however), aussi (also), par contre (on the other hand). For Higher tier, add subordinate clauses: qui est (who is), qui a (who has) — Ma sœur, qui est très artistique, aime peindre.

Exemple

Mon cousin Étienne, qui habite à Montréal au Québec, est de taille moyenne avec les cheveux châtains et les yeux marron. Il est très ambitieux mais parfois un peu têtu. — My cousin Étienne, who lives in Montreal in Québec, is medium height with chestnut hair and brown eyes. He is very ambitious but sometimes a bit stubborn.

Questions Supplémentaires

Q4: Décris ta meilleure amie ou ton meilleur ami en français. Écris au moins 60 mots. (Describe your best friend in French. Write at least 60 words.)

Q5: Comparez-vous et votre frère ou sœur. Utilisez des comparatifs. (Compare yourself and your brother or sister. Use comparatives.)

Réponses Supplémentaires

  1. Ma meilleure amie s'appelle Camille. Elle a seize ans et elle habite à Marseille. Elle est grande et mince avec les cheveux bruns et les yeux bleus. Elle est très sociable, généreuse et loyale. Elle aime rire et elle est toujours drôle. Par contre, elle est parfois têtue. Je l'admire parce qu'elle est ambitieuse et travaille très dur à l'école. — My best friend is called Camille. She is sixteen and lives in Marseille. She is tall and slim with brown hair and blue eyes. She is very sociable, generous and loyal. She loves to laugh and she is always funny. On the other hand, she is sometimes stubborn. I admire her because she is ambitious and works very hard at school.
  2. Mon frère est plus âgé que moi — il a dix-huit ans et j'en ai quinze. Je suis plus grande que lui, mais il est plus fort. Je suis plus travailleuse à l'école, cependant il est plus drôle et plus sociable. Nous sommes aussi différents que semblables! — My brother is older than me — he is eighteen and I am fifteen. I am taller than him, but he is stronger. I am more hardworking at school, however he is funnier and more sociable. We are as different as we are similar!
