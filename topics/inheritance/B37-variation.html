B37: Variation

Foundation Higher

Causes of variation within and between species

Key Definitions

Variation: The differences between individuals within a species. Variation can be caused by genetic factors, environmental factors, or a combination of both.
Mutation: A change in the DNA sequence of a gene or chromosome. Most mutations have no effect, some are harmful, and a very small number are beneficial. Mutations are the ultimate source of new alleles.

Genetic Variation

Genetic variation is caused by differences in the alleles inherited from parents. The three sources of genetic variation are: mutations, meiosis (independent assortment and crossing over), and random fertilisation.
SourceHow It Causes Variation
MutationsChanges in the DNA sequence can create new alleles, producing new characteristics
MeiosisDuring meiosis, chromosomes are shuffled (independent assortment) and sections of DNA are exchanged (crossing over), producing genetically unique gametes
Random fertilisationAny sperm can fertilise any egg — the combination of alleles is random, producing genetically unique zygotes
Example 1: Genetic Variation from Meiosis

Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes. During meiosis, the way each pair lines up and separates is random (independent assortment). The number of possible combinations from meiosis alone is 223 = over 8 million different gametes. When you consider random fertilisation, the number of possible zygotes is 8 million × 8 million = over 70 trillion — which is why siblings can look so different from each other.

Environmental Variation

Environmental variation is caused by differences in the environment in which an organism lives and develops. Environmental factors include diet, exercise, climate, light, scars, and injuries.
Example 2: Environmental Variation
  • Diet: Two people with the same genes for height will grow to different heights if one has poor nutrition
  • Exercise: Identical twins have the same genes, but one who exercises will have larger muscles
  • Climate: Arctic hares grow white fur in winter and brown fur in summer (same genes, different environment)
  • Scars: A scar is caused by the environment (injury), not genes
  • Light: Plants grown in the dark become tall and pale (etiolation) — same genes, different light conditions

Example 3: Continuous Variation — Height

Human height is an example of continuous variation. People do not fall into “tall” or “short” categories — there is a smooth range of heights from very short to very tall. When plotted on a graph, height forms a normal distribution curve (bell-shaped), with most people near the middle and fewer at the extremes. Height is influenced by genes (many genes contribute) AND the environment (nutrition during childhood).

Example 4: Discontinuous Variation — Blood Group

Human blood group is an example of discontinuous variation. You can only have blood group A, B, AB, or O — there are no intermediate values. Blood group is controlled by a single gene with three alleles. The environment has no effect on blood group — it is entirely genetically determined.

Mutations

A mutation is a change in the DNA sequence of a gene or chromosome. Most mutations have no effect on the organism. Some mutations are harmful (e.g. causing cancer or genetic disorders). A very small number are beneficial (e.g. producing a new allele that gives a survival advantage).

Mutations can be caused by mutagens — agents that increase the rate of mutation:

  • Ionising radiation — X-rays, UV light, gamma rays
  • Chemical mutagens — certain chemicals in tobacco, asbestos, some industrial chemicals

Mutations occur spontaneously but are more likely when organisms are exposed to mutagens. Mutations are the ultimate source of new alleles and are essential for evolution, even though most are neutral or harmful.

Practice Questions

Q1: Foundation State three causes of genetic variation.

Q2: Foundation Explain the difference between continuous and discontinuous variation. Give one example of each.

Q3: Foundation Give two examples of environmental variation and explain how the environment causes the difference.

Q4: Higher Explain why height shows continuous variation but blood group shows discontinuous variation.

Q5: Higher What is a mutation? Name two mutagens and explain why most mutations have no effect.

Answers

  1. 1. Mutations — changes in DNA that create new alleles. 2. Meiosis — produces genetically unique gametes through independent assortment and crossing over. 3. Random fertilisation — any sperm can fertilise any egg, creating unique combinations of alleles.
  2. Continuous variation has a range of values with no distinct categories (e.g. height — any value between a range is possible). It produces a normal distribution curve and is influenced by both genes and environment. Discontinuous variation has distinct categories with no intermediate values (e.g. blood group — only A, B, AB, or O). It is controlled by genes alone and is not affected by the environment.
  3. 1. Muscle size: A person who exercises regularly will develop larger muscles than someone with the same genes who does not exercise — the environment (physical activity) causes the difference. 2. Plant growth in different light: A plant grown in bright light will be short and green, while a genetically identical plant grown in the dark will be tall and pale (etiolation) — the light environment causes the difference.
  4. Height is controlled by many genes (polygenic) and is also influenced by the environment (e.g. nutrition). The combination of many genes with different alleles plus environmental effects creates a smooth range of values — continuous variation. Blood group is controlled by a single gene with three alleles. The environment has no effect. Only four possible phenotypes exist (A, B, AB, O) with no intermediate values — discontinuous variation.
  5. A mutation is a change in the DNA sequence of a gene or chromosome. Two mutagens are: ionising radiation (e.g. UV light, X-rays) and chemical mutagens (e.g. chemicals in tobacco). Most mutations have no effect because: the genetic code is degenerate (multiple codons code for the same amino acid), so a change may not alter the protein produced; or the mutation may occur in non-coding DNA that does not affect protein production; or the change may not significantly affect the protein’s function.

Exam Tips

  • Remember: continuous variation = range of values + normal distribution + both genetic AND environmental. Discontinuous variation = distinct categories + genetic only. This is a very common exam question
  • When asked about the sources of genetic variation, mention all three: mutations, meiosis, and random fertilisation
  • Most characteristics are influenced by both genes and environment — only a few (like blood group) are purely genetic
  • Mutations are random — they are not caused by the environment. Mutagens increase the rate of mutations but don’t direct what the mutation will be
