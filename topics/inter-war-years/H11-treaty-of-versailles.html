H11: The Treaty of Versailles

Foundation Higher All Boards

The aims of the Big Three, the terms of the Treaty, German reactions, and the creation of the League of Nations.

🤝 The Big Three

Key Fact: The Treaty of Versailles was signed on 28 June 1919 in the Hall of Mirrors at the Palace of Versailles, exactly 5 years after the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand. It was primarily negotiated by three leaders: Wilson (USA), Clemenceau (France), and Lloyd George (Britain). Their conflicting aims made a balanced settlement almost impossible.
Leader Country Main Aims Motivation
Woodrow Wilson USA Fourteen Points; League of Nations; self-determination; fair and lasting peace Wanted to prevent future wars through international cooperation; idealistic approach
Georges Clemenceau France Punish Germany severely; weaken Germany permanently; recover Alsace-Lorraine; massive reparations France had suffered most: 1.3 million dead; northern France devastated; feared future German attack
David Lloyd George Britain Punish Germany but not too harshly; maintain British naval supremacy; protect trade; German recovery for trade Public wanted punishment; but Britain needed a strong Germany for trade; compromise position

Key Conflicts Between the Big Three

  • Wilson vs Clemenceau: Wilson wanted a fair peace based on the Fourteen Points; Clemenceau wanted to crush Germany. Wilson threatened to make a separate peace if Clemenceau was too harsh
  • Lloyd George vs Clemenceau: Lloyd George wanted Germany to recover economically for British trade; Clemenceau wanted to permanently weaken Germany
  • Wilson vs Lloyd George: Wilson wanted free trade; Lloyd George wanted to protect British imperial trade preferences. Both wanted control of German colonies
  • The Italian Prime Minister Orlando walked out when his territorial demands were not met
Example: Wilson's Fourteen Points

Wilson's Fourteen Points (January 1918) included: open diplomacy (no secret treaties); freedom of the seas; removal of trade barriers; reduction of armaments; self-determination for national groups; and a League of Nations. Germany had agreed to an armistice on the basis of the Fourteen Points, making the harsh Treaty feel like a betrayal. Many Germans asked: if the peace was based on the Fourteen Points, where was the fairness?

📜 The Terms of the Treaty

Key Fact: The Treaty of Versailles had five main categories: War Guilt, Reparations, Military restrictions, Territorial losses, and the League of Nations. Germans called it the "Diktat" (dictated peace) because they were not allowed to negotiate the terms.

1. War Guilt (Article 231)

Article 231 - The "War Guilt" Clause:
"Germany accepts the responsibility of Germany and her allies for causing all the loss and damage... imposed upon them by the aggression of Germany and her allies."

This clause was the foundation for reparations. Germany had to accept TOTAL blame for the war - which most Germans saw as deeply unfair, given that other nations had also contributed to the causes of WWI.

2. Reparations

  • Germany was required to pay for all civilian damages caused by the war
  • The final figure was set at £6.6 billion (132 billion gold marks) in 1921
  • Payments were to be made in annual instalments, in cash and in kind (coal, steel, timber)
  • Germany paid the first instalment of £50 million in 1921 but defaulted in 1922, leading to the Ruhr Crisis

3. Military Restrictions

Military Area Restriction
Army Limited to 100,000 men; no conscription (volunteers only); no tanks or military aircraft
Navy 6 battleships only; no submarines; 15,000 sailors maximum
Rhineland Demilitarised zone - no German troops or fortifications within 50 km of the Rhine; Allied troops stationed there for 15 years
Air Force Completely abolished - no military aircraft allowed
Weapons No import or manufacture of weapons, poison gas, or armoured vehicles

4. Territorial Losses

Territory Lost To Whom Significance
Alsace-Lorraine France Recovered by France; lost by France in 1871
Eupen and Malmédy Belgium Small border territories
Posen and West Prussia Poland Gave Poland access to the sea ("Polish Corridor"); separated East Prussia from the rest of Germany
Danzig Free City (League of Nations) Port city for Poland; Germans outraged
Saar League of Nations (15 years) Coal-rich region; France given coal mines; plebiscite after 15 years
All colonies Mandates to Britain, France, Japan Germany lost all overseas empire (Tanganyika, Cameroon, Togoland, Rwanda, Pacific islands)
Key Fact: Germany lost 13% of its European territory, 10% of its population, and all of its overseas empire. The "Polish Corridor" was particularly resented because it separated East Prussia from the rest of Germany and placed millions of ethnic Germans under Polish rule.

😡 German Reactions

Key Fact: Germans called the Treaty a "Diktat" (dictated peace) because they were given no opportunity to negotiate. The German delegation was shown the terms on 7 May 1919 and told to sign within 15 days. When they proposed modifications, the Allies rejected all significant changes. The German government initially refused to sign, but agreed after the Allies threatened to invade.

Why Germans Hated the Treaty

  • "Diktat": Germany was not allowed to negotiate - the terms were imposed
  • War Guilt: Article 231 was the most hated clause - Germans did not accept sole responsibility for the war
  • Reparations: £6.6 billion seemed impossible to pay and would cripple the economy for generations
  • Military humiliation: 100,000 men could not defend a major nation; the restrictions made Germany weak and vulnerable
  • Territorial losses: 13% of land lost; the Polish Corridor separated German territory; millions of Germans now lived under foreign rule
  • War guilt "betrayal": Germany had agreed to the Armistice based on Wilson's Fourteen Points; the Treaty violated many of these promises
Example: The "Stab-in-the-Back" Myth

Many Germans believed the army had not been defeated on the battlefield but had been "stabbed in the back" by civilian politicians who signed the Armistice and the Treaty. This myth was promoted by General Ludendorff and later exploited by Hitler. In reality, Germany's military situation was hopeless by 1918 - the Spring Offensive had failed, the Allies were advancing, and Germany was running out of supplies. But the myth was powerful because it allowed Germans to blame the Weimar Republic rather than accept military defeat.

🌍 Creation of the League of Nations

Key Fact: The League of Nations was created as part of the Treaty of Versailles. Wilson's Fourteenth Point called for "a general association of nations" to maintain peace. The League's Covenant (rules) was incorporated into all the peace treaties. However, the US Senate refused to ratify the Treaty, meaning America never joined the League - undermining it from the start.

Key Features of the League

  • Assembly: All member states met once a year; each had one vote; decisions required unanimity
  • Council: Permanent members (Britain, France, Italy, Japan) plus 4 non-permanent members; met 4-5 times per year to handle crises
  • Permanent Court of International Justice: Based at The Hague; could settle legal disputes between nations
  • International Labour Organisation (ILO): Aimed to improve working conditions worldwide
  • Secretariat: Administrative body based in Geneva; carried out the League's work
Why the USA Did Not Join:
The US Senate rejected the Treaty of Versailles and League membership because: (1) many Americans were isolationist - they wanted nothing to do with European conflicts; (2) they feared the League would drag America into future wars; (3) they objected to Article 10, which required members to defend other members; (4) there was political opposition to Wilson personally. Without American membership, the League was always weaker than Wilson intended.

⚖️ Was the Treaty Fair?

Arguments It Was Fair Arguments It Was Unfair
Germany had caused enormous damage: 1.3 million French dead; northern France devastated Germany was not solely responsible for WWI - all major powers contributed to the causes
Germany had imposed harsh terms on Russia at Brest-Litovsk (March 1918) The Treaty was a "Diktat" - Germany was not allowed to negotiate
The terms were less harsh than Germany imposed on France in 1871 Reparations of £6.6 billion were economically devastating and impossible to pay
Self-determination was applied to former German territories Self-determination was NOT applied to Germans in the Polish Corridor or Austria
Military restrictions were reasonable for a country that started a devastating war 100,000 troops left Germany defenceless and humiliated
Some territorial losses returned land to its rightful owners (Alsace-Lorraine to France) Germans were placed under foreign rule in the Polish Corridor and Sudetenland

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two reasons why Clemenceau wanted a harsh treaty. (4 marks)

Q2: How far were the Big Three satisfied with the Treaty of Versailles? (8 marks)

Q3: "The Treaty of Versailles was completely unfair to Germany." How far do you agree? (12 marks)

Q4: Explain why Germans hated the Treaty of Versailles. (8 marks)

Q5: "The Treaty of Versailles made another war inevitable." How far do you agree? (16 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. France had suffered the most in WWI: 1.3 million dead and northern France devastated by German occupation and fighting. Clemenceau feared future German attacks and wanted to permanently weaken Germany so it could never invade again. He also wanted to recover Alsace-Lorraine (lost in 1871) and gain massive reparations to rebuild France's destroyed regions.
  2. Clemenceau was partially satisfied: he gained Alsace-Lorraine, reparations, and military restrictions on Germany. However, he wanted even harsher terms - an independent Rhineland and the permanent breakup of Germany. Lloyd George was partially satisfied: he gained German colonies and naval restrictions, but worried the Treaty was too harsh and would cause future problems. Wilson was the least satisfied: the League of Nations was created, but the Senate refused to let America join; many of his Fourteen Points were ignored; the Treaty was far harsher than his idealistic vision.
  3. The Treaty was unfair in several respects: Germany was not allowed to negotiate (a "Diktat"); Article 231 assigned total war guilt, which was historically inaccurate; reparations of £6.6 billion were economically crippling; self-determination was not applied to Germans in the Polish Corridor; and the military restrictions left Germany vulnerable. However, some aspects were fair: Alsace-Lorraine was rightfully returned to France; Germany had itself imposed harsh terms on Russia at Brest-Litovsk; the damage caused by Germany justified compensation; and some territorial losses gave independence to peoples who wanted it. The Treaty was not "completely" unfair, but it was harsh enough to create lasting resentment.
  4. Germans hated the Treaty because: (1) Article 231 forced them to accept total war guilt, which they saw as a lie; (2) reparations of £6.6 billion seemed impossible to pay; (3) military restrictions to 100,000 men were humiliating for a proud nation; (4) territorial losses including the Polish Corridor separated German territory and placed Germans under foreign rule; (5) it was a "Diktat" - they could not negotiate; (6) they had expected a fair peace based on Wilson's Fourteen Points but received a punitive settlement; (7) the loss of all colonies and the Saar's coal mines damaged the economy. Every aspect of the Treaty was resented.
  5. The Treaty made another war more likely but not inevitable. It created the conditions for future conflict: German resentment fuelled revisionism; the "stab-in-the-back" myth undermined the Weimar Republic; economic hardship from reparations destabilised German democracy; the Polish Corridor and Sudetenland created ongoing disputes. However, war was not inevitable: the Dawes Plan (1924) and Locarno Pact (1925) showed that reconciliation was possible; the League of Nations provided a framework for resolving disputes; and many Germans accepted the Treaty in practice. It was the Wall Street Crash (1929), Hitler's rise (1933), and the policy of appeasement that actually caused WWII - the Treaty created grievances that Hitler exploited, but other paths were possible. The Treaty made war possible but not inevitable.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know the exact date: Treaty signed 28 June 1919 - 5 years after Franz Ferdinand's assassination
  • Always use the five categories: War Guilt, Reparations, Military, Territory, League of Nations
  • Reparations were £6.6 billion (132 billion gold marks) - set in 1921, not 1919
  • For "fair/unfair" questions, always give BOTH sides before making a judgement
  • The Brest-Litovsk comparison is powerful: Germany imposed harsher terms on Russia in March 1918
  • Article 231 is NOT the same as reparations - it's the WAR GUILT clause that PROVIDED the basis for reparations
  • The USA never joined the League - this is crucial for explaining the League's later failures

📝 Exam Technique

History Exam Tips:
For the Treaty of Versailles, know each term and its impact. For the League, distinguish between 1920s successes and 1930s failures. For appeasement, present both sides of the debate before reaching your own conclusion. Use specific dates and events.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think the treaty of versailles was entirely unfair. Wrong: The Treaty of Versailles was entirely unfair Correct: While harsh, some terms were reasonable. The reparations were later reduced. Germany's treatment of Russia at Brest-Litovsk 1918 was far harsher.

Students often think the league of nations was a complete failure. Wrong: The League of Nations was a complete failure Correct: The League had significant successes in the 1920s: resolving the Aaland Islands dispute, the Greece-Bulgaria border conflict, and extensive humanitarian work.

Students often think appeasement was simply cowardice. Wrong: Appeasement was simply cowardice Correct: Appeasement had logical reasons: Britain's military was unready, the public feared another war, and the USSR was seen as a greater threat than Germany.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

8 marks: Explain why the Treaty of Versailles was unpopular.

The Treaty of Versailles was deeply unpopular in Germany for several reasons. First, Article 231 — the War Guilt Clause — forced Germany to accept total responsibility for causing the war, which most Germans saw as deeply unfair given that all major powers had contributed to the causes of WWI. This clause provided the legal basis for reparations, making it doubly resented.

Second, Germany lost 13% of its European territory and 10% of its population. The Polish Corridor was particularly hated: it separated East Prussia from the rest of Germany and placed millions of ethnic Germans under Polish rule. Danzig was made a free city under League of Nations control. All overseas colonies were taken as mandates for Britain, France, and Japan. The Saar's coal mines were given to France for 15 years. Alsace-Lorraine was returned to France.

Third, military restrictions were humiliating: the army was limited to 100,000 men with no conscription, no tanks, no military aircraft, and no submarines. The Rhineland was demilitarised and occupied by Allied troops for 15 years. Germany was left virtually defenceless. Fourth, reparations were set at £6.6 billion (132 billion gold marks) in 1921, an amount Germans believed would cripple the economy for generations.

Finally, the Treaty was a "Diktat" — a dictated peace. Germany was not allowed to negotiate the terms and was given just 15 days to sign. The "stab-in-the-back" myth (Dolchstosslegende) spread by right-wing nationalists claimed the army had not been defeated on the battlefield but had been betrayed by civilian politicians who signed the Armistice and the Treaty. This myth was exploited by Hitler and undermined the Weimar Republic from the start. Germany had agreed to the Armistice based on Wilson's Fourteen Points, making the harsh Treaty feel like a betrayal.

Mark scheme: 2 marks per explained reason with specific evidence, range of reasons for top level
