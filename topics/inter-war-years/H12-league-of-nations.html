H12: The League of Nations

Foundation Higher All Boards

The League's Covenant, structure, membership problems, successes in the 1920s, and humanitarian work.

🌐 The Creation of the League

Key Fact: The League of Nations was created as part of the Treaty of Versailles in June 1919. It was Woodrow Wilson's idea - his Fourteenth Point called for "a general association of nations." The League's headquarters were in Geneva, Switzerland. Its first meeting was held on 15 November 1920 with 42 member states.

Wilson's Vision

  • Wilson believed that the League could prevent future wars through collective security
  • All member nations would agree to protect each other from aggression
  • Disputes would be settled through arbitration and negotiation, not war
  • The League would oversee disarmament and promote international cooperation
  • However, Wilson failed to persuade the US Senate to ratify the Treaty - America never joined the League
Example: America's Absence

The USA's failure to join the League was catastrophic for its credibility. Without America, the League lacked the world's most powerful economy and military. Wilson had a stroke in September 1919 while campaigning for the Treaty, and the Senate rejected it in March 1920 by 49-35 (7 votes short of the two-thirds majority needed). American isolationism meant the League could never call on US power to enforce its decisions.

📜 The Covenant

Key Fact: The League's Covenant (its founding document) set out its key principles: collective security, arbitration of disputes, disarmament, and improved living and working conditions worldwide. Article 10 of the Covenant committed all members to "respect and preserve" each other's territorial integrity.

Key Principles

  • Collective security: An attack on one member was an attack on all; members would act together to stop aggression
  • Arbitration: Disputes would be settled by the Permanent Court of International Justice, not by war
  • Disarmament: The League would encourage all nations to reduce their armaments
  • Sanctions: The League could impose economic sanctions (stopping trade) on aggressor nations
  • Moral condemnation: The League could publicly condemn aggression, relying on the power of world opinion
The League's Three Weapons:
1. Moral condemnation - publicly condemning an aggressor (weakest weapon)
2. Economic sanctions - cutting off trade with the aggressor (moderate weapon)
3. Military force - sending an army to stop the aggressor (strongest weapon - but the League had NO army of its own and had to rely on member states)

🏛️ Structure of the League

Body Membership Role Frequency
The Assembly All member states; each had one vote Discussed international issues; admitted new members; approved the budget Met once a year in Geneva
The Council Permanent members (Britain, France, Italy, Japan) plus 4-9 non-permanent members Main decision-making body; dealt with crises and emergencies Met several times a year and in emergencies
Permanent Court of International Justice 15 judges from different nations Settled legal disputes between nations; gave advisory opinions Based at The Hague, Netherlands
Secretariat Permanent officials Administrative work; prepared reports; organised meetings Based in Geneva; permanent
International Labour Organisation (ILO) Government, employer, and worker representatives Improved working conditions worldwide; set international labour standards Met annually
Key Fact: The Council was the most powerful body. It had permanent members (Britain, France, Italy, Japan) who could veto decisions. However, ALL Council decisions had to be UNANIMOUS - any member could veto action. This meant the League was often paralysed by disagreement. A single "no" vote could prevent the League from acting against an aggressor.

⚠️ Membership Problems

Key Fact: The League's membership was fundamentally flawed from the start. The USA never joined; Germany was excluded until 1926; the USSR was excluded until 1934; and Japan and Italy left in the 1930s. No organisation can maintain world peace without the world's most powerful nations.
Nation Membership Status Why It Mattered
USA NEVER joined (Senate rejected Treaty of Versailles) World's strongest economy; most powerful military; absence destroyed League credibility
Germany Excluded 1919-1926; left 1933 Europe's largest economy; key to European peace; exclusion was a deliberate humiliation
USSR Excluded 1919-1934; expelled 1939 Major power; Communist state distrusted by Western democracies; excluded for ideological reasons
Italy Founding member; left 1937 Permanent Council member; left after Abyssinia sanctions failed
Japan Founding member; left 1933 Permanent Council member; left after condemnation over Manchuria
Fundamental Weaknesses of the League:
1. No army - could not enforce decisions militarily
2. No USA - lacked the world's most powerful nation
3. Unanimity rule - any member could veto action
4. No authority over non-members - could not act against non-members
5. Self-interest - member nations prioritised their own interests over collective security
6. Tied to Versailles - seen as a "winners' club" enforcing a harsh peace treaty

✅ Successes of the 1920s

Key Fact: The League was more successful in the 1920s than is often remembered. It resolved several international disputes peacefully and carried out important humanitarian work. The problems of the 1930s should not overshadow the genuine achievements of the 1920s.

Successful Dispute Settlements

Dispute Year What Happened League's Action
Aaland Islands 1921 Both Sweden and Finland claimed the islands (population was Swedish-speaking) League awarded islands to Finland; Sweden accepted peacefully
Upper Silesia 1921 Disputed territory between Germany and Poland; both claimed the industrial area League divided the territory based on a plebiscite; both sides accepted
Greece-Bulgaria 1925 Greek troops invaded Bulgaria after a border incident League condemned Greece; ordered withdrawal; Greece complied and paid compensation
Memel 1923 Lithuania seized the port of Memel from Allied control League arranged settlement: Memel given to Lithuania as autonomous region
Example: The Aaland Islands 1921

The Aaland Islands in the Baltic Sea were claimed by both Sweden and Finland. The population was Swedish-speaking but the islands had been governed by Finland (then part of Russia). When Finland became independent, the islanders wanted to join Sweden. The League investigated and decided the islands should belong to Finland but with guarantees of cultural autonomy for the Swedish-speaking population. Both sides accepted. This showed the League at its best: peaceful investigation, fair compromise, and both parties accepting the outcome.

Why the League Was More Successful in the 1920s

  • The 1920s were a period of relative international cooperation - the "Locarno Spring"
  • The disputes were minor and involved small nations - neither had the power to defy the League
  • Major powers were willing to support the League's decisions because it suited their interests
  • The economic prosperity of the 1920s reduced international tensions
  • The horrors of WWI were still fresh in people's memories, making nations more willing to compromise

💉 Humanitarian Work

The League's humanitarian achievements were among its most lasting successes:

Area Achievement
Health Organisation Combatted malaria, typhus, and leprosy; improved public health in developing countries; standardised medicines
Refugees Fridtjof Nansen helped repatriate 425,000 prisoners of war after WWI; the "Nansen Passport" gave identity documents to stateless refugees
International Labour Organisation Won 8-hour working day in many countries; banned lead paint; introduced minimum wage; 77 conventions adopted by 1939
Drug trafficking Reduced opium production; established controls on dangerous drugs; shut down drug trafficking networks
Slavery Freed over 200,000 slaves in Sierra Leone and Liberia; campaigned against forced labour worldwide
Transport Standardised road signs and traffic rules; introduced international shipping lanes; improved aviation safety
Key Fact: Fridtjof Nansen, the League's High Commissioner for Refugees, won the Nobel Peace Prize in 1922 for his work. The "Nansen Passport" he created provided identity documents for stateless refugees and was recognised by over 50 countries. The ILO won the Nobel Peace Prize in 1969 for its decades of work improving labour conditions.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two weaknesses of the League of Nations. (4 marks)

Q2: How important was America's absence from the League of Nations? (8 marks)

Q3: "The League of Nations was a complete failure." How far do you agree? (12 marks)

Q4: Explain why the League was more successful in the 1920s than the 1930s. (8 marks)

Q5: "The League's humanitarian work was its greatest achievement." How far do you agree? (16 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. The League had no army of its own - it could not enforce decisions militarily and had to rely on member states to provide troops, who often refused. The unanimity rule meant that any Council member could veto action, so a single "no" vote could prevent the League from responding to aggression.
  2. America's absence was critically important. Without the USA, the League lacked the world's most powerful economy and military. Economic sanctions were weaker without American trade; other powers knew the League could not call on American support; and the League's moral authority was undermined. However, even with America, the League might still have struggled: the unanimity rule would still have applied; national self-interest would still have undermined collective security. America's absence was devastating but not the only weakness.
  3. The League was NOT a complete failure. In the 1920s, it successfully resolved disputes (Aaland Islands 1921, Greece-Bulgaria 1925, Upper Silesia 1921). Its humanitarian work was genuinely impressive: Nansen helped 425,000 refugees; the ILO improved working conditions; the Health Organisation combatted disease; and 200,000 slaves were freed. However, the League failed catastrophically in the 1930s: it could not stop Japan in Manchuria (1931) or Italy in Abyssinia (1935); it failed on disarmament; and it collapsed as a peacekeeping body. It was not a "complete" failure, but it failed in its primary purpose of preventing war.
  4. The League was more successful in the 1920s because: the international climate was more cooperative (Locarno Spring); the disputes involved small nations who could not defy the League; the major powers supported League decisions because it suited their interests; and the economic prosperity reduced tensions. In the 1930s, the Great Depression made nations more aggressive and self-interested; the disputes involved major powers who could defy the League; the rise of dictatorships undermined the cooperative spirit; and the League's weaknesses were exposed by serious crises.
  5. The humanitarian work was certainly impressive: the Health Organisation, ILO, Nansen's refugee work, anti-slavery campaigns, and drug control all achieved real results that outlasted the League itself. The ILO still exists today as a UN agency. However, the League's PRIMARY purpose was to prevent war through collective security - and in this it failed completely. If the League's greatest achievement was humanitarian rather than peacekeeping, that demonstrates how far it fell short of Wilson's vision. The humanitarian work was important but secondary to the League's core mission.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • The League's THREE WEAPONS (moral condemnation, economic sanctions, military force) are essential to know
  • Always distinguish between 1920s successes and 1930s failures - the League's record is not uniformly bad
  • Aaland Islands (1921) and Greece-Bulgaria (1925) are the best examples of League successes
  • The UNANIMITY RULE is crucial - any member could veto any action, making the League powerless
  • America's absence is the single most important weakness - always include it in your answer
  • The League had NO ARMY - this is fundamental to understanding why it could not stop aggression
  • The humanitarian work (Nansen, ILO, Health Organisation) is often overlooked but important for balanced answers

📝 Exam Technique

History Exam Tips:
For the Treaty of Versailles, know each term and its impact. For the League, distinguish between 1920s successes and 1930s failures. For appeasement, present both sides of the debate before reaching your own conclusion. Use specific dates and events.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think the treaty of versailles was entirely unfair. Wrong: The Treaty of Versailles was entirely unfair Correct: While harsh, some terms were reasonable. The reparations were later reduced. Germany's treatment of Russia at Brest-Litovsk 1918 was far harsher.

Students often think the league of nations was a complete failure. Wrong: The League of Nations was a complete failure Correct: The League had significant successes in the 1920s: resolving the Aaland Islands dispute, the Greece-Bulgaria border conflict, and extensive humanitarian work.

Students often think appeasement was simply cowardice. Wrong: Appeasement was simply cowardice Correct: Appeasement had logical reasons: Britain's military was unready, the public feared another war, and the USSR was seen as a greater threat than Germany.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

8 marks: Explain why the League was weak from the start.

The League of Nations was fundamentally weak from its creation in 1920 for several structural reasons. First, the USA never joined. Despite the League being Woodrow Wilson's idea — his Fourteenth Point — the US Senate rejected the Treaty of Versailles in March 1920 by 49-35, 7 votes short of the two-thirds majority needed. Without America, the League lacked the world's most powerful economy and military, making economic sanctions far less effective and undermining the League's credibility from the outset.

Second, the USSR was excluded until 1934. The world's largest country and a major military power was kept out because the Western democracies distrusted the Communist state. This meant the League could not call on Soviet support against aggression and pushed Stalin towards making his own arrangements — culminating in the Nazi-Soviet Pact of August 1939.

Third, the League had no army of its own. It relied on member states to provide troops to enforce its decisions, but members were reluctant to commit forces. Without an independent military capability, the League's strongest weapon — military action against aggressors — was essentially bluff. As the Abyssinian crisis showed, without forces to back up its condemnation, the League was powerless.

Fourth, the unanimity rule meant that any Council member could veto action with a single "no" vote. The Council's permanent members (Britain, France, Italy, Japan) could each block decisions that threatened their interests. This paralysed the League when decisive action was needed. The League was also tied to the Treaty of Versailles, making it appear as a "winners' club" enforcing a harsh peace rather than a genuinely impartial international body. In conclusion, these built-in weaknesses meant the League was structurally incapable of maintaining collective security when faced with determined aggression by major powers.

Mark scheme: 2 marks per explained weakness with specific evidence, range of factors for top level
