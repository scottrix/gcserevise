H15: Appeasement

Foundation Higher All Boards

The reasons for appeasement, Chamberlain's policy, the Munich Agreement, arguments for and against, and the end of appeasement.

🤝 What Was Appeasement?

Key Fact: Appeasement was the policy of giving in to some of Hitler's demands in the hope of avoiding war. It was most associated with British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain (1937-1940), though it had been practised by earlier governments. The policy was based on the belief that some of Germany's grievances were legitimate and that negotiated concessions could satisfy Hitler and prevent another devastating war.

Key Principles of Appeasement

  • Some of Hitler's demands were reasonable - Germany had been treated too harshly at Versailles
  • Negotiation and concession were better than war
  • By giving Hitler what he wanted, he would be satisfied and stop making further demands
  • War must be avoided at all costs - the horrors of WWI were still fresh in people's minds
  • Britain and France needed time to rearm
Example: Chamberlain's Belief

Chamberlain genuinely believed that Hitler was a reasonable statesman who could be negotiated with. After the Munich Agreement, he declared: "I believe it is peace for our time. We thank you from the bottom of our hearts. Go home and get a nice quiet sleep." He believed that by addressing German grievances, he had removed the causes of war. Within 6 months, Hitler had seized the rest of Czechoslovakia, proving Chamberlain catastrophically wrong.

📋 Reasons for Appeasement

Key Fact: Appeasement was not simply cowardice or stupidity. It was a rational policy based on genuine concerns: war weariness, military unpreparedness, economic problems, fear of communism, and the belief that some German grievances were legitimate.

1. War Weariness

  • The memory of WWI was still raw: 750,000 British dead, 1.3 million French dead, millions more wounded
  • Public opinion was strongly anti-war - the Oxford Union voted in 1933 that "this House will not fight for King and Country"
  • The Peace Pledge Union gained 100,000 members who swore never to support war
  • Many people believed another war would destroy civilisation

2. Military Unpreparedness

Country Military Situation in 1938
Britain Small army; RAF needed expansion; no continental army; only began serious rearmament in 1938
France Army was large but defensive (Maginot Line mentality); politically unstable; no offensive capability without Britain
Germany Army of 550,000+; growing Luftwaffe; but NOT yet ready for a long war in 1938

3. Economic Problems

  • The Great Depression had devastated economies; Britain had 2 million unemployed in 1938
  • Many believed military spending would damage economic recovery
  • The cost of rearmament was enormous - Britain spent £1.5 billion on defence between 1933-1939
  • Some economists argued that another war would bankrupt the nation

4. Fear of Communism

  • Many British conservatives saw Hitler as a bulwark against Soviet communism
  • Stalin's USSR was seen as a greater threat than Nazi Germany by some
  • Hitler's anti-Communism was viewed positively by right-wing politicians
  • Some hoped a strong Germany would contain the spread of Soviet influence

5. Belief in Fairness

  • Many believed the Treaty of Versailles had been too harsh on Germany
  • German grievances about the Polish Corridor, reparations, and disarmament were seen as legitimate
  • Self-determination for Germans in Austria and the Sudetenland seemed reasonable
  • Appeasement was seen as correcting the mistakes of Versailles, not as weakness
The Five Reasons for Appeasement:
1. War weariness - the horror of WWI made people desperate to avoid another war
2. Military unpreparedness - Britain and France were not ready to fight in 1938
3. Economic problems - the Depression had left economies weak
4. Fear of communism - some preferred Hitler to Stalin
5. Belief in fairness - some German grievances seemed legitimate

🏛️ Chamberlain's Policy

Key Fact: Neville Chamberlain became Prime Minister in May 1937 and made appeasement the cornerstone of British foreign policy. He believed that personal diplomacy could solve international problems and conducted his own negotiations with Hitler, bypassing the Foreign Office.

Key Steps in Appeasement

Date Action Chamberlain's Justification
June 1935 Anglo-German Naval Agreement Limiting German naval expansion was better than unrestricted rearmament
November 1937 Lord Halifax praised Hitler's achievements Building a relationship with Hitler through personal diplomacy
March 1938 Accepted Anschluss without protest Austrians were Germans; self-determination seemed reasonable
September 1938 Three visits to Germany; Munich Agreement Avoided war; addressed legitimate German grievances; "peace for our time"

Chamberlain's Character and Approach

  • He was a successful businessman who believed in rational negotiation
  • He distrusted the Foreign Office and conducted diplomacy personally
  • He underestimated Hitler - he believed Hitler was a reasonable man who could be trusted
  • He was determined to avoid war at almost any cost
  • He was NOT simply a coward - he genuinely believed appeasement was the best way to preserve peace

⚖️ Arguments For and Against Appeasement

Arguments FOR Appeasement Arguments AGAINST Appeasement
It avoided war in 1938 when Britain was unprepared - the RAF needed time It encouraged Hitler - each concession made him more confident and demanding
Some German grievances were legitimate - Versailles had been too harsh It was based on trusting Hitler, who had proved he could not be trusted
It bought time for British rearmament - especially radar and Spitfire production It abandoned Czechoslovakia, a democratic ally with a strong army and defences
Public opinion was anti-war - democracy required the government to reflect this It showed weakness to Hitler and Mussolini, encouraging further aggression
The USSR was a greater long-term threat than Germany (some argued) It was morally wrong to abandon smaller nations to Hitler
War in 1938 might have been lost - Britain's defences were not ready If Britain and France had stood firm at Munich, Hitler might have backed down
Self-determination for Sudeten Germans seemed reasonable It allowed Hitler to gain the Czech army's weapons and the Skoda arms works
Key Fact: The strongest argument for appeasement was that it bought time for rearmament. In 1938, Britain had only 5 radar stations and very few Spitfires. By September 1939, the radar chain was complete and the RAF had over 300 Spitfires and Hurricanes. However, Germany also used the time to rearm, so the balance of power may not have changed significantly in Britain's favour.

🔚 The End of Appeasement

Key Fact: Appeasement ended on 31 March 1939 when Chamberlain guaranteed Poland's independence, promising that Britain would defend Poland if Germany attacked. This was a direct response to Hitler's seizure of Czechoslovakia on 15 March 1939, which proved that his aims went beyond self-determination and that he could not be trusted.

Why Appeasement Was Abandoned

  • Czechoslovakia (March 1939): Hitler seized non-German territory, proving his aims were conquest, not self-determination
  • Broken promises: At Munich, Hitler had promised the Sudetenland was his "last territorial demand." He broke this within 6 months
  • Public opinion shifted: The British public now saw Hitler as an untrustworthy aggressor
  • Military preparations: Britain had made progress on rearmament and felt more confident
  • Memel (March 1939): Hitler seized the Lithuanian port of Memel just days after Czechoslovakia
  • Italian invasion of Albania (April 1939): Showed Mussolini was also an aggressor

The Guarantee to Poland (31 March 1939)

  • Chamberlain announced that Britain and France would guarantee Polish independence
  • This was a clear warning to Hitler that further aggression would mean war
  • However, Britain and France had no practical way to help Poland if it was attacked
  • The guarantee was more a political statement than a military commitment
  • It also failed to secure a military alliance with the USSR, which would have been the most effective deterrent
Example: The Failure to Ally with the USSR

After the guarantee to Poland, Britain and France attempted to negotiate an alliance with the USSR. However, the talks dragged on through the summer of 1939 and ultimately failed. Stalin was suspicious of British motives (they had appeased Hitler for years); the Poles refused to allow Soviet troops on Polish territory; and the British sent a low-level delegation by slow boat rather than by air. Hitler, by contrast, moved quickly and signed the Nazi-Soviet Pact on 23 August 1939. The failure to ally with the USSR was perhaps the greatest failure of the post-appeasement period.

📊 Appeasement: Assessment

Was Appeasement Justified?
In 1938: It seemed reasonable - Britain was unprepared, public opinion opposed war, and German grievances appeared legitimate. Avoiding war was a sensible goal.

With hindsight: It was a disaster - it encouraged Hitler, abandoned allies, and convinced Hitler that Britain and France would not resist. The Munich Agreement was the low point.

Balanced view: Appeasement was understandable given the circumstances but failed because it misjudged Hitler's character and aims. It might have worked with a reasonable leader, but Hitler was determined on war and conquest.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two reasons why Britain followed a policy of appeasement. (4 marks)

Q2: How far was appeasement justified? (8 marks)

Q3: "Appeasement was cowardly and misguided." How far do you agree? (12 marks)

Q4: Explain why appeasement was abandoned in March 1939. (8 marks)

Q5: "Chamberlain was wrong to pursue appeasement." How far do you agree? (16 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. War weariness: the memory of WWI (750,000 British dead) made the public and politicians desperate to avoid another war. Military unpreparedness: Britain's armed forces were not ready for war in 1938 - the RAF needed expansion and the army was too small for continental warfare. Appeasement bought time for rearmament.
  2. Appeasement was partially justified in 1938: Britain was militarily unprepared; the RAF needed time to develop radar and Spitfires; public opinion strongly opposed war; and some German grievances (especially self-determination for Sudeten Germans) appeared legitimate. However, it was also misguided: it encouraged Hitler by showing that aggression would not be resisted; it abandoned Czechoslovakia, a valuable ally; it was based on trusting Hitler, who proved untrustworthy; and it failed to recognise that Hitler's aims went far beyond Versailles revision. With hindsight, appeasement was a mistake, but the alternatives in 1938 were limited.
  3. Appeasement was not cowardly - it was a rational policy based on genuine concerns: avoiding another catastrophic war, military unpreparedness, and the belief that some German grievances were legitimate. Chamberlain was brave enough to fly to Germany three times to negotiate. However, it was misguided because it fundamentally misjudged Hitler. It assumed he was a reasonable statesman who could be satisfied by concessions, when in fact he was determined on conquest. Each concession encouraged further demands. The abandonment of Czechoslovakia at Munich was particularly misguided, as it gave Hitler the Czech army's weapons. Appeasement was understandable but based on a fatal miscalculation.
  4. Appeasement was abandoned because Hitler's seizure of Czechoslovakia (15 March 1939) proved that his aims were conquest, not self-determination. He had broken his Munich promise that the Sudetenland was his "last territorial demand." The British public and Parliament now saw Hitler as an untrustworthy aggressor. The seizure of Memel and Italy's invasion of Albania reinforced the threat. Chamberlain issued the guarantee to Poland (31 March 1939) as a clear warning that further aggression would mean war. Appeasement had failed because Hitler could not be trusted to keep his promises.
  5. Chamberlain was wrong to pursue appeasement in the sense that it misjudged Hitler's character and aims. He trusted a dictator who had already shown his ruthlessness (Night of the Long Knives, persecution of Jews). Each concession made Hitler stronger and more confident. The Munich Agreement abandoned a democratic ally and gave Germany Czech military assets. However, Chamberlain was right that Britain needed time to rearm - the RAF was significantly stronger in September 1939 than in September 1938. He also reflected genuine public opinion, which was overwhelmingly anti-war. The real error was not appeasement itself but failing to recognise when to stop - after the Rhineland (1936) or certainly after Czechoslovakia (March 1939), it should have been clear that Hitler could not be satisfied.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know the five reasons for appeasement: war weariness, military weakness, economic problems, fear of communism, belief in fairness
  • Chamberlain's "peace for our time" quote (30 September 1938) is essential
  • Always give BALANCED arguments - appeasement had genuine justifications even if it was ultimately wrong
  • The Czech takeover (March 1939) is the turning point when appeasement was abandoned
  • Link appeasement to the wider failures of the League of Nations - they are connected topics
  • The guarantee to Poland (31 March 1939) marked the formal end of appeasement
  • For "was appeasement justified" questions, always consider what the alternatives were at the time

📝 Exam Technique

History Exam Tips:
For the Treaty of Versailles, know each term and its impact. For the League, distinguish between 1920s successes and 1930s failures. For appeasement, present both sides of the debate before reaching your own conclusion. Use specific dates and events.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think the treaty of versailles was entirely unfair. Wrong: The Treaty of Versailles was entirely unfair Correct: While harsh, some terms were reasonable. The reparations were later reduced. Germany's treatment of Russia at Brest-Litovsk 1918 was far harsher.

Students often think the league of nations was a complete failure. Wrong: The League of Nations was a complete failure Correct: The League had significant successes in the 1920s: resolving the Aaland Islands dispute, the Greece-Bulgaria border conflict, and extensive humanitarian work.

Students often think appeasement was simply cowardice. Wrong: Appeasement was simply cowardice Correct: Appeasement had logical reasons: Britain's military was unready, the public feared another war, and the USSR was seen as a greater threat than Germany.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: How far was appeasement justified?

Appeasement was justified to some extent by the circumstances of the 1930s. Britain was militarily unprepared: in 1938, the RAF had only 5 radar stations and very few Spitfires. By September 1939, the radar chain was complete and the RAF had over 300 Spitfires and Hurricanes. Appeasement bought vital time for rearmament. The memory of WWI was also powerful — 750,000 British dead and 1.3 million French dead made the public desperate to avoid another war. The Oxford Union voted in 1933 that "this House will not fight for King and Country." The Peace Pledge Union gained 100,000 members. The Great Depression had left economies weak: Britain had 2 million unemployed in 1938, and rearmament cost £1.5 billion between 1933 and 1939. Furthermore, some German grievances seemed legitimate — the Treaty of Versailles had been harsh, and self-determination for Germans in Austria and the Sudetenland appeared reasonable. Some conservatives even saw Hitler as a bulwark against Soviet communism.

However, appeasement was not justified in practice because it fundamentally misjudged Hitler. Each concession emboldened him: the Rhineland (March 1936) encouraged Anschluss (March 1938), which encouraged the Sudetenland demand (September 1938). At Munich, Chamberlain abandoned Czechoslovakia — a democratic ally with 34 army divisions and defensive fortifications — without a fight. Hitler then seized the rest of Czechoslovakia on 15 March 1939, proving his promise of "no more territorial demands" was a lie. This gave Germany the Czech army's equipment, including 469 tanks and 1,000 aircraft, and the Skoda armaments works. Appeasement was based on the assumption that Hitler was a reasonable statesman who could be satisfied — but his aims were conquest (Lebensraum), not merely Versailles revision. If Britain and France had stood firm at the Rhineland in 1936, when Hitler's forces were too weak to fight, he would have had to withdraw.

In conclusion, appeasement was understandable given war weariness, military weakness, and economic problems, but it was not justified because it encouraged aggression, abandoned allies, and was based on a fatal miscalculation about Hitler's character and intentions. It might have worked with a reasonable leader, but Hitler was determined on war and conquest.

Mark scheme: 3-4 marks per explained argument with specific evidence, balanced judgement for top level
