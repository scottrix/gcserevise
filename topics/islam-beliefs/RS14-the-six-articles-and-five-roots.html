RS14: The Six Articles and Five Roots

All Boards

The six articles of faith in Sunni Islam; the five roots of Usul ad-Din in Shi'a Islam; key similarities and differences

☪️ The Six Articles and Five Roots

Key Fact: Sunni and Shi'a Muslims have different frameworks for their core beliefs. Sunni Islam is based on the Six Articles of Faith; Shi'a Islam is based on the Five Roots of Usul ad-Din. They share most beliefs but emphasise different aspects.

📋 The Six Articles of Faith (Sunni)

  1. Tawhid — the oneness of God
  2. Mala'ikah — belief in angels
  3. Kutub — belief in the holy books (Qur'an, Torah, Psalms, Gospel)
  4. Rusul — belief in the prophets (from Adam to Muhammad)
  5. Akhirah — belief in life after death
  6. Qadr — belief in predestination (God's decree)

📋 The Five Roots of Usul ad-Din (Shi'a)

  1. Tawhid — the oneness of God
  2. Risalah — prophethood (the prophets from Adam to Muhammad)
  3. Nubuwwah — the prophets and the special role of Muhammad as the final prophet
  4. Imamate — belief that God appointed twelve Imams to guide the Muslim community after Muhammad
  5. Qiyamah — the Day of Judgement and life after death
Exam Tip: Both traditions share belief in Tawhid, prophethood and the afterlife. The key difference is the Imamate — Shi'a Muslims believe God appointed twelve Imams as spiritual leaders after Muhammad, while Sunni Muslims do not accept this. Sunni Islam includes belief in angels and holy books as separate articles; Shi'a Islam combines prophethood into one root.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two differences between Sunni and Shi'a beliefs.

Q2: Why do Sunni and Shi'a Muslims have different beliefs?

✅ Answers

  1. 1) The Imamate — Shi'a Muslims believe God appointed twelve Imams to guide the Muslim community after Muhammad's death. These Imams are infallible and divinely chosen. Sunni Muslims do not accept the Imamate and believe leadership should be determined by the community. 2) Structure of belief — Sunni Islam organises core beliefs into the Six Articles of Faith (including angels and holy books as separate articles), while Shi'a Islam organises them into the Five Roots of Usul ad-Din (combining prophethood into one root and adding the Imamate as a distinct root).
  2. The division originated after Muhammad's death in 632 CE over who should lead the Muslim community. Sunni Muslims believed the leader should be chosen by the community (Abu Bakr was selected). Shi'a Muslims believed leadership should pass through Muhammad's family (Ali, his cousin and son-in-law). Over centuries, this political division developed into theological differences, with Shi'a Islam developing the doctrine of the Imamate and distinct beliefs about religious authority.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Islam beliefs questions: Distinguish between Sunni (Six Articles of Faith) and Shi'a (Five Roots of Usul ad-Din) beliefs. Use Arabic terms precisely (tawhid, risalah, akhirah). Quote the Qur'an where relevant (e.g. Surah 112 for tawhid). Explain both similarities and differences between Sunni and Shi'a perspectives.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse Sunni and Shi'a beliefs about predestination. Wrong: "All Muslims believe everything is predestined and humans have no free will." Correct: "Most Muslims believe God has foreknowledge but humans have free will to choose; the balance between predestination and free will is understood differently across traditions."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that the six articles of faith in sunni islam; the five roots of usul ad-din in shi'a islam; key similarities and differences.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
