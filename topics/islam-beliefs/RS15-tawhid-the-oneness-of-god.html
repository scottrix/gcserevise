RS15: Tawhid: The Oneness of God

All Boards

The nature of God: omnipotence, beneficence, mercy, fairness and justice; immanence and transcendence

☪️ Tawhid: The Oneness of God

Key Fact: Tawhid is the central belief of Islam — there is only one God (Allah), with no partners, equals or divisions. It is the first part of the Shahadah: "There is no god but Allah." Surah 112 (Al-Ikhlas) expresses tawhid: "He is Allah, the One and Only."

🌟 The Nature of Allah

Muslims believe Allah has 99 beautiful names (Asma ul-Husna) that describe his nature:

  • Ar-Rahman (The Beneficent) — Allah is merciful and compassionate to all
  • Ar-Rahim (The Merciful) — Allah shows special mercy to believers
  • Al-Alim (The All-Knowing) — Allah knows everything past, present and future
  • Al-Adl (The Just) — Allah is perfectly fair and will judge justly
  • Al-Qadir (The All-Powerful) — Allah can do anything

🔝 Immanence and Transcendence

Muslims believe Allah is both:

  • Transcendent — above and beyond the universe; beyond human understanding; not like anything in creation ("There is nothing like Him" — Surah 42:11)
  • Immanent — close to his creation; present everywhere; "closer to a person than their jugular vein" (Surah 50:16)
Exam Tip: Tawhid influences every aspect of Muslim life: it forbids shirk (associating partners with God), which is the greatest sin in Islam. This is why Muslims reject the Christian Trinity (they see it as shirk) and why images of Allah or Muhammad are forbidden in mosques (to prevent idolatry).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two Muslim beliefs about the nature of Allah.

Q2: "Tawhid is the most important Muslim belief." Evaluate this statement.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Allah is transcendent — he is above and beyond the created universe and beyond full human understanding. Surah 42:11 states "There is nothing like Him," meaning Allah cannot be compared to anything in creation. 2) Allah is immanent — he is close to his creation and present everywhere. Surah 50:16 says Allah is "closer to a person than their jugular vein," showing that although Allah is beyond creation, he is also intimately involved with it.
  2. Tawhid is indeed the foundation of Islam — it is the first part of the Shahadah and the message of all prophets. Without tawhid, no other belief makes sense. However, some might argue that other beliefs are equally important for daily life: the Five Pillars give structure to Muslim practice, and belief in akhirah motivates moral behaviour. Ultimately, tawhid is the root from which all other Islamic beliefs and practices grow, making it the most fundamental.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse Sunni and Shi'a beliefs about predestination. Wrong: "All Muslims believe everything is predestined and humans have no free will." Correct: "Most Muslims believe God has foreknowledge but humans have free will to choose; the balance between predestination and free will is understood differently across traditions."

📖 Detailed Notes: Tawhid: The Oneness of God

💡 The Nature of Allah in the Qur’an

Tawhid — the absolute oneness of God — is the central and most important belief in Islam. Surah 112 (Al-Ikhlas) expresses tawhid with extraordinary concision: “Say: He is Allah, the One and Only; Allah, the Eternal, Absolute; He begets not, nor is He begotten; And there is none comparable unto Him.” This short surah is sometimes described as being worth one-third of the Qur’an because it distils the essence of Islamic monotheism. Muslims believe that Allah has 99 beautiful names (Asma ul-Husna) that describe his nature, including Ar-Rahman (The Beneficent), Ar-Rahim (The Merciful), Al-Alim (The All-Knowing), Al-Adl (The Just) and Al-Qadir (The All-Powerful). Each name reveals an aspect of Allah’s character and is used in prayer and worship. The Basmalah — “In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful” — is recited at the beginning of every surah except one, and at the start of every significant action in a Muslim’s life.

Muslims believe that Allah is both transcendent (above and beyond the universe, beyond full human understanding) and immanent (close to creation, present everywhere). Surah 42:11 states: “There is nothing like Him,” emphasising Allah’s transcendence — humans cannot fully comprehend God. Yet Surah 50:16 says: “We are closer to him than his jugular vein,” emphasising Allah’s immanence — God is intimately involved with his creation. This balance between transcendence and immanence is unique to Islamic theology and distinguishes it from both Christianity (which Muslims believe compromises tawhid through the Trinity) and some forms of Hinduism (which Muslims believe associate partners with God).

UK Context: The Qur’an describes Allah’s creative power: “He it is who has created the heavens and the earth in six days and His Throne was upon the waters” (Surah 11:7). For Muslims, the natural world — its beauty, complexity and order — is evidence of Allah’s existence and power, inspiring gratitude and worship.

🔍 Shirk: The Greatest Sin in Islam

Shirk — associating partners with Allah — is the greatest sin in Islam, the one sin that Allah will not forgive if a person dies without repenting (Surah 4:48). Shirk takes many forms: idol worship (praying to statues, images or saints); polytheism (believing in multiple gods); the Trinity (which Muslims see as compromising the oneness of God by dividing God into three persons); and ‘hidden shirk’ — making anything more important than Allah, such as wealth, power or ego. The Qur’an warns: “Allah does not forgive that partners should be set up with Him, but He forgives anything else to whom He pleases” (Surah 4:48). This is why the Shahadah specifically rejects shirk: “There is no god but Allah” means that nothing else deserves worship.

The prohibition on shirk has practical consequences. Muslims do not use images of Allah or the Prophet in mosques or religious texts, to prevent idolatry — mosques are decorated with calligraphy and geometric patterns instead. Muslims reject the idea that anyone can intercede between a worshipper and Allah — salah is a direct conversation with God, with no priest needed. The concept of shirk also influences Muslim attitudes to other religions: while Islam respects Christians and Jews as ‘People of the Book’ (ahl al-kitab), Muslims believe that the Christian doctrine of the Trinity and the divinity of Jesus constitute shirk. However, it is important to note that criticising a theological belief is not the same as criticising the people who hold it — Islam teaches respect for all people, even when disagreeing with their beliefs.

However, the vast majority of Muslim scholars condemned the destruction, arguing that Islamic law does not require the destruction of other people’s religious symbols and that tolerance of other faiths is a Qur’anic principle.

⚖️ Tawhid and Muslim Life in Britain

For British Muslims, tawhid is the belief that shapes every aspect of daily life. The recognition that Allah is the only God means that all actions should be performed in accordance with His will — whether at work, at school, in the family or in the community. The concept of ‘insha’Allah’ (‘if Allah wills’) reflects tawhid: Muslims acknowledge that everything happens by Allah’s permission, and that human plans are subject to God’s will. The principle of tawhid also motivates charitable giving (all wealth belongs to Allah, so sharing it is a duty), honesty (Allah sees everything, so there is no point in deception) and perseverance (Allah tests believers with hardship, and patience is rewarded).

In a secular, multicultural society like Britain, maintaining tawhid can be both a source of strength and a challenge. On one hand, tawhid gives Muslims a clear framework for living — a single, unshakeable belief that provides meaning and direction. On the other hand, the emphasis on Allah’s supremacy can be misunderstood by non-Muslims, who may see it as exclusivist or intolerant. British Muslims must navigate between faithfully maintaining their belief in tawhid and engaging respectfully with people of other faiths and none. Many British Muslims argue that tawhid actually promotes tolerance: since Allah is the God of all humanity, all people deserve respect and dignity regardless of their beliefs.

UK Context: British Muslim athletes such as Mo Farah have spoken about how tawhid motivates their discipline and dedication. Farah has described his running career as an expression of gratitude to Allah for the talent he has been given, and he performs sujud (prostration) in gratitude after winning races.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain two Muslim beliefs about the nature of Allah.

Q: “Tawhid is the most important Muslim belief.” Evaluate this statement.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. First, Muslims believe Allah is transcendent — above and beyond the created universe and beyond full human understanding. Surah 42:11 states “There is nothing like Him,” meaning Allah cannot be compared to anything in creation. This is why Muslims reject images of Allah and why mosques are decorated with calligraphy rather than pictures. Second, Muslims believe Allah is immanent — close to his creation and present everywhere. Surah 50:16 says Allah is “closer to a person than their jugular vein,” showing that although Allah is beyond creation, he is also intimately involved with it. This balance means Muslims can have a personal relationship with Allah while recognising his supreme otherness.
  2. Tawhid is indeed the foundation of Islam — it is the first part of the Shahadah and the message of all prophets. Without tawhid, no other belief makes sense: prayer, fasting, charity and pilgrimage all depend on belief in one God. However, some might argue that other beliefs are equally important for daily life: the Five Pillars give structure to Muslim practice, belief in akhirah motivates moral behaviour, and risalah (prophethood) provides guidance for living. Ultimately, tawhid is the root from which all other Islamic beliefs and practices grow, making it the most fundamental. The Qur’an itself states that associating partners with Allah (shirk) is the only unforgivable sin, underscoring tawhid’s supreme importance.
