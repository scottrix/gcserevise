RS16: Angels

All Boards

The nature and role of angels including Jibril and Mika'il

☪️ Angels in Islam

Key Fact: Muslims believe angels (mala'ikah) are created from light and serve as God's messengers. They have no free will — they always obey Allah. Belief in angels is one of the Six Articles of Faith in Sunni Islam.

🔍 Why Angels Matter

  • Angels show that Allah is active in the world, sending messages and carrying out his will
  • Belief in recording angels motivates Muslims to live morally — every deed is recorded
  • Jibril's role in revealing the Qur'an connects angels to the most important event in Islam
  • Angels protect humans — each person has two guardian angels
  • Angels have no free will, unlike humans and jinn — they are pure servants of Allah

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two Muslim beliefs about angels.

Q2: "Belief in angels is not important for Muslims today." Evaluate this statement.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Jibril is the chief angel who brought revelation from Allah to the prophets, most importantly delivering the Qur'an to Muhammad over 23 years. Without Jibril, Muslims would not have the Qur'an. 2) Raqib and Atid are recording angels who write down every good and bad deed a person does. These records will be used on the Day of Judgement, which motivates Muslims to live morally because they know every action is recorded.
  2. Some might argue angels are irrelevant because they are invisible and cannot be proven scientifically. However, for Muslims, belief in angels is essential — it is one of the Six Articles of Faith. Angels demonstrate that Allah is actively involved in the world, they record human deeds for Judgement Day, and Jibril delivered the Qur'an itself. Without angels, the connection between Allah and humanity would be broken. Belief in angels strengthens faith and encourages moral behaviour.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse Sunni and Shi'a beliefs about predestination. Wrong: "All Muslims believe everything is predestined and humans have no free will." Correct: "Most Muslims believe God has foreknowledge but humans have free will to choose; the balance between predestination and free will is understood differently across traditions."

📖 Detailed Notes: Angels

💡 The Nature and Role of Angels in Islam

Angels (mala’ikah) are believed to be created beings made from light (nur) who serve Allah and carry out His commands without question. They have no free will and cannot disobey Allah — they are pure, sinless beings. Key angels include: Jibril (Gabriel), who brought the Qur’an to Muhammad and revealed Allah’s messages to all prophets; Mika’il (Michael), who controls the weather and natural forces; Isra’fil, who will blow the trumpet to signal the Day of Judgement; and Malik, the guardian of Hell. Each person also has two recording angels (kiraman katibin) who record their good and bad deeds. The Qur’an states: “Each person has been assigned angels who guard him in front and behind, by the command of Allah” (Surah 13:11). Belief in angels is the second Sunni Article of Faith and is shared by Shi’a Muslims.

Angels play crucial roles in Islamic belief. Jibril is the most important, as the angel of revelation who transmitted the Qur’an to Muhammad over 23 years. The Night of Power (Laylat al-Qadr) commemorates the first revelation: “The Night of Power is better than a thousand months. The angels and the Spirit descend therein by permission of their Lord for every matter” (Surah 97:3–4). Angels also comfort believers, assist at the moment of death (extracting the soul), question the dead in the grave about their faith, and will testify on the Day of Judgement. Muslims do not pray to angels or seek their intercession — angels serve Allah, not humans. Shi’a Muslims believe that angels also support the Imams in their mission to guide humanity.

🔍 Angels and Human Free Will

A key difference between angels and humans in Islamic theology is that angels have no free will — they obey Allah perfectly and cannot sin. Humans, by contrast, have been given free will and the capacity to choose between right and wrong. This is why Allah created humans as his khalifah (vicegerent or representative) on earth, a role that angels initially questioned: “Will You place upon it one who causes corruption and sheds blood, while we declare Your praise and sanctify You?” (Surah 2:30). Allah responded that He knew what the angels did not know — that humans, despite their capacity for wrongdoing, also have the capacity for greater moral and spiritual achievement than beings who merely follow commands.

The story of Iblis (Satan) illustrates the relationship between angels and free will. According to Islamic tradition, Iblis was a jinn (a being made from smokeless fire, not an angel) who refused Allah’s command to bow to Adam, arguing that he was superior because he was created from fire while Adam was created from clay. This refusal — born of pride and arrogance — led to Iblis’s expulsion from paradise and his vow to mislead humans. Unlike angels, who obey without question, Iblis exercised his free will to disobey, and his choice has consequences for all humanity. This narrative reinforces the Islamic view that free will is both a gift and a test.

⚖️ Angels in Daily Muslim Life

Belief in angels has practical implications for Muslim daily life. The Prophet taught that angels do not enter a house containing dogs or images, which is why some Muslims avoid keeping dogs as pets (except for working dogs) and prefer not to display pictures of living beings in their homes. The practice of saying ‘Bismillah’ (In the name of Allah) before eating, entering a house or beginning any action is partly to seek Allah’s blessing through the angels. The Prophet also taught that angels bring blessings to gatherings where Allah is remembered and where people study religious knowledge.

In the British context, belief in angels provides spiritual comfort and moral accountability. Many British Muslims describe feeling reassured by the presence of guardian angels, particularly during times of difficulty or danger. The recording angels (kiraman katibin) serve as a constant reminder that all actions — even those done in private — are witnessed, which encourages honesty, kindness and self-discipline. The concept of angels also features in British Muslim children’s education, with madrasahs teaching about Jibril, Mika’il and the recording angels as part of religious instruction.

UK Context: During the COVID-19 pandemic, many British Muslims found comfort in the belief that Allah’s angels were watching over them. Imams reminded congregations that the recording angels witnessed both their patience in hardship and their kindness to others during the crisis.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain two Muslim beliefs about angels.

Q: Evaluate whether belief in angels is still relevant for Muslims living in Britain today.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. First, Muslims believe angels are created beings made from light who serve Allah perfectly without free will. They carry out Allah’s commands, such as Jibril bringing revelation to the prophets and the recording angels noting every human deed. Second, Muslims believe each person has two recording angels (kiraman katibin) who document their good and bad actions. The Qur’an states that these angels are “noble and recording; they know whatever you do” (Surah 82:11–12). This belief motivates moral behaviour, as Muslims know that all actions — even secret ones — are witnessed and will be accounted for on the Day of Judgement.
  2. On one hand, belief in angels is central to Islamic faith — it is the second Sunni Article of Faith and is shared by all Muslims. It provides comfort (guardian angels protect believers), accountability (recording angels witness all deeds), and connects Muslims to the Qur’anic worldview. On the other hand, some might argue that in a scientific, rationalist society like Britain, belief in unseen beings can appear irrational or outdated. However, this view misunderstands the nature of religious belief: for Muslims, angels are a matter of faith and revelation, not empirical science. A balanced view is that belief in angels remains deeply relevant for British Muslims, providing spiritual meaning, moral motivation and a connection to the broader Islamic tradition that transcends cultural context.
