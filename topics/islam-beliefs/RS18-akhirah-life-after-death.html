RS18: Akhirah: Life After Death

All Boards

Human responsibility and accountability; resurrection; heaven and hell

☪️ Akhirah: Life After Death

Key Fact: Muslims believe that physical death is not the end — there is an afterlife (akhirah). After death, the soul is questioned in the grave by Munkar and Nakir, and on the Day of Judgement (Yawm al-Din), all people will be resurrected and judged by Allah.

📋 Key Beliefs About Akhirah

  • Barzakh — the state after death before the Day of Judgement; the soul waits in the grave
  • Munkar and Nakir — angels who question the dead about their faith
  • Day of Judgement (Yawm al-Din) — Allah will judge all people based on their deeds
  • The books of deeds — each person's good and bad actions are recorded and will be presented
  • Jannah (Paradise/Heaven) — eternal reward for the righteous; a garden of delight and peace
  • Jahannam (Hell) — punishment for those who rejected Allah or lived wickedly

🔍 The Impact of Belief in Akhirah

  • Motivates Muslims to follow Allah's guidance and live morally
  • Provides comfort that injustice will be addressed — even if the wicked prosper in this life, they will face judgement
  • Gives meaning to suffering — patience in this life will be rewarded
  • Makes the Five Pillars worthwhile — prayer, fasting and charity store up reward for the afterlife
  • Encourages Muslims to take responsibility for their actions
Exam Tip: Belief in akhirah is one of the Six Articles of Faith. Without it, the whole Islamic moral framework would collapse — there would be no ultimate justice and no reason to follow Allah's commands if this life is all there is.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two Muslim beliefs about akhirah.

Q2: "Belief in life after death gives life meaning." Evaluate this statement with reference to Islamic teaching.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) The Day of Judgement — Muslims believe Allah will resurrect all people and judge them based on their deeds. The books of deeds (recorded by Raqib and Atid) will be presented, and people will be sent to Jannah (Paradise) or Jahannam (Hell). 2) Barzakh — after death, the soul enters a state of waiting in the grave. The angels Munkar and Nakir question the deceased about their faith. This state continues until the Day of Judgement.
  2. Muslims would strongly agree — without akhirah, life would lack ultimate purpose and justice. The wicked could escape consequences and the righteous could suffer without reward. Akhirah motivates moral behaviour and gives comfort. However, some non-religious people find meaning in this life through relationships, contribution to society and personal growth. For Muslims, though, earthly life (dunya) is a test for the eternal life (akhirah) — meaning comes from knowing this life is a preparation for something greater.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse Sunni and Shi'a beliefs about predestination. Wrong: "All Muslims believe everything is predestined and humans have no free will." Correct: "Most Muslims believe God has foreknowledge but humans have free will to choose; the balance between predestination and free will is understood differently across traditions."

📖 Detailed Notes: Akhirah: Life After Death

💡 Muslim Beliefs About Death and the Afterlife

Akhirah (life after death) is a central belief in Islam. Muslims believe that this life (dunya) is a test and that the real, eternal life begins after death. The Qur’an states: “Every soul will taste death. And you will only receive your full reward on the Day of Resurrection” (Surah 3:185). When a person dies, the soul is questioned in the grave by the angels Munkar and Nakir about their faith and deeds. The righteous experience peace in the grave (a ‘portion of paradise’), while the wicked experience torment. This intermediate state (barzakh) continues until the Day of Judgement (yawm al-din), when all people will be resurrected and judged by Allah based on their deeds.

On the Day of Judgement, each person’s deeds will be weighed on scales (mizan). Those whose good deeds outweigh their bad will enter Jannah (paradise), described in the Qur’an as a garden of bliss with rivers, shade and eternal happiness. Those whose bad deeds outweigh their good will enter Jahannam (hell), described as a place of fire and torment. However, Muslims believe that Allah is merciful and that believers who have sinned may eventually be forgiven and admitted to paradise. The Prophet taught: “Allah has divided mercy into one hundred parts, and He kept ninety-nine parts with Him and sent down one part to the earth” (Sahih Bukhari). Shi’a Muslims also believe that the intercession (shafa’a) of the Prophet and the Imams may help believers on the Day of Judgement.

Mourners recite the Shahadah and pray for the deceased’s forgiveness. In the UK, Muslim burials must comply with both Islamic law (which requires burial, not cremation) and UK cemetery regulations.

🔍 Jannah and Jahannam in the Qur’an

The Qur’an describes Jannah (paradise) in vivid imagery: “Gardens of perpetual bliss; they will enter them with the righteous among their parents, their spouses and their offspring. And the angels will enter upon them from every gate, saying: Peace be upon you for what you patiently endured. And excellent is the final home” (Surah 13:23–24). Paradise is described as a place of rivers, shade, fruits, fine clothing and eternal happiness, where believers are reunited with loved ones and enjoy the closest possible relationship with Allah. However, many Muslim scholars caution against reading these descriptions too literally — they may be metaphors for spiritual realities that humans cannot fully comprehend.

Jahannam (hell) is described as a place of fire, boiling water and torment for those who rejected Allah’s guidance: “Indeed, those who disbelieve in Our verses — We will drive them into a fire. Every time their skins are roasted through, We will replace them with other skins so they may taste the punishment” (Surah 4:56). While these descriptions are vivid, Muslim theologians debate whether hell is eternal for all its inhabitants or whether some may eventually be freed through Allah’s mercy. Most Sunni scholars believe that believers who enter hell as punishment for sins will eventually be released and admitted to paradise, while those who died in a state of disbelief will remain eternally. This view reflects the Qur’anic emphasis on Allah’s mercy alongside His justice.

⚖️ Akhirah and Muslim Life in Britain

Belief in akhirah profoundly shapes how Muslims live in Britain. It provides a moral framework: since every action will be judged, Muslims are motivated to act ethically even when no human authority is watching. The recording angels and the certainty of judgement encourage honesty in business, kindness to neighbours, and generosity to the poor. Belief in akhirah also provides comfort during hardship: the injustices and sufferings of this life will be rectified in the next, and those who endured with patience will be rewarded. The Qur’an states: “And do not think that Allah is unaware of what the wrongdoers do. He only delays them for a Day when eyes will stare in horror” (Surah 14:42).

For British Muslims, belief in akhirah also shapes attitudes to death and dying. Islamic law requires burial (not cremation), and the funeral should take place as soon as possible after death. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the need for rapid burial created challenges when mosques and cemeteries were affected by restrictions. The Muslim Burial Council and local authorities worked together to ensure that Islamic funeral requirements could be met within UK law. The Muslim Council of Britain published guidance on funeral arrangements during the pandemic, balancing Islamic obligations with public health requirements.

UK Context: In 2023, Birmingham City Council approved a new Muslim burial section at Brandwood End Cemetery, reflecting the growing need for Islamic burial facilities in the city, which has one of the largest Muslim populations in the UK outside London.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain two Muslim beliefs about akhirah.

Q: “Belief in akhirah has no practical relevance for Muslims in modern Britain.” Evaluate this statement.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. First, Muslims believe in bodily resurrection and the Day of Judgement, when all people will be raised from the dead and judged by Allah based on their deeds. The Qur’an states: “Every soul will taste death... and you will only receive your full reward on the Day of Resurrection” (Surah 3:185). Each person’s good and bad deeds will be weighed on scales (mizan), determining whether they enter Jannah or Jahannam. Second, Muslims believe in barzakh — the intermediate state between death and resurrection, where the soul is questioned by angels in the grave. The righteous experience a portion of paradise, while the wicked experience torment. This belief motivates Muslims to live righteously, knowing that death is not the end but the beginning of eternal life.
  2. On one hand, some might argue that in a secular society focused on the present life, belief in the afterlife seems irrelevant to daily concerns like work, education and relationships. On the other hand, belief in akhirah profoundly shapes British Muslim behaviour: it motivates ethical conduct (knowing that all deeds are recorded and will be judged), encourages charitable giving (zakah and sadaqah are investments in the afterlife), provides comfort during hardship (injustices will be rectified by Allah), and shapes attitudes to death and dying (requiring burial, not cremation). A balanced view is that akhirah is one of the most practically relevant Islamic beliefs for British Muslims, providing moral motivation, existential meaning and comfort in a way that secular worldviews do not offer.
