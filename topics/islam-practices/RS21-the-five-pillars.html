RS21: The Five Pillars

All Boards

Shahadah: declaration of faith and its place in Muslim practice

☪️ Shahadah: The Declaration of Faith

Key Fact: The Shahadah is the first and most important Pillar of Islam: "There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is his messenger." This declaration is said daily in prayer and is the only thing required to become a Muslim.

📋 The Two Parts of the Shahadah

  • "There is no god but Allah" — affirms tawhid (the oneness of God); rejects all other gods and idols; the foundation of all Islamic belief
  • "Muhammad is his messenger" — affirms risalah (prophethood); acknowledges Muhammad as the final prophet; accepts the Qur'an as God's word

The Shahadah is whispered into the ear of a newborn baby and is the last words a Muslim tries to say before death. It is recited in every salah (prayer) and in the call to prayer (adhan).

🔍 Significance of the Shahadah

  • It sums up the entire Islamic faith in one sentence
  • It is the entry point to Islam — saying it sincerely makes you a Muslim
  • It distinguishes Muslims from followers of other religions
  • It is a constant reminder of the core beliefs — recited at least 17 times daily in prayer
  • It unites all Muslims worldwide regardless of nationality, language or culture

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain why the Shahadah is the most important Pillar of Islam.

✅ Answers

  1. The Shahadah is the most important Pillar because it sums up the entire Islamic faith: tawhid (one God) and risalah (Muhammad as prophet). Without the Shahadah, none of the other Pillars would make sense — prayer, fasting, charity and pilgrimage all depend on the belief that Allah is the one God and Muhammad is his messenger. It is the entry point to Islam and is recited constantly throughout a Muslim's life, from birth to death.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Islam practices questions: Know the Five Pillars in detail and be able to explain each one. Distinguish between Sunni and Shi'a practices (e.g. additional Ten Obligatory Acts in Shi'a Islam). Explain the difference between greater and lesser jihad. Use specific examples for Hajj (Ka'aba, Arafat, Muzdalifah, Mina).

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think jihad means "holy war." Wrong: "Jihad means holy war." Correct: "Jihad means "struggle" or "striving." Greater jihad is the internal spiritual struggle to live as a good Muslim. Lesser jihad is armed struggle, which has strict conditions and is a last resort."

📖 Detailed Notes: The Five Pillars

💡 The Shahadah in Daily Muslim Life

The Shahadah (“There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is his messenger”) is not merely a statement of belief but the foundation upon which every aspect of Muslim life is built. It is recited in every rak’ah (unit) of prayer, meaning a practising Muslim says it at least 17 times daily during the five daily prayers. The Shahadah is whispered into the ear of a newborn baby, symbolising that the first words a person hears are the declaration of faith. It is also the last words a Muslim strives to say before death. When a person wishes to convert to Islam, reciting the Shahadah sincerely before witnesses is all that is required — there is no elaborate ceremony or initiation ritual. The importance of the Shahadah is emphasised in the Hadith: “Islam is based on five principles: to testify that none has the right to be worshipped but Allah and Muhammad is Allah’s Messenger...” (Sahih Bukhari).

The Shahadah has two inseparable parts that define Muslim belief and practice. The first part (“There is no god but Allah”) affirms tawhid — the absolute oneness of God — and rejects all forms of shirk (associating partners with God). This is why Muslims reject the Christian concept of the Trinity and the Hindu worship of multiple deities: both are seen as compromising the oneness of God. The second part (“Muhammad is his messenger”) affirms risalah — the belief that Muhammad is the final prophet in a line of prophets that includes Ibrahim (Abraham), Musa (Moses) and Isa (Jesus). Accepting Muhammad as Allah’s messenger means accepting the Qur’an as God’s final and complete revelation. Surah 112 (Al-Ikhlas), which succinctly expresses tawhid, is sometimes called ‘the equivalent of one-third of the Qur’an’ because it distils the essence of the faith.

🔍 Sunni and Shi’a Understandings of the Pillars

While all Muslims recognise the Five Pillars, Sunni and Shi’a traditions express their core practices differently. Sunni Muslims follow the Five Pillars as the essential framework of Islamic practice: Shahadah, Salah, Zakah, Sawm and Hajj. These Five Pillars are considered obligatory (fard) for all Sunni Muslims. Shi’a Muslims follow the Ten Obligatory Acts (Furu al-Din), which include the Five Pillars plus additional practices: Khums (a 20% tax on surplus income paid to religious leaders), Jihad (striving in the way of Allah), Amr bil Ma’ruf (encouraging good), Nahi an al-Munkar (forbidding evil), and Tawalla (loving the Ahl al-Bayt — the family of the Prophet) and Tabarra (disassociating from the enemies of the Ahl al-Bayt).

The differences reflect distinct theological and historical traditions. For Sunni Muslims, the Five Pillars represent the practical expression of the Shahadah, the minimum obligations that define a Muslim’s relationship with Allah. They are rooted in the Qur’an and the Sunnah (the practice of the Prophet). For Shi’a Muslims, the Ten Obligatory Acts reflect the guidance of the Imams, whom they believe were divinely appointed to lead the Muslim community after the Prophet’s death. The inclusion of khums (a 20% tax rather than the Sunni 2.5% zakah) reflects the Shi’a belief in the authority of the Imams and their descendants to receive and distribute religious funds. Despite these differences, both traditions share the same core practices of prayer, fasting, charity and pilgrimage, and both affirm the Shahadah as the central declaration of faith.

⚖️ The Five Pillars and Muslim Identity in Britain

For Muslims in Britain, the Five Pillars provide a framework for living as a religious minority in a secular society. Observing the Five Pillars requires commitment and sometimes sacrifice: praying five times daily means fitting prayer around work or school schedules; fasting during Ramadan in a society where most people are eating and drinking normally requires willpower; paying zakah requires financial planning; and performing Hajj requires saving for travel and taking extended leave from work. Despite these challenges, the 2021 Census recorded approximately 3.9 million Muslims in England and Wales (6.5% of the population), and surveys consistently show high levels of religious observance among British Muslims.

The Five Pillars also shape British Muslim community life. Jummah (Friday congregational) prayers bring Muslims together weekly, strengthening community bonds. During Ramadan, mosques host iftar (fast-breaking) meals open to all, and charitable giving increases significantly — Islamic Relief UK raises millions of pounds annually, much of it during Ramadan. The Hajj journey creates bonds between British Muslims and the global ummah, as approximately 25,000 British Muslims perform Hajj each year. However, some British Muslims find it challenging to maintain all Five Pillars in a secular environment: shift work may disrupt prayer times; school or university schedules may conflict with Ramadan fasting; and the cost of Hajj (typically £5,000–£10,000) puts it beyond the reach of many. These challenges have led to creative adaptations, such as prayer apps, online zakah calculators and flexible Hajj travel packages.

UK Context: During Ramadan in the UK, where summer fasting days can last over 18 hours, British Muslims demonstrate remarkable commitment. Many continue to work full shifts while fasting, and mosques across the country organise community iftars. In 2023, the East London Mosque served over 1,000 free meals every evening during Ramadan, demonstrating how the Pillars of fasting and charity combine to strengthen community life.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain two reasons why the Shahadah is the most important Pillar of Islam.

Q: “The Five Pillars are impossible to follow in modern Britain.” Evaluate this statement.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. First, the Shahadah is the foundation of the entire Islamic faith: it declares tawhid (the oneness of God) and risalah (Muhammad as the final prophet). Without the Shahadah, the other Pillars have no meaning — prayer, fasting, charity and pilgrimage all depend on belief in one God and acceptance of Muhammad’s prophethood. Second, the Shahadah is the entry point to Islam: saying it sincerely makes a person a Muslim, and it is the first words a newborn hears and the last words a dying Muslim tries to say. It is recited at least 17 times daily in prayer, constantly reinforcing the core belief.
  2. On one hand, following the Five Pillars in Britain presents challenges: finding time and space for five daily prayers in a secular workplace; fasting during long summer days when colleagues eat normally; saving for Hajj on limited incomes; and calculating zakah alongside UK taxes. On the other hand, millions of British Muslims successfully observe the Five Pillars: employers are increasingly accommodating prayer needs; Ramadan fasting is legally protected; Islamic Relief makes zakah straightforward; and Hajj travel agencies offer flexible payment plans. The Equality Act 2010 protects religious observance. A balanced view is that while the Five Pillars require significant commitment in a non-Muslim society, they are far from impossible to follow, and the challenges can actually strengthen Muslim faith and identity.
