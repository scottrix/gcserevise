RS23: Sawm: Fasting

All Boards

The role and significance of fasting during Ramadan; origins, duties, exceptions, Night of Power

☪️ Sawm: Fasting During Ramadan

Key Fact: Sawm (fasting) during Ramadan is the third Pillar of Islam. Muslims do not eat, drink, smoke or engage in sexual activity from dawn (Fajr) to sunset (Maghrib) for the entire month of Ramadan — the month when the Qur'an was first revealed to Muhammad.

📋 Key Practices During Ramadan

  • Suhoor — the pre-dawn meal eaten before Fajr prayer
  • Iftar — the evening meal that breaks the fast after Maghrib prayer; often starts with dates and water
  • Tarawih — special night prayers during Ramadan, often reciting the whole Qur'an over the month
  • Laylat al-Qadr (Night of Power) — the night when the Qur'an was first revealed; falls in the last 10 days of Ramadan; "better than a thousand months" (Surah 97:3)
  • Exemptions — children, the elderly, the sick, pregnant/nursing women, travellers and those with mental health conditions are exempt from fasting

🔍 Why Fasting Matters

  • It develops taqwa (God-consciousness) — self-discipline strengthens faith
  • It teaches self-discipline — controlling physical desires strengthens moral character
  • It creates empathy with the poor — experiencing hunger reminds Muslims of those who go hungry every day
  • It brings the ummah together — all Muslims fast simultaneously worldwide
  • It is a time for spiritual renewal — more prayer, Qur'an reading and charity

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two reasons why Muslims fast during Ramadan.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Self-discipline and taqwa — fasting teaches Muslims to control their physical desires and focus on spiritual matters. By resisting the urge to eat and drink, Muslims strengthen their willpower and their devotion to Allah. 2) Empathy with the poor — experiencing hunger during daylight hours helps Muslims understand the daily suffering of those who do not have enough food. This motivates greater charity (zakah) and compassion.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Islam practices questions: Know the Five Pillars in detail and be able to explain each one. Distinguish between Sunni and Shi'a practices (e.g. additional Ten Obligatory Acts in Shi'a Islam). Explain the difference between greater and lesser jihad. Use specific examples for Hajj (Ka'aba, Arafat, Muzdalifah, Mina).

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think jihad means "holy war." Wrong: "Jihad means holy war." Correct: "Jihad means "struggle" or "striving." Greater jihad is the internal spiritual struggle to live as a good Muslim. Lesser jihad is armed struggle, which has strict conditions and is a last resort."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that the role and significance of fasting during ramadan; origins, duties, exceptions, night of power.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
