RS23: Sawm: Fasting

All Boards

The role and significance of fasting during Ramadan; origins, duties, exceptions, Night of Power

☪️ Sawm: Fasting During Ramadan

Key Fact: Sawm (fasting) during Ramadan is the third Pillar of Islam. Muslims do not eat, drink, smoke or engage in sexual activity from dawn (Fajr) to sunset (Maghrib) for the entire month of Ramadan — the month when the Qur'an was first revealed to Muhammad.

📋 Key Practices During Ramadan

  • Suhoor — the pre-dawn meal eaten before Fajr prayer
  • Iftar — the evening meal that breaks the fast after Maghrib prayer; often starts with dates and water
  • Tarawih — special night prayers during Ramadan, often reciting the whole Qur'an over the month
  • Laylat al-Qadr (Night of Power) — the night when the Qur'an was first revealed; falls in the last 10 days of Ramadan; "better than a thousand months" (Surah 97:3)
  • Exemptions — children, the elderly, the sick, pregnant/nursing women, travellers and those with mental health conditions are exempt from fasting

🔍 Why Fasting Matters

  • It develops taqwa (God-consciousness) — self-discipline strengthens faith
  • It teaches self-discipline — controlling physical desires strengthens moral character
  • It creates empathy with the poor — experiencing hunger reminds Muslims of those who go hungry every day
  • It brings the ummah together — all Muslims fast simultaneously worldwide
  • It is a time for spiritual renewal — more prayer, Qur'an reading and charity

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two reasons why Muslims fast during Ramadan.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Self-discipline and taqwa — fasting teaches Muslims to control their physical desires and focus on spiritual matters. By resisting the urge to eat and drink, Muslims strengthen their willpower and their devotion to Allah. 2) Empathy with the poor — experiencing hunger during daylight hours helps Muslims understand the daily suffering of those who do not have enough food. This motivates greater charity (zakah) and compassion.

📖 Detailed Notes: Sawm: Fasting

💡 The Practice and Purpose of Fasting During Ramadan

Sawm (fasting) during Ramadan is the third Pillar of Islam. During the ninth month of the Islamic calendar, Muslims abstain from all food, drink (including water), smoking and sexual activity from dawn (Fajr) to sunset (Maghrib). The fast is broken each evening with iftar, traditionally starting with dates and water, following the Prophet’s example, followed by a full meal. Before dawn, Muslims eat suhoor (a pre-fast meal) to sustain them through the day. Ramadan is significant because it commemorates the month in which the Qur’an was first revealed to the Prophet Muhammad: “The month of Ramadan in which the Qur’an was revealed, a guidance for mankind...” (Surah 2:185). The Night of Power (Laylat al-Qadr), believed to occur in the last ten days of Ramadan, marks the specific night of the first revelation and is considered holier than a thousand months of worship (Surah 97).

The purpose of fasting goes far beyond physical abstention. It is a spiritual discipline that teaches self-control, devotion to Allah and empathy with the poor. By experiencing hunger and thirst, Muslims gain a direct understanding of what it means to live without food — this should inspire greater generosity through zakah and sadaqah (voluntary charity). Fasting also provides an opportunity for spiritual renewal: Muslims are encouraged to read the entire Qur’an during Ramadan (it is divided into 30 sections, one for each day), to attend tarawih prayers at the mosque each night, and to give more charity. The Prophet said: “When Ramadan comes, the gates of Paradise are opened and the gates of Hell are closed and the devils are chained” (Sahih Bukhari), meaning that Ramadan is a time of special spiritual opportunity.

🔍 Exemptions, Conditions and the Deeper Meaning of Sawm

Islam is a practical religion and recognises that fasting is not appropriate for everyone. Those exempt from fasting include: children who have not reached puberty; the elderly and frail; pregnant, breastfeeding or menstruating women; people with serious illnesses (including diabetes, where fasting would be medically dangerous); and travellers. Those who miss fasts due to temporary conditions (e.g. menstruation, illness) must make up the missed days later in the year. Those with permanent conditions (e.g. chronic illness, old age) may pay fidyah — feeding one poor person for each missed fast — as a substitute. However, many Muslims who are technically exempt choose to fast anyway if they feel able, reflecting the deep spiritual significance of the practice.

The concept of niyyah (intention) is essential to sawm: a fast is only valid if the person intends to fast for the sake of Allah before dawn each day. This means that merely not eating is not sufficient — the spiritual intention must be present. Sawm also involves restraining the whole self from sin: the Prophet taught that if a person does not give up false speech and acting falsely during Ramadan, then Allah has no need for them to give up food and drink (Sahih Bukhari). This broader understanding means that Ramadan is a time for avoiding gossip, anger, lying and all forms of wrongdoing, not just food and drink. Many Muslims also use Ramadan to strengthen family and community bonds: iftar meals are often shared with neighbours, the poor and people of other faiths, making the fast a communal as well as individual experience.

⚖️ Ramadan in the British Muslim Community

For the approximately 3.9 million Muslims in Britain, Ramadan is both a spiritual and a communal experience. Mosques host nightly tarawih prayers, often attracting hundreds of worshippers. Community iftars bring people together to break the fast, and mosques, charities and individuals distribute free meals to those in need. Islamic Relief UK reports that donations typically double during Ramadan, as Muslims fulfil their zakah obligations and give additional sadaqah. The British media increasingly acknowledges Ramadan, with the BBC and major newspapers publishing features about the Muslim holy month and some employers making accommodations for fasting staff.

However, fasting in Britain also presents unique challenges. In summer, fasting days can be extremely long — in northern parts of the UK, the sun may set after 10pm and rise before 3am, meaning Muslims fast for up to 19 hours. Unlike in Muslim-majority countries, where working hours are typically reduced during Ramadan, most British Muslims continue their normal work and school schedules. Schools may not always understand the impact of fasting on concentration and energy levels, and some fasting students have reported difficulty completing exams during Ramadan. Despite these challenges, many British Muslims describe Ramadan as their most spiritually fulfilling month. The communal aspect of fasting — knowing that millions of Muslims in Britain and billions worldwide are sharing the same experience — creates a powerful sense of belonging to the ummah (global Muslim community).

UK Context: In 2024, the Muslim Council of Britain issued guidance to schools on supporting fasting students during exam season, recommending that schools provide quiet rest areas, allow students to avoid strenuous PE activities, and schedule important exams for the morning when fasting students have more energy. This reflects the growing recognition of Ramadan as an important part of British life.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain two reasons why Muslims fast during Ramadan.

Q: “Fasting during Ramadan is impossible for Muslims living in Britain.” Evaluate this statement.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. First, fasting is a direct command from Allah: “O you who believe! Fasting is prescribed for you as it was prescribed for those before you, that you may become righteous” (Surah 2:183). It is the third Pillar of Islam and therefore an obligatory act of worship. Second, fasting teaches empathy with the poor and hungry. By voluntarily experiencing hunger and thirst, Muslims develop a direct understanding of what it means to live without enough food, which should inspire them to be more generous through zakah and sadaqah. The physical discomfort also reminds Muslims of their dependence on Allah for all provision and strengthens their gratitude.
  2. On one hand, fasting in Britain presents significant challenges: summer fasting days can last up to 19 hours; most workplaces and schools do not adjust schedules for Ramadan; and the lack of a shared fasting culture means Muslims fast while most people around them eat normally. On the other hand, over 90% of British Muslims successfully fast each Ramadan, demonstrating that it is far from impossible. Communities provide extensive support through mosque iftars, charity distributions and family networks. Islamic law provides concessions for those who genuinely cannot fast. A balanced view is that fasting in Britain is demanding but achievable, and the difficulty itself is seen by many Muslims as part of the spiritual test that makes Ramadan meaningful.
