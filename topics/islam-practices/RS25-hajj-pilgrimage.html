RS25: Hajj: Pilgrimage

All Boards

The role and significance of pilgrimage to Makkah; Ka'aba, Mina, Arafat, Muzdalifah

☪️ Hajj: Pilgrimage to Makkah

Key Fact: Hajj is the fifth Pillar of Islam — every Muslim who is physically and financially able must make the pilgrimage to Makkah at least once in their lifetime. Over 2 million Muslims perform Hajj each year during the month of Dhul-Hijjah.

📋 The Stages of Hajj

  1. Ihram — entering a state of purity; wearing simple white garments (two unstitched sheets for men); no cutting hair or nails, no perfume, no arguments
  2. Tawaf — walking seven times around the Ka'bah in the Grand Mosque, the holiest site in Islam
  3. Sa'y — running seven times between the hills of Safa and Marwah, remembering Hajar's search for water
  4. Mina — travelling to Mina; spending the day in prayer and reflection
  5. Arafat — the most important day; standing on the Plain of Arafat from noon to sunset, asking Allah for forgiveness. This is the Day of Arafah — the "essence of Hajj"
  6. Muzdalifah — collecting pebbles after leaving Arafat; spending the night under the stars
  7. Ramy al-Jamarat — stoning the three pillars at Mina, representing rejection of the devil (following Ibrahim's example)
  8. Eid al-Adha — sacrificing an animal (or paying for one to be sacrificed), remembering Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son
  9. Tawaf al-Ifadah — final circling of the Ka'bah before leaving Makkah

🔍 Why Hajj Matters

  • It unites the ummah — Muslims of every race and nationality gather as equals in simple white garments
  • It follows Ibrahim's example — the Ka'bah was built by Ibrahim and the stoning remembers his rejection of the devil
  • It brings forgiveness — a properly performed Hajj wipes out all past sins
  • It is a journey of a lifetime — Muslims save for years and prepare spiritually
  • It reminds Muslims of the Day of Judgement — standing before Allah in white garments, all equal

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two reasons why Hajj is important for Muslims.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Hajj unites the ummah — over 2 million Muslims from every country gather in Makkah, wearing identical simple white garments. This removes all distinctions of wealth, race and status, showing that all Muslims are equal before Allah. 2) Hajj brings forgiveness — Muslims believe a properly performed Hajj wipes out all past sins. Standing on the Plain of Arafat and sincerely repenting is the most powerful moment, when Muslims feel closest to Allah and experience his mercy.

📖 Detailed Notes: Hajj: Pilgrimage

💡 The Rituals and Significance of Hajj

Hajj is the fifth Pillar of Islam — every Muslim who is physically and financially able must make the pilgrimage to Makkah at least once in their lifetime. Over 2 million Muslims perform Hajj each year during the month of Dhul-Hijjah, making it the largest annual gathering of people on Earth. The pilgrimage commemorates the actions of Ibrahim (Abraham), his wife Hajar and their son Isma’il, who are central figures in Islamic tradition. The Ka’aba — the cube-shaped building at the centre of the Grand Mosque in Makkah — is believed to have been built by Ibrahim and Isma’il as the first house of worship dedicated to the one God. Muslims face the Ka’aba in prayer every day, but during Hajj they actually walk around it seven times (tawaf), expressing their devotion and unity with the global ummah.

The key rituals of Hajj include: Ihram — entering a state of purity by wearing simple white garments (two unstitched sheets for men, modest dress for women) that eliminate all distinctions of wealth, class and nationality; Tawaf — walking seven times around the Ka’aba; Sa’y — running seven times between the hills of Safa and Marwah, commemorating Hajar’s desperate search for water for her infant son Isma’il; Wuquf — standing on the plain of Arafat, where the Prophet Muhammad delivered his farewell sermon, considered the spiritual climax of Hajj; Rami al-Jamarat — throwing stones at three pillars representing Shaytan (Satan), commemorating Ibrahim’s rejection of the devil’s temptation; and Eid al-Adha — the Festival of Sacrifice, commemorating Ibrahim’s willingness to sacrifice his son at Allah’s command.

🔍 The Spiritual Impact of Hajj

Hajj is described as a transformative experience that has a profound spiritual impact on the pilgrim. The uniformity of ihram — all pilgrims wearing the same simple white garments — symbolises the Islamic principle of equality before Allah: a king and a labourer stand side by side, indistinguishable in their dress. This physical equality reinforces the spiritual truth that all people are equal in the sight of God, regardless of wealth, status or race. The Prophet Muhammad said: “All mankind is from Adam and Eve. An Arab has no superiority over a non-Arab, nor does a non-Arab have any superiority over an Arab; a white has no superiority over a black, nor does a black have any superiority over a white; [none have superiority] except by piety and good action” (Farewell Sermon).

Standing on the plain of Arafat (wuquf) is considered the spiritual climax of Hajj. Pilgrims spend the afternoon in prayer, reflection and repentance, seeking Allah’s forgiveness for their sins. Many describe this as the most intense spiritual experience of their lives. The Qur’an states: “When you depart from Arafat, remember Allah at the sacred monument” (Surah 2:198). A successful Hajj, performed with sincere devotion, is believed to wipe clean all past sins, returning the pilgrim to a state of spiritual purity like that of a newborn. The Prophet said: “Whoever performs Hajj for Allah’s pleasure and does not commit sexual relations or sinful behaviour, will return as if he were born anew” (Sahih Bukhari). The experience of being among millions of fellow Muslims from every nation on earth also powerfully reinforces the concept of the ummah — the global community of believers.

UK Context: Malcolm X’s experience of Hajj in 1964 transformed his views on race. Writing from Makkah, he described seeing “blonde-haired, blued-eyed white-skinned Muslims” praying alongside Black Africans and Asians, all equal in ihram. This experience led him to reject the racial separatism of the Nation of Islam and embrace orthodox Sunni Islam’s message of universal brotherhood.

⚖️ Hajj and the British Muslim Community

For British Muslims, performing Hajj is both a lifelong aspiration and a significant logistical undertaking. The cost of Hajj from the UK typically ranges from £5,000 to £10,000, including flights, accommodation and visa fees, which puts it beyond the immediate reach of many families. Many British Muslims save for years or pool family resources to enable one family member to perform the pilgrimage. The Saudi government allocates a quota of Hajj visas to each country, and the UK quota is approximately 25,000 places per year, meaning demand often exceeds supply. Travelling to Saudi Arabia also requires careful preparation: vaccinations, obtaining a Hajj visa through an approved travel agent, and arranging accommodation in Makkah.

Despite the challenges, British Muslims consistently rate Hajj as one of their most important religious goals. The Council of British Hajjis provides advice and support to UK pilgrims, and the British government maintains a consular presence in Saudi Arabia during the Hajj season to assist citizens in difficulty. Many British Muslims describe their Hajj experience as life-changing, returning with renewed faith and a stronger sense of connection to the global ummah. Some British Muslims also combine Hajj with Umrah (a lesser pilgrimage that can be performed at any time of year), which is more accessible because it does not have the same time restrictions and is generally less expensive. The experience of Makkah — being in the place where the Prophet received the first revelation and where millions of Muslims have worshipped for over 1,400 years — carries immense spiritual weight.

UK Context: The UK government’s Hajj Advisory Group, established in 2018, provides guidance on avoiding fraudulent travel operators, understanding Saudi health requirements and staying safe during the intense heat and crowds of the pilgrimage. In 2023, the group warned British pilgrims about temperatures exceeding 45°C during the Hajj season and advised on heat precautions.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain two reasons why Hajj is important for Muslims.

Q: “Hajj is too expensive for most British Muslims.” Evaluate this statement.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. First, Hajj is a direct command from Allah and the fifth Pillar of Islam: “And Hajj to the House (Ka’aba) is a duty that mankind owes to Allah, those who can afford the expenses” (Surah 3:97). It is obligatory for all Muslims who are physically and financially able. Second, Hajj transforms the pilgrim spiritually: standing on the plain of Arafat and seeking forgiveness is believed to wipe clean all past sins, returning the pilgrim to spiritual purity. The experience of worshipping alongside millions of Muslims from every nation also powerfully reinforces the concept of the ummah — the global community of believers united by faith.
  2. On one hand, the cost of Hajj from the UK (typically £5,000–£10,000) is prohibitive for many British Muslims, particularly those on low incomes or with large families. Visa quotas (approximately 25,000 for the UK) mean that demand exceeds supply, and fraudulent travel operators sometimes exploit pilgrims. On the other hand, many British Muslims save for years or use community-based savings schemes (such as ROSCAs) to fund the pilgrimage. Umrah, a lesser pilgrimage, is more affordable and can be performed at any time. Islamic teaching emphasises that Hajj is only required for those who are financially able, and those who cannot afford it are not sinful for missing it. A balanced view is that while the cost is a genuine barrier for many, it is not insurmountable for most, and the spiritual importance of Hajj motivates British Muslims to find ways to fulfil this Pillar.
