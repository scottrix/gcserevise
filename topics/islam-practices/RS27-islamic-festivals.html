RS27: Islamic Festivals

All Boards

Id-ul-Adha, Id-ul-Fitr and Ashura; their origins, meanings and importance in Britain today

☪️ Islamic Festivals

Key Fact: The two main Islamic festivals are Id-ul-Adha (Festival of Sacrifice) and Id-ul-Fitr (Festival of Fast-Breaking). Shi'a Muslims also observe Ashura, commemorating the martyrdom of Imam Husayn.

📋 Id-ul-Adha

  • Celebrates Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son at Allah's command
  • Takes place during Hajj on the 10th day of Dhul-Hijjah
  • Muslims sacrifice an animal (sheep, goat, cow) — the meat is shared: one-third for family, one-third for friends, one-third for the poor
  • Muslims who are not on Hajj attend the mosque for special Id prayers
  • It emphasises obedience to Allah, sharing with the poor, and the importance of faith

📋 Id-ul-Fitr

  • Celebrates the end of Ramadan and the completion of fasting
  • Muslims attend the mosque for special Id prayers in the morning
  • Families celebrate with special food, new clothes and gifts
  • Before the Id prayer, Muslims must pay Zakah al-Fitr — a special charity to ensure the poor can also celebrate
  • It is a joyful occasion thanking Allah for the strength to complete the fast

📋 Ashura (Shi'a Islam)

  • Observed on the 10th of Muharram
  • Commemorates the martyrdom of Imam Husayn (Muhammad's grandson) at the Battle of Karbala in 680 CE
  • Shi'a Muslims mourn with processions, recitations and sometimes self-flagellation
  • It represents the struggle against injustice and oppression
  • Sunni Muslims observe Ashura as a day of optional fasting, remembering Musa's deliverance from Pharaoh

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two Islamic festivals.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Id-ul-Adha — the Festival of Sacrifice, celebrated during Hajj. Muslims sacrifice an animal to remember Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son at Allah's command. The meat is shared between family, friends and the poor. It teaches obedience to Allah and generosity. 2) Id-ul-Fitr — the Festival of Fast-Breaking, marking the end of Ramadan. Muslims attend special prayers, celebrate with food and gifts, and pay Zakah al-Fitr so the poor can also celebrate. It is a joyful thanksgiving for the strength to complete the fast.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Islam practices questions: Know the Five Pillars in detail and be able to explain each one. Distinguish between Sunni and Shi'a practices (e.g. additional Ten Obligatory Acts in Shi'a Islam). Explain the difference between greater and lesser jihad. Use specific examples for Hajj (Ka'aba, Arafat, Muzdalifah, Mina).

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think jihad means "holy war." Wrong: "Jihad means holy war." Correct: "Jihad means "struggle" or "striving." Greater jihad is the internal spiritual struggle to live as a good Muslim. Lesser jihad is armed struggle, which has strict conditions and is a last resort."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that id-ul-adha, id-ul-fitr and ashura; their origins, meanings and importance in britain today.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
