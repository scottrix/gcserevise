Cases & Their Uses

Understanding the six Latin cases (nominative, vocative, accusative, genitive, dative, ablative) and their grammatical functions in sentences.

Key Fact: Latin has six cases: nominative, vocative, accusative, genitive, dative, and ablative. Each case signals a different grammatical function.
Key Fact: Nominative: subject of the verb and complement after verbs like sum (e.g. puella currit — the girl runs; puella est Regina — the girl is queen).
Key Fact: Vocative: used for direct address (e.g. O puella! — O girl!). It is usually identical to the nominative except in second declension masculine singular (e.g. Marce, not Marcus).
Key Fact: Accusative: direct object of a transitive verb, and object of many prepositions (e.g. puellam video — I see the girl; ad villam — to the villa).
Key Fact: Genitive: possession or relationship, answering 'of whom?' or 'whose?' (e.g. villa patris — the house of the father / the father's house). Partitive genitive with nouns of quantity.
Key Fact: Dative: indirect object, answering 'to/for whom?' (e.g. puellae librum dedi — I gave a book to the girl). Also used with compound verbs and certain adjectives.
Key Fact: Ablative: separation, means, accompaniment, time when/within which, and object of many prepositions (e.g. gladio pugnavit — he fought with a sword; ab urbe — from the city).
Key Fact: Prepositions take either the accusative (motion towards: ad, to; in, into; per, through) or the ablative (position: in, in/on; ex, from; cum, with; de, about/from).
Key Fact: Some prepositions can take both cases with different meanings: in + ablative = in/on (position), in + accusative = into/onto (motion towards).
Key Fact: The ablative absolute uses a noun and participle in the ablative case to set the background circumstances: urbe capta, milites fugerunt (the city having been captured, the soldiers fled).

✅ Answers

  1. It marks the subject of the verb and the complement after verbs like sum (to be). For example: puella currit (the girl runs).
  2. Only in the second declension masculine singular, where -us becomes -e (e.g. Marce, not Marcus) and -ius becomes -i (e.g. fili, not filius).
  3. The accusative marks the direct object (what is directly acted upon: puellam video — I see the girl). The dative marks the indirect object (to/for whom: puellae librum dedi — I gave a book to the girl).
  4. Means: gladio pugnavit (he fought with a sword). Accompaniment: cum amicis venit (he came with friends). Time when: prima luce (at first light). Also: separation (ab urbe — from the city) or prepositional object (in villa — in the house).
  5. In + accusative = motion into/onto (in villam — into the house). In + ablative = position in/on (in villa — in the house).
  6. The partitive genitive indicates 'of the whole' with nouns of quantity. Example: magna pars militum (a great part of the soldiers); numerus hostium (the number of the enemy).
  7. A grammatical construction using a noun and participle in the ablative to set background circumstances, grammatically independent of the main clause. Example: urbe capta, milites fugerunt (the city having been captured, the soldiers fled).
  8. Accusative: ad (to), in (into), per (through), trans (across), contra (against). Ablative: ab (from), ex (out of), cum (with), de (about/from), in (in/on). Some, like in, take both with different meanings.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When translating, always identify the case of each noun — the ending tells you its grammatical role, not word order.
  • Learn which prepositions take which case: ad + acc., ab + abl., in + acc./abl. — this is frequently tested.
  • The dative is often missed in translation: always check for 'to' or 'for' when you see dative endings.
  • For the ablative absolute, translate as 'with the [noun] having been [participle]' or 'when/after the [noun] was [participle]'.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Latin word order determines grammatical function, just like in English. ✓ In Latin, case endings (not word order) determine grammatical function. A noun in the accusative is the object regardless of where it appears in the sentence.

✗ The genitive case always means 'of' as in belonging to someone. ✓ While possession is the most common use, the genitive also expresses partitive (of the whole), descriptive (of a kind), and objective (of the action) relationships.

✗ The ablative case is always used with a preposition. ✓ The ablative is used without a preposition for means (gladio — with a sword), accompaniment with cum (cum amicis), time when (prima luce), and the ablative absolute. Prepositions like ab, ex, de also take the ablative.

✗ The accusative is only used for the direct object. ✓ The accusative also marks the object of many prepositions (ad, per, trans, contra), the subject of an infinitive clause, extent of time/space (decem annos — for ten years), and the inner object (vitam vivere — to live a life).

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain the six Latin cases and their main functions, giving an example for each. (8 marks)

The nominative marks the subject of the verb and the complement after sum: puella currit (the girl runs — puella is the subject). The vocative is used for direct address: O puella! (O girl!); it is identical to the nominative except in 2nd declension masculine singular (Marce, not Marcus). The accusative marks the direct object of a transitive verb and the object of many prepositions: puellam video (I see the girl — puellam is the direct object); ad villam (to the villa — ad takes the accusative). The genitive indicates possession or 'of': villa patris (the house of the father — patris shows whose house it is). The dative marks the indirect object, answering 'to/for whom?': puellae librum dedi (I gave a book to the girl — puellae is the indirect object, the recipient). The ablative has multiple uses: means (gladio pugnavit — he fought with/by means of a sword), accompaniment with cum (cum amicis — with friends), and object of prepositions like ab, ex, de (ab urbe — from the city). The ablative absolute uses a noun and participle together: urbe capta (the city having been captured). Together, these six cases allow Latin to indicate every grammatical relationship through word endings rather than word order, making Latin syntax flexible and precise.
