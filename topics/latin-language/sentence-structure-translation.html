Sentence Structure & Translation

Understanding Latin sentence structure, word order, agreement of adjectives, use of conjunctions, and strategies for translating Latin to English.

Key Fact: Latin word order is much freer than English because grammatical relationships are shown by case endings, not position. The most common order is Subject-Object-Verb (SOV).
Key Fact: The verb typically comes at or near the end of a Latin sentence, which is the biggest difference from English SVO order.
Key Fact: Adjectives must agree with the noun they modify in gender, number, and case (GNC): puella bona (good girl, fem. nom. sg.), servum bonum (good slave, masc. acc. sg.).
Key Fact: Relative clauses use the relative pronoun qui, quae, quod (who, which, that) which must agree with its antecedent in gender and number, but takes its case from its own clause.
Key Fact: Common conjunctions: et (and), sed (but), aut (or), -que (and, attached to the second word), tamen (however), igitur (therefore), nam/quia (because).
Key Fact: Indirect statement (accusative + infinitive construction): the subject of the reported speech is in the accusative, and the verb is an infinitive (e.g. dicit puellam venire — he says the girl is coming).
Key Fact: Purpose clauses use ut + subjunctive for positive purpose (in order to) and ne + subjunctive for negative purpose (in order not to).
Key Fact: Result clauses use ut + subjunctive with a word like tam, tantus, or ita in the main clause (so...that...).
Key Fact: Temporal clauses use cum + subjunctive (when/since/although, depending on context) and postquam/ubi + indicative (after/when).
Key Fact: When translating, read the whole sentence first, identify the verb, then work out the case of each noun to determine its function.

✅ Answers

  1. Subject-Object-Verb (SOV). The verb usually comes at or near the end. However, word order is flexible because case endings show grammatical relationships.
  2. Gender, Number, Case: an adjective must match the noun it describes in all three. For example, puella bona (fem. nom. sg.) but servum bonum (masc. acc. sg.).
  3. The relative pronoun agrees with its antecedent in gender and number, but its case is determined by its function within the relative clause itself.
  4. Indirect statement: the subject of reported speech is in the accusative, and the verb becomes an infinitive. Example: dicit puellam venire (he says the girl is coming).
  5. Both use ut + subjunctive, but a purpose clause translates as 'in order to' and has no signal word in the main clause. A result clause translates as 'so...that' and is signalled by tam, tantus, ita, or similar in the main clause.
  6. Cum can mean 'when' (temporal), 'since' (causal), or 'although' (concessive), depending on context. All three use the subjunctive mood.
  7. Ne + subjunctive forms a negative purpose clause ('in order not to') or a negative indirect command ('not to'). Example: venit ne pater videret (he came so that his father would not see).
  8. -que means 'and' but is attached to the end of the second word (puellae puerique — girls and boys), whereas et stands as a separate word. -que is more emphatic, joining the pair closely.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always identify the main verb first when translating — it anchors the sentence and tells you the tense and subject.
  • For relative pronouns, find the antecedent first (match gender and number), then work out the pronoun's case from its clause.
  • Accusative + infinitive is tested in every exam: look for a verb of saying/thinking/knowing followed by accusative noun + infinitive.
  • When you see ut + subjunctive, check the main clause for tam/tantus/ita to decide if it is purpose or result.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Latin sentences must be translated word by word in order. ✓ Latin word order does not determine meaning. You must identify the verb, then use case endings to work out each noun's function before producing idiomatic English.

✗ The relative pronoun always has the same case as its antecedent. ✓ The relative pronoun agrees with its antecedent in gender and number only. Its case is determined by its grammatical role within the relative clause (e.g. as subject, object, etc.).

✗ Ut always means 'in order to'. ✓ Ut + subjunctive can mean 'in order to' (purpose), 'so that' (result), or 'to' (indirect command). Context and signal words (tam, tantus) determine which meaning applies.

✗ Indirect statement in Latin uses 'that' with a finite verb, like English. ✓ Latin indirect statement uses the accusative + infinitive construction, not 'that' + finite verb. The subject of the reported speech becomes accusative, and the verb becomes an infinitive.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how to approach translating a complex Latin sentence, including how to identify subordinate clauses and the accusative + infinitive construction. (6 marks)

When translating a complex Latin sentence, first read the whole sentence to get the general sense. Identify the main verb (usually at or near the end) and determine its person, tense, and voice — this anchors the sentence. Next, identify each noun's case from its ending to determine its grammatical function (nominative = subject, accusative = object, etc.). Then look for subordinate clauses. If you find a relative pronoun (qui/quae/quod), trace it back to its antecedent (agreeing in gender and number) and work out the pronoun's case from its own clause. For the accusative + infinitive construction, look for a verb of saying, thinking, or knowing (dicit, putat, scit) followed by an accusative noun or pronoun and an infinitive verb — translate as 'he says/thinks/knows that [subject] [verb]'. For example: dicit hostes venire means 'he says that the enemy are coming' (hostes is accusative subject, venire is present infinitive). Purpose clauses use ut/ne + subjunctive ('in order to/not to'), while result clauses have tam/tantus in the main clause plus ut + subjunctive ('so...that'). Finally, rearrange the elements into natural English word order (SVO) while preserving the precise meaning conveyed by the Latin case endings, producing a translation that is both accurate and idiomatic.
