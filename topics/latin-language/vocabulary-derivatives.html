Vocabulary & English Derivatives

Building Latin vocabulary through the Defined Vocabulary List (DVL), and understanding how Latin words have given rise to many English derivatives.

Key Fact: The Defined Vocabulary List (DVL) contains the words that may appear in GCSE Latin translation exams — approximately 400-500 words depending on the board.
Key Fact: Learning Latin vocabulary through English derivatives is highly effective: e.g. ambulare (to walk) → ambulance; videre (to see) → video; scribere (to write) → scribe/script.
Key Fact: Common Latin prefixes: trans- (across → transport), sub- (under → submarine), pre- (before → predict), re- (back/again → return), ex/e- (out of → export).
Key Fact: Common Latin suffixes: -tion (action/state from verb stems → narration from narro), -al (pertaining to → naval from navis), -ous (full of → furious from furia).
Key Fact: Many English words come directly from Latin: aqua (water → aquatic), luna (moon → lunar), stella (star → stellar), terra (earth → terrestrial).
Key Fact: Legal and scientific vocabulary is heavily Latin-based: habeas corpus, subpoena, pro bono, quorum; and binomial nomenclature (Homo sapiens, Canis lupus).
Key Fact: The OCR DVL is published in the specification and must be memorised — words outside the DVL are glossed in the exam.
Key Fact: Learning noun-adjective pairs together helps retention: puella bona (good girl), vir fortis (brave man), bellum magnum (great war).
Key Fact: Important high-frequency words: sum (to be), habeo (to have), dico (to say), facio (to make/do), venio (to come), video (to see), mitto (to send).
Key Fact: Latin abbreviations survive in English: etc. (et cetera), i.e. (id est), e.g. (exempli gratia), N.B. (nota bene), vs. (versus), a.m. (ante meridiem), p.m. (post meridiem).

✅ Answers

  1. The DVL is the official list of Latin words that may appear in the GCSE translation papers. Words outside the DVL are glossed in exams. It contains approximately 400-500 words that must be memorised.
  2. Videre (to see) → video; scribere (to write) → scribble, scribe, script; audire (to hear) → audible, audience; dicere (to say) → dictate, diction; facere (to make/do) → factory, fact (any three).
  3. Aqua (water) → aquatic, aqueduct, aquarium. Luna (moon) → lunar, lunatic. Terra (earth) → terrestrial, territory, terrain.
  4. Trans- (across) → transport, translate. Sub- (under) → submarine, subordinate. Pre- (before) → predict, prefix. Re- (back/again) → return, revisit. Ex/e- (out of) → export, eject (any five).
  5. i.e. = id est (that is); e.g. = exempli gratia (for the sake of example); N.B. = nota bene (note well).
  6. Audible from audire (to hear). Dictation from dicere (to say). Manuscript from manus (hand) + scribere (to write) — written by hand.
  7. Sum (to be), habeo (to have), dico (to say), facio (to make/do), venio (to come), video (to see), mitto (to send), capio (to take), ago (to do/drive), pono (to place) (any five).
  8. It creates memory links: knowing that 'portare' means 'to carry' makes it easy to remember transport, portable, porter, export, import, and support. Derivatives provide English-language hooks for each Latin word.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Systematically learn the DVL — use flashcards or spaced repetition, and test yourself on both Latin-to-English and English-to-Latin.
  • When you encounter an unfamiliar word in the exam, check for a recognisable root or derivative to work out the meaning.
  • Learn vocabulary in context: practise with whole sentences, not just isolated word lists.
  • Pay attention to words that look similar but have different meanings (e.g. dare = to give, not 'to dare'; ferre = to carry, not 'to fear').

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Latin Exam Tips:
Always use specific Latin quotations to support your answers. For literature questions, apply the PEA method (Point, Evidence, Analysis). Use precise grammatical terminology when discussing language. For civilisation questions, reference named ancient sources. When translating, identify the main verb first and then work out each noun's case before producing idiomatic English.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ All English words come from Latin. ✓ English has Germanic origins and also borrows from French, Norse, Greek, and many other languages. However, approximately 60% of English words have Latin or French (Latin-derived) roots, especially in academic, legal, and scientific vocabulary.

✗ The Latin word 'dare' means "to dare." ✓ Dare means 'to give' in Latin (da mihi — give me). The English 'dare' comes from Old Norse and is unrelated. Similarly, ferre means 'to carry', not 'to fear'.

✗ If a Latin word looks like an English word, they must have the same meaning. ✓ False friends exist: Latin 'virtus' means courage/manliness (not virtue in the modern sense), 'forma' means shape/beauty (not form in all English senses), and 'pars' means part/share but is not related to English 'parse'.

✗ You only need to know the nominative form of each noun. ✓ You must learn the nominative AND genitive singular of every noun, because the genitive identifies the declension and reveals the stem. For verbs, you must learn all four principal parts.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how Latin vocabulary has influenced English, giving at least six specific examples of derivatives from different Latin roots. (6 marks)

Latin has profoundly shaped English vocabulary, particularly in academic, legal, and scientific domains. Many English words derive directly from Latin roots. From the verb videre (to see), we get video, visible, and vision. From audire (to hear), come audible, audience, and audio. From scribere (to write), we derive scribe, scribble, manuscript (manus + scribere — written by hand), and prescription. From portare (to carry), English takes transport, portable, export (out + carry), and import (in + carry). From dicere (to say), come dictate, diction, predict (say before), and verdict (true saying). From the noun aqua (water), derive aquatic, aqueduct (water channel), and aquarium. These derivatives work through Latin prefixes and suffixes: ex- (out) + portare = exportare (to carry out); trans- (across) + portare = transportare (to carry across). Understanding these patterns enables students to decode unfamiliar English words from their Latin roots and, conversely, to remember Latin vocabulary more effectively by linking each word to its English descendants. This etymological approach transforms vocabulary learning from rote memorisation into a connected network of meaning.
