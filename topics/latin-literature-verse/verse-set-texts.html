Verse Set Texts

Understanding the verse set texts prescribed for GCSE Latin, including epic and lyric poetry, their cultural and literary significance.

Key Fact: GCSE Latin verse set texts are prescribed by each exam board and change every two years. Check which texts are current for your exam year.
Key Fact: Common verse authors include Virgil (Aeneid), Ovid (Metamorphoses), Catullus (poems), and Horace (Odes and Satires).
Key Fact: Virgil's Aeneid is the national epic of Rome, telling how Aeneas fled Troy and founded the Roman race in Italy. Key themes: pietas, fate, the cost of empire.
Key Fact: Ovid's Metamorphoses tells myths of transformation, often with wit, irony, and playful subversion of epic conventions.
Key Fact: Catullus wrote personal lyric poetry about love, friendship, and social satire. His poems to Lesbia are among the most famous love poetry in any language.
Key Fact: Horace's Odes are polished, philosophical lyrics reflecting on life, death, love, and Roman values with understated elegance.
Key Fact: The Aeneid was written under Augustus (29-19 BC) and explores the tension between personal happiness and public duty.
Key Fact: Ovid was exiled by Augustus; the Metamorphoses (AD 8) can be read as subtly questioning Augustan values.
Key Fact: Understanding metre is essential: the hexameter is the metre of epic, while lyric poets use various metres (Sapphic, Alcaic, elegiac couplet).
Key Fact: Each board prescribes approximately 110-120 lines of verse text for detailed study of translation, analysis, and evaluation.

Q: Name the four most common verse authors studied at GCSE Latin.

✅ Answers

  1. An epic poem by Virgil (29-19 BC) telling how the Trojan hero Aeneas fled Troy, wandered the Mediterranean, and reached Italy to found the Roman race. It explores themes of pietas, fate, and the cost of empire.
  2. Ovid's long narrative poem (AD 8) collecting myths of transformation. Ovid is distinctive for his wit, irony, playful subversion of epic conventions, and sympathetic portrayal of the victims of power.
  3. Catullus (c.84-54 BC) was a Roman lyric poet whose work covers passionate love (poems to Lesbia), friendship, political invective, and social commentary.
  4. The Aeneid celebrates Rome's destiny and Augustus's rule by showing Aeneas as the ideal Roman who sacrifices personal happiness for duty. However, Virgil also shows the human cost of this destiny.
  5. Ovid was exiled by Augustus in AD 8, partly because of the Metamorphoses and Ars Amatoria. His exile suggests his poetry was seen as politically subversive.
  6. Every two years. You must check your exam board's specification for the current prescribed texts for your year of examination.
  7. Dactylic hexameter: six feet per line, each foot being a dactyl (- uu) or spondee (--). This is the metre of the Aeneid and Metamorphoses.
  8. Virgil (Aeneid), Ovid (Metamorphoses), Catullus (lyric poems), and Horace (Odes).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always confirm which verse texts are prescribed for YOUR exam year and board.
  • Learn the background of each author: their life, historical period, and reasons for writing.
  • For the Aeneid, know the overall plot even though you only study prescribed passages.
  • Prepare detailed notes on each passage: translation, stylistic features, key themes, and links to the wider work.

GCSE Latin Exam Tips — Verse Set Texts:

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ The Aeneid is purely a celebration of Augustus and Roman power. ✓ While the Aeneid celebrates Rome's destiny, Virgil also powerfully expresses the cost of that destiny: Dido's death, Turnus's killing, and Aeneas's own suffering all complicate any simple pro-Augustan reading.

✗ Ovid's Metamorphoses is just a collection of entertaining stories. ✓ The Metamorphoses is a sophisticated work that questions power, authority, and the nature of civilisation. Many episodes can be read as political allegory criticising Augustan Rome.

✗ You only need to know the English meaning of the set text. ✓ You must be able to translate from the Latin, analyse poetic techniques, and evaluate themes and literary significance. English translation alone is not sufficient.

✗ Catullus only wrote love poetry. ✓ While famous for his love poems to Lesbia, Catullus also wrote invective, epithalamia (wedding songs), elegy, and translations of Greek poetry.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Compare how Virgil and Ovid approach the theme of power in their verse set texts. (8 marks)

Virgil and Ovid both engage with the theme of power but from very different perspectives. Virgil's Aeneid presents power as a divine imperative: Aeneas must fulfil his fate (fatum) to found Rome, ordained by Jupiter. Power is legitimate when exercised in service of Rome's destined future, and Aeneas's pietas demands he subordinate personal desires to this mission. However, Virgil does not present this uncritically: Dido's death shows the devastating human cost of imperial destiny, and Aeneas's final killing of Turnus in rage raises questions about whether power corrupts even the most dutiful hero. Ovid's Metamorphoses is far more sceptical. The gods repeatedly abuse their power to exploit mortals: Jupiter pursues Io, Europa, and Callisto; Apollo pursues Daphne; and divine power is shown as arbitrary, selfish, and destructive. Where Virgil's gods direct fate for Rome's glory, Ovid's gods act from personal desire. Ovid also shows transformation itself as an exercise of power, stripping mortals of agency. This can be read as a subtle critique of Augustus, who 'transformed' Rome from Republic to Empire. Where Virgil legitimises power through destiny, Ovid exposes its cruelty and self-interest.

Assessment Objective Analysis
