Consumer protection law: the Consumer Rights Act 2015 and trading standards

Consumer Rights

Consumer protection law: the Consumer Rights Act 2015 and trading standards

Key Fact: The Consumer Rights Act 2015 is the main law protecting consumers in contracts with businesses
Key Fact: Goods must be of satisfactory quality, fit for purpose and as described — if not, the consumer has rights to reject, repair or replacement
Key Fact: For faulty goods, the consumer has 30 days to reject for a full refund; after 30 days, they can request repair or replacement
Key Fact: If a repair or replacement fails, the consumer is entitled to a refund (which may be reduced for use)
Key Fact: Services must be performed with reasonable care and skill, within a reasonable time and at a reasonable price
Key Fact: Digital content (downloads, apps, games) must also be of satisfactory quality, fit for purpose and as described under the 2015 Act
Key Fact: Unfair terms in consumer contracts are not binding on the consumer — terms that create a significant imbalance favouring the trader are unfair
Key Fact: The trader cannot exclude liability for death or personal injury caused by negligence — other exclusions must be reasonable
Key Fact: Trading Standards enforces consumer protection law — it can prosecute rogue traders and advise consumers
Key Fact: Citizens Advice provides free consumer advice and can help resolve disputes with traders
Key Fact: Misrepresentation is a false statement of fact that induces a person into a contract — remedies include rescission and damages
Key Fact: The Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) investigates anti-competitive practices and enforces consumer law at a national level

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Consumer Rights, you must know:

  • Consumer Rights Act 2015: The key legislation protecting consumers — it sets standards for goods, services and digital content and provides remedies when these standards are not met
  • Satisfactory quality: Goods must meet the standard a reasonable person would regard as satisfactory, considering price, description and any other relevant factors
  • Fit for purpose: Goods must be fit for the purpose the consumer told the trader they wanted them for, or for any normal purpose of that type of goods
  • Unfair terms: Contract terms that create a significant imbalance between the trader and consumer, to the consumer's detriment — such terms are not binding on the consumer
  • Trading Standards: Local authority services that enforce consumer protection law, investigate complaints and prosecute rogue traders
  • Misrepresentation: A false statement of fact made by one party that induces the other party to enter into a contract — the misled party can rescind the contract and may claim damages

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What are the three statutory rights when buying goods?

Q: What remedies are available for faulty goods under the Consumer Rights Act 2015?

Q: What rights do consumers have when services are poor?

Q: What is an unfair term in a consumer contract?

Q: How is consumer protection law enforced?

✅ Answers

  1. Goods must be of satisfactory quality, fit for purpose and match their description. These are implied terms under the Consumer Rights Act 2015 — if any is breached, the consumer has a right to a remedy.
  2. Within 30 days: full refund (short-term right to reject). After 30 days but within 6 months: repair or replacement. If repair or replacement fails: refund (may be reduced for use). After 6 months: consumer must prove the fault was present at delivery.
  3. Services must be performed with reasonable care and skill, within a reasonable time and at a reasonable price. If not, the consumer can require the trader to redo the service or, if that is impossible, claim a price reduction.
  4. A term that creates a significant imbalance between the trader and consumer, to the consumer's detriment. Examples include excessive cancellation fees, excluding liability for breach, and changing terms without notice. Unfair terms are not binding on the consumer.
  5. Trading Standards officers enforce the law locally — they investigate complaints, inspect businesses and prosecute rogue traders. The Competition and Markets Authority enforces at national level. Citizens Advice provides free consumer advice and helps resolve disputes.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know the three implied terms for goods (satisfactory quality, fit for purpose, as described) — always mention all three
  • The 30-day short-term right to reject is a key reform of the 2015 Act — know the timeline of remedies
  • Unfair terms are frequently tested — know examples and be able to explain why they are unfair
  • Distinguish between remedies for goods, services and digital content — they differ slightly
  • Know the roles of Trading Standards, CMA and Citizens Advice in enforcing consumer rights

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Thinking consumers can always get a full refund for faulty goods ✓ A full refund is only available within 30 days (short-term right to reject). After 30 days, the consumer is entitled to repair or replacement first — a refund may be reduced for use.

✗ Confusing the Consumer Rights Act 2015 with the Sale of Goods Act 1979 ✓ The Consumer Rights Act 2015 replaced key parts of the Sale of Goods Act for consumer contracts — it covers goods, services AND digital content, which the older Act did not

✗ Saying unfair terms make the whole contract void ✓ Only the unfair term itself is not binding on the consumer — the rest of the contract continues to operate if it is fair

✗ Thinking the consumer must prove a fault existed at delivery within 6 months ✓ Within 6 months, the law presumes the fault was present at delivery — the TRADER must prove otherwise. After 6 months, the consumer must prove the fault existed at delivery.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How effectively does the Consumer Rights Act 2015 protect consumers? (15 marks)

The Consumer Rights Act 2015 provides the strongest consumer protection framework in English legal history, but enforcement gaps and practical barriers mean that protection is not always effective in reality. The Act consolidates and modernises consumer law into a single, accessible statute, making rights clearer for both consumers and traders. The three implied terms for goods — satisfactory quality, fit for purpose and as described — set clear minimum standards that traders must meet. The short-term right to reject within 30 days gives consumers a powerful remedy that the old law did not provide. The inclusion of digital content is a significant modernisation, recognising that consumers buy apps, downloads and games that were previously unprotected. Unfair terms provisions prevent traders from imposing one-sided conditions, and the transparency requirement ensures that key terms are brought to the consumer's attention. However, the Act has significant limitations. Its protections depend on consumers knowing their rights — research consistently shows low awareness of consumer rights, particularly among vulnerable groups. Enforcement relies on Trading Standards, which has suffered severe funding cuts, meaning many rogue traders go unprosecuted. The small claims limit means that many consumers must use the courts to enforce rights, which is daunting and time-consuming even without legal costs. The six-month reverse burden of proof is helpful, but after that period consumers face the difficult task of proving the fault existed at delivery. Online shopping from overseas sellers creates enforcement problems — the Act may not apply, and practical remedies are limited. Alternative dispute resolution schemes exist but are voluntary for traders, limiting their effectiveness. Overall, the Consumer Rights Act 2015 provides excellent protection in law, but the gap between legal rights and practical enforcement means many consumers cannot effectively exercise their rights.
