L2: Courts & Court Structure

OCR J271

The hierarchy of criminal and civil courts in England and Wales

Courts & Court Structure

The hierarchy of criminal and civil courts in England and Wales

Key Fact: Criminal courts deal with offences against the state; civil courts deal with disputes between individuals or organisations
Key Fact: The Magistrates' Court hears 95% of criminal cases — summary offences and either-way offences
Key Fact: The Crown Court hears indictable offences (serious crimes) and appeals from the Magistrates' Court
Key Fact: The High Court hears the most serious civil cases, divided into three divisions: Queen's Bench, Chancery and Family
Key Fact: The County Court handles most civil claims including contract disputes, tort and debt recovery
Key Fact: The Court of Appeal (Criminal and Civil Divisions) hears appeals on points of law and sentence
Key Fact: The Supreme Court is the highest court — it hears appeals on points of law of general public importance
Key Fact: Tribunals resolve specialist disputes (employment, immigration, tax) more informally than courts
Key Fact: The Youth Court handles criminal cases involving defendants aged 10-17
Key Fact: The court hierarchy ensures that lower courts follow higher courts' decisions under the doctrine of precedent
Key Fact: Appeal routes allow cases to be reviewed by higher courts if there are errors of law or unduly harsh sentences
Key Fact: Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) — mediation, arbitration and negotiation — resolves disputes outside court

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Courts & Court Structure, you must know:

  • Summary offence: A minor criminal offence tried only in the Magistrates' Court (e.g. motoring offences, minor assault)
  • Indictable offence: A serious criminal offence tried in the Crown Court (e.g. murder, rape, robbery)
  • Either-way offence: An offence that can be tried in either the Magistrates' Court or Crown Court (e.g. theft, burglary)
  • Tribunal: A specialist body that resolves disputes in specific areas (employment, immigration, tax) more informally than courts
  • Alternative Dispute Resolution: Methods of resolving disputes outside court — mediation, arbitration and negotiation
  • Appellate court: A court that hears appeals from lower courts — the Court of Appeal and Supreme Court are appellate courts

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the difference between criminal and civil courts?

Q: What cases does the Crown Court hear?

Q: What are the three divisions of the High Court?

Q: Why are tribunals used instead of courts for some disputes?

Q: What is the role of the Supreme Court?

✅ Answers

  1. Criminal courts deal with offences against the state (prosecuted by the Crown); civil courts deal with disputes between individuals or organisations, where the claimant sues the defendant.
  2. Indictable offences (serious crimes like murder and rape), either-way offences sent from the Magistrates' Court, and appeals from the Magistrates' Court against conviction or sentence.
  3. Queen's Bench (contract and tort), Chancery (business, trusts, tax) and Family (divorce, children, financial remedies).
  4. Tribunals are cheaper, faster, less formal and have specialist expertise in their area. They are more accessible for ordinary people.
  5. It is the highest court in the UK, hearing appeals on points of law of general public importance. Its decisions bind all lower courts.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know the full court hierarchy for both criminal and civil cases
  • Use the correct names: Magistrates' Court (not 'magistrate court'), Crown Court, High Court
  • Explain the difference between summary, either-way and indictable offences
  • Tribunals are increasingly important — know their advantages over courts
  • ADR is often examined — know the three types and their benefits

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Confusing criminal and civil court hierarchies ✓ Criminal: Magistrates' Court → Crown Court → Court of Appeal (Criminal) → Supreme Court. Civil: County Court/High Court → Court of Appeal (Civil) → Supreme Court

✗ Thinking the Magistrates' Court only deals with minor cases ✓ The Magistrates' Court hears 95% of all criminal cases and also handles some civil matters and youth cases

✗ Confusing tribunals with courts ✓ Tribunals are specialist bodies that are cheaper, less formal and have expertise in specific areas — they are not courts

✗ Forgetting the Youth Court as part of the criminal court system ✓ The Youth Court handles criminal cases for defendants aged 10-17 and has special procedures to protect young people

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How effective is the court hierarchy in delivering justice? (15 marks)

The court hierarchy is broadly effective in delivering justice because it ensures consistency, provides clear appeal routes and assigns cases to appropriate levels of formality and seriousness. The doctrine of precedent, which depends on the hierarchy, ensures that lower courts follow decisions of higher courts, creating predictability and fairness in the law. Appeal routes allow errors to be corrected — a defendant convicted unfairly in the Magistrates' Court can appeal to the Crown Court, and points of law can reach the Supreme Court for final determination. The division between summary, either-way and indictable offences means that minor cases are dealt with quickly and cheaply in the Magistrates' Court, while serious cases receive the full formality and safeguards of the Crown Court, including jury trial. Tribunals extend access to justice by providing specialist, cheaper and less formal procedures for particular disputes. However, the court hierarchy has significant weaknesses. Delays are a serious problem — Crown Court cases can take months to come to trial, denying justice to both defendants and victims. The Magistrates' Court, while efficient, lacks the safeguards of jury trial and relies on lay volunteers who may not understand complex legal issues. Cost is a major barrier — the higher up the hierarchy, the more expensive justice becomes, meaning effective access to justice often depends on wealth. Furthermore, the complexity of the system disadvantages litigants in person. Overall, the court hierarchy provides an effective framework for justice in principle, but delays, costs and complexity undermine its effectiveness in practice.
