The English Legal System

Overview of the English legal system: sources of law, court structure and legal personnel

Key Fact: The English legal system is based on common law (judge-made law) and statute law (Acts of Parliament)
Key Fact: Parliament is the supreme law-making body — parliamentary sovereignty means no Parliament can bind its successors
Key Fact: The court hierarchy ensures that lower courts follow the decisions of higher courts (doctrine of precedent)
Key Fact: The Supreme Court is the highest court in the UK for civil and criminal matters
Key Fact: Legal personnel include judges, magistrates, solicitors, barristers and legal executives
Key Fact: Judges are independent — they are not controlled by the government and must decide cases impartially
Key Fact: Magistrates are unpaid volunteers who hear around 95% of criminal cases in the Magistrates' Court
Key Fact: Solicitors advise clients, prepare cases and can now represent clients in all courts
Key Fact: Barristers specialise in courtroom advocacy and give specialist legal opinions
Key Fact: The legal system aims to be fair, accessible and efficient — but access to justice remains a concern
Key Fact: Legal aid provides funding for those who cannot afford legal representation
Key Fact: The rule of law means everyone, including the government, is subject to the law

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For The English Legal System, you must know:

  • Common law: Law developed through judicial decisions (case law) rather than Acts of Parliament
  • Statute law: Law made by Parliament in the form of Acts (primary legislation)
  • Parliamentary sovereignty: The principle that Parliament is the supreme law-making body and its laws cannot be overridden by other bodies
  • Doctrine of precedent: The principle that courts must follow decisions of higher courts in similar cases (stare decisis)
  • Rule of law: The principle that everyone is subject to the law, including the government, and the law should be applied equally
  • Legal aid: Government funding to help people who cannot afford legal representation

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the difference between common law and statute law?

Q: Why is parliamentary sovereignty important?

Q: What is the doctrine of precedent?

Q: How do magistrates differ from judges?

Q: What is the rule of law?

✅ Answers

  1. Common law is judge-made law developed through court decisions over centuries; statute law is written law made by Parliament through Acts.
  2. It means Parliament is the supreme law-making body — its laws cannot be overridden by courts or other bodies, and no Parliament can bind a future Parliament.
  3. Courts must follow decisions made by higher courts in similar cases. This ensures consistency and predictability in the law.
  4. Magistrates are unpaid volunteers without formal legal qualifications who sit in Magistrates' Courts. Judges are legally qualified professionals appointed to hear cases in higher courts.
  5. The principle that everyone, including the government, is subject to the same law and the law must be applied equally to all.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know the difference between common law and statute law — both are sources of law
  • Parliamentary sovereignty is a key concept — be able to explain and evaluate it
  • The court hierarchy is essential knowledge — know which courts hear which cases
  • Use specific examples of legal personnel and their roles
  • Legal aid is often examined as a contemporary issue — know its scope and limitations

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Confusing common law with statute law ✓ Common law = judge-made through court decisions; statute law = made by Parliament through Acts

✗ Saying Parliament is controlled by the courts ✓ Parliamentary sovereignty means courts cannot override Acts of Parliament — courts interpret and apply them

✗ Thinking magistrates are professional judges ✓ Magistrates are unpaid volunteers — they are not legally qualified (though they have legal advisers)

✗ Describing the rule of law as just 'following rules' ✓ The rule of law means everyone, including government, is subject to law applied equally — it limits arbitrary power

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How far does the English legal system provide equal access to justice? (15 marks)

The English legal system aims to provide equal access to justice but falls short in practice, particularly for those who cannot afford legal representation. Legal aid exists to help those on low incomes, ensuring that the most serious criminal charges and some civil matters can be funded. The availability of duty solicitors at police stations and Magistrates' Courts provides immediate access to advice for anyone accused of a crime. The small claims track in civil courts allows individuals to represent themselves without expensive lawyers. Conditional fee agreements (no win, no fee) provide access to civil justice for those who cannot pay upfront. However, legal aid has been severely cut since LASPO 2012, removing funding for most civil cases including family law, housing and debt. This has created 'deserts' where no legal aid lawyers are available. The complexity of the legal system disadvantages those without representation — litigants in person often struggle to navigate procedures and present their cases effectively. Court fees and legal costs remain prohibitively expensive for many. Furthermore, the geographical distribution of lawyers means rural areas often lack access to legal services. While the system is designed to be fair, the reality is that wealth significantly affects the quality of justice a person receives. Overall, the English legal system provides equal access in principle but not consistently in practice.
