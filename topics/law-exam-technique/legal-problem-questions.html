L23: Legal Problem Questions

OCR J271

How to apply legal rules to factual scenarios in problem questions

Legal Problem Questions

How to apply legal rules to factual scenarios in problem questions

Key Fact: Problem questions present a factual scenario and ask you to advise on the legal position — they test your ability to apply law to facts, not just recall legal rules
Key Fact: The ILAC method provides a clear structure: Issue (identify the legal question), Law (state the relevant legal rules), Application (apply the law to the facts), Conclusion (state the likely outcome)
Key Fact: Identify all the legal issues in the scenario — there are usually multiple issues, such as actus reus, mens rea and any defences in a criminal problem
Key Fact: State the law accurately — name the relevant statute or case, define the legal principle precisely and set out the elements that must be proved
Key Fact: Apply the law to the facts — use the specific details from the scenario to show how the legal rule would operate, explaining each step of your reasoning
Key Fact: Consider alternative arguments — acknowledge where the outcome is uncertain and explain what the prosecution or defence would argue
Key Fact: Reach a clear conclusion — state the likely legal outcome based on your application, acknowledging any areas of uncertainty
Key Fact: In criminal problem questions, work through actus reus first, then mens rea, then any relevant defences, then the likely verdict
Key Fact: In civil problem questions, identify the cause of action (e.g. negligence, contract), set out each element, apply the facts and assess liability and remedies
Key Fact: Always use the specific facts given — the examiner has included them for a reason; do not invent additional facts or ignore those provided
Key Fact: Use tentative language where appropriate — 'it is likely that', 'the court would probably find', 'this suggests that' — the law is not always certain
Key Fact: Time management is crucial: spend about 5 minutes reading and planning, then write systematically through each issue

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Legal Problem Questions, you must know:

  • ILAC: A structured method for answering legal problem questions: Issue (identify the legal question), Law (state the relevant rules), Application (apply law to the facts), Conclusion (state the likely outcome)
  • Application: The process of connecting legal rules to the specific facts of a scenario, explaining how the law would operate in that particular situation — the most important skill in problem questions
  • Legal issue: The legal question that needs to be resolved in a problem scenario, such as whether the defendant has the required mens rea or whether a duty of care exists
  • Alternative argument: Presenting the counter-position or the argument the other side would make, demonstrating that you understand the law has more than one possible interpretation
  • Criminal liability: The legal responsibility for a criminal act, requiring proof of both actus reus (guilty act) and mens rea (guilty mind) beyond reasonable doubt, along with the absence of any valid defence
  • Civil liability: Legal responsibility for a civil wrong such as negligence or breach of contract, requiring proof of the relevant elements on the balance of probabilities

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the ILAC method and why is it useful?

Q: How should you approach a criminal law problem question?

Q: Why is it important to use the specific facts from the scenario?

Q: What language should you use when the legal outcome is uncertain?

Q: How do you handle a scenario with multiple legal issues?

✅ Answers

  1. ILAC stands for Issue, Law, Application, Conclusion. It provides a structured approach to problem questions: first identify the legal issue, then state the relevant legal rules, then apply those rules to the specific facts of the scenario, and finally reach a conclusion about the likely outcome. It ensures you cover all necessary elements and demonstrates legal reasoning to the examiner.
  2. First identify the offence and legal issues. Then work through: (1) actus reus — does the scenario show a guilty act, omission or state of affairs? (2) mens rea — does the defendant have the required mental element (intention or recklessness)? (3) any defences — could the defendant rely on a defence such as self-defence, duress or insanity? (4) conclusion — is the defendant likely to be guilty or not guilty?
  3. The examiner includes specific facts for a reason — they are meant to trigger particular legal principles or tests. Using the specific facts demonstrates that you can apply the law rather than just state it. Ignoring facts or failing to connect them to legal rules loses marks because it shows you have not truly applied the law to the scenario.
  4. Use tentative language such as 'it is likely that', 'the court would probably find', 'this suggests', 'on balance' or 'it could be argued that'. This shows you recognise the law can be uncertain and that different interpretations are possible. Avoid stating outcomes as certain when reasonable arguments exist on both sides.
  5. Address each issue separately and in a logical order. In criminal law, deal with each offence or element in turn (actus reus, mens rea, defences). In civil law, address each cause of action or element. Use clear paragraphing or headings to show the examiner you have identified each issue. Deal with the most significant issues first and allocate your time proportionally.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Read the scenario at least twice — once to get the overall picture and once to identify each legal issue and relevant fact
  • Use the ILAC structure for each issue in the scenario — do not mix issues together
  • Always apply the law to the facts — this is where most marks are gained; stating the law alone is not enough
  • Acknowledge both sides where the outcome is uncertain — show the examiner you can argue from both the prosecution and defence perspectives
  • Underline key facts in the scenario as you read — these are the triggers for specific legal points

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Law Exam Tips — Legal Problem Questions:
1. For Legal Problem Questions questions, identify the relevant area of law and state the legal principles clearly
2. Apply legal rules to the facts of the scenario — don't just state the law
3. When evaluating, consider both the effectiveness and fairness of legal rules
4. Reference relevant cases and statutes when discussing Legal Problem Questions
5. Consider reform proposals and alternative approaches where relevant

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Stating the law without applying it to the scenario facts ✓ You must apply the law to the specific facts — for each legal rule, explain how it operates in the particular scenario you have been given

✗ Missing legal issues in the scenario ✓ Read the scenario carefully and identify every legal issue — there are usually multiple points to address, and missing one means losing marks

✗ Inventing facts that are not in the scenario ✓ Only use the facts given — do not add assumptions or new information. If a fact is unclear, acknowledge the ambiguity and explain how it might affect the outcome either way

✗ Giving a definite answer when the law is uncertain ✓ Use tentative language where the outcome could go either way — say 'the court would likely find' rather than 'the defendant is definitely guilty', and acknowledge counter-arguments

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How should you apply the ILAC method to a criminal law scenario involving assault? (15 marks)

When applying ILAC to a criminal law assault scenario, begin by identifying the Issue: the question is whether the defendant is liable for assault (or battery, ABH, GBH depending on the scenario). Next, state the Law: define the offence precisely. For assault under the Criminal Justice Act 1988, the actus reus is an act which causes the victim to apprehend immediate unlawful violence, and the mens rea is intention or recklessness as to causing that apprehension. For battery, the actus reus is the application of unlawful force and the mens rea is intention or recklessness. Cite the relevant case law — for example, Fagan v MPC confirms that battery requires unlawful force, and R v Ireland establishes that psychiatric harm can amount to bodily harm. Then apply the law to the facts: use the specific details from the scenario. If the defendant raised their fist and moved towards the victim, explain that this constitutes the actus reus of assault because the victim would apprehend immediate unlawful violence. If the defendant claims they were joking, discuss whether the defendant had the mens rea — was it intentional or reckless? Apply R v Venna to explain that recklessness means the defendant was aware of the risk that their actions would cause apprehension. Then consider any defences: could self-defence apply? Use the scenario facts to assess whether the defendant genuinely believed they needed to defend themselves and whether the force used was reasonable (Palmer v R). Finally, reach a Conclusion: on the facts given, the defendant is likely/unlikely to be guilty because... Always acknowledge uncertainty where the facts support different interpretations.
