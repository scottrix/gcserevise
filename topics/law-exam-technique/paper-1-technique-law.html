Paper 1: The Legal System Technique

Specific techniques for Paper 1 of OCR J271 GCSE Law

Key Fact: Paper 1 of OCR J271 GCSE Law covers The Legal System and Criminal Law — it is worth 80 marks and lasts 1 hour 30 minutes
Key Fact: Paper 1 Section A focuses on The Legal System: law making, the court system, legal personnel and access to justice
Key Fact: Paper 1 Section B focuses on Criminal Law: elements of crime, homicide, non-fatal offences, property offences and general defences
Key Fact: The paper contains a mix of short-answer questions (1-4 marks), medium-answer questions (5-10 marks) and extended-response questions (15 marks)
Key Fact: Allocate your time carefully: 90 minutes for 80 marks means roughly 1 minute per mark — spend no more than 15 minutes on a 15-mark question
Key Fact: For short-answer questions (1-4 marks), be concise and direct — one clear point per mark, no elaboration needed
Key Fact: For medium-answer questions (5-10 marks), make your point, explain it and support with an example — PEEL paragraphs work well
Key Fact: For 15-mark questions, use a structured essay format: introduction, two to three paragraphs of balanced analysis, and a justified conclusion
Key Fact: In Section A, focus on accuracy of legal terminology — use the correct names for courts, personnel and processes
Key Fact: In Section B, problem questions require the ILAC method — identify the offence, state the actus reus and mens rea, apply to the facts, consider defences and conclude
Key Fact: Always look at the number of marks — it tells you how much detail is expected. A 2-mark question needs two distinct points; a 15-mark question needs sustained analysis
Key Fact: Read each question twice before answering to ensure you have identified the correct command word and topic

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Paper 1: The Legal System Technique, you must know:

  • Paper 1: The first exam paper for OCR J271 GCSE Law covering The Legal System and Criminal Law, worth 80 marks and lasting 1 hour 30 minutes
  • Section A: The first section of Paper 1 covering The Legal System: law making (Parliament and precedent), the court system, legal personnel and access to justice
  • Section B: The second section of Paper 1 covering Criminal Law: elements of crime, homicide, non-fatal offences, property offences and general defences
  • Mark allocation: The number of marks assigned to each question, indicating how much detail and time is expected — roughly 1 minute per mark and 1 point per mark for short answers
  • PEEL paragraph: A structured paragraph format: Point (make your argument), Evidence (support with a legal example), Explanation (explain how the evidence supports the point), Link (connect back to the question)
  • Timing strategy: A planned approach to dividing exam time proportionally across questions, ensuring all questions are attempted and marks are not lost to running out of time

✅ Answers

  1. Paper 1 covers two areas: Section A — The Legal System (law making through Parliament and precedent, the court system, legal personnel including judges, solicitors and barristers, and access to justice including legal aid); Section B — Criminal Law (elements of crime including actus reus and mens rea, homicide including murder and voluntary manslaughter, non-fatal offences, property offences including theft and robbery, and general defences).
  2. Paper 1 is 90 minutes for 80 marks — roughly 1 minute per mark. For a 15-mark question, spend about 15 minutes (2-3 minutes planning, 10-12 minutes writing, 1-2 minutes checking). For shorter questions, keep answers proportionate. Leave 2-3 minutes at the end to review your answers. Never spend so long on one question that you cannot attempt others.
  3. Section A (Legal System) questions tend to be more knowledge-based — they require accurate recall of legal structures, processes and personnel. Section B (Criminal Law) questions are more likely to include problem scenarios that require application — using the ILAC method to apply legal rules to the facts. Both sections can include evaluation questions requiring balanced analysis.
  4. For a 4-mark question, aim for four distinct, relevant points. Each point should be a separate piece of information — do not repeat the same point in different words. Be concise: one sentence per point is usually sufficient. Do not write an essay for a short-answer question.
  5. If running out of time, prioritise attempting all questions — even a brief answer can gain marks, while a blank answer scores zero. For a 15-mark question, write at least a brief introduction and conclusion with one or two key points rather than leaving it blank. For short answers, give the key points concisely rather than writing in full sentences.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Start with the questions you are most confident about — this builds momentum and ensures you gain marks early
  • For Section A, use precise legal terminology — examiners reward accuracy of terms such as 'magistrate', 'lay person', 'statutory interpretation'
  • For Section B criminal scenarios, always work through actus reus then mens rea then defences — this systematic approach prevents you from missing marks
  • In 15-mark questions, write a quick plan with three to four key points before you start — this produces a more coherent answer
  • If a question asks about a specific topic, do not drift into related areas — stay focused on what the question asks

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Law Exam Tips — Paper 1: The Legal System Technique:
1. For Paper 1: The Legal System Technique questions, identify the relevant area of law and state the legal principles clearly
2. Apply legal rules to the facts of the scenario — don't just state the law
3. When evaluating, consider both the effectiveness and fairness of legal rules
4. Reference relevant cases and statutes when discussing Paper 1: The Legal System Technique
5. Consider reform proposals and alternative approaches where relevant

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Spending too long on early questions and running out of time for later ones ✓ Stick to 1 minute per mark — if a question is taking too long, move on and return to it if time allows; every question must be attempted

✗ Writing too much for low-mark questions ✓ A 2-mark question needs two concise points — writing a paragraph wastes time that is needed for higher-mark questions later

✗ Confusing criminal and civil law in Section B answers ✓ Section B is specifically about criminal law — do not discuss civil remedies or civil court procedures unless the question explicitly asks for comparison

✗ Failing to address the command word in Paper 1 questions ✓ Identify the command word before answering — 'describe' requires facts, 'explain' requires reasons, 'evaluate' requires balanced analysis and a judgement

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How should you structure your approach to Paper 1 of OCR J271 GCSE Law? (15 marks)

A successful approach to Paper 1 requires careful planning, time management and appropriate technique for each question type. Before the exam, revise both Section A (The Legal System) and Section B (Criminal Law) thoroughly, ensuring you know key cases, statutes and legal terminology for each topic. When you open the paper, spend 2-3 minutes reading through all questions and planning your time — with 90 minutes for 80 marks, allocate roughly 1 minute per mark. Start with the questions you feel most confident about, as this builds momentum. For short-answer questions (1-4 marks), give concise, direct responses with one point per mark — no elaboration is needed. For medium-answer questions (5-10 marks), use PEEL paragraphs: make a point, provide evidence (a case or statute), explain the connection and link back to the question. For 15-mark evaluation questions, use a structured essay: brief introduction, two to three paragraphs of balanced analysis presenting both sides with specific legal examples, and a justified conclusion. For criminal law problem questions in Section B, use the ILAC method: identify the legal issue, state the relevant law (actus reus, mens rea, defences), apply the law to the specific facts of the scenario, and reach a conclusion. Always check the command word before writing and ensure your answer matches what is asked. Finally, leave 2-3 minutes at the end to review your answers for errors or omissions. This systematic approach ensures you maximise marks across the whole paper rather than excelling on one question at the expense of others.
