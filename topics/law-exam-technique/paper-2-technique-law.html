Paper 2: Law in Action Technique

Specific techniques for Paper 2 of OCR J271 GCSE Law

Key Fact: Paper 2 of OCR J271 GCSE Law covers Law in Action — it is worth 80 marks and lasts 1 hour 30 minutes
Key Fact: Paper 2 Section A covers the Law of Tort: negligence, occupiers' liability, nuisance and remedies
Key Fact: Paper 2 Section B covers two options from: Law of Contract, Consumer Protection, Company Law, Employment Law, Intellectual Property and Data Protection
Key Fact: Alternatively, Section B may cover Rights and Responsibilities and Criminal Law in Context depending on the school's chosen options
Key Fact: Paper 2 contains the same mix of question types as Paper 1: short-answer (1-4 marks), medium-answer (5-10 marks) and extended-response (15 marks)
Key Fact: The same timing principle applies: 90 minutes for 80 marks, roughly 1 minute per mark
Key Fact: For tort scenario questions in Section A, use the ILAC method: identify the tort, state each element (duty of care, breach, damage for negligence), apply to the facts and conclude on liability
Key Fact: For negligence questions, always work through the three elements in order: duty of care (Caparo test), breach (reasonable person standard), and damage (causation and remoteness)
Key Fact: For Section B, ensure you have revised the specific optional topics your school has chosen — do not prepare topics that will not appear on your paper
Key Fact: Evaluation questions in Paper 2 often ask you to assess the effectiveness of a legal rule or the fairness of a legal principle — use the same balanced approach as Paper 1
Key Fact: Scenario questions in tort law require careful factual analysis — each element of the tort must be connected to specific facts from the scenario
Key Fact: Always use case authority to support your analysis — for negligence, key cases include Donoghue v Stevenson, Caparo v Dickman, Bolitho v City and Hackney HA and Page v Smith

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Paper 2: Law in Action Technique, you must know:

  • Paper 2: The second exam paper for OCR J271 GCSE Law covering Law in Action, including the Law of Tort and optional topics, worth 80 marks and lasting 1 hour 30 minutes
  • Law of Tort: A branch of civil law dealing with civil wrongs that cause harm, including negligence, occupiers' liability and nuisance — the focus of Paper 2 Section A
  • Duty of care: The first element of negligence: a legal obligation to take reasonable care to avoid acts or omissions likely to cause harm to a person who is reasonably foreseeable as being affected
  • Breach of duty: The second element of negligence: failing to meet the standard of care that a reasonable person would exercise in the circumstances, assessed objectively
  • Causation: The link between the defendant's breach and the claimant's damage — both factual causation (but for test) and legal causation (remoteness of damage) must be established
  • Caparo test: The three-stage test for establishing a duty of care from Caparo v Dickman: foreseeability of harm, proximity of relationship, and it being fair, just and reasonable to impose a duty

✅ Answers

  1. Paper 2 Section A covers the Law of Tort: negligence (duty of care, breach, damage, occupiers' liability), nuisance and remedies. Section B covers two optional topics chosen by the school — these may include the Law of Contract, Consumer Protection, Employment Law, Rights and Responsibilities, or Criminal Law in Context. Check with your teacher which options you are studying.
  2. Use ILAC: identify the legal issue as potential negligence. State the law: duty of care (apply the Caparo test — foreseeability, proximity, fair just and reasonable), breach (the standard of the reasonable person, relevant cases), and damage (factual causation using the 'but for' test, legal causation and remoteness). Apply each element to the specific facts of the scenario. Consider any defences (contributory negligence). Conclude on liability.
  3. Paper 1 focuses on the legal system and criminal law, requiring knowledge of structures, processes and criminal elements. Paper 2 focuses on civil law (tort and optional topics), requiring more emphasis on civil liability, the balance of probabilities standard and remedies. The ILAC method applies to both, but the legal content differs — criminal elements versus tort elements, criminal defences versus civil defences, criminal sanctions versus civil remedies.
  4. The law of tort is heavily case-based — the principles of duty of care, breach and damage have been developed through judicial decisions. Key cases like Donoghue v Stevenson, Caparo v Dickman and Bolitho define the legal tests. Citing these cases demonstrates understanding of how the law works and supports your application of legal rules to the scenario facts.
  5. Only revise the two optional topics your school has chosen — confirm this with your teacher well before the exam. For each option, learn the key legal principles, important cases and statutes, and practise applying them to scenarios. Use the same ILAC and evaluation techniques as for Section A. Do not attempt questions on options you have not studied.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • For negligence questions, always address the three elements in order: duty of care, breach and damage — missing any element loses marks
  • Name the key case for each legal test: Donoghue v Stevenson for duty of care, Caparo v Dickman for the three-stage test, Bolam for professional standard
  • In tort scenarios, consider whether the claimant's own negligence contributed to the harm — contributory negligence under the Law Reform Act 1945 can reduce damages
  • For Section B optional topics, check past papers to see how questions are typically phrased and structure your answers accordingly
  • Always discuss remedies when concluding on liability — damages (compensation), injunctions and specific performance where relevant

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Law Exam Tips:
Use precise classical terminology in all answers. When analysing sources, always consider provenance (who created it, when, and why). For literary questions, reference specific episodes and book numbers from set texts. For thematic questions, compare Greek and Roman approaches where relevant. For 'how far' questions, present a balanced argument with a clear judgement. OCR J271 rewards answers that demonstrate understanding of the classical world through use of specific evidence from both literary and visual/material sources.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Discussing criminal law principles in a tort question ✓ Tort is civil law — use civil terminology (claimant/defendant, liability, damages, balance of probabilities) not criminal terminology (prosecution, guilty, beyond reasonable doubt)

✗ Forgetting to discuss all three elements of negligence ✓ Every negligence answer must address duty of care, breach and damage — missing any element means the claim cannot succeed and you lose marks

✗ Applying the criminal standard of proof to tort questions ✓ The standard of proof in civil cases is the balance of probabilities (more likely than not), not beyond reasonable doubt — this is a fundamental difference from criminal law

✗ Attempting Section B questions on topics not studied ✓ Only answer questions on the two optional topics your school has chosen — answering on a topic you have not studied will almost certainly result in a poor mark

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How should you approach a negligence scenario in Paper 2 of OCR J271 GCSE Law? (15 marks)

When approaching a negligence scenario in Paper 2, use the ILAC method systematically. First, identify the Issue: the question is whether the defendant is liable in negligence for the claimant's injuries or loss. Second, state the Law: negligence requires three elements. For duty of care, apply the Caparo v Dickman three-stage test — was the claimant a foreseeable victim (Donoghue v Stevenson), was there a proximate relationship, and is it fair, just and reasonable to impose a duty? For breach, state that the defendant must meet the standard of the reasonable person — what would a reasonable person have done in those circumstances? If the defendant is a professional, the Bolam test applies: the standard is that of a reasonably competent practitioner in that field, though Bolitho adds that the court must be satisfied the professional opinion is logical. For damage, establish factual causation using the 'but for' test — would the damage have occurred but for the defendant's breach? Then check legal causation: is the damage too remote? Under the Wagon Mound test, only damage that is a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the breach is recoverable. Third, apply the law to the facts: use every relevant detail from the scenario. For example, if the scenario says the defendant was a doctor who failed to read a patient's notes, apply the Bolam test to the specific facts — a reasonable doctor would have read the notes, so there is a breach. If the claimant was warned of risks but proceeded anyway, consider contributory negligence under the Law Reform Act 1945. Fourth, reach a Conclusion: state whether the defendant is likely liable and what remedies may be available, primarily damages for the injury suffered. Acknowledge any areas of uncertainty in your reasoning.
