Law Reform & Contemporary Issues

How and why the law changes: the Law Commission, technology, and modern challenges

Key Fact: Law reform is necessary because society changes and the law must adapt to address new situations, correct injustices and keep pace with technology and social values
Key Fact: The Law Commission is an independent body established by the Law Commissions Act 1965 with a duty to keep the law under review and recommend reform
Key Fact: The Law Commission produces reports with draft Bills; however, implementation depends on Parliament — only around 70% of Law Commission recommendations are eventually implemented, often with significant delay
Key Fact: Parliamentary reform through Acts of Parliament is the primary method of law reform; Parliament can change any area of law and is not bound by previous legislation
Key Fact: Judges reform the law through the development of precedent — the Supreme Court and Court of Appeal can depart from previous decisions to update the law (e.g. the Supreme Court in Montgomery changed the law on informed consent)
Key Fact: The 1966 Practice Statement allowed the House of Lords (now Supreme Court) to depart from its own previous decisions when it appears right to do so
Key Fact: Technology creates new legal challenges: cybercrime (Computer Misuse Act 1990), data protection (UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018), online harassment and the regulation of social media
Key Fact: The Online Safety Act 2023 places duties on platforms to protect users from harmful content, including illegal content and content harmful to children
Key Fact: Artificial intelligence raises legal questions about liability, intellectual property, privacy and the potential need for new regulatory frameworks
Key Fact: Human rights reform remains debated: proposals to replace the HRA 1998 with a Bill of Rights raise questions about the future of Convention rights in UK law
Key Fact: Contemporary issues include: the impact of Brexit on legal certainty and retained EU law, reform of the criminal justice system (court backlogs, legal aid), and debates about policing practices
Key Fact: Pressure groups, media campaigns and public opinion all influence law reform — Parliament responds to public concern, as seen with changes to stalking law and domestic violence legislation

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Law Reform & Contemporary Issues, you must know:

  • Law Commission: An independent statutory body established in 1965 that keeps the law under review and recommends reforms to Parliament through reports and draft Bills
  • Law reform: The process of changing the law to make it more just, efficient, accessible or responsive to social, economic and technological change
  • Practice Statement 1966: A statement by the House of Lords (now Supreme Court) that it may depart from its own previous decisions when it appears right to do so, providing flexibility for judicial law reform
  • Cybercrime: Criminal activity that uses computers or the internet, including hacking, online fraud, identity theft and distribution of malicious software, governed by the Computer Misuse Act 1990
  • UK GDPR: The UK General Data Protection Regulation, retained from EU law, governing the processing of personal data to protect individuals' privacy rights, alongside the Data Protection Act 2018
  • Online Safety Act 2023: Legislation placing duties on online platforms and service providers to protect users from illegal content and content harmful to children, with enforcement by Ofcom

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the Law Commission and how does it contribute to law reform?

Q: How can judges contribute to law reform?

Q: Why is technology creating challenges for the legal system?

Q: What is the Online Safety Act 2023 and what does it do?

Q: How do pressure groups influence law reform?

✅ Answers

  1. The Law Commission is an independent body established by the Law Commissions Act 1965. It reviews areas of law, identifies defects and recommends reforms through published reports containing draft Bills. While its recommendations are influential, implementation depends on Parliament — only about 70% of recommendations are eventually implemented, often with delay.
  2. Judges reform the law through the development of judicial precedent. The Supreme Court can depart from its own previous decisions under the 1966 Practice Statement when it appears right to do so. The Court of Appeal can also depart from its own previous decisions in limited circumstances. However, judicial reform is incremental and constrained by the doctrine of precedent and the principle that judges declare rather than make law.
  3. Technology creates challenges because the law often develops more slowly than technology. Cybercrime evolves faster than legislation can address, online platforms facilitate new forms of harassment and fraud, data protection must balance privacy with the free flow of information, and artificial intelligence raises questions about liability, intellectual property and regulation that existing law was not designed to answer.
  4. The Online Safety Act 2023 places legal duties on online platforms and service providers to protect users from illegal content and content harmful to children. It requires platforms to assess risks, implement systems to remove or limit harmful content, and is enforced by Ofcom with powers to issue fines and business disruption measures for non-compliance.
  5. Pressure groups influence law reform by raising public awareness of issues, lobbying Parliament and government, providing expert evidence to consultations, and organising campaigns that generate media attention and public support. Successful examples include campaigns that led to the Domestic Abuse Act 2021 and changes to stalking legislation. However, their influence depends on resources, public sympathy and political willingness to act.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When discussing law reform, consider all three agents: Parliament, the judiciary and the Law Commission — evaluate the strengths and limitations of each
  • Use specific examples of law reform to illustrate your points — e.g. the Domestic Abuse Act 2021, Online Safety Act 2023, or Montgomery on consent
  • When evaluating the Law Commission, always note the implementation gap — recommendations are not automatically enacted
  • For technology questions, show awareness of specific legislation: Computer Misuse Act 1990, Data Protection Act 2018, Online Safety Act 2023
  • Remember that law reform involves balancing competing interests: security vs privacy, innovation vs regulation, tradition vs modernisation

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Saying the Law Commission can change the law directly ✓ The Law Commission can only recommend changes; it has no power to enact legislation — Parliament must pass the reforms into law

✗ Thinking judges cannot change the law at all ✓ Judges develop and reform the law through precedent; the Supreme Court can depart from its own decisions under the 1966 Practice Statement, and the Court of Appeal has limited flexibility to depart from its own previous decisions

✗ Confusing the Computer Misuse Act with data protection law ✓ The Computer Misuse Act 1990 criminalises hacking and unauthorised access to computers; data protection (DPA 2018 and UK GDPR) governs the processing and protection of personal data — they are separate areas of law

✗ Saying the Online Safety Act bans all harmful content ✓ The Act requires platforms to address illegal content and content harmful to children; for adult users, it focuses on giving users more control rather than banning content outright, and legal speech is protected

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How effective is the Law Commission in achieving law reform? (15 marks)

The Law Commission plays a vital role in identifying the need for law reform and producing well-researched recommendations, but its effectiveness is limited by its dependence on Parliament for implementation. The Commission's strengths are significant: it provides expert, independent analysis of defective or outdated law, consulting widely with legal professionals, interest groups and the public. Its reports are thorough and often accompanied by draft Bills that are ready for parliamentary consideration, saving legislative time. Many important reforms originated from Law Commission work, including the Fraud Act 2006, the Corporate Manslaughter and Corporate Homicide Act 2007 and reforms to the law of bribery. However, the Commission's effectiveness is severely limited by the implementation gap. Only around 70% of recommendations are eventually implemented, and many take years to reach the statute book. The Commission has no power to compel Parliament to act, and government priorities may not align with the Commission's recommendations. Some reports have been left unimplemented for over a decade. Furthermore, the Commission can only review areas referred to it by the government or on its own initiative within its limited resources. It cannot respond quickly to urgent social problems. Despite these limitations, the Law Commission remains the most systematic and expert mechanism for law reform in England and Wales. Its influence extends beyond direct implementation: its analysis shapes legal debate and its recommendations are often adopted incrementally through judicial decisions or partial legislation. Overall, while the Law Commission is highly effective at identifying and designing reforms, its impact is constrained by parliamentary inertia and political priorities.
