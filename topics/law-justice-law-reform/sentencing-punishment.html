Sentencing & Punishment

Aims of sentencing, types of sentence and the principles of punishment

Key Fact: The five main aims of sentencing are: retribution (punishing the offender for what they have done), deterrence (discouraging the offender or others from reoffending), rehabilitation (reforming the offender to prevent future crime), incapacitation (protecting the public by removing dangerous offenders) and reparation (making the offender compensate the victim or society)
Key Fact: Deterrence is divided into individual deterrence (discouraging the specific offender) and general deterrence (discouraging others from committing similar offences)
Key Fact: The Sentencing Council issues guidelines that courts must follow unless it would be contrary to the interests of justice to do so, ensuring consistency in sentencing
Key Fact: Custodial sentences include: detention for offenders under 18, imprisonment for adult offenders, and life sentences for the most serious offences including murder (which carries a mandatory life sentence)
Key Fact: Community orders under the Sentencing Act 2020 can include requirements such as unpaid work, curfew, drug rehabilitation, attendance at specified activities and prohibitions on travel
Key Fact: Fines are the most common sentence in the Magistrates' Court; they must be based on the offender's ability to pay and the seriousness of the offence
Key Fact: Discharges are used for minor offences: absolute discharge (no penalty at all) or conditional discharge (no penalty unless the offender commits a further offence within a set period)
Key Fact: Aggravating factors increase the seriousness of an offence and may lead to a harsher sentence — e.g. use of a weapon, vulnerability of the victim, previous convictions, racially or religiously motivated offending
Key Fact: Mitigating factors reduce the seriousness and may lead to a more lenient sentence — e.g. pleading guilty, lack of previous convictions, the offender's age, mental health issues, acting under duress
Key Fact: A guilty plea typically attracts a reduction in sentence of up to one-third if made at the earliest opportunity, under the Sentencing Council's reduction for guilty plea guideline
Key Fact: Sentencing for young offenders (under 18) prioritises rehabilitation and uses different sentences including referral orders, youth rehabilitation orders and detention at a young offender institution
Key Fact: The principle of proportionality requires that the sentence must be proportionate to the seriousness of the offence and the culpability of the offender

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Sentencing & Punishment, you must know:

  • Retribution: The aim of sentencing that focuses on punishing the offender because they deserve it for the wrong they have done, based on the principle of just deserts
  • Deterrence: The aim of sentencing that seeks to discourage the offender from reoffending (individual deterrence) or to discourage others from committing similar offences (general deterrence)
  • Rehabilitation: The aim of sentencing that focuses on reforming the offender through education, treatment or training so they do not reoffend; particularly important for young offenders
  • Community order: A non-custodial sentence under the Sentencing Act 2020 that imposes one or more requirements on the offender in the community, such as unpaid work, curfew or drug treatment
  • Aggravating factor: A circumstance that makes an offence more serious and may increase the sentence, such as use of a weapon, targeting a vulnerable victim or previous convictions
  • Mitigating factor: A circumstance that reduces the seriousness of an offence or the offender's culpability and may lead to a more lenient sentence, such as a guilty plea, lack of previous convictions or mental health issues

❓ Practice Questions

Q: Explain the five main aims of sentencing.

Q: What is the role of the Sentencing Council?

Q: What is the difference between aggravating and mitigating factors?

Q: What are community orders and what requirements can they include?

Q: Why does the law treat young offenders differently from adults in sentencing?

✅ Answers

  1. Retribution — punishing the offender because they deserve it. Deterrence — discouraging the offender (individual) or others (general) from reoffending. Rehabilitation — reforming the offender through treatment or training. Incapacitation — protecting the public by removing dangerous offenders from society. Reparation — making the offender compensate the victim or make amends to society.
  2. The Sentencing Council produces guidelines that set out the appropriate sentence for different offences, taking into account culpability and harm. Courts must follow these guidelines unless it would be contrary to the interests of justice to do so. The Council also promotes awareness of sentencing and monitors the effectiveness of guidelines.
  3. Aggravating factors make the offence more serious and tend to increase the sentence (e.g. use of a weapon, vulnerable victim, previous convictions). Mitigating factors reduce the offender's culpability or the seriousness and tend to decrease the sentence (e.g. guilty plea, first offence, mental health, age, provocation).
  4. Community orders are non-custodial sentences that impose requirements on the offender to serve in the community. Requirements can include unpaid work (up to 300 hours), curfew (with electronic monitoring), drug or alcohol treatment, attendance at specified activities, prohibited activities, residence requirement, exclusion from areas, and a rehabilitation activity requirement.
  5. Young offenders are treated differently because the primary aim for under-18s is rehabilitation rather than punishment, reflecting their reduced maturity and greater capacity for change. Sentences include referral orders, youth rehabilitation orders and, only as a last resort, detention. The principle is that criminalising young people can entrench offending behaviour, so the focus is on education and reform.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • When discussing aims of sentencing, always define the aim and give a specific example of how it works in practice
  • Use the terms 'aggravating' and 'mitigating' factors correctly — not 'factors that make it worse/better'
  • When evaluating a sentence, consider which aims of sentencing it serves and whether it achieves them effectively
  • Remember that murder carries a mandatory life sentence — the judge has no discretion on this, only on the minimum term (tariff)
  • For young offenders, always emphasise rehabilitation as the primary aim and detention as a last resort

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Confusing retribution with revenge ✓ Retribution is a principled and proportionate punishment based on the offender's desert; revenge is personal, unregulated and may be disproportionate

✗ Saying community orders are only for minor offences ✓ Community orders can be imposed for a wide range of offences where custody is not justified; they can include demanding requirements and are not limited to trivial cases

✗ Confusing individual and general deterrence ✓ Individual deterrence aims to stop the specific offender from reoffending; general deterrence aims to discourage other potential offenders by making an example of the sentenced person

✗ Thinking a life sentence means the offender spends their whole life in prison ✓ A life sentence means the offender is subject to life licence; the judge sets a minimum term (tariff) after which they may be released on licence, but can be recalled to prison at any time if they breach conditions

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How far do custodial sentences achieve the aims of sentencing? (15 marks)

Custodial sentences achieve some aims of sentencing more effectively than others. They are most effective at incapacitation, removing dangerous offenders from society and preventing them from causing further harm during their imprisonment. They also serve retribution by imposing a severe punishment that reflects the seriousness of the offence and satisfies society's expectation that serious wrongdoing is punished. General deterrence may be partially achieved, as the knowledge that serious offences lead to imprisonment may discourage some potential offenders. However, custodial sentences are much less effective at rehabilitation. The prison environment can entrench criminal behaviour through association with other offenders, and limited access to education, treatment and training means many prisoners are not reformed. Recidivism rates are high — around 48% of adults reoffend within one year of release, suggesting imprisonment fails to rehabilitate. Individual deterrence is also questionable, as many offenders reoffend after release. Furthermore, short custodial sentences (under 12 months) are particularly ineffective, with higher reoffending rates than community orders. Prison can also cause collateral damage: loss of employment, housing and family relationships, and negative effects on mental health, all of which increase the risk of reoffending. Overall, custodial sentences effectively achieve incapacitation and retribution but are largely ineffective at rehabilitation and individual deterrence, particularly for shorter sentences.
