G19: Managing Cold Environments

Foundation Higher All Boards

The value of cold environments as wilderness areas, balancing economic development with conservation, international agreements and the role of conservation groups.

🌍 Why are Cold Environments Valuable?

Key Concept: Cold environments have value as wilderness - areas largely undisturbed by human activity. Their scientific, ecological, cultural and aesthetic importance must be balanced against the economic benefits of development.

Ecological Value

  • Home to unique species found nowhere else on Earth (polar bears, Arctic foxes, narwhals)
  • Simple food webs that are highly sensitive to disturbance
  • Important breeding grounds for migratory birds (millions nest in the tundra each summer)
  • Rich marine ecosystems supported by cold, nutrient-rich waters
  • Biodiversity hotspots including endemic species isolated by glaciation

Scientific Value

  • Ice cores provide records of past climate spanning 800,000+ years
  • Permafrost preserves ancient DNA and biological specimens
  • Monitoring stations track climate change at the front line of global warming
  • The Arctic acts as an early warning system for global environmental change
  • Unique organisms have potential medical and biotechnological applications

Cultural and Spiritual Value

  • Indigenous peoples (Inuit, Sámi, Nenets) have lived in cold environments for thousands of years
  • Traditional knowledge and cultural practices are intimately tied to the land
  • Wilderness has intrinsic value beyond economic measurement
  • Many cultures view polar regions as having spiritual significance

Economic Value

  • Vast reserves of oil, natural gas and minerals (e.g., Arctic holds ~13% of world's undiscovered oil)
  • Fishing grounds worth billions of dollars annually
  • Growing tourism industry
  • Potential new shipping routes as ice retreats
Value Type Why It Matters Threat From Development
Ecological Unique species and fragile food webs Habitat destruction, pollution, disturbance
Scientific Climate records and environmental monitoring Contamination, altered natural systems
Cultural Indigenous traditions and knowledge Displacement, cultural erosion
Economic Resources, fishing, tourism, shipping Over-exploitation, unsustainable use

⚖️ Balancing Development and Conservation

The Dilemma: Cold environments contain valuable resources that countries want to exploit for economic growth, but their fragility means development can cause irreversible damage. The challenge is finding a balance that allows sustainable use while protecting wilderness value.

Arguments For Development

  • Oil and gas resources reduce dependence on imports and support energy security
  • Mineral extraction creates jobs and economic growth for remote communities
  • Fishing provides food and livelihoods for millions worldwide
  • New shipping routes could reduce global transport costs and emissions
  • Indigenous communities have the right to economic development
  • Tourism brings revenue that can fund conservation

Arguments For Conservation

  • Ecosystems are extremely fragile and recover very slowly from damage
  • Oil spills in Arctic waters would be impossible to clean up effectively
  • Climate change is already stressing cold environments - additional pressure is unsustainable
  • Wilderness areas have irreplaceable scientific and ecological value
  • Indigenous peoples' traditional ways of life are threatened
  • Once wilderness is lost, it cannot be restored
Case Study: Arctic National Wildlife Refuge (ANWR)

The ANWR in northeast Alaska covers 78,000 km² and is America's largest wildlife refuge. Its coastal plain is estimated to contain 4.3-11.8 billion barrels of oil, but it is also the calving ground for the Porcupine caribou herd (200,000 animals) and sacred land to the Gwich'in people. The debate over whether to allow drilling has continued for over 40 years. In 2017, the US Congress opened part of the coastal plain to leasing, but no leases have been sold as of 2024 due to legal challenges and low industry interest. This exemplifies the tension between energy security and wilderness preservation.

📜 International Agreements

The Antarctic Treaty System

Key Fact: The Antarctic Treaty (1959) is one of the most successful international agreements ever signed. It demilitarised Antarctica, prohibited nuclear waste disposal, and suspended all territorial claims. The 1998 Madrid Protocol banned all mineral extraction for at least 50 years.
  • Antarctic Treaty (1959): 56 countries have signed; sets aside Antarctica for peace and science
  • Madrid Protocol (1998): Designated Antarctica as a "natural reserve devoted to peace and science"; banned mining for at least 50 years
  • CCAMLR (1982): Convention on the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources; manages krill and fish stocks
  • Environmental Protocol: Requires environmental impact assessments for all activities

Arctic Governance

Unlike Antarctica, the Arctic has no single comprehensive treaty. Instead, multiple frameworks apply:

  • Arctic Council (1996): Intergovernmental forum of 8 Arctic nations plus indigenous organisations; promotes cooperation on environmental protection and sustainable development
  • UNCLOS (1982): United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea governs territorial claims and resource rights in Arctic waters
  • Paris Agreement (2015): Climate targets affect Arctic warming rates directly
  • Svalbard Treaty (1920): Grants Norway sovereignty over Svalbard but allows equal access to all signatory nations for research and economic activities
Agreement Year Coverage Key Provisions
Antarctic Treaty 1959 Antarctica Demilitarisation; scientific freedom; no territorial claims
Madrid Protocol 1998 Antarctica Mining ban; environmental protection; waste management
Arctic Council 1996 Arctic region Cooperation on environment and sustainable development
UNCLOS 1982 Global oceans Territorial waters; continental shelf rights; resource access
Svalbard Treaty 1920 Svalbard Norwegian sovereignty; equal access for signatories

🦋 Conservation Groups

Greenpeace

  • Campaigned against Arctic oil drilling with the "Save the Arctic" movement
  • Over 8 million people have signed their Arctic Declaration
  • Protested at drilling sites, raising global awareness
  • Campaigned for a global sanctuary around the North Pole

WWF (World Wildlife Fund)

  • Runs the "Arctic Programme" focusing on climate change, shipping and oil/gas
  • Supports indigenous peoples' rights and community-based conservation
  • Monitors polar bear populations and pushes for habitat protection
  • Advocates for marine protected areas in the Arctic

Other Organisations

  • IUCN: International Union for Conservation of Nature - designates protected areas and monitors species status
  • SCAR: Scientific Committee on Antarctic Research - coordinates international research and advises the Antarctic Treaty system
  • BAS: British Antarctic Survey - conducts research and monitors environmental change
  • Indigenous organisations: The Inuit Circumpolar Council represents 160,000 Inuit across 4 countries and advocates for indigenous rights in Arctic governance
Evaluating Conservation Approaches:
International treaties = strong legal framework but slow to implement and difficult to enforce
Conservation groups = raise awareness and apply pressure but have no legal power
Protected areas = effective where enforced but cover only a small proportion of cold environments
Sustainable development = allows economic activity while limiting environmental damage but requires monitoring and compliance

🏗️ Sustainable Management Strategies

  • Environmental Impact Assessments (EIAs): Required before any development; identify potential damage and mitigation measures
  • Protected areas: National parks and wildlife refuges restrict damaging activities (e.g., Northeast Greenland National Park - 972,000 km²)
  • Technology controls: Double-hulled ships; elevated pipelines; closed-loop drilling systems
  • Seasonal restrictions: Limiting activities during breeding seasons or sensitive periods
  • Quotas and limits: CCAMLR sets catch limits for krill and fish stocks
  • Indigenous co-management: Involving local and indigenous peoples in decision-making
  • Tourism codes of conduct: IAATO guidelines limit visitor numbers and require minimum distances from wildlife
Example: IAATO and Antarctic Tourism

The International Association of Antarctica Tour Operators (IAATO) was founded in 1991. It limits cruise ship sizes to 500 passengers, requires a maximum of 100 visitors ashore at any time, enforces 5-metre minimum distances from wildlife, and bans items that could introduce non-native species. Despite growing visitor numbers (over 100,000 in 2022-23), no significant environmental damage from tourism has been documented, demonstrating effective self-regulation.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain why cold environments are considered valuable as wilderness areas. (4 marks)

Q2: Describe one international agreement that helps protect cold environments. (4 marks)

Q3: "Economic development in cold environments should always be prevented." To what extent do you agree? (6 marks)

Q4: Assess the role of conservation groups in protecting cold environments. (6 marks)

Q5: Evaluate the effectiveness of international agreements in balancing development and conservation in cold environments. (9 marks)

✅ Answers

  1. Cold environments are valuable as wilderness because they contain unique species found nowhere else, such as polar bears and Arctic foxes, maintaining global biodiversity (1 mark). They have irreplaceable scientific value - ice cores provide 800,000+ years of climate data essential for understanding climate change (1 mark). Indigenous peoples' traditional knowledge and cultural practices are tied to these landscapes (1 mark). Their aesthetic and spiritual value as the last large undisturbed wilderness areas on Earth is immeasurable (1 mark).
  2. The Antarctic Treaty (1959) involves 56 signatory nations and designates Antarctica for peace and science only (1 mark). It prohibits military activity, nuclear testing and waste disposal (1 mark). The 1998 Madrid Protocol added a ban on all mineral extraction for at least 50 years (1 mark). Environmental impact assessments are required for all activities, and the treaty is enforced through mutual inspection rights between signatories (1 mark).
  3. I disagree that development should always be prevented. Cold environments contain resources that countries need for energy security and economic growth, and indigenous peoples have the right to development (1 mark). However, some areas should be fully protected because of their ecological fragility and scientific importance (1 mark). Sustainable development with strict environmental controls can allow limited economic activity while protecting key areas (1 mark). International agreements like the Madrid Protocol show that protection and limited development can coexist (1 mark). The key is careful regulation rather than total prohibition or unrestricted development (1 mark). Each case should be assessed on its own merits with full environmental impact assessments (1 mark).
  4. Conservation groups like Greenpeace and WWF play important roles in protecting cold environments by raising public awareness through campaigns such as "Save the Arctic" (1 mark). They apply political pressure on governments and corporations to adopt sustainable practices and respect environmental regulations (1 mark). WWF's Arctic Programme supports scientific research and community-based conservation (1 mark). However, conservation groups have no legal enforcement power and rely on persuasion and public opinion (1 mark). Their effectiveness depends on public support and political will - when governments prioritise economic interests, conservation groups' influence is limited (1 mark). They are most effective when working alongside legal frameworks like international treaties (1 mark).
  5. International agreements have been partially effective in balancing development and conservation. The Antarctic Treaty is widely regarded as highly successful - it has prevented militarisation, mining and environmental damage in Antarctica for over 60 years (1 mark). The Madrid Protocol's mining ban provides strong legal protection, enforced through mutual inspections (1 mark). However, the Arctic lacks an equivalent comprehensive treaty, relying instead on the Arctic Council which can only make recommendations, not binding laws (1 mark). UNCLOS provides a legal framework for territorial claims but has been criticised for enabling resource exploitation (1 mark). Enforcement remains a major challenge - remote areas are difficult to monitor, and some nations may prioritise economic interests over environmental commitments (1 mark). The Svalbard Treaty demonstrates how sovereignty and international access can be balanced but also shows tensions between conservation and commercial activities (1 mark). Climate change is undermining many agreements as melting ice opens new areas to development that existing treaties didn't anticipate (1 mark). On balance, international agreements provide essential frameworks but need strengthening, updating and better enforcement to truly balance development and conservation (1 mark). Without them, cold environments would face far greater threats from uncontrolled exploitation (1 mark).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always name specific agreements and groups rather than writing generally about "laws" or "charities"
  • Explain HOW each agreement works, not just what it is called
  • For evaluation questions, consider both effectiveness AND limitations
  • Use the Antarctic Treaty as your strongest example of effective international cooperation
  • Contrast Arctic and Antarctic governance to show why Antarctica is better protected
  • Link conservation to climate change - warming is the biggest long-term threat

📝 Exam Technique

Geography Exam Tips:
Use named examples for each biome (Amazon for rainforests, Thar Desert for hot deserts, Svalbard for cold environments). Explain interdependence between biotic and abiotic components. For sustainability, evaluate strategies rather than just listing them.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often write vague answers without specific geographical evidence. Wrong: Writing generalised statements like 'it causes problems' Correct: Using specific data and named examples, e.g. 'the 2010 Haiti earthquake killed over 200,000 people due to poor building quality'

Students often confuse causes and effects. Wrong: Mixing up what caused the event with what resulted from it Correct: Clearly separate causes (why it happened) from effects (what happened as a result)

Students often describe rather than evaluate. Wrong: Listing strategies without assessing their effectiveness Correct: Weighing up strengths and weaknesses of each approach and reaching a supported judgement

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

6 marks: Explain the key factors affecting managing cold environments.

Managing Cold Environments involves multiple interconnected factors that geographers must understand. The key concepts include the processes that create and change managing cold environments, the impacts on both people and environment, and the strategies used to manage associated challenges. For a comprehensive answer, specific case study evidence should be used throughout, with named examples and data to support each point. Geographical terminology should be used precisely, and the interrelationship between physical and human factors should be demonstrated. Top-level responses evaluate the relative importance of different factors and consider how the situation varies between locations.

Mark scheme: 2 marks for identifying key factors, 2 marks for explaining processes with detail, 2 marks for using specific evidence

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 requires knowledge of the key facts and processes related to managing cold environments. AO2 demands understanding of how and why these processes operate, and their implications. AO3 asks you to analyse, evaluate and make judgements — this is where grade 9 answers stand out by weighing up competing perspectives and reaching supported conclusions. AO4 may involve interpreting maps, graphs or data related to this topic. To move from grade 5 to grade 9: use precise geographical terminology, support every point with specific case study evidence, and always evaluate rather than just describe.
