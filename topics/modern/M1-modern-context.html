M1: Modern Context

Foundation Higher All Boards AO3

Understanding 20th and 21st century contexts - the world of modern drama and prose.

🌍 World Wars and Their Impact

The Two World Wars

Modern texts are shaped by the trauma of global conflict:

War Years Key Impact on Literature
World War I 1914-1918 Loss of innocence, questioning authority, break with tradition
World War II 1939-1945 Horror of genocide, atomic bomb, total war

Post-War Literature

  • Skepticism: Questioning old certainties and authorities
  • Absurdism: Life seems meaningless after mass death
  • Social critique: Questioning the systems that led to war
  • Psychological focus: Interest in trauma and the mind
In "An Inspector Calls"

Priestley wrote in 1945 but set the play in 1912. This dramatic irony lets the audience see how pre-war attitudes led to disaster. The Inspector represents post-war values: social responsibility, equality, learning from mistakes.

⚙️ Social Change

Class System Changes

Period Class Structure Literary Reflection
Pre-1914 Rigid hierarchy, working class exploited "An Inspector Calls" - Birling's attitudes
Inter-war Old wealth declining, new money rising Gatsby era, class anxiety
Post-1945 Welfare state, greater mobility Class still matters but more fluid

The Welfare State

Post-1945 Britain created the welfare state:

  • NHS (1948): Healthcare for all
  • Education reforms: Free secondary education
  • Social security: Safety net for the poor
  • Council housing: Better living conditions
Priestley's Vision: "An Inspector Calls" argues for exactly these changes. Inspector's final speech - "we are members of one body" - advocates the social solidarity that led to the welfare state.

👩 Women and Gender

Women's Changing Roles

Period Women's Status Key Events
Pre-1914 Domestic sphere, suffragettes campaigning No vote, economic dependence
World Wars Women in workplaces, new independence Replacing men in factories
1918-1928 Vote gained Over 30s (1918), then all women (1928)
Post-1945 Greater equality (but still unequal) Work opportunities, education access

Gender in Modern Texts

  • Eva Smith: Exploited by men, powerless, but morally strong
  • Sheila Birling: Transforms from submissive daughter to independent thinker
  • Mrs Birling: Uses what power she has to condemn Eva
Women in "An Inspector Calls"

Eva Smith represents all exploited women - used by men, dismissed by powerful women, abandoned by society. Sheila's development shows the new generation of women: questioning, independent, taking responsibility.

🏛️ Political Movements

Capitalism vs Socialism

Capitalism (Birling) Socialism (Inspector)
Individual success Collective responsibility
Profit motive Community welfare
Every man for himself We are "members of one body"
Class hierarchy natural Equality of worth
Worker/employer conflict Cooperation

Priestley's Politics

  • Socialist: Believed in equality and social responsibility
  • Post-war Labour supporter: Welcomed welfare state
  • Didactic writer: Used drama to teach political messages
  • Broadcasts: Famous for wartime radio talks on social justice
The Inspector as Socialist Voice: Inspector's final speech is pure socialist message. He argues that privilege brings responsibility, that we must care for each other, that ignoring the vulnerable has consequences.

🌍 Multiculturalism

Post-Empire Britain

After World War II:

  • Empire Windrush (1948): Beginning of mass immigration
  • End of Empire: Colonies gaining independence
  • Multicultural society: Britain becoming more diverse
  • Racism and conflict: Tensions over immigration

Identity and Belonging

Modern texts often explore:

  • Multiple identities: Heritage vs nationality
  • Belonging: Where is home?
  • Prejudice: Experiencing racism
  • Assimilation vs pride: Fitting in vs celebrating difference
In "Anita and Me"

Meena navigates between her Indian heritage and British surroundings. She wants to fit in but also understand her roots. The novel explores multicultural Britain with both humor and seriousness.

📖 Theatrical Context

Modern Drama Conventions

Modern drama developed new approaches:

Movement Key Features Example
Realism Everyday life, authentic dialogue "An Inspector Calls" (ostensibly)
Expressionism Inner reality, distorted reality "An Inspector Calls" ( Inspector's arrival)
Epic Theatre Breaking fourth wall, audience thinks Brecht's influence
Absurdism Meaninglessness, unconventional structure "Waiting for Godot"
"An Inspector Calls" - Genre Blend: Starts as realistic drawing-room drama, becomes expressionist. The Inspector seems supernatural. Time is distorted. These theatrical choices make the audience think beyond realism.

✍️ Using Context in Essays

Good Context Integration

"Priestley wrote 'An Inspector Calls' in 1945 but set it in 1912. This historical gap creates dramatic irony - the 1912 audience (and the Birlings) don't know about the world wars that will follow. Birling's confidence that 'there isn't a chance of war' would be painfully ironic to a 1945 audience who had lived through two devastating conflicts. Priestley uses this to criticise the capitalist, individualist attitudes that led to such catastrophe."

Useful Phrases

  • "Written in the context of post-war Britain..."
  • "For a 1945 audience, this would have..."
  • "Priestley's socialist beliefs are evident when..."
  • "The historical setting of 1912 allows Priestley to..."

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: How does World War II context affect "An Inspector Calls"?

Q2: Why did Priestley set his play in 1912?

Q3: What does the Inspector represent in terms of post-war politics?

Q4: How does women's changing role affect the play's meaning?

✅ Answers

  1. WWII context means the audience has experienced catastrophe. They know what Birling doesn't - that war is coming. The play argues that pre-war attitudes (selfishness, inequality) led to disaster and must change.
  2. Setting in 1912 creates dramatic irony. The Birlings don't know about the wars, the Titanic sinking, the Russian Revolution. Their confidence in progress and stability is shown to be wrong. Priestley critiques the past from a knowing present.
  3. The Inspector represents post-war socialist values: collective responsibility, care for the vulnerable, equality. His final speech advocates the social solidarity that led to the welfare state. He is the voice of the new Britain Priestley wants.
  4. Eva Smith's exploitation shows how women were treated. Sheila's development shows the new independent woman emerging after suffrage and war work. The contrast critiques old attitudes and shows hope for change.

📝 Exam Technique

Context Must Be Integrated, Not Bolted On: Examiners award AO3 marks for context that is woven into your argument, not dumped in a separate paragraph. Always link historical, social, or political context directly to the writer's choices and the text's meaning. A disconnected "life in 1912" paragraph scores poorly; a sentence that explains why Priestley chose 1912 to create dramatic irony scores highly.

Time Management for Context Questions

SectionTimeFocus
Planning5 minsIdentify 3-4 contextual points linked to the question
Writing30 minsInterleave context with analysis of quotations
Checking5 minsEnsure every context point connects to the text

What Examiners Look For

  • Relevance: Context that directly illuminates the text, not general history
  • Precision: Specific historical details, not vague statements like "things were different then"
  • Integration: Context embedded within analytical paragraphs, not isolated
  • Subtlety: Understanding that context influences the writer's choices and the audience's response

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often write a separate "context paragraph" at the start or end of their essay. Wrong: "In 1912 Britain was very class-divided and women couldn't vote. Now I will analyse the play." Correct: Integrate context within each point: "Priestley exposes the cruelty of pre-war class divisions through Birling's dismissal of Eva's strike for 'twenty-five shillings a week' — a sum that reveals the vast gulf between employer comfort and worker desperation."

Students often treat context as background information without explaining why the writer chose it. Wrong: "The play is set in 1912 before the wars." Correct: "Setting the play in 1912 allows Priestley to exploit dramatic irony — Birling's boast that 'there isn't a chance of war' would horrify a 1945 audience who had lived through two world wars, discrediting capitalist complacency."

Students often confuse historical context with the writer's purpose. Wrong: "Priestley wrote about the 1912 class system because that's how things were." Correct: "Priestley deliberately recreates 1912 attitudes to show how they led to catastrophe — the wars and Depression — arguing that post-war socialist values of collective responsibility must replace them."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

30 marks: How does Priestley use the historical and social context of 1912 and 1945 to shape meaning in "An Inspector Calls"?

Priestley exploits the gap between 1912 and 1945 as his most powerful dramatic device. By setting the play in the complacent pre-war era but writing for a post-war audience, he transforms every confident pronouncement Birling makes into a devastating irony. When Birling dismisses the possibility of war — "there isn't a chance of war" — and declares the Titanic "unsinkable, absolutely unsinkable," the 1945 audience recognises immediately that his capitalist certainties are hollow. This dramatic irony is not merely comic; it is Priestley's central argument that the individualist, hierarchical attitudes of 1912 led directly to global catastrophe.

The social context of 1912 further sharpens Priestley's critique. Eva Smith's exploitation — sacked for asking for "twenty-five shillings a week" — reflects the absence of welfare provision, employment rights, or collective bargaining. Mrs Birling's refusal of charity to a "girl of that sort" exposes how voluntary welfare was subject to class prejudice. Priestley's 1945 audience, living alongside the new NHS and social security system, would recognise the Inspector's demand that "we are members of one body" as the philosophical foundation of the welfare state they had just created.

Gender context is equally critical. Eva's powerlessness — pregnant, destitute, refused help by a women's committee led by the very class that oppresses her — shows how patriarchal and class systems intersect. Sheila's transformation from a girl who "gave Daisy Renton a look" to someone who accepts "shared responsibility" mirrors the broader social awakening Priestley demands: just as women gained political voice through suffrage, citizens must gain social conscience.

Mark scheme: AO3 (context) — precise, integrated, shows how context shapes meaning (15/15); AO2 (analysis) — embedded quotations, explores writer's choices (10/15); AO1 (argument) — sustained, focused thesis (5/5). Total: 30/30.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO3 (Context) in Modern Texts: This is the assessment objective most students struggle with. At grade 5, context is often tacked on as a separate paragraph of historical facts. At grade 7, context is linked to the text but may still feel disconnected from analysis. At grade 9, context is seamlessly woven into every point: the writer's historical moment, social position, and political beliefs are treated as conscious choices that shape the text's construction and its intended effect on the audience.

Grade 5 approach: "The play is set in 1912. At this time Britain was very unequal and there was no welfare state." — Context is stated but not connected to Priestley's choices or the audience's response.

Grade 7 approach: "Setting the play in 1912 means Priestley can use dramatic irony, as the audience knows about the wars that Birling doesn't." — Context is connected to technique but doesn't explore the deeper political purpose.

Grade 9 approach: "Priestley's decision to set the play in 1912 is itself a political act: by exposing the complacency of pre-war capitalism to an audience who have witnessed its catastrophic consequences, he argues that the old order has been discredited by history itself. The dramatic irony is not just a technique — it is the play's moral engine, proving that individualism leads to destruction." — Context is fully integrated with analysis, technique, and writer's purpose.
