NEA & Coursework Guidance

Guidance for the Non-Exam Assessment (NEA): understanding the brief, meeting assessment criteria, managing the production process, and producing high-quality coursework.

Key Fact: The NEA (Non-Exam Assessment) is the coursework component of GCSE Media Studies, typically worth 30% of the final grade. It involves creating a media product in response to a set brief.
Key Fact: AQA NEA: students create a media product for an intended audience, demonstrating application of knowledge and understanding of the theoretical framework. The brief changes annually.
Key Fact: OCR NEA: students create a media product and a written evaluation, demonstrating understanding of media language, representation, and audiences. Component 3 is worth 30%.
Key Fact: Eduqas NEA: students create a media product from a choice of briefs, demonstrating understanding of media theory and practice. Unit 3 is worth 30%.
Key Fact: Understanding the brief: read it carefully, identify the media form, genre, target audience, and purpose. Every creative decision must respond to the brief requirements.
Key Fact: Assessment criteria typically evaluate: research and planning, the quality of the finished product, understanding of media language and representation, and evaluation/reflection.
Key Fact: Research phase: analyse existing products in the same media form and genre, identifying conventions that your product should follow or subvert. Document this research thoroughly.
Key Fact: Planning phase: produce all required pre-production documents (storyboards, scripts, mood boards, schedules). Show how each decision is informed by research and audience targeting.
Key Fact: Production phase: create the product using appropriate techniques and technology. Document the process with screenshots, photos, and production logs.
Key Fact: Post-production phase: refine the product through editing, sound design, and visual effects. Show evidence of iterative improvement.
Key Fact: Evaluation: critically assess the finished product against the brief, discussing strengths and weaknesses, how well it targets the audience, and what you would do differently.
Key Fact: Time management: the NEA is a long project — plan a schedule and stick to it. Leave time for unexpected problems and for thorough evaluation.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the NEA and how is it assessed?

Q: How should you approach the NEA brief?

Q: What makes a strong NEA evaluation?

✅ Answers

  1. The NEA (Non-Exam Assessment) is the coursework component worth approximately 30% of GCSE Media Studies. Students create a media product in response to a set brief. Assessment typically covers: research and planning (understanding the form, audience, and conventions), the quality of the finished product (technical competence and creative effectiveness), and evaluation (critical reflection on the product and process).
  2. Read the brief carefully and identify: the media form (film, print, online, etc.), the required genre or style, the target audience, the purpose of the product, and any specific requirements. Then research existing products in that form/genre, plan your product with clear audience and intention, produce it using appropriate techniques, and evaluate it critically against the brief requirements.
  3. A strong evaluation: refers specifically to the brief requirements, uses media studies terminology (codes, conventions, representation, audience), provides evidence from the product to support claims, discusses both strengths and weaknesses honestly, links creative decisions to audience and intention, considers what you would do differently with hindsight, and demonstrates understanding of the theoretical framework.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Read the brief at least three times before starting — missing a requirement loses marks
  • Document EVERY decision and justify it with reference to audience, intention, and research
  • The evaluation is worth significant marks — don't rush it

📝 Exam Technique

Media Studies Exam Tips:
Always use specific examples from CSPs in your answers. Apply media theories by name (Mulvey, Hall, Goodwin, etc.) and explain how they help analyse the product. For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Use precise media terminology throughout.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ The NEA is just about making a good-looking media product. ✓ The NEA assesses understanding of media concepts as much as technical quality. A product that looks good but doesn't demonstrate understanding of audience, representation, and media language will score lower than a less polished product with strong theoretical understanding.

✗ You should copy an existing product for your NEA. ✓ While research into existing products is essential, copying them directly shows no understanding or creativity. The best NEA products use conventions appropriately while adding original elements that demonstrate creative thinking and audience awareness.

✗ The evaluation should only say positive things about your product. ✓ Honest evaluation that discusses weaknesses and what you would improve scores higher than uncritical praise. Examiners value critical reflection — acknowledging problems shows deeper understanding than claiming everything worked perfectly.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain how you would approach the NEA to produce a high-quality media product. [12 marks]

I would approach the NEA systematically, ensuring each phase demonstrates understanding of the media framework and responds to the brief. First, I would analyse the brief carefully, identifying the media form, genre, target audience, and specific requirements. I would note any mandatory elements (e.g. required print products or video durations) to ensure compliance. In the research phase, I would analyse at least three existing products in the same form and genre, identifying key conventions of media language (visual, audio, written codes), representation patterns, and audience targeting strategies. I would document this research with annotated examples. In the planning phase, I would produce all required pre-production documents, with each decision justified by my research and audience profiling. My mood board would establish visual style; my storyboard would plan narrative and camerawork; my script would detail dialogue and action. During production, I would follow professional practice: using appropriate equipment, directing performances, and capturing high-quality footage. I would document the process with production logs and behind-the-scenes evidence. In post-production, I would edit for narrative clarity and pace, add sound design, colour grade for atmosphere, and add titles. I would show evidence of iterative improvement through different edit versions. Finally, my evaluation would critically assess the product against the brief, using media terminology to analyse how effectively I applied media language, addressed representation, and targeted my audience. I would honestly discuss strengths, weaknesses, and what I would do differently.
