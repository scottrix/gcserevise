MS1: Media Language

Foundation Higher AQA 8572, OCR J200, Eduqas C680QS

Media Language

How media products communicate meaning through codes, conventions, and techniques. Understanding denotation, connotation, and how media language creates messages for audiences.

Key Fact: Media language refers to the ways in which media products communicate meaning to audiences through visual, audio, written, and technical codes.
Key Fact: Denotation is the literal or surface meaning of a sign (what you see). Connotation is the deeper or associated meaning (what it suggests). For example, a red rose denotes a flower but connotes love and romance.
Key Fact: Technical codes include camerawork (close-up, wide shot, low angle), editing (cross-cutting, montage, pace), sound (diegetic, non-diegetic, sound bridge), and mise-en-scene (setting, costume, lighting, props).
Key Fact: Symbolic codes convey meaning through objects, colours, and visual elements that carry cultural significance — e.g. a wedding ring symbolises commitment, dark lighting connotes danger.
Key Fact: Written codes include headlines, captions, taglines, and dialogue — the language choices that shape meaning and audience response.
Key Fact: Genre conventions are the shared features audiences expect from a particular type of media product — e.g. horror films use low-key lighting, eerie music, and isolated settings.
Key Fact: Intertextuality is when one media product references or borrows from another — e.g. The Simpsons referencing classic films, or a music video imitating a famous painting.
Key Fact: Anchorage is text or elements that fix the meaning of an image — e.g. a newspaper caption that tells readers how to interpret a photograph.
Key Fact: Polysemy means a media text can have multiple meanings depending on the audience — different viewers may interpret the same product differently based on their background and experiences.
Key Fact: Semiotics (the study of signs) was developed by Ferdinand de Saussure, who identified the signifier (the physical form) and the signified (the concept it represents).
Key Fact: Roland Barthes extended semiotics with the concept of myth — how connotations become naturalised so they seem like common sense rather than cultural constructions.
Key Fact: Media language analysis requires identifying specific codes and explaining how they create meaning for the intended audience.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the difference between denotation and connotation?

Q: Explain the three types of technical codes used in film and television.

Q: What is intertextuality and why do media producers use it?

Q: Evaluate the usefulness of semiotics in analysing media products.

✅ Answers

  1. Denotation is the literal, surface-level meaning of a sign — what you actually see (e.g. a red rose is a flower). Connotation is the deeper cultural meaning it suggests (e.g. a red rose connotes love, passion, romance).
  2. Camerawork: shot types (close-up, wide shot, low angle) and movement that frame the action. Editing: cuts, transitions, pace and rhythm that shape narrative and emotion. Sound: diegetic (within the world) and non-diegetic (added) sound that creates atmosphere and meaning.
  3. Intertextuality is when one media product references or borrows from another — e.g. The Simpsons parodying a film scene. Producers use it to create humour, show sophistication, appeal to knowledgeable audiences, and add layers of meaning.
  4. Semiotics provides a systematic framework for analysing how signs create meaning, helping students move beyond personal opinion to structured analysis of codes and connotations. However, it can over-interpret meanings that audiences may not consciously recognise, and polysemy means the same sign can mean different things to different viewers, making single interpretations problematic.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use specific examples from CSPs when discussing media language
  • Identify the code FIRST, then explain what it connotes — don't just describe what you see
  • Use the correct terminology: 'connote' not 'mean', 'signifier' not 'sign'

📝 Exam Technique

Media Studies Exam Tips — Media Language:
1. For Media Language, use media terminology precisely (e.g. connotation, denotation, representation, ideology)
2. Always analyse how media language creates meaning for specific audiences
3. Reference specific examples from set products to support your analysis
4. When evaluating Media Language, consider the role of media industries and cultural contexts
5. For extended answers, debate different interpretations before reaching your own

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Denotation and connotation mean the same thing. ✓ Denotation is the literal meaning (what you see); connotation is the suggested meaning (what it implies culturally).

✗ Every audience reads a media text the same way. ✓ Media texts are polysemic — different audiences can interpret the same product differently based on their cultural background, age, gender, and experiences.

✗ Semiotics only applies to images. ✓ Semiotics applies to all signs — images, words, sounds, objects, and colours all function as signs with signifiers and signifieds.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Analyse how media language creates meaning in one of your CSPs. [15 marks]

In the advert for 'This Girl Can' (BBC, 2017), media language is used to challenge conventional representations of women in sports media. The visual code shows a close-up of a woman mid-exercise, sweating and smiling. The denotation is a woman exercising; the connotation is that fitness is for enjoyment and empowerment, not just appearance. This subverts the conventional media language of women's fitness adverts, which typically use long shots emphasising slim bodies and polished appearance. The bold, sans-serif typography of 'This Girl Can' uses an informal, confident register. The word 'can' is an active verb that connotes ability and determination, contrasting with the passive representation of women in many media products. The colour palette uses bright, energetic colours (pinks, oranges) that connote vitality and energy rather than traditional feminine delicacy. The tagline 'I'm building my strengths and you can see it' anchors the image with a direct, first-person statement that connotes empowerment and visibility. The use of first person creates an intimate, personal connection with the audience. The advert uses intertextuality by referencing the language of motivational posters and fitness campaigns, but subverts their conventions by showing a realistic, relatable body rather than an idealised one. Overall, the media language of this CSP deliberately challenges dominant codes and conventions to create a progressive, empowering message that redefines how women's participation in sport is represented.
