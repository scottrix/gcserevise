Media Representation

How media products represent people, places, events, and ideas. Understanding stereotypes, selection and omission, and how representations reflect and shape social attitudes.

Key Fact: Media representation is how media products portray people, groups, places, events, and ideas — representations are always constructed, never a simple reflection of reality.
Key Fact: Selection and omission: media producers choose what to include and exclude, which shapes how the subject is represented. What is left out is as important as what is included.
Key Fact: Stereotypes are simplified, widely recognised representations of groups — e.g. the 'dumb blonde', the 'action hero', the 'grumpy old man'. They reduce complex identities to a few traits.
Key Fact: Dominant representations are the most common and widely accepted portrayals in society. Alternative representations challenge or subvert these norms.
Key Fact: Gender representation: women have historically been underrepresented and stereotyped (damsel in distress, sex object, nagging wife). The Bechdel Test measures whether women talk to each other about something other than men.
Key Fact: Laura Mulvey's 'male gaze' theory argues that mainstream media represents women from a heterosexual male perspective, objectifying them for male pleasure.
Key Fact: Representation of race and ethnicity: ethnic minorities have been marginalised, stereotyped (criminals, terrorists, exotic others), or underrepresented. Representation has improved but problems persist.
Key Fact: Representation of age: young people are often represented as rebellious, lazy, or vulnerable; elderly people as frail, wise, or burdensome. These stereotypes affect how groups are treated.
Key Fact: Representation of class: working-class people are often stereotyped as lazy, uneducated, or criminal; middle-class people as aspirational and respectable.
Key Fact: Stuart Hall's reception theory: audiences can accept, negotiate, or oppose the preferred reading of a media text, depending on their own experiences and cultural position.
Key Fact: Mediation is the process by which media producers select, organise, and present information — all media representations are mediated versions of reality.
Key Fact: Context matters: representations change over time and vary across cultures. What seems progressive now may seem stereotypical in future, and vice versa.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the difference between selection and omission in media representation?

Q: Explain Laura Mulvey's concept of the 'male gaze'.

Q: How can media producers challenge stereotypes?

Q: Evaluate the view that media representations are improving for minority groups.

✅ Answers

  1. Selection is what media producers choose to include in a representation, emphasising certain aspects. Omission is what they choose to leave out. Both shape the representation — e.g. selecting only violent protests and omitting peaceful ones represents protesters as violent.
  2. Mulvey argued that mainstream cinema and media represent women through a heterosexual male perspective, objectifying their bodies for male pleasure. The camera 'looks' at women as objects to be viewed, and audiences are positioned to identify with this male viewpoint regardless of their own gender.
  3. Producers can challenge stereotypes by: creating complex, multi-dimensional characters that defy expectations; giving marginalised groups central rather than peripheral roles; using counter-stereotypical casting and narratives; and subverting audience expectations through representation that contradicts dominant norms.
  4. Representations have improved: more diverse casting, complex characters for ethnic minorities and LGBTQ+ groups, and regulatory requirements for inclusion. However, progress is uneven — some groups remain stereotyped, tokenism persists, and backlash against 'woke' casting shows resistance to change. Streaming platforms have driven more diverse content than traditional broadcasters.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always analyse WHO is being represented, HOW they are represented, and WHY
  • Use specific examples from CSPs — never write in general terms alone
  • Discuss the effect of representations on audiences and society

📝 Exam Technique

Media Studies Exam Tips:
Always use specific examples from CSPs in your answers. Apply media theories by name (Mulvey, Hall, Goodwin, etc.) and explain how they help analyse the product. For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Use precise media terminology throughout.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Media representations are just reflections of reality. ✓ All media representations are constructed through selection, mediation, and omission. They are never a neutral reflection of reality — they always reflect the values and perspectives of their producers.

✗ Stereotypes are always negative. ✓ Stereotypes can be positive (e.g. the 'noble savage', the 'model minority') but are still reductive because they reduce complex groups to a few simplified traits.

✗ Audiences always accept the representations they see in the media. ✓ Stuart Hall's reception theory shows audiences can accept, negotiate, or oppose the preferred reading. Different viewers respond differently based on their own experiences and cultural position.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate how representations of gender have changed in contemporary media products. Refer to your CSPs. [15 marks]

Representations of gender in contemporary media have shifted significantly from traditional stereotypes, though change is uneven across different media forms. In the 'This Girl Can' campaign (BBC, 2017), women are represented as active, powerful, and engaged in sport for enjoyment rather than appearance. This directly challenges the 'male gaze' identified by Mulvey, where women are positioned as objects for male visual pleasure. The campaign shows women of different body types, ages, and ethnicities, rejecting the dominant representation of women in sports media as slim, young, and conventionally attractive. However, traditional representations persist in other areas. Video game characters still frequently sexualise women (e.g. Lara Croft's original design), and female characters in action films are often still defined by their appearance. The Bechdel Test continues to be failed by many mainstream films. In men's representation, there has been some diversification. The 'new man' representation shows men as emotionally articulate and nurturing, as seen in some contemporary advertising. But hyper-masculine representations remain dominant in action films and gaming, potentially reinforcing harmful expectations. Intersectionality matters: white, middle-class women are better represented than women of colour or working-class women. Trans and non-binary representation is improving but remains limited. Overall, while contemporary media products increasingly challenge traditional gender stereotypes, progress is partial and uneven. Some products drive change while others reinforce traditional representations.
