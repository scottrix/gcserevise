Online, Social & Participatory Media

Online, social, and participatory media: websites, social media platforms, user-generated content, algorithms, and the democratisation and risks of digital media.

Key Fact: Online and social media encompass websites, social networking platforms, blogs, vlogs, wikis, and apps — media products that exist on the internet and often enable audience participation.
Key Fact: Websites use conventions: homepage, navigation menu, banner, content sections, hyperlinks, multimedia (images, video, audio), and calls to action.
Key Fact: Social media platforms (Instagram, TikTok, X/Twitter, YouTube, Snapchat) enable user-generated content, social networking, and algorithmic content distribution.
Key Fact: Prosumer culture: audiences are no longer just consumers — they produce, share, and remix content. This democratises media but also creates challenges with misinformation and quality.
Key Fact: Algorithms determine what content users see on social media, based on engagement data. This creates filter bubbles (echo chambers) where users see content that reinforces their views.
Key Fact: Viral content: media that spreads rapidly through sharing on social networks. Virality is driven by emotional appeal, relatability, humour, and shareability rather than quality or importance.
Key Fact: Influencers: social media users with large followings who monetise their audience through brand partnerships, advertising, and merchandise. They blur the line between audience and producer.
Key Fact: Participatory culture (Jenkins): fans actively participate in media communities, creating fan fiction, art, videos, and discussions. This gives audiences creative agency but also free labour for media industries.
Key Fact: Data and privacy: online platforms collect user data for targeted advertising and algorithmic personalisation. This raises ethical concerns about surveillance, consent, and manipulation.
Key Fact: Online regulation: the Online Safety Act 2023 requires platforms to protect users from harmful content. Ofcom enforces this, but the global nature of the internet makes regulation difficult.
Key Fact: Convergence: smartphones combine multiple media forms (camera, music player, social media, web browser) in one device. This convergence has transformed how audiences consume and produce media.
Key Fact: Digital divide: not everyone has equal access to online media — age, income, location, and disability affect access. This creates inequalities in who can participate in digital culture.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the 'prosumer' and how has digital media created this role?

Q: How do algorithms affect what audiences see on social media?

Q: Evaluate the impact of social media on democratic participation.

✅ Answers

  1. A prosumer is someone who both produces and consumes media content. Digital media — smartphones, social media platforms, editing apps — has enabled audiences to create and share content, not just passively consume it. Examples include YouTube vloggers, TikTok creators, and fan fiction writers.
  2. Algorithms analyse user behaviour (likes, shares, watch time) to personalise content feeds. They prioritise content likely to generate engagement, creating filter bubbles where users see posts that reinforce their existing views. This can polarise opinions and limit exposure to diverse perspectives.
  3. Social media enables direct political engagement, rapid mobilisation (Arab Spring, BLM), and gives voice to marginalised groups. However, filter bubbles polarise debate, misinformation spreads faster than truth, and algorithmic amplification rewards extreme content. The net effect depends on regulation, media literacy, and platform design.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always name specific platforms (TikTok, Instagram, YouTube) and their conventions
  • Discuss algorithms and their impact on audiences and content
  • Consider both the democratising potential and the risks of online media

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Social media is completely unregulated. ✓ The Online Safety Act 2023 requires platforms to protect users from harmful content, and Ofcom enforces this. The ASA regulates online advertising. However, enforcement is challenging given the volume of content and global nature of platforms.

✗ User-generated content is always trustworthy because it comes from real people. ✓ User-generated content can be valuable but also includes misinformation, deepfakes, and biased content. Trust should be based on verification, not source type. Professional journalism has editorial standards that UGC lacks.

✗ Everyone has equal access to online media. ✓ The digital divide means access varies by age, income, location, and disability. Rural areas may have poor broadband, older people may lack digital skills, and poverty limits device access.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the impact of social media on media audiences and industries. [15 marks]

Social media has fundamentally transformed the relationship between media audiences and industries, creating both opportunities and challenges. For audiences, social media has empowered participation. Prosumers create and share content, challenging the traditional producer-audience hierarchy. Jenkins' participatory culture shows fans as active creators, not passive consumers. Audiences can engage directly with producers, access diverse content, and build communities around shared interests. However, algorithms shape what audiences see. Filter bubbles created by personalisation can narrow perspectives and polarise debate. The attention economy rewards engaging content over accurate content, enabling misinformation. The viral spread of content prioritises emotional impact over truth. For industries, social media has created new business models. Influencer marketing has become a major revenue stream. Brands can target audiences with unprecedented precision using data analytics. Streaming services use social media for distribution and promotion. But industries have also lost control. Audiences can publicly criticise brands, negative content can go viral, and the speed of social media means crises escalate rapidly. Traditional gatekeepers (newspapers, broadcasters) have less power to set the agenda. Regulation struggles to keep pace. The Online Safety Act addresses harmful content, but the global nature of platforms and the volume of user-generated content make enforcement difficult. Overall, social media has democratised media participation but created new risks. The key challenge is balancing freedom with protection, and participation with quality.
