Television

Television as a media form: scheduling, streaming, genre conventions, narrative structures, and the evolution of TV from broadcast to on-demand.

Key Fact: Television is one of the most influential media forms, combining visual, audio, and written codes to tell stories and communicate ideas to mass and niche audiences.
Key Fact: Traditional broadcast TV used scheduling to target audiences — primetime (7-10pm) for mass audiences, late night for niche content, daytime for specific demographics.
Key Fact: Streaming services (Netflix, Amazon Prime, Disney+) have disrupted traditional broadcasting — audiences can watch what they want, when they want, on any device.
Key Fact: TV drama conventions: ensemble casts, multiple storylines, cliffhangers, series arcs, episodic and serial structures. Quality TV / 'peak TV' features cinematic production values.
Key Fact: Narrative structures: linear (chronological), non-linear (flashbacks, flash-forwards), open (unresolved), closed (resolved), single-strand, multi-strand.
Key Fact: Todorov's narrative theory: equilibrium → disruption → recognition → attempt to repair → new equilibrium. Many TV dramas follow this pattern within episodes and across series.
Key Fact: Levi-Strauss's binary opposition: narratives are structured around opposing forces — good/evil, nature/civilisation, rich/poor. Identifying these helps analyse meaning.
Key Fact: Barthes' narrative codes: enigma code (mysteries that drive the plot), action code (events that move the story forward), and semic code (connotations that build character).
Key Fact: Genre in TV: drama, comedy, documentary, news, reality TV, sci-fi, crime. Hybrids blend genres (e.g. dramedy). Genre is used to target audiences and set expectations.
Key Fact: Public service broadcasting (BBC, Channel 4) has a remit to inform, educate, and entertain. Commercial TV (ITV, Sky) is funded by advertising and must attract mass audiences.
Key Fact: Ofcom regulates UK broadcasting — it sets standards for content, protects audiences from harmful material, and ensures fair competition between broadcasters.
Key Fact: Watercooler moments: major TV events that generate widespread social discussion — e.g. live episodes, character deaths, or twists. Social media amplifies these shared experiences.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How has streaming changed television as a media form?

Q: Apply Todorov's narrative theory to a TV drama you have studied.

Q: What is the difference between public service and commercial television?

✅ Answers

  1. Streaming has changed TV by: removing scheduling (audiences watch on demand), enabling binge-watching of entire series, using data to commission targeted content, increasing production budgets for 'quality TV', and creating global distribution. However, traditional broadcast TV still reaches mass audiences for live events.
  2. Todorov's theory identifies five stages: equilibrium (normal state), disruption (something changes), recognition (characters realise), attempt to repair (they try to fix it), new equilibrium (new normal). In many TV dramas, each episode follows this pattern while the overall series arc provides a longer narrative. An episode might begin with a stable community, face a threat, and end with a new status quo.
  3. Public service TV (BBC, Channel 4) is funded by the licence fee or public ownership and has a remit to inform, educate, and entertain diverse audiences. Commercial TV (ITV, Sky) is funded by advertising and subscription, prioritising content that attracts large audiences and revenue. This affects the types of programmes each produces.

📝 Exam Technique

Media Studies Exam Tips:
Always use specific examples from CSPs in your answers. Apply media theories by name (Mulvey, Hall, Goodwin, etc.) and explain how they help analyse the product. For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Use precise media terminology throughout.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Streaming has completely replaced broadcast television. ✓ Streaming has disrupted broadcasting but not replaced it. Live events (sport, news), reality TV, and scheduled programming still attract large audiences. Broadcast and streaming coexist with different strengths.

✗ Todorov's theory applies to every TV programme. ✓ Todorov's theory is a useful framework but doesn't fit all narratives. Some TV deliberately disrupts equilibrium without resolution, and postmodern narratives may reject the structure entirely.

✗ All TV drama follows the same conventions. ✓ While TV dramas share some conventions (character arcs, cliffhangers), they vary significantly across sub-genres. Crime dramas, sci-fi, and soap operas have very different conventions.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Analyse how television conventions are used to engage audiences in one of your TV CSPs. [15 marks]

Contemporary TV drama uses a range of conventions to engage audiences, combining traditional narrative techniques with innovations driven by streaming. Enigma codes are central to audience engagement — unanswered questions drive viewers to keep watching. In many TV dramas, each episode ends with a cliffhanger that creates narrative suspense, while longer series arcs develop mysteries across multiple episodes. Barthes argued that enigma codes are the 'engine' of narrative, and TV drama relies heavily on them. Multi-strand narratives engage audiences by interweaving different storylines, creating complexity that rewards sustained viewing. Streaming services have amplified this convention — series designed for binge-watching can develop more complex narratives across an entire season, since audiences watch episodes in quick succession rather than waiting a week. Character-driven narratives are a key convention of 'quality TV'. Complex, morally ambiguous protagonists engage audiences emotionally and intellectually — viewers debate characters' motivations and decisions on social media, extending engagement beyond the screen. Visual conventions including cinematic camerawork, production design, and colour grading create a sense of quality that distinguishes 'peak TV' from standard broadcasting. High production values signal to audiences that the content is worth their time investment. Overall, TV drama conventions are evolving as streaming changes how audiences watch, but the fundamental techniques of enigma, character engagement, and visual quality remain central to audience engagement.
