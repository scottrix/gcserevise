Music Video

Music video as a media form: purposes, conventions, performance and narrative types, and how artists use video to construct star image and target audiences.

Key Fact: Music videos serve multiple purposes: promoting the song and artist, extending the artist's star image, creating visual narratives, and generating additional revenue and audience engagement.
Key Fact: Performance videos feature the artist performing (singing, dancing, playing instruments), creating a sense of authenticity and connecting the audience with the artist's live persona.
Key Fact: Narrative videos tell a story — either illustrating the song's lyrics (amplification) or creating a separate narrative. Some combine performance and narrative elements.
Key Fact: Conventions of music video: quick cuts and editing to the beat, close-ups of the artist, stylised lighting and colour, choreography, and visual motifs linked to the song.
Key Fact: Star image (Dyer): the music video constructs and maintains the artist's star persona. Stars are not real people — their image is carefully crafted through visual codes and narratives.
Key Fact: Andrew Goodwin identified six key features of music videos: genre characteristics, relationship between lyrics and visuals, relationship between music and visuals, demands of the record label (close-ups), frequent reference to notion of looking, and intertextual reference.
Key Fact: Voyeurism: many music videos feature the 'male gaze' (Mulvey), particularly in genres like hip-hop and pop, where women are presented as objects for visual pleasure.
Key Fact: Intertextuality in music videos: videos frequently reference films, other music videos, art, fashion, and cultural texts — creating pleasure for audiences who recognise the references.
Key Fact: Digital platforms: YouTube is the primary platform for music videos. The shift from TV channels (MTV) to online has changed how audiences discover and share music videos.
Key Fact: Budget and production values vary enormously — major label videos can cost millions, while independent artists create low-budget videos that may still go viral.
Key Fact: Censorship and controversy: some music videos have been banned or restricted for sexual content, violence, or political messages. This can generate publicity but limits distribution.
Key Fact: Social media: TikTok and Instagram have changed how music videos are promoted — clips, dance challenges, and behind-the-scenes content extend the video's reach.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What are the main purposes of a music video?

Q: Explain Andrew Goodwin's key features of music videos.

Q: Evaluate the role of music videos in constructing star image.

✅ Answers

  1. Music videos serve to: promote the song and increase sales/streams, construct and extend the artist's star image, create a visual narrative that enhances the song's meaning, generate media coverage and social media sharing, and extend the commercial life of a single through additional content.
  2. Goodwin identified: 1) Genre characteristics (conventions of the genre), 2) Relationship between lyrics and visuals (illustrating, amplifying, or contradicting), 3) Relationship between music and visuals (editing to the beat), 4) Record label demands (close-ups of the artist), 5) Notion of looking (screens, mirrors, cameras), 6) Intertextual references to other media.
  3. Music videos are crucial for constructing star image because they visualise the artist's persona. Every element — costume, setting, performance style, narrative — contributes to the star's identity. Dyer argues stars are constructed, not real, and the video is a key site of this construction. However, audiences may negotiate or oppose the preferred image, and social media now allows artists to construct their image more directly.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Music videos just show the artist singing the song. ✓ Music videos use performance, narrative, and conceptual elements to create meaning beyond the song. They construct star image, create visual narratives, and use intertextuality and symbolic codes to communicate with audiences.

✗ All music videos objectify women. ✓ While many videos do objectify women (the male gaze), this is not universal. Some artists deliberately challenge this convention, and female artists increasingly control their own representation. Generalising ignores the diversity of music video production.

✗ YouTube killed music television. ✓ YouTube replaced MTV as the primary platform for music videos, but MTV adapted by shifting to reality TV and other content. Music videos still exist but are consumed differently — on demand rather than scheduled.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Analyse how one of your music video CSPs constructs the artist's star image. [12 marks]

Music videos are a primary site for the construction and maintenance of star image, using visual, audio, and narrative codes to create a persona that audiences connect with. Dyer argues that stars are constructed images, not real people. In music videos, this construction happens through deliberate choices of costume, setting, performance style, and narrative positioning. The artist's star image must be both familiar enough to be recognisable and fresh enough to maintain audience interest. Goodwin's features help analyse this construction. Record labels demand frequent close-ups of the artist because the star image is the primary selling point — audiences connect with the face and persona, not just the music. Performance videos create authenticity (the artist as musician), while narrative videos add depth (the artist as storyteller or character). The relationship between lyrics and visuals is crucial for star image. Amplification — where the video expands on the song's themes rather than simply illustrating them — creates a richer star persona. This shows the artist as creative and thoughtful, adding cultural value. Intertextual references position the artist within a cultural lineage, showing sophistication and cultural knowledge. This appeals to audiences who value artistic credibility. The artist's star image may also be constructed through what Goodwin calls the 'notion of looking' — cameras, mirrors, and screens within the video draw attention to the act of looking itself, making the audience aware of their own gaze and the artist's awareness of being watched. Overall, music videos are essential tools for star construction, and analysing their codes reveals how the artist's persona is deliberately crafted for commercial and cultural purposes.
