Advertising & Marketing

Advertising and marketing as media forms: persuasive techniques, regulation, digital advertising, and how brands target and influence audiences.

Key Fact: Advertising is a paid form of persuasive communication that aims to promote products, services, or ideas to target audiences through various media platforms.
Key Fact: Marketing encompasses the broader strategy of identifying, anticipating, and satisfying customer requirements profitably — advertising is one tool within the marketing mix.
Key Fact: Persuasive techniques include: emotional appeal, fear, humour, celebrity endorsement, bandwagon (everyone's doing it), testimonials, rhetorical questions, and superlatives ('the best').
Key Fact: AIDA model: Attention (grab the audience), Interest (hold it), Desire (create want), Action (prompt purchase). Most adverts follow this structure.
Key Fact: Print advertising conventions: headline, visual image, body copy, logo, slogan, call to action. Layout follows the Z-pattern (how eyes scan a page).
Key Fact: Digital advertising: social media ads, influencer marketing, programmatic advertising (automated buying), native advertising (content that looks like editorial), and retargeting.
Key Fact: Regulation: the ASA (Advertising Standards Authority) regulates UK advertising — adverts must be legal, decent, honest, and truthful. CAP and BCAP codes set the rules.
Key Fact: Targeting: advertisers use demographics and psychographics to target specific audiences. Digital advertising uses data tracking for personalised targeting (behavioural targeting).
Key Fact: Branding: a brand is not just a logo but a set of associations in the consumer's mind. Successful brands create emotional connections that go beyond the product's functional qualities.
Key Fact: Cultural impact: advertising both reflects and shapes cultural values. Critics argue advertising creates false needs, promotes materialism, and reinforces stereotypes.
Key Fact: Product placement: brands pay to have their products featured within media content (films, TV, music videos). This is regulated but increasingly common.
Key Fact: Influencer marketing: social media influencers promote products to their followers, often in a way that seems authentic and personal. This raises regulatory questions about transparency.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the AIDA model and how do advertisers use it?

Q: How has digital technology changed advertising?

Q: Evaluate the regulation of advertising in the UK.

✅ Answers

  1. AIDA stands for Attention (grab the audience with a striking image or headline), Interest (hold attention with engaging content), Desire (make the audience want the product by showing benefits), and Action (tell them what to do — 'buy now', 'visit our website'). Most adverts follow this structure to move audiences from awareness to purchase.
  2. Digital technology has enabled: personalised behavioural targeting using data tracking, programmatic advertising (automated buying), social media and influencer marketing, interactive and video ads, native advertising that blends with content, and real-time performance measurement. This makes advertising more targeted but raises privacy concerns.
  3. The ASA provides independent regulation, requiring adverts to be legal, decent, honest, and truthful. This protects consumers from misleading claims and harmful content. However, regulation struggles to keep pace with digital advertising — influencer marketing, native advertising, and online targeting often fall outside traditional rules. Enforcement relies on complaints rather than proactive monitoring.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always identify the persuasive techniques used in specific CSP adverts
  • Discuss the target audience using demographic and psychographic terms
  • Consider regulatory context — ASA rules, CAP code, Ofcom for broadcast ads

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Advertising just means TV commercials. ✓ Advertising encompasses many forms: print, digital, outdoor, social media, influencer, native, and product placement. TV commercials are just one type within a diverse media form.

✗ All advertising is manipulative and dishonest. ✓ While some advertising is misleading, the ASA requires adverts to be honest and truthful. Many adverts are creative and culturally significant. The question is about influence and ethics, not blanket condemnation.

✗ Digital advertising is unregulated. ✓ Digital advertising is regulated by the ASA under the CAP Code. Influencer marketing must disclose paid partnerships, and there are rules about targeting children and making claims. However, enforcement is challenging.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Analyse how one of your advertising CSPs targets and appeals to its audience. [15 marks]

Advertising uses a range of media language techniques to target and appeal to specific audiences, guided by the AIDA model and informed by detailed audience profiling. The visual codes of the advert are designed to attract the target audience's attention. The choice of imagery — whether featuring aspirational lifestyles, relatable people, or striking visuals — is determined by psychographic profiling. For example, adverts targeting 'aspirers' (psychographic group) use images of success, luxury, and social status to create desire. The written codes reinforce the visual message. Headlines use direct address ('You deserve this') to create personal connection, while body copy emphasises benefits over features. Slogans create memorable brand associations through repetition and linguistic devices (alliteration, rhyme, puns). Celebrity endorsement leverages the star's cultural meaning (Dyer's star theory) to transfer positive associations to the product. The celebrity's star image — carefully constructed through media appearances — carries connotations that the brand wishes to associate with. The mode of address is crucial: informal, direct language targets younger audiences; sophisticated, understated language targets affluent audiences. This demonstrates how language choices are determined by audience profiling. Digital targeting has refined this further. Behavioural data allows advertisers to show different versions of adverts to different audience segments, even within the same campaign. This personalisation increases effectiveness but raises ethical questions about data privacy and manipulation. Overall, advertising is a highly targeted media form where every element is designed to appeal to a specific audience profile, using psychological techniques to move consumers through the AIDA process.
