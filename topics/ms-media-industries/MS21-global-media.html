MS21: Global Media

Global Media

Globalisation of media: cultural imperialism, glocalisation, transnational media, and how global media flows affect local cultures and industries.

Key Fact: Global media refers to media products and companies that operate across national boundaries, distributing content to worldwide audiences.
Key Fact: Cultural imperialism: the theory that Western (particularly American) media dominates global markets, overwhelming local cultures and imposing Western values. Hollywood films and American TV are distributed worldwide.
Key Fact: Glocalisation: global media products are adapted for local markets — e.g. McDonald's menus varying by country, or Netflix commissioning local-language content (Squid Game, Money Heist).
Key Fact: Transnational media companies: corporations that operate across multiple countries — e.g. Disney, Netflix, Sony. They produce and distribute content globally, but adapt strategies for local markets.
Key Fact: Media flows: media content flows in multiple directions, not just from West to East. Korean pop (K-pop), Bollywood films, and Latin American telenovelas are consumed globally.
Key Fact: The success of non-Western media challenges cultural imperialism theory. Parasite (South Korea) won Best Picture at the Oscars; Squid Game was Netflix's most-watched series.
Key Fact: Subtitles vs dubbing: the growth of subtitled content (facilitated by streaming) has increased access to foreign-language media, challenging the dominance of English-language content.
Key Fact: Local industries: national media industries (Bollywood, Nollywood, K-drama) produce content for local and global audiences, maintaining cultural identity while competing internationally.
Key Fact: YouTube and social media enable global distribution of local content — creators can reach worldwide audiences without traditional distribution infrastructure.
Key Fact: Regulatory challenges: global media platforms operate across jurisdictions, making national regulation difficult. Different countries have different standards for content, advertising, and data protection.
Key Fact: Cultural hybridity: global media increasingly blends cultural elements — e.g. Black Panther combining African cultural elements with Hollywood production values and superhero conventions.
Key Fact: The digital divide: not all regions have equal access to global media — internet penetration, device ownership, and language barriers affect who can participate in global media culture.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is cultural imperialism and how does it apply to media?

Q: How does glocalisation challenge cultural imperialism?

Q: Evaluate whether global media threatens local cultures.

✅ Answers

  1. Cultural imperialism is the theory that dominant Western (particularly American) media exports its culture worldwide, overwhelming local cultures and imposing Western values and lifestyles. Hollywood films, American TV, and global tech platforms distribute US cultural norms globally, potentially reducing cultural diversity and local identity.
  2. Glocalisation means global products are adapted for local markets — e.g. Netflix commissions local-language content like Squid Game (Korea) and Money Heist (Spain). This challenges cultural imperialism because local cultures aren't simply overwhelmed — they produce globally successful content on their own terms, and global companies adapt to local preferences.
  3. Global media can threaten local cultures by overwhelming them with dominant (often American) content, reducing demand for local productions and eroding cultural identity. However, local cultures resist through glocalisation, producing globally successful local content, and using digital platforms to distribute their own stories. The evidence suggests a complex interplay rather than simple cultural domination.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Use specific examples of global media flows — not just American exports
  • Apply both cultural imperialism AND glocalisation to get both sides
  • Discuss how streaming has changed global media distribution

📝 Exam Technique

Media Studies Exam Tips:
Always use specific examples from CSPs in your answers. Apply media theories by name (Mulvey, Hall, Goodwin, etc.) and explain how they help analyse the product. For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Use precise media terminology throughout.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Globalisation means everyone watches the same media. ✓ While global media products reach worldwide audiences, consumption varies by culture, language, and preference. Glocalisation means global products are adapted locally, and audiences choose differently. Media flows are multi-directional, not one-way.

✗ Cultural imperialism is no longer relevant. ✓ While glocalisation and reverse flows challenge the theory, Western media still dominates global markets. Hollywood, American tech platforms, and English-language content remain disproportionately influential. Cultural imperialism persists but is more contested than before.

✗ Bollywood and Nollywood are just imitations of Hollywood. ✓ Bollywood and Nollywood have their own distinct conventions, aesthetics, and cultural contexts. They are not imitations but independent media industries with different production practices, narrative styles, and audience relationships.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the impact of globalisation on media industries and audiences. [15 marks]

Globalisation has profoundly reshaped media industries and audiences, creating both opportunities and concerns. For industries, globalisation enables massive scale. Transnational corporations like Disney and Netflix produce and distribute content worldwide, achieving economies of scale that national industries cannot match. Streaming platforms commission local content for global distribution — Squid Game (South Korea) became Netflix's most-watched series, demonstrating that local content can achieve global reach. However, the dominance of Western conglomerates raises cultural imperialism concerns. Hollywood's global reach means American values, lifestyles, and perspectives are disproportionately represented worldwide. Local industries may struggle to compete, potentially reducing cultural diversity. For audiences, globalisation increases access to diverse content. Streaming services offer content from multiple countries, subtitled and available globally. Audiences can discover Korean dramas, Spanish thrillers, and Indian films that traditional distribution would never have reached them. This challenges the one-directional flow assumed by cultural imperialism. Yet the digital divide means not everyone benefits equally. Regions with poor internet, limited device access, or non-dominant languages may be excluded from global media culture. English-language content still dominates, creating linguistic inequality. Glocalisation represents a middle ground — global companies adapting to local markets while local industries reaching global audiences. This suggests a more complex picture than simple cultural domination. Overall, globalisation has created a more interconnected media landscape where local content can reach global audiences and global companies must adapt locally, but power imbalances remain significant.
