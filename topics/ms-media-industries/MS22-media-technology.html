MS22: Media & Technology

Foundation Higher AQA 8572, OCR J200, Eduqas C680QS

Media & Technology

How technology shapes media: the impact of digital transformation, social media, streaming, AI, and emerging technologies on media production, distribution, and consumption.

Key Fact: Technology has always shaped media — from the printing press to broadcast radio to the internet. Each technological shift transforms how media is produced, distributed, and consumed.
Key Fact: Digital transformation: the shift from analogue to digital has affected every media form — digital cameras, non-linear editing, streaming, online publishing, and social media.
Key Fact: Convergence: smartphones combine multiple media forms (camera, music player, social media, web browser, TV). This has changed audience behaviour — we carry entire media libraries in our pockets.
Key Fact: Streaming revolution: Netflix, Amazon Prime, Disney+ have disrupted cinema, broadcast TV, and physical media. Content is available on demand, anywhere, on any device.
Key Fact: Social media as a media platform: Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube are now major media distributors, reaching billions of users. They blur the line between producer and audience.
Key Fact: Algorithmic distribution: social media and streaming platforms use algorithms to personalise content feeds, creating filter bubbles and influencing what audiences see.
Key Fact: AI and media: artificial intelligence is used for content recommendation, automated news writing, deepfake technology, and targeted advertising. This raises ethical questions about authenticity and manipulation.
Key Fact: Big data: media companies collect vast amounts of user data to personalise content, target advertising, and make commissioning decisions. This raises privacy concerns.
Key Fact: The attention economy: digital platforms compete for user attention, designing features (autoplay, notifications, infinite scroll) that maximise engagement. This has been linked to addiction concerns.
Key Fact: User-generated content: technology enables audiences to create and distribute content — YouTube, TikTok, podcasts, blogs. This democratises media but also creates quality and misinformation challenges.
Key Fact: Virtual and augmented reality: emerging technologies offer new media experiences. VR cinema and AR gaming create immersive forms that challenge traditional media conventions.
Key Fact: Technology and access: while digital technology has increased access, the digital divide means not everyone benefits equally — age, income, location, and disability affect participation.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How has digital convergence changed audience behaviour?

Q: What are the ethical concerns of AI in media?

Q: Evaluate the impact of streaming on media consumption habits.

✅ Answers

  1. Digital convergence (smartphones combining multiple media functions) means audiences can access all media forms on a single device, anywhere, anytime. This has changed behaviour: audiences expect on-demand content, switch between media forms seamlessly, and create as well as consume content. Traditional scheduled media consumption has been replaced by personalised, portable media experiences.
  2. AI raises concerns about: deepfakes (realistic but fake video/audio) undermining trust in media; algorithmic bias reinforcing stereotypes; automated content replacing human creativity; data collection for AI training without consent; and personalisation creating filter bubbles that limit diverse perspectives. Each of these challenges requires regulatory and ethical responses.
  3. Streaming has created binge-watching culture, on-demand viewing, and personalised recommendations. Audiences expect content when and where they want it. However, algorithms create filter bubbles, the sheer volume of content creates 'choice paralysis', and the attention economy raises addiction concerns. Overall, streaming has increased convenience but also created new problems.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always link technology changes to specific media forms and CSPs
  • Discuss both the opportunities and the risks of technological change
  • Consider the digital divide — not everyone benefits equally

📝 Exam Technique

Media Studies Exam Tips — Media & Technology:
1. For Media & Technology, use media terminology precisely (e.g. connotation, denotation, representation, ideology)
2. Always analyse how media language creates meaning for specific audiences
3. Reference specific examples from set products to support your analysis
4. When evaluating Media & Technology, consider the role of media industries and cultural contexts
5. For extended answers, debate different interpretations before reaching your own

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Technology always improves media. ✓ Technology creates opportunities (more access, diverse content, new forms) but also risks (misinformation, addiction, privacy loss, digital divide). The impact of technology is ambivalent, not uniformly positive.

✗ AI will replace all media professionals. ✓ AI automates some tasks (recommendations, basic reporting) but cannot replicate human creativity, editorial judgement, or investigative journalism. AI is a tool that changes rather than replaces human roles in media production.

✗ Everyone has equal access to digital media. ✓ The digital divide means access varies by age, income, location, and disability. Approximately 10% of UK adults have never used the internet, and many more have limited digital skills. Access is not equal.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the impact of digital technology on media audiences. [15 marks]

Digital technology has transformed how audiences access, consume, and participate in media, creating both empowerment and new vulnerabilities. The most significant change is the shift from scheduled to on-demand consumption. Streaming services allow audiences to watch what they want, when they want, on any device. This gives audiences unprecedented choice and control over their media experience. Social media enables participation — audiences are now prosumers who create and share content as well as consuming it. However, this control is partly illusory. Algorithms determine what content appears in feeds and recommendations, creating filter bubbles where audiences see content that reinforces their existing views. The attention economy designs platforms to maximise engagement, using psychological techniques (autoplay, notifications, infinite scroll) that can lead to compulsive use. This is particularly concerning for younger audiences. Digital technology has also increased access to diverse content. Audiences can discover global media, niche interests, and alternative voices that traditional media gatekeepers would not have distributed. Streaming services commission content for diverse audience segments that broadcast TV, seeking mass audiences, would not serve. But the digital divide means not everyone benefits. Older audiences, lower-income households, and people in rural areas with poor broadband have less access to digital media. This creates a two-tier media system where digitally connected audiences have more choice and voice than those who are offline. Privacy is another concern. Data tracking enables personalised content and advertising, but many audiences are unaware of how their data is collected and used. The lack of transparency about algorithmic decision-making undermines informed consent. Overall, digital technology has empowered audiences with choice and participation, but it has also created new forms of influence and inequality that require critical awareness and regulatory attention.
