Gender Representation

How gender is represented across media forms: male and female stereotypes, the male gaze, changing representations, and LGBTQ+ visibility.

Key Fact: Gender representation has been a central concern of media studies because media products both reflect and shape cultural attitudes about masculinity and femininity.
Key Fact: Laura Mulvey's 'male gaze' (1975): mainstream cinema positions audiences to view women through a heterosexual male perspective, objectifying their bodies for visual pleasure.
Key Fact: Traditional female stereotypes: damsel in distress, sex object, nagging wife, career woman (as threat), mother/housewife, femme fatale. These reduce complex identities to simple traits.
Key Fact: Traditional male stereotypes: action hero, family man, buffoon (in sitcoms), businessman, villain, lad. Masculinity is often linked to physical strength, stoicism, and control.
Key Fact: The Bechdel Test: a film passes if it has two named female characters who talk to each other about something other than a man. Many mainstream films still fail this basic test.
Key Fact: Changing representations: contemporary media increasingly features complex, non-stereotypical female characters — e.g. strong female protagonists, women in leadership roles, and diverse body types.
Key Fact: The 'new man': a representation of men as emotionally expressive, nurturing, and domestically competent — a contrast to traditional hyper-masculinity. Advertising has adopted this representation.
Key Fact: Toxic masculinity: media representations that equate masculinity with aggression, emotional suppression, and dominance can reinforce harmful gender expectations for men.
Key Fact: LGBTQ+ representation: historically marginalised, stereotyped (the 'sissy', the 'predatory lesbian'), or invisible. Recent progress includes more diverse and central LGBTQ+ characters.
Key Fact: Intersectionality: gender representation intersects with race, class, age, sexuality, and disability. White, middle-class women are better represented than women of colour, working-class women, or disabled women.
Key Fact: Representation is improving but uneven — some media forms (streaming, indie games) are more progressive, while others (blockbuster films, music videos) remain stereotypical.
Key Fact: Audience response matters: social media enables audiences to critique and challenge poor representations, creating pressure on producers to improve (e.g. #OscarsSoWhite).

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the 'male gaze' and how does it operate in media products?

Q: How have representations of masculinity changed in contemporary media?

Q: Evaluate whether media representations of gender are improving.

✅ Answers

  1. The male gaze (Mulvey, 1975) argues that mainstream media positions the audience to view women through a heterosexual male lens, objectifying their bodies for visual pleasure. The camera lingers on women's bodies, and female characters are often defined by their appearance rather than their actions or personality. Audiences of any gender are positioned to identify with this male viewpoint.
  2. Traditional hyper-masculinity (strong, silent, aggressive) is still common in action films and games, but the 'new man' representation shows men as emotionally expressive, nurturing, and domestically competent. Advertising increasingly uses this representation. However, toxic masculinity persists in some media, reinforcing harmful expectations about male behaviour.
  3. Improvements are evident: more female protagonists, stronger Bechdel Test results, the 'new man' representation, and growing LGBTQ+ visibility. Streaming platforms commission diverse content. However, progress is uneven — the male gaze persists in music videos and games, intersectional representation lags behind, and backlash against 'woke' casting shows resistance. Overall, progress is real but incomplete.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Apply Mulvey's male gaze to specific CSPs with visual evidence
  • Always consider both male and female representations
  • Discuss intersectionality — gender intersects with race, class, age

📝 Exam Technique

Media Studies Exam Tips:
Always use specific examples from CSPs in your answers. Apply media theories by name (Mulvey, Hall, Goodwin, etc.) and explain how they help analyse the product. For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Use precise media terminology throughout.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ The male gaze only affects male viewers. ✓ Mulvey argues that the male gaze positions ALL viewers — regardless of gender — to identify with the heterosexual male perspective. Female viewers learn to view other women (and themselves) through this lens.

✗ Feminism has solved gender representation in media. ✓ While progress has been made, the male gaze, stereotyping, and underrepresentation persist. The Bechdel Test continues to be failed by mainstream films, and the gender pay gap in the media industry remains.

✗ Stereotypes only affect the groups being stereotyped. ✓ Stereotypes affect everyone — they shape how all audiences perceive social groups and reinforce cultural norms. Male stereotypes (e.g. the 'action hero') also create harmful expectations for men.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that media representations of gender are becoming more progressive. Refer to your CSPs. [15 marks]

Media representations of gender have become more progressive in some areas, but change is uneven across media forms and intersecting identities. Progressive representations are increasingly visible. The 'This Girl Can' campaign (BBC, 2017) deliberately challenges the male gaze by representing women as active, powerful, and diverse in body type. It subverts the conventional media language of fitness advertising, which positions women as objects of visual pleasure. Similarly, contemporary TV dramas feature complex female protagonists who drive narrative rather than serving as plot devices for male characters. However, traditional representations persist in other media forms. Music videos continue to objectify women, particularly in hip-hop and pop genres, where female dancers are presented as decorative background. Video games still frequently feature sexualised female character designs and male-dominated protagonist rosters. The 'new man' representation has complicated male representation. While some advertising and TV drama show emotionally articulate, nurturing men, hyper-masculinity remains the dominant representation in action films, video games, and men's lifestyle media. Toxic masculinity is reinforced rather than challenged in many products. Intersectionality reveals further limitations. White, middle-class, young, able-bodied women are the primary beneficiaries of progressive representation. Women of colour, older women, working-class women, disabled women, and trans women remain underrepresented and stereotyped. Overall, while the direction of change is progressive, the pace and extent vary significantly. Some media forms and some groups have seen substantial improvement, while others remain trapped in traditional stereotypes.
