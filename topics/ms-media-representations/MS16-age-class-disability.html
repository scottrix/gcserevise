Age, Class & Disability Representation

How age, social class, and disability are represented in media: stereotypes, marginalisation, and the push for more authentic and diverse representation.

Key Fact: Age, class, and disability are under-analysed but important aspects of media representation, affecting how large sections of the population are perceived and treated.
Key Fact: Youth representation: young people are often stereotyped as lazy, rebellious, tech-obsessed, vulnerable, or dangerous. News media frequently portrays youth negatively — crime, anti-social behaviour, unemployment.
Key Fact: Elderly representation: older people are often invisible in media or stereotyped as frail, dependent, wise, or comical. Their underrepresentation reflects ageism in the media industries.
Key Fact: Middle-aged representation: often the 'default' in media — professionals, parents, authority figures. This age group is the most represented because it matches the demographic of media producers.
Key Fact: Social class representation: working-class people are stereotyped as lazy, uneducated, or criminal (e.g. 'chav' stereotypes). Middle-class people are represented as the norm. Upper-class people are portrayed as privileged or eccentric.
Key Fact: Benefits stigma: television programmes like Benefits Street have been criticised for constructing negative representations of working-class and benefit-claiming communities.
Key Fact: Disability representation: disabled people are significantly underrepresented in media (around 1% of characters despite 22% of the population having a disability).
Key Fact: Disability stereotypes: the villain (the 'evil cripple'), the victim/supercrip (tragic or inspirational), the clown (comic relief), and the burden (on family/society).
Key Fact: The social model of disability: disability is caused by society's barriers (physical, attitudinal, institutional), not by impairment. Media representation can reinforce or challenge these barriers.
Key Fact: Authentic representation: disabled characters should be played by disabled actors and written with input from disabled people. This challenges stereotypical and inauthentic portrayals.
Key Fact: Intersectionality: disabled women, working-class ethnic minorities, and elderly LGBTQ+ people face multiple layers of stereotyping and marginalisation.
Key Fact: Social media has enabled marginalised groups to create their own representations and challenge mainstream stereotypes — e.g. disabled YouTubers, working-class podcasters.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: How are young people typically represented in the media?

Q: What is the social model of disability and how does it relate to media representation?

Q: Evaluate media representations of social class.

✅ Answers

  1. Young people are frequently stereotyped negatively — as lazy, rebellious, tech-obsessed, or dangerous. News media often focuses on youth crime, anti-social behaviour, and unemployment. Positive representations (creative, politically engaged, community-active) are less common. This negative bias affects how young people are treated in society.
  2. The social model argues that disability is created by society's barriers (physical, attitudinal, institutional) rather than by the impairment itself. In media terms, this means representation can either reinforce barriers (stereotyping disabled people as victims or burdens) or challenge them (showing disabled people as full, complex members of society).
  3. Working-class people are routinely stereotyped as lazy, uneducated, or criminal — e.g. 'chav' caricatures and benefits-stigma programming. Middle-class life is presented as the norm. This reflects the class background of media producers (predominantly middle-class) and serves commercial interests (sensational stories about 'scroungers' attract audiences). However, some recent productions offer more authentic working-class representation.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always identify which group is being represented and the specific stereotypes used
  • Use the social model of disability when analysing disability representations
  • Discuss class representation with awareness of media industry demographics

📝 Exam Technique

Media Studies Exam Tips:
Always use specific examples from CSPs in your answers. Apply media theories by name (Mulvey, Hall, Goodwin, etc.) and explain how they help analyse the product. For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Use precise media terminology throughout.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Disability representation is fine now because there are more disabled characters. ✓ While numbers have improved, quality matters. Many disabled characters are still stereotyped (victims, villains, inspirations) and most are played by non-disabled actors. Authentic representation requires both inclusion and accuracy.

✗ Class doesn't matter in modern media. ✓ Class representation is significant — it shapes how different social groups are perceived and treated. Working-class stereotypes in particular affect policy attitudes (e.g. benefits) and reinforce social divisions.

✗ All age groups are equally represented. ✓ Middle-aged people are overrepresented because they dominate the media industry. Young people and elderly people are underrepresented or stereotyped. The demographic of producers shapes the demographic of representations.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the representation of one marginalised group in media products. Refer to your CSPs. [15 marks]

Disabled people remain one of the most marginalised groups in media representation, despite constituting 22% of the UK population. They represent approximately 1% of characters on screen, revealing a profound representational gap. When disabled people do appear, they are often confined to stereotypical roles. The 'supercrip' narrative — where a disabled person achieves something remarkable despite their disability — is common but problematic. It positions disability as something to be overcome rather than accepted, and implies that disabled people who don't achieve extraordinary things have somehow failed. The 'victim' representation presents disabled people as objects of pity, reinforcing the medical model that defines them by their impairment. The social model of disability argues that these representations are themselves disabling — they create attitudinal barriers that prevent full social participation. When media repeatedly shows disabled people as helpless or inspirational, it shapes how non-disabled people perceive and interact with disabled people. However, change is happening. Disabled creators on social media (YouTube, TikTok) produce authentic self-representation that challenges mainstream stereotypes. Some TV dramas now feature disabled characters whose disability is incidental rather than defining. The key issue is behind-the-camera representation. Without disabled writers, directors, and consultants, even well-intentioned representations risk inaccuracy and stereotype. The debate over non-disabled actors playing disabled roles mirrors broader representation debates. Overall, disability representation is improving from a very low base, but progress requires not just more disabled characters but more disabled creators controlling how they are represented.
