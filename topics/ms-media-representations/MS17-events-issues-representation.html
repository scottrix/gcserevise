Events & Issues Representation

How media represents events and social issues: agenda setting, framing, moral panics, and the role of media in shaping public understanding of major events.

Key Fact: Media doesn't just report events — it constructs them through selection, framing, and emphasis, shaping how audiences understand social issues.
Key Fact: Agenda setting: the media doesn't tell people what to think, but it tells them what to think about. By choosing which stories to cover, media sets the public agenda.
Key Fact: Framing: how a story is presented (the angle, language, images) shapes its meaning. The same event can be framed as a 'protest' or a 'riot', a 'freedom fighter' or a 'terrorist'.
Key Fact: Moral panics (Stanley Cohen): when the media defines a group or behaviour as a threat to social values, creating public fear and demanding action. E.g. mods and rockers, video nasties, knife crime.
Key Fact: Deviancy amplification spiral: media coverage of a deviant group increases their visibility, which leads to more coverage, which amplifies the perceived threat.
Key Fact: Ideology: media products carry implicit values and beliefs about how society should be organised. These are often presented as natural and common sense rather than contested.
Key Fact: Representation of protest: media often focuses on confrontation and violence rather than the issues, marginalising the protesters' message.
Key Fact: Representation of immigration: tabloid media frequently frames immigration negatively (as invasion, threat, or burden), while broadsheet coverage may offer more nuanced analysis.
Key Fact: Representation of crime: media over-represents violent crime relative to its actual frequency, creating fear disproportionate to risk. This is known as the 'mean world syndrome'.
Key Fact: War and conflict: media representation of war is shaped by national interests — 'our' soldiers are heroes, 'their' fighters are terrorists. Language choices reveal ideological positions.
Key Fact: Crisis representation: during crises (pandemics, terrorism, natural disasters), media plays a crucial role in informing the public but can also amplify fear or spread misinformation.
Key Fact: Social media has changed events representation: citizens can now report and share their own accounts, challenging mainstream media framing. However, this also enables misinformation.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is agenda setting and how does it work?

Q: Explain Stanley Cohen's concept of the moral panic.

Q: How does media framing affect audience understanding of events?

✅ Answers

  1. Agenda setting is the media's ability to influence which issues the public considers important by choosing which stories to cover prominently. The media doesn't tell people what to think about an issue, but it determines which issues people think about. Stories given front-page coverage or prime-time slots are perceived as more important.
  2. A moral panic occurs when the media defines a group, behaviour, or condition as a threat to societal values. Cohen studied the media response to mods and rockers, showing how exaggerated reporting created a deviancy amplification spiral — more coverage led to more public concern, which led to more coverage, amplifying the perceived threat beyond its actual scale.
  3. Framing determines how an event is understood — the same event can be framed differently. A strike can be framed as workers demanding fair pay or as unions holding the country to ransom. A protest can be framed as peaceful demonstration or violent disorder. The language, images, and context chosen by the media shape audience interpretation.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always analyse specific language choices (headlines, labels) when discussing framing
  • Use Cohen's moral panic theory with specific examples
  • Consider how different media outlets frame the same event differently

📝 Exam Technique

Media Studies Exam Tips:
Always use specific examples from CSPs in your answers. Apply media theories by name (Mulvey, Hall, Goodwin, etc.) and explain how they help analyse the product. For evaluation questions, present both sides before reaching a reasoned conclusion. Use precise media terminology throughout.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ The media simply reports events objectively. ✓ All media coverage involves construction — selection, framing, language choices, and image choices shape how events are represented. There is no such thing as purely objective reporting; all representation involves mediation.

✗ Moral panics are always about trivial concerns. ✓ Moral panics can arise from genuine social issues, but the media response amplifies the concern beyond proportion. The issue may be real; the panic is the disproportionate public reaction driven by media coverage.

✗ Audiences are easily manipulated by media framing. ✓ While framing influences understanding, audiences are not passive. Hall's reception theory shows audiences can negotiate or oppose the preferred reading. Active audiences may recognise and challenge biased framing.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the role of media in constructing moral panics. [12 marks]

The media plays a central role in constructing moral panics by amplifying perceived threats to social values, often beyond their actual scale. Cohen's study of the mods and rockers showed how media exaggeration created a deviancy amplification spiral: sensational reporting made the groups seem more threatening, which led to more public concern, more policing, and more media coverage. The media didn't just report the events — it constructed a social problem. Moral panics follow a pattern: a group or behaviour is defined as deviant; the media exaggerates the scale and threat; moral entrepreneurs (politicians, experts) call for action; authorities respond with crackdowns; the panic subsides but the stereotype persists. Examples include video nasties in the 1980s, knife crime in the 2000s, and social media harms today. However, moral panic theory has limitations. Some threats are genuine — knife crime does kill people. Dismissing all public concern as moral panic can minimise real problems. The theory also assumes a passive audience, whereas Hall's reception theory suggests audiences may negotiate or resist the preferred reading. Social media has changed moral panics. Online, panics can escalate faster and spread further, but counter-narratives also emerge quickly. The gatekeeping function of traditional media is weakened. Overall, while moral panic theory remains valuable for understanding how media amplifies social anxieties, it must be applied critically — not all public concern is a moral panic, and audiences are more active than the theory suggests.
