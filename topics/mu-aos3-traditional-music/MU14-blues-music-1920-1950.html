Blues Music 1920-1950

Blues music from 1920-1950; 12-bar blues structure; blue notes (flattened 3rd, 5th, 7th); call and response; vocal techniques (melisma, falsetto, slide); walking bass; shuffle rhythm; AAB lyric structure; Delta blues to Chicago electric blues; Robert Johnson; Muddy Waters.

Key Fact: Blue notes (flattened 3rd, 5th and 7th of the major scale) create the characteristic blues sound — they fall between the notes of the Western scale.
Key Fact: 12-bar blues follows a I-IV-V chord progression over three four-bar phrases: the AAB lyric structure mirrors this harmonic pattern.
Key Fact: Call and response between vocalist and guitar/harp creates dialogue — a fundamental feature inherited from African musical traditions.
Key Fact: Shuffle rhythm (long-short subdivision of the beat) gives blues its distinctive swing feel — it is notated in 12/8 or with triplets in 4/4.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe how blues music 1920-1950 are used in a piece of music you have studied. Use musical vocabulary.

Q2: Compare how two different pieces use blues music 1920-1950. Explain the effect in each case.

Q3: How could you use blues music 1920-1950 in your own composition or performance? Explain your choices.

✅ Answers

  1. A detailed description using precise musical vocabulary (e.g. specific terms from the AQA elements list), referencing a named piece with accurate observations about how the elements are used and their effect.
  2. A comparison of two pieces identifying similarities and differences in how blues music 1920-1950 are used, explaining the different effects created in each musical context, using comparative language.
  3. A practical application showing understanding of how blues music 1920-1950 can be manipulated in composition or performance, with specific examples of choices made and their intended effect.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use PRECISE musical vocabulary — not vague descriptions like it sounds nice
  • Reference SPECIFIC musical elements when analysing any piece
  • For listening questions: identify elements FIRST, then explain their effect
  • For study pieces: know the context, structure and key features in detail
  • For performing: focus on accuracy, expression and interpretation
  • For composing: show development of ideas, not just repetition

📝 Exam Technique

Music Exam Tips:
Identify 12-bar blues by chord pattern (I-IV-V). Name blue notes (flattened 3rd, 5th, 7th). Explain call and response. Identify shuffle rhythm and AAB form.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Blues is just sad music with guitar. Correct: Blues has SPECIFIC musical features: 12-bar blues structure, blue notes, call and response, AAB lyric form, shuffle rhythm, walking bass, and melismatic vocals. These are technical features the exam tests — not an emotional description. Identify features by name.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Analyse a blues track, identifying at least five characteristic blues features using AQA musical vocabulary. Explain how each feature contributes to the style.

A grade 9 response will: identify 12-bar blues structure with Roman numerals; name blue notes and their scale degrees; explain call and response; identify shuffle rhythm; discuss the evolution from Delta to Chicago blues; use precise terminology.

Mark scheme: AO3 (analyse and evaluate) + AO4 (knowledge and understanding) = total marks for listening; AO1 (perform) + AO2 (compose) for coursework components

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Music has three components: Understanding Music (40% — written exam with listening and study-piece questions), Performing Music (30% — one solo and one ensemble performance), Composing Music (30% — one free composition and one to a brief). The four Assessment Objectives are: AO1 Perform with technical control, expression and interpretation (30%); AO2 Compose and develop musical ideas with technical control and coherence (30%); AO3 Analyse and evaluate music using musical vocabulary (20%); AO4 Demonstrate knowledge and understanding of musical elements, contexts and language (20%). For grade 9, you must show exceptional technical control, creative and coherent composition, perceptive analysis using precise terminology, and sophisticated understanding of how musical elements create meaning in context.
