Melody Writing

Writing melodies for composition; conjunct and disjunct movement; phrase structure (question-answer, antecedent-consequent); melodic development (sequence, inversion, augmentation, fragmentation); choosing effective rhythms; matching melody to harmony; genre-specific melodic conventions; using motifs; staff notation requirements.

Key Fact: Strong melodies have clear phrase structure — typically 4-bar or 8-bar phrases with a sense of question (antecedent) and answer (consequent).
Key Fact: Develop melodies through: sequence (repeating at a different pitch), inversion (turning upside down), augmentation (stretching), fragmentation (using part of the idea).
Key Fact: Match rhythm to genre: rock riffs use syncopation and repetition; Classical melodies use balanced phrasing; jazz melodies use swing and blue notes.
Key Fact: Staff notation requirements: you must submit a score in staff notation (up to 4 sharps/flats) — notation quality affects the AO2 mark for technical control.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use PRECISE musical vocabulary — not vague descriptions like it sounds nice

  • For performing: focus on accuracy, expression and interpretation
  • For composing: show development of ideas, not just repetition

📝 Exam Technique

Music Exam Tips:
Use balanced phrasing (4+4 bars). Apply development techniques (sequence, inversion). Match style to genre. Notate accurately. Annotate your decisions.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: A good melody just means something catchy that you like. Correct: Effective melody writing uses specific TECHNIQUES: balanced phrasing, motivic development (sequence, inversion, augmentation), appropriate rhythmic character for the genre, and coherent harmonic relationship. The exam assesses these technical skills, not whether the melody is catchy.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Compose an 8-bar melody in a specific style. Annotate your composition explaining the melodic techniques you used and how they serve the intended style.

A grade 9 response will: show balanced phrase structure; use at least two development techniques; match melodic style to genre; notate accurately in staff notation; annotate with precise musical vocabulary; explain how melodic choices create the intended character.

Mark scheme: AO3 (analyse and evaluate) + AO4 (knowledge and understanding) = total marks for listening; AO1 (perform) + AO2 (compose) for coursework components

Detailed Notes

Understanding Melody Writing

Melody Writing is a key element of GCSE Music requiring theoretical understanding and practical application. The AQA specification requires you to identify, describe and analyse this element in set works and unfamiliar music. Focus on the specific AQA terminology — examiners reward precise vocabulary over vague descriptions. Understanding how melody writing contributes to the overall character and effect of a piece is essential for achieving high marks in the listening exam.

In your GCSE listening exam, you may be asked to describe how melody writing is used in an extract. Use precise terminology, describe specific features you hear, and explain the effect of these features on the listener. Always link technical description to expressive effect.

Context is important: melody writing in Baroque music serves different expressive purposes than in Romantic or popular music. Understanding how melody writing functions within different historical periods and genres allows you to make perceptive analytical observations.

GCSE Example: Understanding Melody Writing

A Grade 9 exam answer about melody writing identifies specific features using AQA terminology, describes how they are used with reference to specific moments, and explains the effect on the character of the music — connecting technical observation to expressive meaning.

Applying Melody Writing in Performance and Composition

Understanding melody writing informs your performing (AO1) and composing (AO2) work. In performance, awareness of melody writing helps you make interpretive decisions that serve the music. In composition, melody writing provides the building blocks of your creative work — the choices you make about this element shape the identity and coherence of your piece.

For composition, consider how your use of melody writing creates structure and development. The AQA mark scheme specifically rewards compositions that demonstrate development and intentionality — a piece that shows clear initial ideas, systematic development, and structural coherence will score highly.

When preparing for your solo and ensemble performances, consider how your handling of melody writing contributes to the assessment criteria: accuracy, technical control, and expression and interpretation.

GCSE Example: Applying Melody Writing in Performance and Composition

In a GCSE composition, a student who writes an opening idea, develops it through sequence and contrast, and returns to the original material in a transformed version demonstrates the structural coherence that the AQA mark scheme rewards.

Exam Technique for Melody Writing Questions

In the AQA GCSE Music listening exam, questions about melody writing typically ask you to: identify features by ear; describe how they are used; explain their effect; compare features between extracts. The key to success is precise terminology — never use vague language.

For 8-mark extended writing questions, use PEEL paragraphs: Point (identify the feature), Evidence (describe the specific moment), Explanation (explain how it creates its effect), Link (connect to the overall character of the piece).

Common mistakes: using non-musical language instead of terminology; failing to reference specific moments; describing features without explaining their effect.

GCSE Example: Exam Technique for Melody Writing Questions

A model exam answer: ‘The extract uses [specific feature] at [specific moment]. This creates [specific effect] because [explanation]. This contributes to the overall character by [linking to the piece’s expressive purpose].’
