Melody Writing

Writing melodies for composition; conjunct and disjunct movement; phrase structure (question-answer, antecedent-consequent); melodic development (sequence, inversion, augmentation, fragmentation); choosing effective rhythms; matching melody to harmony; genre-specific melodic conventions; using motifs; staff notation requirements.

Key Fact: Strong melodies have clear phrase structure — typically 4-bar or 8-bar phrases with a sense of question (antecedent) and answer (consequent).
Key Fact: Develop melodies through: sequence (repeating at a different pitch), inversion (turning upside down), augmentation (stretching), fragmentation (using part of the idea).
Key Fact: Match rhythm to genre: rock riffs use syncopation and repetition; Classical melodies use balanced phrasing; jazz melodies use swing and blue notes.
Key Fact: Staff notation requirements: you must submit a score in staff notation (up to 4 sharps/flats) — notation quality affects the AO2 mark for technical control.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Melody Writing, you must know:

  • Topic-specific musical terminology and vocabulary
  • How to identify and describe relevant musical elements
  • How to analyse music using appropriate terminology
  • How to apply this knowledge to performance, composition and listening
  • Relevant contextual information about genres, styles and composers

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe how melody writing are used in a piece of music you have studied. Use musical vocabulary.

Q2: Compare how two different pieces use melody writing. Explain the effect in each case.

Q3: How could you use melody writing in your own composition or performance? Explain your choices.

✅ Answers

  1. A detailed description using precise musical vocabulary (e.g. specific terms from the AQA elements list), referencing a named piece with accurate observations about how the elements are used and their effect.
  2. A comparison of two pieces identifying similarities and differences in how melody writing are used, explaining the different effects created in each musical context, using comparative language.
  3. A practical application showing understanding of how melody writing can be manipulated in composition or performance, with specific examples of choices made and their intended effect.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use PRECISE musical vocabulary — not vague descriptions like it sounds nice
  • Reference SPECIFIC musical elements when analysing any piece
  • For listening questions: identify elements FIRST, then explain their effect
  • For study pieces: know the context, structure and key features in detail
  • For performing: focus on accuracy, expression and interpretation
  • For composing: show development of ideas, not just repetition

📝 Exam Technique

Music Exam Tips:
Use balanced phrasing (4+4 bars). Apply development techniques (sequence, inversion). Match style to genre. Notate accurately. Annotate your decisions.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: A good melody just means something catchy that you like. Correct: Effective melody writing uses specific TECHNIQUES: balanced phrasing, motivic development (sequence, inversion, augmentation), appropriate rhythmic character for the genre, and coherent harmonic relationship. The exam assesses these technical skills, not whether the melody is catchy.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Compose an 8-bar melody in a specific style. Annotate your composition explaining the melodic techniques you used and how they serve the intended style.

A grade 9 response will: show balanced phrase structure; use at least two development techniques; match melodic style to genre; notate accurately in staff notation; annotate with precise musical vocabulary; explain how melodic choices create the intended character.

Mark scheme: AO3 (analyse and evaluate) + AO4 (knowledge and understanding) = total marks for listening; AO1 (perform) + AO2 (compose) for coursework components

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Music has three components: Understanding Music (40% — written exam with listening and study-piece questions), Performing Music (30% — one solo and one ensemble performance), Composing Music (30% — one free composition and one to a brief). The four Assessment Objectives are: AO1 Perform with technical control, expression and interpretation (30%); AO2 Compose and develop musical ideas with technical control and coherence (30%); AO3 Analyse and evaluate music using musical vocabulary (20%); AO4 Demonstrate knowledge and understanding of musical elements, contexts and language (20%). For grade 9, you must show exceptional technical control, creative and coherent composition, perceptive analysis using precise terminology, and sophisticated understanding of how musical elements create meaning in context.
