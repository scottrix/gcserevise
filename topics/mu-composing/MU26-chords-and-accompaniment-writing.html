Chords and Accompaniment Writing

Writing chord progressions and accompaniments; major and minor chords; dominant sevenths; chord symbols; stock progressions (I-V-vi-IV, 12-bar blues); Alberti bass; broken chord patterns; riff-based accompaniment; pedal notes; drone; harmonic rhythm; matching harmony to genre.

Key Fact: Common pop progressions: I-V-vi-IV (four-chord loop), I-IV-V (rock/blues), ii-V-I (jazz cadence) — each creates a different harmonic character.
Key Fact: Accompaniment styles define genre: Alberti bass (Classical), riff (rock/pop), broken chords (Romantic), ostinato (minimalist), pedal/drone (folk/traditional).
Key Fact: Harmonic rhythm (how often chords change) affects momentum: fast harmonic rhythm creates drive; slow harmonic rhythm creates stasis and calm.
Key Fact: Dominant seventh chords (V7) create tension that resolves to the tonic — they are essential for cadences and key-defining progressions.

✅ Answers

  1. A detailed description using precise musical vocabulary (e.g. specific terms from the AQA elements list), referencing a named piece with accurate observations about how the elements are used and their effect.
  2. A comparison of two pieces identifying similarities and differences in how chords and accompaniment writing are used, explaining the different effects created in each musical context, using comparative language.
  3. A practical application showing understanding of how chords and accompaniment writing can be manipulated in composition or performance, with specific examples of choices made and their intended effect.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use PRECISE musical vocabulary — not vague descriptions like it sounds nice
  • Reference SPECIFIC musical elements when analysing any piece
  • For listening questions: identify elements FIRST, then explain their effect
  • For study pieces: know the context, structure and key features in detail
  • For performing: focus on accuracy, expression and interpretation
  • For composing: show development of ideas, not just repetition

📝 Exam Technique

Music Exam Tips:
Choose genre-appropriate progressions. Match accompaniment style to genre. Use cadences intentionally. Notate chord symbols. Annotate harmonic decisions.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: You just put chords over the melody and it sounds fine. Correct: Chord choices and accompaniment patterns must be INTENTIONAL and GENRE-APPROPRIATE. Classical styles use functional harmony with cadences; popular styles use stock progressions and riffs; folk styles use drones and modal harmony. The examiner assesses whether your harmonic choices serve your compositional intention.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Compose a chord progression and accompaniment pattern for a melody you have written. Annotate explaining your harmonic choices and how the accompaniment style serves the genre.

A grade 9 response will: use genre-appropriate chord progressions; choose accompaniment patterns that match the style; show understanding of cadences; use chord symbols correctly; annotate with precise harmonic vocabulary; demonstrate coherent harmonic direction.

Mark scheme: AO3 (analyse and evaluate) + AO4 (knowledge and understanding) = total marks for listening; AO1 (perform) + AO2 (compose) for coursework components

Detailed Notes

Understanding Chords and Accompaniment Writing

Chords and Accompaniment Writing is a key element of GCSE Music requiring theoretical understanding and practical application. The AQA specification requires you to identify, describe and analyse this element in set works and unfamiliar music. Focus on the specific AQA terminology — examiners reward precise vocabulary over vague descriptions. Understanding how chords and accompaniment writing contributes to the overall character and effect of a piece is essential for achieving high marks in the listening exam.

In your GCSE listening exam, you may be asked to describe how chords and accompaniment writing is used in an extract. Use precise terminology, describe specific features you hear, and explain the effect of these features on the listener. Always link technical description to expressive effect.

Context is important: chords and accompaniment writing in Baroque music serves different expressive purposes than in Romantic or popular music. Understanding how chords and accompaniment writing functions within different historical periods and genres allows you to make perceptive analytical observations.

GCSE Example: Understanding Chords and Accompaniment Writing

A Grade 9 exam answer about chords and accompaniment writing identifies specific features using AQA terminology, describes how they are used with reference to specific moments, and explains the effect on the character of the music — connecting technical observation to expressive meaning.

Applying Chords and Accompaniment Writing in Performance and Composition

Understanding chords and accompaniment writing informs your performing (AO1) and composing (AO2) work. In performance, awareness of chords and accompaniment writing helps you make interpretive decisions that serve the music. In composition, chords and accompaniment writing provides the building blocks of your creative work — the choices you make about this element shape the identity and coherence of your piece.

For composition, consider how your use of chords and accompaniment writing creates structure and development. The AQA mark scheme specifically rewards compositions that demonstrate development and intentionality — a piece that shows clear initial ideas, systematic development, and structural coherence will score highly.

When preparing for your solo and ensemble performances, consider how your handling of chords and accompaniment writing contributes to the assessment criteria: accuracy, technical control, and expression and interpretation.

GCSE Example: Applying Chords and Accompaniment Writing in Performance and Composition

In a GCSE composition, a student who writes an opening idea, develops it through sequence and contrast, and returns to the original material in a transformed version demonstrates the structural coherence that the AQA mark scheme rewards.

Exam Technique for Chords and Accompaniment Writing Questions

In the AQA GCSE Music listening exam, questions about chords and accompaniment writing typically ask you to: identify features by ear; describe how they are used; explain their effect; compare features between extracts. The key to success is precise terminology — never use vague language.

For 8-mark extended writing questions, use PEEL paragraphs: Point (identify the feature), Evidence (describe the specific moment), Explanation (explain how it creates its effect), Link (connect to the overall character of the piece).

Common mistakes: using non-musical language instead of terminology; failing to reference specific moments; describing features without explaining their effect.

GCSE Example: Exam Technique for Chords and Accompaniment Writing Questions

A model exam answer: ‘The extract uses [specific feature] at [specific moment]. This creates [specific effect] because [explanation]. This contributes to the overall character by [linking to the piece’s expressive purpose].’
