Free Composition and Composing to a Brief

Free composition (own choice) and composition to a brief; AQA composition briefs; interpreting a brief; choosing a title or stimulus; minimum 1 minute duration; staff notation score; recording; annotation; AO2 criteria; technical control; coherence; creative development; submission requirements.

Key Fact: Free composition allows you to choose your own starting point — a melody, chord progression, rhythm or concept that inspires you.
Key Fact: Composing to a brief requires interpreting AQA's stimulus: read carefully, identify the musical implications, plan how you will respond to each element.
Key Fact: Both compositions must be at least 1 minute each — the score must be in staff notation (up to 4 sharps/flats) with a recording.
Key Fact: AO2 marks: technical control (accuracy of notation, appropriate writing for instruments), coherence (logical structure, development), creative development (inventive use of elements).

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Free Composition and Composing to a Brief, you must know:

  • Topic-specific musical terminology and vocabulary
  • How to identify and describe relevant musical elements
  • How to analyse music using appropriate terminology
  • How to apply this knowledge to performance, composition and listening
  • Relevant contextual information about genres, styles and composers

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe how free composition and composing to a brief are used in a piece of music you have studied. Use musical vocabulary.

Q2: Compare how two different pieces use free composition and composing to a brief. Explain the effect in each case.

Q3: How could you use free composition and composing to a brief in your own composition or performance? Explain your choices.

✅ Answers

  1. A detailed description using precise musical vocabulary (e.g. specific terms from the AQA elements list), referencing a named piece with accurate observations about how the elements are used and their effect.
  2. A comparison of two pieces identifying similarities and differences in how free composition and composing to a brief are used, explaining the different effects created in each musical context, using comparative language.
  3. A practical application showing understanding of how free composition and composing to a brief can be manipulated in composition or performance, with specific examples of choices made and their intended effect.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use PRECISE musical vocabulary — not vague descriptions like it sounds nice
  • Reference SPECIFIC musical elements when analysing any piece
  • For listening questions: identify elements FIRST, then explain their effect
  • For study pieces: know the context, structure and key features in detail
  • For performing: focus on accuracy, expression and interpretation
  • For composing: show development of ideas, not just repetition

📝 Exam Technique

Music Exam Tips:
Address EVERY element of the brief. Plan structure and development. Reference AoS influences. Meet notation requirements. Understand AO2 criteria.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The composition brief is just a suggestion — you can write whatever you want. Correct: You MUST respond directly to the brief's requirements — examiners check that your composition addresses each element of the stimulus. Ignoring the brief means losing marks regardless of the music's quality. Read the brief carefully and plan your response to each specified element.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Choose an AQA composition brief and plan your response. Explain how your composition will address each element of the brief, outline your structure, and describe the musical elements you will use.

A grade 9 response will: address every element of the brief explicitly; plan a coherent structure; explain creative choices with precise vocabulary; show understanding of AO2 criteria; demonstrate how AoS knowledge influences the composition; address notation and recording requirements.

Mark scheme: AO3 (analyse and evaluate) + AO4 (knowledge and understanding) = total marks for listening; AO1 (perform) + AO2 (compose) for coursework components

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Music has three components: Understanding Music (40% — written exam with listening and study-piece questions), Performing Music (30% — one solo and one ensemble performance), Composing Music (30% — one free composition and one to a brief). The four Assessment Objectives are: AO1 Perform with technical control, expression and interpretation (30%); AO2 Compose and develop musical ideas with technical control and coherence (30%); AO3 Analyse and evaluate music using musical vocabulary (20%); AO4 Demonstrate knowledge and understanding of musical elements, contexts and language (20%). For grade 9, you must show exceptional technical control, creative and coherent composition, perceptive analysis using precise terminology, and sophisticated understanding of how musical elements create meaning in context.
