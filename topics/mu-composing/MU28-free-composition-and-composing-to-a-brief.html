Key Fact: Free composition allows you to choose your own starting point — a melody, chord progression, rhythm or concept that inspires you.
Key Fact: Composing to a brief requires interpreting AQA's stimulus: read carefully, identify the musical implications, plan how you will respond to each element.
Key Fact: Both compositions must be at least 1 minute each — the score must be in staff notation (up to 4 sharps/flats) with a recording.
Key Fact: AO2 marks: technical control (accuracy of notation, appropriate writing for instruments), coherence (logical structure, development), creative development (inventive use of elements).

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The composition brief is just a suggestion — you can write whatever you want. Correct: You MUST respond directly to the brief's requirements — examiners check that your composition addresses each element of the stimulus. Ignoring the brief means losing marks regardless of the music's quality. Read the brief carefully and plan your response to each specified element.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Music has three components: Understanding Music (40% — written exam with listening and study-piece questions), Performing Music (30% — one solo and one ensemble performance), Composing Music (30% — one free composition and one to a brief). The four Assessment Objectives are: AO1 Perform with technical control, expression and interpretation (30%); AO2 Compose and develop musical ideas with technical control and coherence (30%); AO3 Analyse and evaluate music using musical vocabulary (20%); AO4 Demonstrate knowledge and understanding of musical elements, contexts and language (20%). For grade 9, you must show exceptional technical control, creative and coherent composition, perceptive analysis using precise terminology, and sophisticated understanding of how musical elements create meaning in context.

Free Composition and Composing to a Brief is a key element of GCSE Music requiring theoretical understanding and practical application. Exam Technique for Free Composition and Composing to a Brief Questions

In the AQA GCSE Music listening exam, questions about free composition and composing to a brief typically ask you to: identify features by ear; describe how they are used; explain their effect; compare features between extracts. The key to success is precise terminology — never use vague language.

For 8-mark extended writing questions, use PEEL paragraphs: Point (identify the feature), Evidence (describe the specific moment), Explanation (explain how it creates its effect), Link (connect to the overall character of the piece).

Common mistakes: using non-musical language instead of terminology; failing to reference specific moments; describing features without explaining their effect.
