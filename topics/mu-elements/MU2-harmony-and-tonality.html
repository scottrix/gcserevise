MU2: Harmony and Tonality

Foundation Higher All Boards

Diatonic and chromatic harmony; consonance and dissonance; pedal and drone; cadences (perfect, plagal, imperfect, interrupted, tierce de Picardie); major/minor keys and key signatures to 4 sharps/flats; modulation to dominant, subdominant, relative keys; chord identification using symbols and Roman numerals.

Harmony and Tonality

Diatonic and chromatic harmony; consonance and dissonance; pedal and drone; cadences (perfect, plagal, imperfect, interrupted, tierce de Picardie); major/minor keys and key signatures to 4 sharps/flats; modulation to dominant, subdominant, relative keys; chord identification using symbols and Roman numerals.

Key Fact: Consonant harmony sounds stable and restful; dissonant harmony sounds tense and unresolved — both are deliberate compositional choices.
Key Fact: Cadences are harmonic endings: perfect (V-I, final), plagal (IV-I, amen), imperfect (I-V or IV-V, unfinished), interrupted (V-vi, surprise).
Key Fact: Modulation changes the key centre: to the dominant (5th scale degree), subdominant (4th), or relative major/minor — each creates a different shift of mood.
Key Fact: Pedal notes are sustained or repeated while harmony changes above them — dominant pedals build tension; tonic pedals create stability.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Harmony and Tonality, you must know:

  • Topic-specific musical terminology and vocabulary
  • How to identify and describe relevant musical elements
  • How to analyse music using appropriate terminology
  • How to apply this knowledge to performance, composition and listening
  • Relevant contextual information about genres, styles and composers

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe how harmony and tonality are used in a piece of music you have studied. Use musical vocabulary.

Q2: Compare how two different pieces use harmony and tonality. Explain the effect in each case.

Q3: How could you use harmony and tonality in your own composition or performance? Explain your choices.

✅ Answers

  1. A detailed description using precise musical vocabulary (e.g. specific terms from the AQA elements list), referencing a named piece with accurate observations about how the elements are used and their effect.
  2. A comparison of two pieces identifying similarities and differences in how harmony and tonality are used, explaining the different effects created in each musical context, using comparative language.
  3. A practical application showing understanding of how harmony and tonality can be manipulated in composition or performance, with specific examples of choices made and their intended effect.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use PRECISE musical vocabulary — not vague descriptions like it sounds nice
  • Reference SPECIFIC musical elements when analysing any piece
  • For listening questions: identify elements FIRST, then explain their effect
  • For study pieces: know the context, structure and key features in detail
  • For performing: focus on accuracy, expression and interpretation
  • For composing: show development of ideas, not just repetition

📝 Exam Technique

Music Exam Tips:
Name cadences by TYPE (perfect, plagal, imperfect, interrupted). Use Roman numerals for chord analysis. Identify modulations and their destination keys. Explain the emotional effect of harmonic choices.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: A cadence is just the end of a piece. Correct: A cadence is a harmonic PROGRESSION at the end of a phrase, not necessarily the end of a piece. There are four main types (perfect, plagal, imperfect, interrupted) each with a distinct sound and function. Identifying cadences by TYPE is essential for the exam.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Identify and name the cadences in a piece you have studied. Explain how each cadence contributes to the structure and emotional direction of the music.

A grade 9 response will: name cadences by type using Roman numeral analysis; explain the emotional effect of each cadence; show understanding of how cadences shape phrase structure; identify any modulation and its type; use precise harmonic vocabulary throughout.

Mark scheme: AO3 (analyse and evaluate) + AO4 (knowledge and understanding) = total marks for listening; AO1 (perform) + AO2 (compose) for coursework components

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Music has three components: Understanding Music (40% — written exam with listening and study-piece questions), Performing Music (30% — one solo and one ensemble performance), Composing Music (30% — one free composition and one to a brief). The four Assessment Objectives are: AO1 Perform with technical control, expression and interpretation (30%); AO2 Compose and develop musical ideas with technical control and coherence (30%); AO3 Analyse and evaluate music using musical vocabulary (20%); AO4 Demonstrate knowledge and understanding of musical elements, contexts and language (20%). For grade 9, you must show exceptional technical control, creative and coherent composition, perceptive analysis using precise terminology, and sophisticated understanding of how musical elements create meaning in context.
