MU2: Harmony and Tonality

Foundation Higher All Boards

Diatonic and chromatic harmony; consonance and dissonance; pedal and drone; cadences (perfect, plagal, imperfect, interrupted, tierce de Picardie); major/minor keys and key signatures to 4 sharps/flats; modulation to dominant, subdominant, relative keys; chord identification using symbols and Roman numerals.

Harmony and Tonality

Diatonic and chromatic harmony; consonance and dissonance; pedal and drone; cadences (perfect, plagal, imperfect, interrupted, tierce de Picardie); major/minor keys and key signatures to 4 sharps/flats; modulation to dominant, subdominant, relative keys; chord identification using symbols and Roman numerals.

Key Fact: Consonant harmony sounds stable and restful; dissonant harmony sounds tense and unresolved — both are deliberate compositional choices.
Key Fact: Cadences are harmonic endings: perfect (V-I, final), plagal (IV-I, amen), imperfect (I-V or IV-V, unfinished), interrupted (V-vi, surprise).
Key Fact: Modulation changes the key centre: to the dominant (5th scale degree), subdominant (4th), or relative major/minor — each creates a different shift of mood.
Key Fact: Pedal notes are sustained or repeated while harmony changes above them — dominant pedals build tension; tonic pedals create stability.

📝 Exam Technique

Music Exam Tips:
Name cadences by TYPE (perfect, plagal, imperfect, interrupted). Use Roman numerals for chord analysis. Identify modulations and their destination keys. Explain the emotional effect of harmonic choices.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: A cadence is just the end of a piece. Correct: A cadence is a harmonic PROGRESSION at the end of a phrase, not necessarily the end of a piece. There are four main types (perfect, plagal, imperfect, interrupted) each with a distinct sound and function. Identifying cadences by TYPE is essential for the exam.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Identify and name the cadences in a piece you have studied. Explain how each cadence contributes to the structure and emotional direction of the music.

A grade 9 response will: name cadences by type using Roman numeral analysis; explain the emotional effect of each cadence; show understanding of how cadences shape phrase structure; identify any modulation and its type; use precise harmonic vocabulary throughout.

Mark scheme: AO3 (analyse and evaluate) + AO4 (knowledge and understanding) = total marks for listening; AO1 (perform) + AO2 (compose) for coursework components

Detailed Notes

Understanding Harmony and Tonality

Harmony and Tonality is a key element of GCSE Music requiring theoretical understanding and practical application. The AQA specification requires you to identify, describe and analyse this element in set works and unfamiliar music. Focus on the specific AQA terminology — examiners reward precise vocabulary over vague descriptions. Understanding how harmony and tonality contributes to the overall character and effect of a piece is essential for achieving high marks in the listening exam.

In your GCSE listening exam, you may be asked to describe how harmony and tonality is used in an extract. Use precise terminology, describe specific features you hear, and explain the effect of these features on the listener. Always link technical description to expressive effect.

Context is important: harmony and tonality in Baroque music serves different expressive purposes than in Romantic or popular music. Understanding how harmony and tonality functions within different historical periods and genres allows you to make perceptive analytical observations.

GCSE Example: Understanding Harmony and Tonality

A Grade 9 exam answer about harmony and tonality identifies specific features using AQA terminology, describes how they are used with reference to specific moments, and explains the effect on the character of the music — connecting technical observation to expressive meaning.

Applying Harmony and Tonality in Performance and Composition

Understanding harmony and tonality informs your performing (AO1) and composing (AO2) work. In performance, awareness of harmony and tonality helps you make interpretive decisions that serve the music. In composition, harmony and tonality provides the building blocks of your creative work — the choices you make about this element shape the identity and coherence of your piece.

For composition, consider how your use of harmony and tonality creates structure and development. The AQA mark scheme specifically rewards compositions that demonstrate development and intentionality — a piece that shows clear initial ideas, systematic development, and structural coherence will score highly.

When preparing for your solo and ensemble performances, consider how your handling of harmony and tonality contributes to the assessment criteria: accuracy, technical control, and expression and interpretation.

GCSE Example: Applying Harmony and Tonality in Performance and Composition

In a GCSE composition, a student who writes an opening idea, develops it through sequence and contrast, and returns to the original material in a transformed version demonstrates the structural coherence that the AQA mark scheme rewards.

Exam Technique for Harmony and Tonality Questions

In the AQA GCSE Music listening exam, questions about harmony and tonality typically ask you to: identify features by ear; describe how they are used; explain their effect; compare features between extracts. The key to success is precise terminology — never use vague language.

For 8-mark extended writing questions, use PEEL paragraphs: Point (identify the feature), Evidence (describe the specific moment), Explanation (explain how it creates its effect), Link (connect to the overall character of the piece).

Common mistakes: using non-musical language instead of terminology; failing to reference specific moments; describing features without explaining their effect.

GCSE Example: Exam Technique for Harmony and Tonality Questions

A model exam answer: ‘The extract uses [specific feature] at [specific moment]. This creates [specific effect] because [explanation]. This contributes to the overall character by [linking to the piece’s expressive purpose].’
