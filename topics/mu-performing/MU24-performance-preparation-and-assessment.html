Performance Preparation and Assessment

Preparing performances for assessment; recording requirements; submission deadlines; self-evaluation; dealing with performance anxiety; performing with accompaniment; selecting your best performance; NEA administration; authentication; teacher marking and moderation; what examiners listen for.

Key Fact: You may record multiple takes and submit your BEST performance — but each piece must be a single continuous take, not edited together.
Key Fact: Accompaniment can be live or backing track — the accompaniment level must not dominate your performance. Choose appropriate accompaniment for your genre.
Key Fact: Performance anxiety is normal — manage it through: thorough preparation, slow breathing, positive visualisation, and performing to others before the recording.
Key Fact: The teacher marks your performance against AQA criteria, then moderation adjusts the standard — consistency across the cohort is checked.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Performance Preparation and Assessment, you must know:

  • Topic-specific musical terminology and vocabulary
  • How to identify and describe relevant musical elements
  • How to analyse music using appropriate terminology
  • How to apply this knowledge to performance, composition and listening
  • Relevant contextual information about genres, styles and composers

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe how performance preparation and assessment are used in a piece of music you have studied. Use musical vocabulary.

Q2: Compare how two different pieces use performance preparation and assessment. Explain the effect in each case.

Q3: How could you use performance preparation and assessment in your own composition or performance? Explain your choices.

✅ Answers

  1. A detailed description using precise musical vocabulary (e.g. specific terms from the AQA elements list), referencing a named piece with accurate observations about how the elements are used and their effect.
  2. A comparison of two pieces identifying similarities and differences in how performance preparation and assessment are used, explaining the different effects created in each musical context, using comparative language.
  3. A practical application showing understanding of how performance preparation and assessment can be manipulated in composition or performance, with specific examples of choices made and their intended effect.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always use PRECISE musical vocabulary — not vague descriptions like it sounds nice
  • Reference SPECIFIC musical elements when analysing any piece
  • For listening questions: identify elements FIRST, then explain their effect
  • For study pieces: know the context, structure and key features in detail
  • For performing: focus on accuracy, expression and interpretation
  • For composing: show development of ideas, not just repetition

📝 Exam Technique

Music Exam Tips:
Plan a realistic timeline. Address performance anxiety. Know recording rules (single continuous take, no editing). Submit your BEST complete take.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: You can edit your recording to fix mistakes. Correct: Each performance must be a SINGLE CONTINUOUS TAKE — no editing, splicing or patching is allowed. You may record multiple complete takes and submit the best one, but the submitted recording must be one unbroken performance. Editing invalidates the submission.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Create a preparation timeline for your performing component, from initial repertoire choice through to final recording. Include strategies for dealing with performance anxiety and ensuring your best performance on the day.

A grade 9 response will: create a realistic, detailed timeline; include specific practice strategies for each stage; address performance anxiety with practical techniques; explain recording and submission requirements; demonstrate understanding of the NEA administration process.

Mark scheme: AO3 (analyse and evaluate) + AO4 (knowledge and understanding) = total marks for listening; AO1 (perform) + AO2 (compose) for coursework components

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Music has three components: Understanding Music (40% — written exam with listening and study-piece questions), Performing Music (30% — one solo and one ensemble performance), Composing Music (30% — one free composition and one to a brief). The four Assessment Objectives are: AO1 Perform with technical control, expression and interpretation (30%); AO2 Compose and develop musical ideas with technical control and coherence (30%); AO3 Analyse and evaluate music using musical vocabulary (20%); AO4 Demonstrate knowledge and understanding of musical elements, contexts and language (20%). For grade 9, you must show exceptional technical control, creative and coherent composition, perceptive analysis using precise terminology, and sophisticated understanding of how musical elements create meaning in context.
