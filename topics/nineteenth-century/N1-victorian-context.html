N1: Victorian Context

Foundation Higher All Boards AO3

Understanding the Victorian era and its influence on literature - the world of Dickens, Stevenson, and Bronte.

🏭 Industrial Revolution

Transformation of Britain

The Industrial Revolution (1760-1840s) fundamentally changed British society:

  • Urbanisation: People moved from countryside to cities
  • Factories: Mass production replaced cottage industries
  • Pollution: Cities became overcrowded and dirty
  • Working conditions: Long hours, child labour, dangerous machinery
In Literature

"A Christmas Carol" - Dickens critiques industrial capitalism through Scrooge, a businessman who cares only for profit. The Cratchits represent the deserving poor, exploited by factory owners.

Social Consequences

Change Effect Literary Reflection
Urban poverty Overcrowding, disease, crime Dickens's London slums
Child labour Children in factories, mines "A Christmas Carol" - Tiny Tim
Class division Rich/poor gap widened Scrooge vs Cratchit
Loss of community Anonymity in cities Stevenson's anonymous London

👥 Class Structure

Victorian Class Hierarchy

Class Who Characteristics
Upper Aristocracy, landowners Wealth, inherited status, political power
Middle Factory owners, professionals New money, respectability, morality
Working Factory workers, servants Poverty, hard labour, no vote
Underclass Criminals, beggars Desperate, invisible to society

Middle-Class Values

The rising middle class promoted:

  • Respectability: Proper behaviour, reputation mattered
  • Hard work: Self-made man ideal
  • Morality: Religion, charity, duty
  • Family: Domestic sphere sacred
Class in "Jekyll and Hyde": Jekyll is respectable middle-class professional; Hyde represents the underclass he secretly joins. Class boundaries are both rigid and permeable.

🎭 Gender Roles

The Separate Spheres

Victorian ideology divided life into separate spheres:

Men Women
Public sphere - work, politics, money Private sphere - home, children, morality
Rational, strong, active Emotional, delicate, passive
Provider Angel of the house

Restrictions on Women

  • Could not vote
  • Could not own property (until Married Women's Property Act 1870)
  • Economic dependence on father or husband
  • Expected to be pure, submissive, domestic
Women in Literature

Victorian novels often feature women who challenge or are confined by gender roles. Jane Eyre asserts independence; Bertha Mason represents the "madwoman" who cannot be controlled.

⛪ Religion and Science

The Victorian Crisis of Faith

The 19th century saw a major conflict between religion and science:

Religious View Scientific Challenge
Bible as literal truth Geology showed earth older than Bible suggested
God created species fixed and unchanging Darwin's evolution (1859) - species change over time
Humans special, created by God Humans evolved from apes - not so special after all
Moral certainty from religion Science offered new explanations, undermining faith

Darwin's Influence

Charles Darwin's "On the Origin of Species" (1859) transformed thought:

  • Evolution: Species evolve through natural selection
  • Survival of fittest: Only adapted survive
  • Human origins: Humans part of nature, not above it
  • Challenge to faith: Undermined literal Bible interpretation
Science vs Religion in "Jekyll and Hyde"

Jekyll is a scientist who uses chemistry to transform. His experiments represent Victorian fears about science going too far. The "dual nature" of man reflects Darwin's evolutionary ideas - humans have primitive instincts beneath civilized surface.

🌙 Gothic and the Supernatural

Victorian Gothic

Despite scientific progress, Victorians were fascinated by the supernatural:

  • Ghosts and hauntings - spiritualism was popular
  • Doubles and duality - fascination with split personality
  • Madness and degeneration - fear of mental illness
  • Horror and the grotesque - darker side of respectability

Gothic Conventions in 19th Century Novels

Element Function Example
Dark settings Create atmosphere of fear Foggy London streets, old houses
Supernatural Challenge rationalism Hyde's transformation
Secrets What's hidden behind respectability Jekyll's hidden lab
Duality Public/private self Jekyll and Hyde

📖 Serialised Publication

How Novels Were Published

Many Victorian novels were serialised:

  • Published in installments (monthly or weekly)
  • In magazines or as standalone pamphlets
  • Each installment needed a hook for next
  • Readers had to wait between parts
Effect on Structure: Dickens's novels have cliffhangers at chapter ends because they were originally published in parts. Each installment needed to keep readers hooked.

Cliffhangers and Suspense

Serialisation created:

  • Cliffhanger chapter endings
  • Memorable characters readers cared about
  • Plot twists to generate discussion
  • Cliffhangers resolved in next installment

✍️ Using Victorian Context

Link to Specific Novels

  • Social responsibility: Message of charity and reform
"Jekyll and Hyde"
  • Science vs religion: Jekyll's experiments

  • Evolution: Hyde as primitive, regressive

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: How did the Industrial Revolution affect Victorian literature?

Q2: What was the Victorian "crisis of faith"?

Q3: Why were Victorians fascinated by Gothic horror?

Q4: How did serialised publication affect novel structure?

✅ Answers

  1. Industrial Revolution created urban poverty, class division, and working conditions that novelists critiqued. Dickens exposed poverty; Shelley questioned science; Stevenson explored repression.
  2. Darwin's evolution theory challenged Biblical creation. Geology showed earth was older than Bible suggested. Science undermined religious certainty, creating doubt and debate.
  3. Despite scientific progress, Victorians feared what progress might unleash. Gothic expressed anxieties about science, degeneration, and the dark side of respectability. Hyde represents primitive urges beneath civilized surface.
  4. Serialised novels needed cliffhangers to keep readers buying installments. Each chapter had to hook readers for the next. This created suspenseful, episodic structure.
