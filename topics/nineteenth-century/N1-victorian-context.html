N1: Victorian Context

Foundation Higher All Boards AO3

Understanding the Victorian era and its influence on literature - the world of Dickens, Stevenson, and Bronte.

🏭 Industrial Revolution

Transformation of Britain

The Industrial Revolution (1760-1840s) fundamentally changed British society:

  • Urbanisation: People moved from countryside to cities
  • Factories: Mass production replaced cottage industries
  • Pollution: Cities became overcrowded and dirty
  • Working conditions: Long hours, child labour, dangerous machinery
In Literature

"A Christmas Carol" - Dickens critiques industrial capitalism through Scrooge, a businessman who cares only for profit. The Cratchits represent the deserving poor, exploited by factory owners.

Social Consequences

Change Effect Literary Reflection
Urban poverty Overcrowding, disease, crime Dickens's London slums
Child labour Children in factories, mines "A Christmas Carol" - Tiny Tim
Class division Rich/poor gap widened Scrooge vs Cratchit
Loss of community Anonymity in cities Stevenson's anonymous London

👥 Class Structure

Victorian Class Hierarchy

Class Who Characteristics
Upper Aristocracy, landowners Wealth, inherited status, political power
Middle Factory owners, professionals New money, respectability, morality
Working Factory workers, servants Poverty, hard labour, no vote
Underclass Criminals, beggars Desperate, invisible to society

Middle-Class Values

The rising middle class promoted:

  • Respectability: Proper behaviour, reputation mattered
  • Hard work: Self-made man ideal
  • Morality: Religion, charity, duty
  • Family: Domestic sphere sacred
Class in "Jekyll and Hyde": Jekyll is respectable middle-class professional; Hyde represents the underclass he secretly joins. Class boundaries are both rigid and permeable.

🎭 Gender Roles

The Separate Spheres

Victorian ideology divided life into separate spheres:

Men Women
Public sphere - work, politics, money Private sphere - home, children, morality
Rational, strong, active Emotional, delicate, passive
Provider Angel of the house

Restrictions on Women

  • Could not vote
  • Could not own property (until Married Women's Property Act 1870)
  • Economic dependence on father or husband
  • Expected to be pure, submissive, domestic
Women in Literature

Victorian novels often feature women who challenge or are confined by gender roles. Jane Eyre asserts independence; Bertha Mason represents the "madwoman" who cannot be controlled.

⛪ Religion and Science

The Victorian Crisis of Faith

The 19th century saw a major conflict between religion and science:

Religious View Scientific Challenge
Bible as literal truth Geology showed earth older than Bible suggested
God created species fixed and unchanging Darwin's evolution (1859) - species change over time
Humans special, created by God Humans evolved from apes - not so special after all
Moral certainty from religion Science offered new explanations, undermining faith

Darwin's Influence

Charles Darwin's "On the Origin of Species" (1859) transformed thought:

  • Evolution: Species evolve through natural selection
  • Survival of fittest: Only adapted survive
  • Human origins: Humans part of nature, not above it
  • Challenge to faith: Undermined literal Bible interpretation
Science vs Religion in "Jekyll and Hyde"

Jekyll is a scientist who uses chemistry to transform. His experiments represent Victorian fears about science going too far. The "dual nature" of man reflects Darwin's evolutionary ideas - humans have primitive instincts beneath civilized surface.

🌙 Gothic and the Supernatural

Victorian Gothic

Despite scientific progress, Victorians were fascinated by the supernatural:

  • Ghosts and hauntings - spiritualism was popular
  • Doubles and duality - fascination with split personality
  • Madness and degeneration - fear of mental illness
  • Horror and the grotesque - darker side of respectability

Gothic Conventions in 19th Century Novels

Element Function Example
Dark settings Create atmosphere of fear Foggy London streets, old houses
Supernatural Challenge rationalism Hyde's transformation
Secrets What's hidden behind respectability Jekyll's hidden lab
Duality Public/private self Jekyll and Hyde

📖 Serialised Publication

How Novels Were Published

Many Victorian novels were serialised:

  • Published in installments (monthly or weekly)
  • In magazines or as standalone pamphlets
  • Each installment needed a hook for next
  • Readers had to wait between parts
Effect on Structure: Dickens's novels have cliffhangers at chapter ends because they were originally published in parts. Each installment needed to keep readers hooked.

Cliffhangers and Suspense

Serialisation created:

  • Cliffhanger chapter endings
  • Memorable characters readers cared about
  • Plot twists to generate discussion
  • Cliffhangers resolved in next installment

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: How did the Industrial Revolution affect Victorian literature?

Q2: What was the Victorian "crisis of faith"?

Q3: Why were Victorians fascinated by Gothic horror?

Q4: How did serialised publication affect novel structure?

✅ Answers

  1. Industrial Revolution created urban poverty, class division, and working conditions that novelists critiqued. Dickens exposed poverty; Shelley questioned science; Stevenson explored repression.
  2. Darwin's evolution theory challenged Biblical creation. Geology showed earth was older than Bible suggested. Science undermined religious certainty, creating doubt and debate.
  3. Despite scientific progress, Victorians feared what progress might unleash. Gothic expressed anxieties about science, degeneration, and the dark side of respectability. Hyde represents primitive urges beneath civilized surface.
  4. Serialised novels needed cliffhangers to keep readers buying installments. Each chapter had to hook readers for the next. This created suspenseful, episodic structure.

📝 Exam Technique

AO3 Context Strategy: For the 19th century novel question, AO3 is worth 25% of marks (typically 8 out of 30 marks). Spend roughly 5-7 minutes of a 45-minute response on embedding context. Never bolt context on at the end — weave it into your argument. Examiners reward contextual points that illuminate the text, not historical facts listed for their own sake. A Grade 9 response treats context as the lens through which the text's meaning becomes clear, not a separate paragraph tagged on as an afterthought.

Time Management for Context Questions

StageTimeFocus
Planning5 minsIdentify 3-4 contextual points that directly link to the extract or whole-text question
Writing35 minsIntegrate context into each paragraph: point → quotation → method → contextual significance
Checking5 minsVerify every paragraph has contextual analysis, not just the introduction and conclusion

What Examiners Look For

  • Precision: Specific contextual knowledge (e.g. "the 1834 Poor Law" not "Victorian times")
  • Integration: Context woven into analysis, not dumped in a separate paragraph
  • Significance: Explaining why context matters for meaning, not just stating facts
  • Perceptive links: Showing how context deepens understanding of writer's choices

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often treat context as a separate section rather than weaving it into analysis. Wrong: Writing a paragraph of historical facts then a paragraph of textual analysis. Correct: Embedding context within each analytical paragraph so it explains why the writer made specific choices.

Students often use vague contextual references instead of precise ones. Wrong: "In Victorian times people were poor." Correct: "The 1834 Poor Law meant the destitute were forced into workhouses, a policy Dickens attacks through Scrooge's reference to 'the treadmill and the Poor Law.'"

Students often state context without explaining its significance to meaning. Wrong: "Darwin published his theory in 1859." Correct: "Darwin's theory of evolution (1859) unsettled Victorians by suggesting humans had primitive instincts beneath their civilised surface — precisely the anxiety Stevenson exploits through Hyde's 'troglodytic' appearance."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

30 marks: How does Dickens use the character of Scrooge to comment on Victorian attitudes to poverty? Write about: how Dickens presents Scrooge's views; how Dickens uses Scrooge to criticise Victorian society; the context of poverty in Victorian England.

Dickens constructs Scrooge as a personification of Malthusian economic thinking — the Victorian belief that poverty was inevitable and even necessary. When Scrooge declares that the poor should go to "the treadmill and the Poor Law," he echoes the language of the 1834 Poor Law Amendment Act, which made workhouse conditions deliberately harsh to deter reliance on state support. Dickens's contemporary readers would have recognised this as the dominant attitude of the middle classes, making Scrooge not an aberration but a representative figure. The specificity of Dickens's reference — naming actual legislation — transforms Scrooge from miserly caricature into political critique.

However, Dickens does not merely condemn; he shows how this ideology dehumanises its adherent. Scrooge's refusal to donate to charity is presented through the metaphor of cold: he is "hard and sharp as flint" and carries his "own low temperature" with him. This pathological coldness suggests that Victorian capitalist values do not merely harm the poor — they corrupt the wealthy themselves, stripping them of empathy and connection. When the Ghost of Christmas Present reveals the children Ignorance and Want beneath his robe, Dickens's allegory makes explicit what Malthusian economics denied: that poverty is not an abstract problem but a human crisis demanding moral response. The children's personification forces Scrooge — and the reader — to confront suffering directly rather than through the distorting lens of statistics and policy. Contextually, this was Dickens's explicit purpose; he wrote "A Christmas Carol" (1843) as a social pamphlet, describing it as "a sledgehammer" to bring down the "Ironmaster" of Malthusian economics. The novella's redemptive arc offers a model for Victorian social reform: individual moral transformation as the route to collective responsibility.

Mark scheme: AO1 (12/12) — sustained, perceptive argument with integrated quotations; AO2 (10/10) — analytical discussion of metaphor, personification, allegory and their effects; AO3 (8/8) — precise contextual knowledge (Malthusian economics, 1834 Poor Law, Dickens's purpose) woven into every paragraph to illuminate meaning.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO3 in Context Questions: AO3 requires understanding of the relationship between text and context. For the 19th century novel, this means demonstrating how historical, social, and cultural forces shaped the writer's choices and how contemporary readers would have responded.

Grade 5 approach: Identifies relevant context but treats it as background information. Typically writes: "In Victorian times..." with general historical facts. Context is present but not connected to the writer's methods or the text's meanings.

Grade 7 approach: Links context to specific textual moments. Writes: "Dickens references the Poor Law to show Scrooge's cruelty." Context explains what happens in the text, but analysis stays at the level of content rather than exploring why the writer made that choice or what it reveals about deeper attitudes.

Grade 9 approach: Uses context as an interpretive lens that unlocks meaning. Writes: "Dickens names actual legislation — the treadmill, the Poor Law — not merely to show Scrooge's cruelty but to expose how state policy normalised cruelty. Scrooge is not an outlier; he is Victorian respectability unmasked." This approach shows how context reveals the text's argument, how contemporary readers would have been positioned, and why the writer's choices are significant beyond the page. Grade 9 responses also acknowledge where context creates tension or complexity — for instance, noting that Dickens himself was a self-made man who embodied middle-class values even as he critiqued them.
