N2: Victorian Novel Conventions

Foundation Higher All Boards AO2

Understanding the conventions and techniques of Victorian novels - how they were written and why.

📚 Serialised Publication

The Victorian Publishing Model

Many Victorian novels were published in instalments rather than as complete books:

Format Description Example
Monthly instalments 20-32 pages, 2-3 chapters Dickens's novels
Weekly serials Shorter chapters, faster pace Magazines like Household Words
Three-volume novel "Triple-decker" format for libraries George Eliot's works
Effect on Structure: Serialisation required cliffhangers at chapter ends. Each instalment needed a hook to make readers buy the next. This creates episodic, suspenseful pacing.

Consequences of Serialisation

  • Cliffhangers: Each part ended with tension or mystery
  • Memorable characters: Readers needed to remember them between instalments
  • Reader feedback: Authors sometimes adjusted plots based on reactions
  • Pacing: Regular moments of tension and resolution
In "A Christmas Carol"

Though published as a complete book, Dickens's episodic style reflects his serial background. Each stave (chapter) has a clear focus: Marley's Ghost, the three spirits, and transformation.

👁️ Narrative Perspective

First-Person Narration

Many Victorian novels use first-person narration:

Narrator Type Effect Example
Protagonist Direct experience, limited knowledge Jane Eyre, Pip in Great Expectations
Observer Watching the main character Nick Carraway-type figures
Retrospective Adult looking back on childhood Pip narrating his past self

Unreliable Narrators

First-person narrators can be unreliable:

  • Limited knowledge: They don't know everything
  • Bias: Their perspective shapes the story
  • Memory: Retrospective narrators may misremember
  • Self-deception: They may lie to themselves
In "Great Expectations"

Pip narrates as an adult looking back. He can comment on his younger self's mistakes with mature understanding, creating dramatic irony - we see what young Pip cannot.

🎭 Realism and Social Commentary

Victorian Realism

Victorian novels typically aimed for realistic depiction of society:

  • Detailed settings: Specific places, often real locations
  • Social accuracy: Realistic depictions of class and work
  • Psychological depth: Complex, believable characters
  • Social problems: Poverty, crime, injustice explored

The Novel as Social Criticism

Author Social Focus Key Work
Dickens Poverty, workhouses, class inequality "Oliver Twist", "A Christmas Carol"
Bronte Gender, class, independence "Jane Eyre"
Stevenson Respectability, hypocrisy, science "Jekyll and Hyde"
Shelley Science, creation, responsibility "Frankenstein"
Moral Purpose: Victorian novels often had explicit moral messages. Dickens used fiction to campaign for social reform. Literature was seen as a way to educate and improve society.

📖 Gothic Elements

Gothic Conventions in Victorian Fiction

Many Victorian novels incorporate Gothic elements:

Gothic Element Victorian Use Example
Supernatural Often explained or ambiguous Hyde's transformation
Duality Public vs private self Jekyll and Hyde
Secrets Hidden pasts, locked rooms Bertha Mason in "Jane Eyre"
Atmosphere Fog, darkness, isolation London streets in "Jekyll and Hyde"
Fear What lurks beneath respectability Hyde as repressed desires
Gothic + Realism

Victorian Gothic often combines realistic settings with supernatural elements. "Jekyll and Hyde" is set in recognisable London but features impossible transformations. This makes the horror feel closer to home.

🏛️ Moral Purpose

Didactic Fiction

Victorian novels often taught moral lessons:

  • Good vs evil: Clear moral oppositions
  • Character arcs: Characters learn and change morally
  • Consequences: Actions have moral consequences
  • Redemption: Possibility of moral transformation

Moral Structure

Character Type Role Example
Moral guide Embodies virtue tiny Tim, Jane Eyre
Sinner Needs redemption Scrooge, Jekyll
Corruptor Embodies evil Hyde, Brocklehurst
Redeemed Transforms morally Scrooge at the end
Victorian Values: Novels promoted hard work, charity, self-improvement, and Christian morality. Characters who ignore these values face consequences.

📝 Language and Style

Victorian Prose Style

Victorian novels have distinctive language features:

Feature Description Example
Long sentences Complex, subordinate clauses Dickens's descriptive passages
Detailed description Extended character and setting descriptions Bronte's descriptions
Direct address Narrator speaks to reader "Dear reader..."
Dialect Regional speech for authenticity Joseph in "Wuthering Heights"

Descriptive Technique

Victorian authors used description to:

  • Establish atmosphere and mood
  • Reveal character through appearance
  • Create social commentary through settings
  • Slow or speed the narrative pace

🔑 Key Conventions Summary

Victorian Novel Formula

Structure: Episodic chapters + cliffhangers (from serialisation)

Narration: Often first-person, retrospective

Style: Realistic detail + moral purpose

Content: Social criticism + Gothic elements

Character: Complex individuals with moral arcs

✍️ Using Conventions in Essays

Good Analysis

"Stevenson subverts Victorian conventions by making his protagonist both hero and villain. Unlike Dickens's clear moral opposites (Scrooge vs Cratchit), Jekyll and Hyde are the same person, suggesting morality is more complex than Victorian society admitted."

Useful Phrases

  • "Typical of Victorian novels, this text..."
  • "Following the convention of serialisation..."
  • "The first-person narration creates..."
  • "Like many Victorian writers, [Author] uses fiction to..."

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: How did serialised publication affect Victorian novel structure?

Q2: Why did Victorian novels often have moral purposes?

Q3: What are the advantages of first-person narration?

Q4: How does "Jekyll and Hyde" combine realism and Gothic?

✅ Answers

  1. Serialised publication required cliffhangers to maintain reader interest between instalments. This created episodic structure with regular moments of tension. Chapters often end mid-action or with revelations.
  2. Victorians saw literature as educational. Novels were expected to teach moral lessons and promote social reform. Authors like Dickens used fiction to campaign against poverty and injustice.
  3. First-person narration creates intimacy with readers, shows events from a character's perspective, allows for unreliable narration and dramatic irony, and lets characters reflect on their own development.
  4. "Jekyll and Hyde" is set in realistic London with accurate social details (lawyers, servants, streets). But it includes Gothic elements: transformation, horror, duality, secrets. The realistic setting makes the Gothic more unsettling.
