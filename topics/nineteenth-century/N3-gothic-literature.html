N3: Gothic Literature

Foundation Higher All Boards AO2 AO3

Understanding Gothic conventions in 19th century texts - horror, duality and the supernatural.

🌑 What is Gothic Literature?

Origins and Definition

Gothic literature emerged in the 18th century:

  • Origin: Horace Walpole's "The Castle of Otranto" (1764)
  • Name: From medieval Gothic architecture - dark, ornate, mysterious
  • Purpose: To create fear, explore the irrational, challenge Enlightenment rationality
  • Peak: Flourished in the 19th century with Frankenstein, Jekyll and Hyde, Jane Eyre
Gothic Definition: A genre that combines horror, death, romance and the supernatural. It explores what lies beneath the surface of civilization - our fears, desires and secrets.

Why Victorians Loved Gothic

Despite scientific progress, Victorians were fascinated by Gothic because:

  • Repression: Strict morals forced desires underground - Gothic expressed what was hidden
  • Science fears: What if science goes too far? (Frankenstein, Jekyll)
  • Darwin: Evolution suggested humans have primitive instincts
  • Modernity: Gothic was an escape from industrial reality

😱 Horror and Terror

Two Types of Fear

Concept Description Example
Terror Anticipation of horror - dread, suspense, imagination Waiting for Hyde to appear
Horror The horrific event itself - revulsion, shock Carew's murder
Awe Sublime terror - overwhelmed by power The monster's creation

How Gothic Creates Fear

  • Atmosphere: Darkness, fog, cold, isolation
  • Pacing: Slow build of tension, then sudden horror
  • Unknown: What we can't see is scarier than what we can
  • Familiar made strange: Normal settings become threatening
In "Jekyll and Hyde"

Stevenson creates terror through atmosphere: foggy London streets, the mysterious door, footsteps approaching in the dark. The horror comes when we witness Hyde's violence. The unfamiliar (transformations) is made more frightening by the familiar setting.

👻 The Supernatural

Supernatural Elements in Gothic

Element Function Example
Ghosts Haunted past, guilt, unfinished business Marley's Ghost
Transformation Identity split, degeneration Jekyll to Hyde
Monsters Science gone wrong, playing God Frankenstein's creature
Curses Past actions haunt present Bertha Mason's "curse"

Ambiguity: Explained vs Unexplained

Victorian Gothic often balances the supernatural with rationality:

  • "Jekyll and Hyde": Transformation is supernatural but explained by science
  • "Frankenstein": Monster created by science, not magic
  • "Jane Eyre": Bertha is mad, not supernatural, but described in Gothic terms
Scientific Supernatural: Victorian Gothic often uses science to explain supernatural events. This reflects Victorian anxiety about science going too far - creating horrors through knowledge.

🎭 Duality and Doubling

The Double Self

Gothic explores the divided self:

Aspect Description Example
Doppelganger Evil twin or double Hyde as Jekyll's double
Public vs Private Respectable exterior, dark interior Jekyll's public life vs Hyde
Good vs Evil Internal moral conflict Jekyll's struggle
Civilized vs Primitive Evolution suggests we have animal instincts Hyde as "troglodytic"

Why Duality Matters

Victorian society demanded respectability:

  • People had to appear moral and proper
  • Desires and impulses were repressed
  • Gothic shows what happens when repression fails
  • Hyde represents Jekyll's hidden desires made flesh
Duality in "Jekyll and Hyde"

Jekyll creates Hyde to separate his "good" and "evil" sides. But Hyde grows stronger, taking over. The message: you cannot separate yourself from your darker nature - it must be acknowledged, not denied.

🔒 Secrets and Mysteries

The Gothic Secret

Victorian Gothic is built on secrets:

Type of Secret What It Conceals Example
Hidden rooms Forbidden knowledge or people Jekyll's laboratory, attic in "Jane Eyre"
Hidden identities True nature, past crimes Hyde = Jekyll
Hidden pasts Shameful family history Bertha Mason
Hidden desires What respectable people shouldn't want Jekyll's dark impulses

Gradual Revelation

Gothic narratives often reveal secrets slowly:

  • Mystery: Reader tries to solve the puzzle
  • Suspense: Delay creates tension
  • Dramatic irony: We may know more than characters
  • Final revelation: Climax exposes the truth
The Secret as Society: Gothic secrets reflect Victorian society's secrets - what respectable families hid behind closed doors. The secret reveals the hypocrisy beneath the surface.

🏚️ Setting and Atmosphere

Gothic Settings

Setting Type Characteristics Effect
Old buildings Castles, mansions, decay Haunted by past
Urban Gothic Foggy streets, dark alleys Modernity made threatening
Isolation Remote locations, trapped No escape
Thresholds Doors, windows, boundaries Between safety and danger

Atmospheric Techniques

  • Weather: Fog, storms, wind, rain mirror emotions
  • Time: Night, twilight, dawn - liminal times
  • Sounds: Footsteps, screams, silence
  • Light: Shadows, darkness, flickering candles
London as Gothic Setting

Stevenson transforms modern London into Gothic space: fog obscures vision, streets are maze-like, the respectable city hides criminal underworld. The fog is both literal and metaphorical - hiding truth, blurring moral boundaries.

🔮 The Sublime

Awe and Terror

The sublime is overwhelming beauty mixed with terror:

  • Beyond comprehension: Too vast or powerful to understand
  • Mixed feelings: Pleasure in fear
  • Nature's power: Mountains, storms, oceans
  • Gothic sublime: The monstrous, the supernatural

Gothic Sublime

Example Sublime Element Effect
Frankenstein's creation Playing God, creating life Awe at power, horror at result
Hyde's transformation Impossible change Shocked fascination
Bertha Mason "Madwoman" revealed Terror and pity

📊 Gothic Conventions Summary

Gothic Formula

Fear: Terror (anticipation) + Horror (revulsion)

Setting: Dark, isolated, thresholds and secrets

Character: Divided self, hidden nature, monsters

Plot: Mystery, gradual revelation, supernatural

Theme: Civilized vs primitive, public vs private, science vs nature

✍️ Analysing Gothic in Essays

Good Gothic Analysis

"Stevenson uses Gothic conventions to explore Victorian anxiety. Hyde's 'troglodytic' appearance reflects contemporary fears about degeneration - the idea that humans could 'devolve' into primitive forms. The urban Gothic setting of foggy London makes modern life itself threatening, suggesting the darkness is not in foreign castles but in ordinary streets - and in ordinary people."

Useful Phrases

  • "Typical of Gothic literature, this passage creates..."
  • "The Gothic convention of X allows Stevenson to explore..."
  • "This reflects Victorian fears about..."
  • "The sublime horror of this moment suggests..."

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Why were Victorians fascinated by Gothic literature despite their scientific progress?

Q2: How does Stevenson create terror before horror in "Jekyll and Hyde"?

Q3: What does the duality of Jekyll and Hyde reveal about Victorian society?

Q4: How does Stevenson transform London into a Gothic setting?

✅ Answers

  1. Victorian repression forced desires and fears underground. Gothic expressed what couldn't be spoken about openly: fears about science, degeneration, hidden desires. It explored the darker side of human nature that respectable society denied.
  2. Stevenson creates terror through atmosphere: the fog, the mysterious door, Utterson's growing suspicion. We sense danger before seeing it. The horror comes with Hyde's violent acts (Carew's murder). The delay makes the horror more shocking.
  3. The duality reveals Victorian hypocrisy. Society demanded respectability but people had hidden desires. Jekyll creates Hyde to enjoy forbidden pleasures while staying respectable. The novel shows this division is unsustainable - the hidden self will emerge.
  4. London becomes Gothic through fog (obscuring truth), maze-like streets (losing one's way), the contrast between respectable facades and dark alleys, and the hidden door. The modern city becomes as threatening as any Gothic castle.
