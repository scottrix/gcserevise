N3: Gothic Literature

Foundation Higher All Boards AO2 AO3

Understanding Gothic conventions in 19th century texts - horror, duality and the supernatural.

🌑 What is Gothic Literature?

Origins and Definition

Gothic literature emerged in the 18th century:

  • Origin: Horace Walpole's "The Castle of Otranto" (1764)
  • Name: From medieval Gothic architecture - dark, ornate, mysterious
  • Purpose: To create fear, explore the irrational, challenge Enlightenment rationality
  • Peak: Flourished in the 19th century with Frankenstein, Jekyll and Hyde, Jane Eyre
Gothic Definition: A genre that combines horror, death, romance and the supernatural. It explores what lies beneath the surface of civilization - our fears, desires and secrets.

Why Victorians Loved Gothic

Despite scientific progress, Victorians were fascinated by Gothic because:

  • Repression: Strict morals forced desires underground - Gothic expressed what was hidden
  • Science fears: What if science goes too far? (Frankenstein, Jekyll)
  • Darwin: Evolution suggested humans have primitive instincts
  • Modernity: Gothic was an escape from industrial reality

😱 Horror and Terror

Two Types of Fear

Concept Description Example
Terror Anticipation of horror - dread, suspense, imagination Waiting for Hyde to appear
Horror The horrific event itself - revulsion, shock Carew's murder
Awe Sublime terror - overwhelmed by power The monster's creation

How Gothic Creates Fear

  • Atmosphere: Darkness, fog, cold, isolation
  • Pacing: Slow build of tension, then sudden horror
  • Unknown: What we can't see is scarier than what we can
  • Familiar made strange: Normal settings become threatening
In "Jekyll and Hyde"

Stevenson creates terror through atmosphere: foggy London streets, the mysterious door, footsteps approaching in the dark. The horror comes when we witness Hyde's violence. The unfamiliar (transformations) is made more frightening by the familiar setting.

👻 The Supernatural

Supernatural Elements in Gothic

Element Function Example
Ghosts Haunted past, guilt, unfinished business Marley's Ghost
Transformation Identity split, degeneration Jekyll to Hyde
Monsters Science gone wrong, playing God Frankenstein's creature
Curses Past actions haunt present Bertha Mason's "curse"

Ambiguity: Explained vs Unexplained

Victorian Gothic often balances the supernatural with rationality:

  • "Jekyll and Hyde": Transformation is supernatural but explained by science
  • "Frankenstein": Monster created by science, not magic
  • "Jane Eyre": Bertha is mad, not supernatural, but described in Gothic terms
Scientific Supernatural: Victorian Gothic often uses science to explain supernatural events. This reflects Victorian anxiety about science going too far - creating horrors through knowledge.

🎭 Duality and Doubling

The Double Self

Gothic explores the divided self:

Aspect Description Example
Doppelganger Evil twin or double Hyde as Jekyll's double
Public vs Private Respectable exterior, dark interior Jekyll's public life vs Hyde
Good vs Evil Internal moral conflict Jekyll's struggle
Civilized vs Primitive Evolution suggests we have animal instincts Hyde as "troglodytic"

Why Duality Matters

Victorian society demanded respectability:

  • People had to appear moral and proper
  • Desires and impulses were repressed
  • Gothic shows what happens when repression fails
  • Hyde represents Jekyll's hidden desires made flesh
Duality in "Jekyll and Hyde"

Jekyll creates Hyde to separate his "good" and "evil" sides. But Hyde grows stronger, taking over. The message: you cannot separate yourself from your darker nature - it must be acknowledged, not denied.

🔒 Secrets and Mysteries

The Gothic Secret

Victorian Gothic is built on secrets:

Type of Secret What It Conceals Example
Hidden rooms Forbidden knowledge or people Jekyll's laboratory, attic in "Jane Eyre"
Hidden identities True nature, past crimes Hyde = Jekyll
Hidden pasts Shameful family history Bertha Mason
Hidden desires What respectable people shouldn't want Jekyll's dark impulses

Gradual Revelation

Gothic narratives often reveal secrets slowly:

  • Mystery: Reader tries to solve the puzzle
  • Suspense: Delay creates tension
  • Dramatic irony: We may know more than characters
  • Final revelation: Climax exposes the truth
The Secret as Society: Gothic secrets reflect Victorian society's secrets - what respectable families hid behind closed doors. The secret reveals the hypocrisy beneath the surface.

🏚️ Setting and Atmosphere

Gothic Settings

Setting Type Characteristics Effect
Old buildings Castles, mansions, decay Haunted by past
Urban Gothic Foggy streets, dark alleys Modernity made threatening
Isolation Remote locations, trapped No escape
Thresholds Doors, windows, boundaries Between safety and danger

Atmospheric Techniques

  • Weather: Fog, storms, wind, rain mirror emotions
  • Time: Night, twilight, dawn - liminal times
  • Sounds: Footsteps, screams, silence
  • Light: Shadows, darkness, flickering candles
London as Gothic Setting

Stevenson transforms modern London into Gothic space: fog obscures vision, streets are maze-like, the respectable city hides criminal underworld. The fog is both literal and metaphorical - hiding truth, blurring moral boundaries.

🔮 The Sublime

Awe and Terror

The sublime is overwhelming beauty mixed with terror:

  • Beyond comprehension: Too vast or powerful to understand
  • Mixed feelings: Pleasure in fear
  • Nature's power: Mountains, storms, oceans
  • Gothic sublime: The monstrous, the supernatural

Gothic Sublime

Example Sublime Element Effect
Frankenstein's creation Playing God, creating life Awe at power, horror at result
Hyde's transformation Impossible change Shocked fascination
Bertha Mason "Madwoman" revealed Terror and pity

📊 Gothic Conventions Summary

Gothic Formula

Fear: Terror (anticipation) + Horror (revulsion)

Setting: Dark, isolated, thresholds and secrets

Character: Divided self, hidden nature, monsters

Plot: Mystery, gradual revelation, supernatural

Theme: Civilized vs primitive, public vs private, science vs nature

✍️ Analysing Gothic in Essays

Good Gothic Analysis

"Stevenson uses Gothic conventions to explore Victorian anxiety. Hyde's 'troglodytic' appearance reflects contemporary fears about degeneration - the idea that humans could 'devolve' into primitive forms. The urban Gothic setting of foggy London makes modern life itself threatening, suggesting the darkness is not in foreign castles but in ordinary streets - and in ordinary people."

Useful Phrases

  • "Typical of Gothic literature, this passage creates..."
  • "The Gothic convention of X allows Stevenson to explore..."
  • "This reflects Victorian fears about..."
  • "The sublime horror of this moment suggests..."

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Why were Victorians fascinated by Gothic literature despite their scientific progress?

Q2: How does Stevenson create terror before horror in "Jekyll and Hyde"?

Q3: What does the duality of Jekyll and Hyde reveal about Victorian society?

Q4: How does Stevenson transform London into a Gothic setting?

✅ Answers

  1. Victorian repression forced desires and fears underground. Gothic expressed what couldn't be spoken about openly: fears about science, degeneration, hidden desires. It explored the darker side of human nature that respectable society denied.
  2. Stevenson creates terror through atmosphere: the fog, the mysterious door, Utterson's growing suspicion. We sense danger before seeing it. The horror comes with Hyde's violent acts (Carew's murder). The delay makes the horror more shocking.
  3. The duality reveals Victorian hypocrisy. Society demanded respectability but people had hidden desires. Jekyll creates Hyde to enjoy forbidden pleasures while staying respectable. The novel shows this division is unsustainable - the hidden self will emerge.
  4. London becomes Gothic through fog (obscuring truth), maze-like streets (losing one's way), the contrast between respectable facades and dark alleys, and the hidden door. The modern city becomes as threatening as any Gothic castle.

📝 Exam Technique

Gothic Analysis Strategy: When writing about Gothic elements, the highest marks come from treating Gothic conventions as meaningful choices, not decorative features. Every Gothic element has an intellectual purpose: to explore anxiety, reveal repression, or challenge rationalism. Examiners reward responses that explain what Gothic conventions allow writers to say that realism alone cannot. For Grade 9, show how Gothic conventions function as a language of the unconscious — expressing fears and desires that Victorian society refused to acknowledge openly.

Time Management for Gothic Questions

StageTimeFocus
Planning5 minsIdentify 3-4 Gothic conventions in the extract; note what anxiety or desire each one expresses
Writing35 minsEach paragraph: identify Gothic element → analyse method → link to Victorian anxiety → explore effect on contemporary vs modern reader
Checking5 minsVerify analysis addresses both AO2 (how the convention works technically) and AO3 (why it mattered contextually)

What Examiners Look For

  • Convention as meaning: Not "there is fog" but "the fog obscures moral and visual clarity, reflecting Victorian fear that civilised appearances hide primal chaos"
  • Ambiguity: Acknowledging that Gothic meaning is often unstable — is Hyde supernatural or psychological? Is the horror external or internal?
  • Contextual purpose: Exploring why Gothic flourished when it did — what Victorian anxieties required this genre
  • Reader positioning: How Gothic conventions manipulate the reader into experiencing the text's fears firsthand

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often list Gothic features without analysing their purpose. Wrong: "Stevenson uses Gothic features like fog, darkness and secrets to create a scary atmosphere." Correct: "Stevenson's fog does not merely create atmosphere — it enacts the novel's central concern with hidden truth. Just as fog obscures London's streets, respectability obscures Jekyll's duality, and the reader shares Utterson's frustrated desire to see clearly."

Students often treat Gothic as purely supernatural rather than psychological. Wrong: "Hyde is a supernatural monster who represents evil." Correct: "Hyde's horror lies in his psychological realism — he is not a demon but the repressed self that Victorian morality forced underground. Stevenson's genius is making the supernatural feel like psychology."

Students often ignore how Gothic conventions position the reader. Wrong: "The mystery keeps the reader interested in the story." Correct: "The delayed revelation of Jekyll's secret forces the reader into Utterson's position — suspecting but not knowing — so we experience the same anxious uncertainty that Victorian society felt about what lay behind its neighbours' respectable facades."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

30 marks: How does Stevenson use Gothic conventions to explore Victorian fears in "The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde"? Write about: Stevenson's use of setting; Stevenson's use of the doppelganger; how Stevenson uses terror and horror.

Stevenson transforms London into an urban Gothic landscape, subverting the genre's traditional castles and abbeys to suggest that modern civilisation itself is the site of horror. The "great chocolate-coloured pall" of fog that hangs over the city is not mere atmospheric decoration — it is the visible form of moral obscurity. In a society that prized transparency and respectability, fog becomes a metaphor for everything Victorians could not see about themselves: the desires, violences, and doubleness hidden behind every closed door and drawn curtain. When Utterson sits "treading the measure" of his own fears, the reader is placed in the same position — sensing danger in the familiar, which is precisely the urban Gothic's most unsettling trick.

The doppelganger convention is Stevenson's most powerful Gothic device, but he radicalises it. In traditional Gothic from Walpole to Poe, the double is a separate being — a external threat to be defeated. Stevenson makes Jekyll and Hyde the same person, which shifts the horror from outside to inside. This is not the horror of the invading monster but the horror of self-recognition: Hyde is not other but self. Contextually, this speaks directly to Victorian anxiety about degeneration. Max Nordau's theory (published shortly after the novella) argued that civilisation itself could cause regression; Hyde's "troglodytic" appearance literalises this fear. Stevenson uses the Gothic convention of the doppelganger not to suggest evil exists in the world, but to insist it exists within every individual — a far more disturbing proposition.

Stevenson's manipulation of terror and horror follows Radcliffe's distinction precisely, but to a new purpose. Terror — the anticipation of horror — dominates the early chapters: the fog, the footsteps, the locked door, Utterson's "perpetual" nightmare. This sustained terror mirrors the Victorian condition of repressed anxiety: the fear of what might emerge, of what respectable citizens might be concealing. When horror finally arrives — Carew's murder, witnessed by the maid — its brutality is shocking precisely because the terror has primed the reader's imagination. Stevenson understood that what we imagine is always worse than what we see; the fog of the unknown is more frightening than the clarity of violence. This is Gothic craft at its most sophisticated: terror as the psychic equivalent of repression, horror as the inevitable return of what has been denied.

Mark scheme: AO1 (12/12) — perceptive, sustained argument connecting Gothic conventions to Victorian psychology; AO2 (10/10) — sophisticated analysis of urban Gothic setting, doppelganger subversion, and the terror/horror distinction as structural method; AO3 (8/8) — precise contextual knowledge of degeneration theory, Victorian repression, and Gothic tradition integrated throughout.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO2 and AO3 in Gothic Questions: Gothic literature questions uniquely require the integration of two AOs: AO2 (how the writer uses Gothic methods) and AO3 (what Victorian anxieties those methods express). The strongest responses treat these as inseparable — every Gothic convention has a contextual purpose, and every contextual point is best expressed through the conventions writers chose.

Grade 5 approach: Identifies Gothic features and states some context, but keeps them separate. Writes: "The fog is a Gothic feature. In Victorian times there was a lot of fog and pollution." The convention and context are both present but disconnected; the student does not explain what the Gothic use of fog does that the real fog does not, or why Stevenson chose to weaponise a real phenomenon as a literary device.

Grade 7 approach: Links Gothic conventions to context with some nuance. Writes: "Stevenson uses the Gothic convention of fog to reflect Victorian anxiety — the real London smog becomes a symbol of moral uncertainty, showing how hard it was to tell truth from deception in a society obsessed with appearances." This connects method to context effectively, but the analysis could deepen by considering how the reader's experience of the fog mirrors the characters' — creating empathy as well as understanding.

Grade 9 approach: Fuses AO2 and AO3 so that method and context illuminate each other. Writes: "Stevenson's fog is both literal and metaphorical, both Victorian reality and Gothic device — and this doubleness is itself the point. The real London smog killed thousands; the Gothic fog kills moral clarity. By making an environmental hazard into a literary convention, Stevenson suggests that the physical and moral dangers of modernity are inseparable. The reader, unable to see through Stevenson's prose-fog any more than Utterson can see through the real fog, experiences the same epistemological anxiety that Victorian society felt: the sense that modern life had become unreadable, that behind every respectable facade might lurk a Hyde." Grade 9 responses also acknowledge the genre's self-awareness — that Gothic is always partly ironic, always conscious of its own theatricality — and explore the tension between entertainment and serious social critique that defines Victorian Gothic.
