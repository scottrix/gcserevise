PE13: Health and Fitness

Foundation Higher All Boards

The WHO definition of health, the definition of fitness, and the complex relationship between them.

🏥 What is Health?

WHO Definition: The World Health Organisation defines health as "a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity."

This definition highlights that health has three dimensions:

  • Physical health - the body is free from disease and functions efficiently. Indicators include: good energy levels, healthy body weight, ability to carry out daily tasks without fatigue, and effective immune function.
  • Mental health - a state of psychological well-being. Indicators include: positive self-esteem, ability to cope with stress, feeling confident and motivated, and having a sense of purpose.
  • Social health - the ability to interact positively with others and form healthy relationships. Indicators include: having supportive friendships, feeling connected to a community, and being able to work cooperatively with others.
Important: Health is NOT just the absence of illness. A person can be free from disease but still have poor mental or social health. True health requires well-being in all three areas.

💪 What is Fitness?

Definition: Fitness is "the ability to meet the demands of the environment" or "the ability to meet the demands of a physical task." It refers to how well the body can cope with the physical challenges it faces.

Fitness is specific to the task or activity being performed:

  • A marathon runner needs high cardiovascular endurance and muscular endurance
  • A weightlifter needs high muscular strength and power
  • A gymnast needs high flexibility and balance
  • A sprinter needs high speed and power
Fitness is Task-Specific

A Tour de France cyclist may be extremely fit for their sport (high cardiovascular endurance, muscular endurance and leg strength), but they may not be fit for a sport requiring different components. They may lack the flexibility needed for gymnastics or the upper body strength needed for weightlifting. Fitness means being able to meet the demands of YOUR specific environment or task.

🔗 The Relationship Between Health and Fitness

Key Concept: Health and fitness are related but they are NOT the same thing. A person can be fit but not healthy, or healthy but not fit. Being fit can contribute to good health, but it does not guarantee it.
Scenario Explanation Example
Fit AND healthy The person can meet the physical demands of their activity AND has complete physical, mental and social well-being A recreational runner who trains regularly, eats well, has good social connections and positive mental health
Fit but NOT healthy The person can meet physical demands but has health issues in one or more dimensions An elite athlete with an eating disorder (mental health issue); a footballer playing through injury; an athlete with depression
Healthy but NOT fit The person is free from disease and has good well-being, but cannot meet the physical demands of specific tasks A person who is disease-free and happy but leads a sedentary lifestyle and cannot run for a bus without becoming breathless
Neither fit nor healthy The person has health problems and cannot meet physical demands A person with chronic disease who is physically inactive and socially isolated
Fit but Not Healthy

An elite marathon runner may be extremely physically fit, capable of running 26.2 miles at pace. However, if they suffer from an eating disorder (common in endurance sports where low body weight is perceived as advantageous), their mental health is poor. They are fit but not healthy. Similarly, an athlete competing with a stress fracture is fit enough to perform but not physically healthy.

Ill Health Can Decrease Fitness

If a normally active person gets flu, their fitness will temporarily decrease. They cannot exercise while ill, and the disease itself reduces their body's capacity for physical work. The illness reduces their cardiovascular efficiency, muscle strength and energy levels. This shows how ill health directly reduces fitness.

📊 How Exercise Affects Health and Fitness

Regular exercise improves both health and fitness, but they are improved through different mechanisms:

How Exercise Improves Health

  • Physical health: Reduces risk of heart disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, obesity and some cancers; strengthens immune system; improves bone density
  • Mental health: Releases endorphins (feel-good hormones); reduces stress, anxiety and depression; improves self-esteem and body image; improves sleep quality
  • Social health: Team sports and group exercise build friendships; provides sense of belonging; develops communication and cooperation skills

How Exercise Improves Fitness

  • Cardiovascular adaptations (cardiac hypertrophy, increased stroke volume, capillarisation)
  • Muscular adaptations (hypertrophy, increased mitochondria, improved endurance)
  • Respiratory adaptations (increased vital capacity, stronger respiratory muscles)
  • Improved flexibility, body composition and skill-related fitness

⚠️ Can Fitness Exist Despite Ill Health?

Yes. A person can be physically fit enough to perform a task even while having a health condition. For example, athletes with asthma (like runner Paula Radcliffe) can be extremely fit for their sport while managing a long-term health condition. However, ill health typically makes it harder to achieve and maintain fitness.

The relationship works in both directions:

  • Ill health can decrease fitness - being unwell reduces the body's capacity for physical work and may prevent training
  • Good fitness can support health - being physically fit helps protect against many diseases and improves recovery from illness
  • But fitness does NOT guarantee health - a fit person can still develop physical or mental health problems

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: State the WHO definition of health.

Q2: Define fitness.

Q3: Explain how a person can be fit but not healthy, giving a specific example.

Q4: How can ill health decrease fitness?

Q5: Describe the three dimensions of health and give an indicator for each.

Q6: Explain why fitness does not guarantee good health.

✅ Answers

  1. "A state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity."
  2. Fitness is the ability to meet the demands of the environment / the ability to meet the demands of a physical task.
  3. A person can be physically capable of performing at a high level (fit) but still have health problems. For example, an elite marathon runner may be extremely fit for their sport but may suffer from an eating disorder (poor mental health) or compete through injury (poor physical health). Fitness relates only to physical capacity, while health encompasses physical, mental and social well-being.
  4. Ill health can decrease fitness because: disease reduces the body's capacity for physical work; illness may prevent training and cause de-training; conditions like flu reduce cardiovascular efficiency, muscle strength and energy levels; chronic conditions limit the ability to exercise regularly.
  5. Physical health - body functions efficiently without disease (indicator: healthy body weight, good energy levels). Mental health - psychological well-being (indicator: positive self-esteem, ability to cope with stress). Social health - positive relationships and interactions (indicator: supportive friendships, community involvement).
  6. Fitness refers only to the physical capacity to meet task demands. Health encompasses physical, mental and social well-being. A fit person can still develop mental health issues (e.g. depression, anxiety), social health problems (e.g. isolation despite athletic success), or physical conditions unrelated to fitness (e.g. asthma, diabetes). Additionally, extreme training for fitness can sometimes harm health (e.g. overtraining, eating disorders, injuries).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • You MUST learn the WHO definition of health word-for-word - it is frequently tested
  • Health has three dimensions: physical, mental and social - always mention all three
  • Health ≠ "not being ill" - the WHO definition specifically states it is more than absence of disease
  • Fitness is about meeting the demands of the ENVIRONMENT or TASK - use the exact wording
  • Be ready to explain the relationship between health and fitness with examples
  • Know that fitness is task-specific - a fit marathon runner may not be fit for weightlifting

📝 Exam Technique

PE Exam Tips — Health and Fitness:

2. Support every point with specific evidence or examples

4. Link your understanding of Health and Fitness to real-world contexts where possible

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think fitness and health are the same thing. Wrong: Fitness and health are the same thing Correct: Health is a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being (WHO). Fitness is the ability to meet the demands of the environment. You can be fit but unhealthy (e.g. a marathon runner with flu).

Students often think static stretching is the best warm-up. Wrong: Static stretching is the best warm-up Correct: Current evidence suggests dynamic stretching is more effective in warm-ups as it mimics sporting movements. Static stretching may actually reduce power output if held too long before activity.

Students often think no pain, no gain is a good training principle. Wrong: No pain, no gain is a good training principle Correct: Pain can indicate injury. Progressive overload should be gradual — the 10% rule suggests not increasing training load by more than 10% per week. Ignoring pain leads to overuse injuries.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

6 marks: Explain how health and fitness affects sporting performance.

Health and Fitness has significant effects on sporting performance. [Key concept 1]: explain the mechanism with specific detail. [Key concept 2]: how this applies in a named sporting example. [Key concept 3]: the relationship between this topic and overall performance. A grade 9 answer uses precise anatomical/physiological terminology, specific sporting examples, and evaluates the relative importance of different factors.

Mark scheme: 2 marks per explained point with specific evidence, evaluation for top marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE PE tests four AOs: AO1 (Knowledge, 30%) — recall facts about Health and Fitness including definitions, classifications and specific examples; AO2 (Application, 30%) — apply knowledge to sporting contexts and training scenarios; AO3 (Analysis and Evaluation, 25%) — analyse data (graphs, tables), evaluate training methods or strategies, and justify recommendations; AO4 (Practical, 15%) — demonstrate relevant skills. For grade 9, use precise terminology, support every point with named sporting examples, and evaluate rather than just describe. The difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of application and depth of evaluation.
