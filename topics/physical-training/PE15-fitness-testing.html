PE15: Fitness Testing

Foundation Higher All Boards

Standard fitness tests, their protocols, which components they measure, reasons for testing and limitations.

📋 Why Test Fitness?

Reasons for fitness testing:
  • Identify strengths and weaknesses - find out which components of fitness need improvement
  • Monitor progress - compare test results over time to see if training is working
  • Set baseline scores - establish starting points for setting targets and designing training programmes
  • Compare against normative data - see how results compare to national averages for age and gender
  • Provide motivation - give performers targets to aim for and a sense of achievement
  • Assess suitability for a sport - determine if fitness levels meet the demands of a specific activity

🧪 The Fitness Tests

Test Component Measured Protocol
Illinois Agility Run Agility Run through a course of cones set out in a 10m × 5m grid, including changes of direction, slalom sections and acceleration. Timed to the nearest 0.1 second.
Stork Stand Test Balance (static) Stand on one foot with the other foot placed against the supporting knee. Hands on hips. Time how long balance is maintained without the foot moving or hands leaving hips.
Multi-Stage Fitness Test (Beep Test) Cardiovascular endurance Run 20m shuttles in time with audio beeps that get progressively faster. Continue until unable to keep up with the beeps on two consecutive occasions. Score is the level and shuttle reached.
Wall Toss Test Coordination (hand-eye) Stand 2m from a wall. Throw a tennis ball against the wall with one hand and catch it with the other. Count the number of successful catches in 30 seconds.
Sit and Reach Test Flexibility (hamstrings, lower back) Sit on the floor with legs straight and feet flat against the sit-and-reach box. Reach forward as far as possible without bending the knees. Measure the distance reached in cm.
Sit-Up Bleep Test Muscular endurance (abdominals) Perform sit-ups in time with audio beeps that get progressively faster. Continue until unable to maintain the required pace. Score is the level reached.
Vertical Jump Test (Sargent Jump) Power (leg) Stand side-on to a wall and reach up with one hand (standing reach). Jump as high as possible and touch the wall at the highest point. Subtract standing reach from jump height. Measured in cm.
Ruler Drop Test Reaction time A partner holds a 30cm ruler vertically, with the 0cm mark between the thumb and forefinger of the person being tested. The ruler is released without warning. The distance the ruler falls before being caught is measured. Convert distance to time using the formula t = √(2d/g).
One Rep Max Test Strength Lift the heaviest weight possible for one complete repetition of an exercise (e.g. bench press, squat). The 1RM is the maximum weight that can be lifted once with correct technique.
30m Sprint Test Speed Sprint 30m from a standing start. Time the sprint using electronic timing gates for accuracy. Best of three attempts is recorded.
Handgrip Dynamometer Test Strength (grip) Squeeze a handgrip dynamometer as hard as possible. Record the score in kg. Best of three attempts for each hand is recorded.

📊 Normative Data

Normative data is a set of reference values (averages) that allow you to compare your test results against typical scores for your age and gender. Without normative data, a test score is just a number with no context.
Using Normative Data

If a 15-year-old male scores Level 10.5 on the Multi-Stage Fitness Test, this can be compared to normative data that shows the average for his age group is Level 8.2. This means he is above average for cardiovascular endurance. Without normative data, the score of 10.5 would be meaningless.

⚠️ Limitations of Fitness Testing

Limitations: Fitness tests are not perfect measures. They have several limitations that must be considered:
  • Lack of specificity: Most tests are generic and don't replicate the exact demands of a specific sport. For example, the Illinois Agility Run tests general agility but not the specific agility patterns used in football.
  • Environmental factors: Surface, weather, temperature and venue can all affect results. A test run on grass will produce different results to one on a sports hall floor.
  • Practice effect: Participants may improve simply because they've practised the test, not because their fitness has improved. Familiarity with the test procedure can lead to better scores.
  • Motivation: Results depend on how hard the participant tries. A lack of motivation produces a lower score that doesn't reflect true fitness.
  • Measurement error: Inaccurate timing, equipment calibration issues or human error in recording can affect reliability.
  • Not sport-specific: Standard tests measure general fitness rather than the specific fitness required for a particular sport.
  • One-off testing: A single test may not represent true ability - fatigue, illness or stress on the day can affect results.
Limitation in Practice

A footballer takes the Multi-Stage Fitness Test and scores Level 9. However, the test only measures straight-line running endurance. Football requires repeated sprints, changes of direction and sport-specific movements. The beep test score doesn't fully reflect the footballer's match fitness. A more specific test might involve repeated shuttle sprints with turns.

✅ How to Make Testing More Valid

  • Use sport-specific tests where possible - choose tests that closely match the demands of the performer's sport
  • Standardise conditions - same venue, same surface, same equipment, same time of day
  • Ensure adequate warm-up - participants must be physically prepared
  • Use trained testers - ensure those administering tests know the correct protocols
  • Test regularly - take multiple measures to identify trends rather than relying on a single score
  • Ensure maximum effort - motivate participants to give their best performance
  • Compare against relevant normative data - use data that matches the participant's age, gender and sport

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Which fitness test measures agility and briefly describe its protocol.

Q2: Name the test used to measure cardiovascular endurance and describe how it works.

Q3: Explain three reasons why fitness testing is carried out.

Q4: Describe three limitations of fitness testing.

Q5: Which test would you use to measure each of the following: power, reaction time, flexibility?

Q6: Explain what normative data is and why it is important when interpreting fitness test results.

✅ Answers

  1. The Illinois Agility Run measures agility. The participant runs through a course of cones in a 10m × 5m grid, including changes of direction, slalom sections and acceleration. The time taken to complete the course is measured.
  2. The Multi-Stage Fitness Test (Beep Test) measures cardiovascular endurance. Participants run 20m shuttles in time with audio beeps that get progressively faster. They continue until they cannot reach the line on two consecutive beeps. The level and shuttle reached is the score.
  3. (1) Identify strengths and weaknesses in different components of fitness. (2) Monitor progress over time to see if a training programme is effective. (3) Set baseline scores at the start of a programme to establish starting points for goal setting. (Other valid: compare against normative data, provide motivation, assess suitability for sport.)
  4. (1) Lack of specificity - most tests don't replicate the exact demands of a particular sport. (2) Practice effect - participants may improve just by becoming familiar with the test, not from genuine fitness gains. (3) Motivation - results depend on effort; low motivation produces artificially low scores. (Other valid: environmental factors, measurement error, not sport-specific, one-off testing.)
  5. Power = Vertical Jump Test (Sargent Jump); Reaction time = Ruler Drop Test; Flexibility = Sit and Reach Test.
  6. Normative data is a set of reference values (averages) for different age groups and genders. It is important because it provides context for test results - without it, a score is just a number. Normative data allows you to determine whether a score is above average, average or below average for a particular population.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • You MUST know which test measures which component - this is a very common matching question
  • Learn the basic protocol for each test (you don't need exact cone positions, but know the general procedure)
  • Be ready to explain limitations - "not sport-specific" is the most important limitation
  • Normative data gives CONTEXT to scores - always mention this when discussing test interpretation
  • The Multi-Stage Fitness Test is the most commonly tested - know it in detail
  • Understand the difference between validity (does it measure what it claims?) and reliability (is it consistent?)

📝 Exam Technique

PE Exam Tips:
Know all 10 components of fitness with definitions AND sporting examples. Be able to design a training programme using SPORT and FITT. For fitness testing, know the protocol for each test and its limitations. Calculate training zones using 220 - age.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think fitness and health are the same thing. Wrong: Fitness and health are the same thing Correct: Health is a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being (WHO). Fitness is the ability to meet the demands of the environment. You can be fit but unhealthy (e.g. a marathon runner with flu).

Students often think static stretching is the best warm-up. Wrong: Static stretching is the best warm-up Correct: Current evidence suggests dynamic stretching is more effective in warm-ups as it mimics sporting movements. Static stretching may actually reduce power output if held too long before activity.

Students often think no pain, no gain is a good training principle. Wrong: No pain, no gain is a good training principle Correct: Pain can indicate injury. Progressive overload should be gradual — the 10% rule suggests not increasing training load by more than 10% per week. Ignoring pain leads to overuse injuries.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE PE tests four AOs: AO1 (Knowledge, 30%) — recall facts about Fitness Testing including definitions, classifications and specific examples; AO2 (Application, 30%) — apply knowledge to sporting contexts and training scenarios; AO3 (Analysis and Evaluation, 25%) — analyse data (graphs, tables), evaluate training methods or strategies, and justify recommendations; AO4 (Practical, 15%) — demonstrate relevant skills. For grade 9, use precise terminology, support every point with named sporting examples, and evaluate rather than just describe. The difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of application and depth of evaluation.
