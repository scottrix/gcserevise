PE17: Training Methods

Foundation Higher All Boards

Different training methods, how they work, and their advantages and disadvantages for different fitness goals.

🔄 Circuit Training

Definition: A series of exercises performed one after another at different stations. Each station targets a different muscle group or fitness component, with a set time or number of reps at each station, followed by a short rest before moving to the next.
  • Typically 6-12 stations per circuit
  • Can be adapted to target any component of fitness
  • Stations might include: press-ups, sit-ups, squats, burpees, step-ups, shuttle runs, jump rope, etc.
  • Rest periods between stations: 30-90 seconds
  • Complete 2-3 circuits per session
Advantages Disadvantages
Can target multiple fitness components in one session Requires equipment and space for multiple stations
Varied - reduces tedium (addresses the T in SPORT) May not develop one specific component enough for elite performers
Can be easily adapted for different sports and fitness levels Can be difficult to monitor technique at all stations simultaneously
Suitable for groups and team training Rest periods may not be long enough for full recovery between stations

🏃 Continuous Training

Definition: Exercising at a steady, moderate intensity for a sustained period without rest breaks. The heart rate is maintained at 60-80% of maximum heart rate throughout the session.
  • Duration: typically 20-60 minutes (or longer for endurance athletes)
  • Intensity: 60-80% max HR (aerobic training zone)
  • Activities: running, cycling, swimming, rowing, walking
  • Develops cardiovascular endurance primarily
Advantages Disadvantages
Excellent for developing cardiovascular endurance Can be monotonous and boring (tedium)
Easy to plan and monitor - just maintain pace Does not improve anaerobic fitness or speed
Requires minimal equipment High impact (running) can cause injury to joints
Can be performed anywhere Not specific to sports requiring bursts of effort

🇸🇪 Fartlek Training

Definition: "Speed play" - a form of continuous training where the intensity varies throughout the session. The performer alternates between fast and slow running (or other activities) over varying terrain, without structured rest periods.
  • Combines high and low intensity effort
  • Example session: jog → sprint for 100m → walk → run at 70% effort for 400m → jog → sprint up a hill → walk → jog
  • Develops both aerobic and anaerobic fitness
  • More varied than continuous training - reduces tedium
Advantages Disadvantages
Develops both aerobic and anaerobic systems Difficult to measure intensity and progress accurately
More varied and interesting than continuous training Requires access to varied terrain (hills, trails)
Replicates the variable pace of team sports (e.g. football) Less suitable for beginners who need structure
Reduces tedium compared to steady-state running Hard to monitor and compare sessions

⏱️ Interval / HIIT Training

Definition: Alternating periods of high-intensity exercise with periods of rest or low-intensity recovery. HIIT (High Intensity Interval Training) involves very intense bursts (85-95% max HR) with short recovery periods.
  • Work:rest ratios vary depending on the goal - e.g. 1:3 (10s sprint, 30s rest), 1:1 (30s work, 30s rest), 1:2 (60s work, 120s rest)
  • Develops both aerobic and anaerobic fitness
  • Very time-efficient - significant fitness gains in shorter sessions
  • Example: 8 × 400m at 85% max HR with 2-minute jog recovery
Advantages Disadvantages
Highly effective for improving cardiovascular fitness Very demanding - requires high motivation
Time-efficient compared to continuous training Higher risk of injury due to intense nature
Can be tailored to specific sports using relevant work:rest ratios Not suitable for beginners or those with low fitness levels
Replicates the intermittent nature of team sports Can cause excessive fatigue and DOMS

🏋️ Weight Training

Definition: Using resistance (free weights, machines, body weight) to develop muscular strength, endurance and hypertrophy. The programme can be adapted by changing reps, sets, weight and rest periods.
  • Strength: Heavy weight, low reps (1-6), long rest (3-5 min)
  • Hypertrophy: Moderate weight, moderate reps (8-12), moderate rest (60-90s)
  • Muscular endurance: Light weight, high reps (15+), short rest (30-60s)
  • Power: Explosive movements with moderate-heavy weight, low reps
Advantages Disadvantages
Highly effective for building strength and muscle Requires access to equipment (weights, machines)
Can target specific muscle groups Risk of injury if technique is poor
Easy to measure and track progress (weight lifted) Can be time-consuming
Adaptable for different goals (strength, endurance, power) May not improve cardiovascular fitness

💥 Plyometric Training

Definition: Exercises that involve rapid, powerful movements using the stretch-shortening cycle of muscles. The muscle is stretched (eccentric contraction) then immediately contracted (concentric contraction) to produce an explosive movement.
  • Examples: box jumps, depth jumps, bounding, clap press-ups, jump squats
  • Develops power - the combination of strength and speed
  • Uses the elastic energy stored in muscles during the stretch phase
  • Should only be performed on appropriate surfaces with good technique
Advantages Disadvantages
Excellent for developing explosive power High risk of injury (especially to knees and ankles)
Specific to many sports (jumping, sprinting, throwing) Requires a foundation of strength before starting
Improves the efficiency of the stretch-shortening cycle Causes significant DOMS due to eccentric component
Can improve running economy and speed Not suitable for beginners or young athletes

🧘 Static Stretching

Definition: Stretching a muscle to its maximum length and holding the position still for a period of time (typically 10-30 seconds). This develops flexibility at a joint.
  • Should be performed after a warm-up when muscles are warm
  • Each stretch is held statically - no bouncing or movement
  • Develops flexibility, which can improve performance and reduce injury risk
  • Commonly used as part of a cool-down
Advantages Disadvantages
Improves flexibility and range of movement Can reduce power output if done before explosive activity
Reduces risk of muscle strain injury Does not develop cardiovascular fitness or strength
Easy to perform and requires no equipment Can be boring if done for long periods
Effective as part of cool-down to aid recovery Over-stretching can cause injury

📊 Which Method for Which Goal?

Fitness Goal Best Training Method(s)
Cardiovascular endurance Continuous training, Fartlek training, Interval training
Muscular strength Weight training (heavy weight, low reps)
Muscular endurance Circuit training, weight training (light weight, high reps)
Power Plyometric training, weight training (explosive lifts)
Flexibility Static stretching
Agility Circuit training (agility stations), interval training with changes of direction
Speed Interval training (short sprints), plyometric training
General fitness Circuit training (targets multiple components)

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe circuit training and explain two advantages and two disadvantages.

Q2: What is Fartlek training and which two energy systems does it develop?

Q3: Explain the difference between continuous training and interval training.

Q4: Which training method is most appropriate for developing power? Explain why.

Q5: A footballer wants to improve their cardiovascular endurance. Which two training methods would you recommend and why?

Q6: Describe plyometric training and explain why it carries a high risk of injury.

✅ Answers

  1. Circuit training involves performing a series of exercises at different stations, one after another, with a short rest between stations. Advantages: targets multiple fitness components; reduces tedium through variety. Disadvantages: requires equipment and space; may not develop one specific component enough for elite performers.
  2. Fartlek training ("speed play") is a form of continuous training where the intensity varies - alternating between fast and slow running over varied terrain. It develops both the aerobic system (during slower sections) and the anaerobic system (during sprints and high-intensity bursts).
  3. Continuous training involves exercising at a steady, moderate intensity for a sustained period without rest (e.g. a 30-minute jog at 70% max HR). Interval training alternates periods of high-intensity exercise with periods of rest or low-intensity recovery (e.g. 8 × 400m sprints with 2-minute recovery). Continuous is purely aerobic; interval develops both aerobic and anaerobic fitness.
  4. Plyometric training is most appropriate for developing power. It uses the stretch-shortening cycle of muscles, where a rapid eccentric stretch is followed immediately by an explosive concentric contraction. This develops the ability to produce maximal force in the shortest time (power = strength × speed). Examples include box jumps and depth jumps.
  5. Fartlek training and interval training. Fartlek replicates the variable pace of football (jogging, sprinting, walking) and develops both aerobic and anaerobic fitness. Interval training with short, high-intensity bursts and recovery periods mimics the intermittent nature of football and improves both cardiovascular endurance and the ability to recover quickly between efforts.
  6. Plyometric training involves rapid, powerful movements using the stretch-shortening cycle (e.g. box jumps, depth jumps, bounding). It carries a high risk of injury because the explosive movements place enormous stress on joints (especially knees and ankles) and the eccentric loading on muscles can cause micro-tears. It requires a foundation of strength and good technique to perform safely.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • For each training method, know: definition, which component it develops, advantages AND disadvantages
  • Be ready to RECOMMEND a training method for a specific sport and JUSTIFY your choice
  • Fartlek = "speed play" = variable pace continuous training
  • Interval = work-rest intervals; HIIT = very intense intervals
  • Plyometric = explosive power; Weight training = strength/endurance depending on reps
  • Circuit training is the most versatile - it can be adapted for almost any fitness goal

📝 Exam Technique

PE Exam Tips:
Know all 10 components of fitness with definitions AND sporting examples. Be able to design a training programme using SPORT and FITT. For fitness testing, know the protocol for each test and its limitations. Calculate training zones using 220 - age.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think fitness and health are the same thing. Wrong: Fitness and health are the same thing Correct: Health is a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being (WHO). Fitness is the ability to meet the demands of the environment. You can be fit but unhealthy (e.g. a marathon runner with flu).

Students often think static stretching is the best warm-up. Wrong: Static stretching is the best warm-up Correct: Current evidence suggests dynamic stretching is more effective in warm-ups as it mimics sporting movements. Static stretching may actually reduce power output if held too long before activity.

Students often think no pain, no gain is a good training principle. Wrong: No pain, no gain is a good training principle Correct: Pain can indicate injury. Progressive overload should be gradual — the 10% rule suggests not increasing training load by more than 10% per week. Ignoring pain leads to overuse injuries.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE PE tests four AOs: AO1 (Knowledge, 30%) — recall facts about Training Methods including definitions, classifications and specific examples; AO2 (Application, 30%) — apply knowledge to sporting contexts and training scenarios; AO3 (Analysis and Evaluation, 25%) — analyse data (graphs, tables), evaluate training methods or strategies, and justify recommendations; AO4 (Practical, 15%) — demonstrate relevant skills. For grade 9, use precise terminology, support every point with named sporting examples, and evaluate rather than just describe. The difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of application and depth of evaluation.
