PE20: Seasonal Training

Foundation Higher All Boards

How training programmes change across the year: pre-season, competition/peak and post-season/transition phases.

📅 What is a Training Year?

Definition: A training year (macrocycle) is divided into distinct phases, each with specific training goals. The training programme is structured so the athlete peaks (reaches their best performance) at the right time - usually for the most important competitions.

This is called periodisation - the systematic planning of training to reach the best possible performance at a particular time.

The training year is divided into three main phases:

  1. Pre-season (preparation)
  2. Competition/peak season (competition)
  3. Post-season/transition (recovery)

🏋️ Pre-Season (Preparation Phase)

Goal: Build a base of fitness and develop the specific skills needed for the upcoming competitive season. The aim is to prepare the body and mind for the demands of competition.

Key Features

  • Duration: Typically 6-12 weeks before the competitive season begins
  • High training volume: More hours of training per week than other phases
  • Moderate intensity: Gradual build-up, avoiding peak intensity too early

Training Focus

  • Aerobic base building: Long, steady continuous training to develop cardiovascular endurance. This is the foundation that all other fitness is built upon.
  • Strength training: Weight training to build muscular strength and power. Focus on general strength before moving to sport-specific power.
  • Skill development: Practising and refining sport-specific techniques and tactics.
  • Flexibility and mobility: Stretching programmes to improve range of movement and prevent injury during the season.
Pre-Season in Football

A professional footballer's pre-season (July-August) typically involves: daily training sessions; long-distance running to build cardiovascular endurance (aerobic base); weight training for strength and power; sprint training and agility drills; practice matches to apply skills; gradually increasing intensity as the season approaches. Players return from the off-season break and must rebuild fitness before the first competitive match.

🏆 Competition/Peak Season

Goal: Maintain the fitness built in pre-season while performing at the highest possible level in competitions. Training shifts from building fitness to maintaining it and peaking for key events.

Key Features

  • Duration: The length of the competitive season (varies by sport - e.g. August-May in English football)
  • Reduced training volume: Fewer hours of general fitness training
  • Higher training intensity but shorter sessions: Quality over quantity
  • More competition and less training: Matches/races become the primary physical demand

Training Focus

  • Maintaining fitness: Enough training to prevent reversibility but not so much that it causes fatigue for competitions. Typically 2-3 maintenance sessions per week.
  • Sport-specific training: Tactics, set pieces, game plans and skill refinement. Training that directly transfers to competition performance.
  • Tapering: Reducing training volume in the days before a major competition to allow the body to fully recover and peak. This ensures the athlete is fresh and at maximum capacity for the event.
  • Recovery between competitions: Cool-downs, active recovery, adequate sleep and nutrition to prepare for the next match or race.
Tapering: A planned reduction in training volume (by 40-60%) in the 7-14 days before a major competition. Intensity may remain high but the total training load is reduced. This allows the body to fully recover, replenish energy stores and repair any remaining muscle damage, so the athlete is at peak physical and mental condition for competition.
Competition Season in Athletics

An 800m runner during the summer competition season: competes in races every 1-2 weeks; between races, does short, high-quality interval sessions (e.g. 6 × 200m at race pace) to maintain speed and anaerobic fitness; reduces overall training volume to stay fresh for races; tapers in the week before a major championship (e.g. reduces training by 50% in the final 5 days); focuses on race tactics and mental preparation.

🧘 Post-Season/Transition Phase

Goal: Allow the body and mind to recover from the demands of the competitive season. Prevent burnout, repair injuries and recharge for the next pre-season. This is NOT a period of complete inactivity.

Key Features

  • Duration: Typically 4-8 weeks after the competitive season ends
  • Low training volume and intensity: Significantly reduced training load
  • Active recovery: Light exercise to maintain basic fitness without placing high demands on the body

Training Focus

  • Rest and recovery: The primary goal. The body needs time to heal from the accumulated fatigue and minor injuries of a long season.
  • Active recovery: Light, enjoyable activities such as swimming, cycling, walking, yoga or recreational sport. These maintain some fitness while allowing physical and mental recovery.
  • Rehabilitation: Treating any injuries that occurred during the season. Working with physiotherapists to fully recover before pre-season begins.
  • Light training: Gradually reintroducing some structured exercise towards the end of the transition phase, preparing the body for the increased demands of pre-season.
  • Mental rest: A break from the psychological pressure of competition. Doing different activities to prevent burnout and maintain motivation.
Post-Season in Rugby Union

After a long rugby season (September-May), a player enters the transition phase (June-July). For the first 2-3 weeks, they rest completely from rugby and do only light, enjoyable activity (swimming, cycling, walking). Any injuries are assessed and treated. Over the remaining weeks, they gradually increase activity levels with gym work and light running, preparing the body for the demanding pre-season that begins in late July. Mental recovery is equally important - time away from the sport's pressures refreshes motivation.

🔄 Periodisation Structure

Periodisation terminology:
Macrocycle = the overall training year (e.g. 12 months)
Mesocycle = a block within the macrocycle (e.g. 4-6 weeks, such as pre-season phase)
Microcycle = a single training week within a mesocycle

The structure allows coaches to plan training progressively, ensuring the athlete peaks at the right time while avoiding overtraining and injury.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the training focus during the pre-season phase.

Q2: Explain what tapering is and why it is used before major competitions.

Q3: Why is active recovery preferred over complete rest during the post-season phase?

Q4: Compare the training volume and intensity across the three phases of a training year.

Q5: Explain why a footballer's training programme would look different in July (pre-season) compared to March (competition season).

Q6: What is periodisation and why is it important for athletes?

✅ Answers

  1. Pre-season focuses on building a base of fitness: extensive aerobic training (continuous and Fartlek) for cardiovascular endurance, weight training for strength and power, skill development and refinement, and flexibility work. Training volume is high and intensity gradually increases towards the start of the competitive season.
  2. Tapering is a planned reduction in training volume (by 40-60%) in the 7-14 days before a major competition. It is used to allow the body to fully recover from training, replenish energy stores (glycogen and PC), repair muscle damage and ensure the athlete is at peak physical and mental condition for the competition. Intensity may remain high but total training load is reduced.
  3. Active recovery (light exercise such as swimming, cycling or walking) is preferred over complete rest because it: maintains some fitness and prevents reversibility; keeps blood flowing to aid recovery and repair; helps remove waste products from the body; prevents stiffness and maintains joint mobility; and provides a psychological break from competitive sport without losing all training benefits.
  4. Pre-season: high training volume, moderate-to-high intensity (gradually increasing). Competition season: moderate training volume (reduced), high intensity in shorter sessions. Post-season: low training volume and low intensity. The pattern follows a curve - building up in pre-season, maintaining in competition, and dropping back in post-season.
  5. In July (pre-season), a footballer would do high-volume training: long-distance running for aerobic base, weight training for strength, and gradually increasing intensity. There are no competitive matches, so all energy goes into training. In March (mid-competition season), training volume is reduced to maintain fitness without causing fatigue for matches. Sessions are shorter and more intense, with focus on tactics and sport-specific skills. The priority is performing well in matches, not building new fitness.
  6. Periodisation is the systematic planning of training across a year (macrocycle) into distinct phases (mesocycles and microcycles) with specific goals for each phase. It is important because it ensures the athlete peaks at the right time (for major competitions), prevents overtraining by including recovery periods, allows progressive development of fitness, reduces injury risk, and balances training, competition and rest.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know the three phases in order: pre-season → competition → post-season
  • For each phase, state the goal, training volume, intensity and focus
  • Tapering = reducing training before big events (important Higher-tier concept)
  • Active recovery in post-season prevents reversibility while allowing rest
  • Be ready to compare training programmes across phases for a specific sport
  • Remember: pre-season builds fitness, competition season maintains it, post-season recovers

📝 Exam Technique

PE Exam Tips:
Know all 10 components of fitness with definitions AND sporting examples. Be able to design a training programme using SPORT and FITT. For fitness testing, know the protocol for each test and its limitations. Calculate training zones using 220 - age.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think fitness and health are the same thing. Wrong: Fitness and health are the same thing Correct: Health is a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being (WHO). Fitness is the ability to meet the demands of the environment. You can be fit but unhealthy (e.g. a marathon runner with flu).

Students often think static stretching is the best warm-up. Wrong: Static stretching is the best warm-up Correct: Current evidence suggests dynamic stretching is more effective in warm-ups as it mimics sporting movements. Static stretching may actually reduce power output if held too long before activity.

Students often think no pain, no gain is a good training principle. Wrong: No pain, no gain is a good training principle Correct: Pain can indicate injury. Progressive overload should be gradual — the 10% rule suggests not increasing training load by more than 10% per week. Ignoring pain leads to overuse injuries.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE PE tests four AOs: AO1 (Knowledge, 30%) — recall facts about Seasonal Training including definitions, classifications and specific examples; AO2 (Application, 30%) — apply knowledge to sporting contexts and training scenarios; AO3 (Analysis and Evaluation, 25%) — analyse data (graphs, tables), evaluate training methods or strategies, and justify recommendations; AO4 (Practical, 15%) — demonstrate relevant skills. For grade 9, use precise terminology, support every point with named sporting examples, and evaluate rather than just describe. The difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of application and depth of evaluation.
