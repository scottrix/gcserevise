PE21: Warm Up and Cool Down

Foundation Higher All Boards Paper 1

Understanding the phases, components and benefits of an effective warm up and cool down in physical activity and sport.

🔥 The Warm Up

Definition: A warm up is a period of light exercise that prepares the body for more strenuous activity. It gradually increases heart rate, body temperature and blood flow to working muscles.

An effective warm up has four key phases that should be completed in order:

  1. Pulse raiser - light aerobic activity to gradually increase heart rate
  2. Stretching - preparing muscles and joints for the range of motion required
  3. Skill practice - sport-specific movements to prepare neuromuscular pathways
  4. Mental preparation - focusing the mind on the upcoming performance

💓 Phase 1: Pulse Raiser

The pulse raiser is the first and most essential part of any warm up. It involves light aerobic activity that gradually increases the heart rate and body temperature.

  • Start with gentle jogging, cycling or skipping
  • Gradually increase the intensity over 5-10 minutes
  • Aim to raise heart rate to approximately 120-140 bpm
  • Body temperature should increase, causing mild sweating
Sporting Examples

Football: Light jog around the pitch, gradually increasing pace, side-stepping and high knees.

Swimming: Slow laps using a relaxed stroke before building intensity.

Netball: Jogging around the court with progressive shuttle runs.

🤸 Phase 2: Stretching

Stretching during the warm up prepares muscles and joints for the demands of the activity ahead. Dynamic stretching is most appropriate for warm ups.

  • Dynamic stretching - moving parts of the body through a full range of motion gradually
  • Focus on the muscle groups that will be used in the activity
  • Never stretch cold muscles - always stretch after the pulse raiser
  • Avoid ballistic (bouncing) stretches as these can cause injury
Exam Tip: Dynamic stretching is recommended for warm ups. Static stretching is more appropriate for cool downs. Make sure you know the difference.
Sporting Examples

Gymnastics: Leg swings, arm circles, and hip rotations to prepare for full range of motion.

Rugby: Walking lunges, high knees, and groin stretches for lateral movement.

⚡ Phase 3: Skill Practice

Skill practice involves performing movements that are specific to the sport or activity about to be undertaken. This prepares the neuromuscular system.

  • Practice sport-specific techniques at a low intensity
  • Gradually increase the speed and complexity of movements
  • Engage the same muscle groups and movement patterns used in performance
  • Helps develop coordination and reaction time before competition
Sporting Examples

Tennis: Gentle forehand and backhand rallies from the service line before moving back to the baseline.

Basketball: Lay-up drills, passing sequences and shooting from close range.

Athletics (sprint): Acceleration runs over 30m, practice starts from blocks.

🧠 Phase 4: Mental Preparation

Mental preparation is the final phase of the warm up. It focuses the mind on the task ahead and helps the performer get into the right psychological state.

  • Visualisation - imagining successful performance
  • Concentration - focusing on the upcoming task
  • Motivation - getting mentally ready to perform at maximum effort
  • Self-talk - using positive internal dialogue to build confidence
Mental Preparation Techniques:
Visualisation → See yourself performing successfully
Positive self-talk → "I am prepared and ready"
Concentration → Focus on the task, block out distractions
Goal focus → Remind yourself of your targets for the session

❄️ The Cool Down

Definition: A cool down is a period of light exercise after strenuous activity that allows the body to gradually return to its resting state. It helps remove waste products and speeds up recovery.

The cool down has four key phases:

  1. Maintaining elevated heart rate - keeping blood flowing at a slightly raised rate
  2. Gradual intensity reduction - slowly decreasing the workload on the body
  3. Stretching - static stretching to aid recovery and maintain flexibility
  4. Waste product removal - facilitating the clearance of lactic acid and other metabolites

📉 Cool Down Phases in Detail

Phase 1: Maintaining Elevated Heart Rate

  • Continue with light aerobic activity immediately after exercise
  • Keeps the heart rate elevated above resting level
  • Ensures blood continues to circulate efficiently
  • Prevents blood pooling in the extremities (especially legs)

Phase 2: Gradual Intensity Reduction

  • Slowly reduce the pace and intensity of movement
  • Move from jogging to walking over 5-10 minutes
  • Allows the cardiovascular system to adjust gradually
  • Avoids sudden drops in blood pressure that can cause dizziness

Phase 3: Stretching

  • Use static stretching (hold each stretch for 15-30 seconds)
  • Focus on the major muscle groups used during the activity
  • Helps muscles return to their resting length
  • Maintains and can improve flexibility over time

Phase 4: Waste Product Removal

  • Continued blood flow helps transport lactic acid to the liver
  • Lactic acid is converted back to glycogen or broken down
  • Reduces muscle soreness and stiffness (DOMS)
  • Speeds up the recovery process for the next training session

✅ Benefits of a Warm Up

Physical Benefit Explanation
Increased heart rate Delivers oxygen and nutrients to working muscles more quickly
Increased body temperature Makes muscles more pliable and reduces risk of injury
Increased blood flow More oxygen delivered to muscles, improving aerobic respiration
Faster nerve impulse conduction Improves reaction time and coordination
Increased joint range of motion Synovial fluid released, lubricating joints for freer movement
Faster muscle contraction Warm muscles contract more quickly and powerfully
Reduced risk of injury Muscles, tendons and ligaments are better prepared for stress
Mental preparation Performer focuses on the task, improving concentration and motivation

✅ Benefits of a Cool Down

Benefit Explanation
Prevents blood pooling Maintaining movement keeps blood returning to the heart, preventing dizziness
Removes lactic acid Continued oxygen delivery allows lactic acid to be broken down and removed
Reduces DOMS Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness is minimised by gradually clearing waste products
Maintains flexibility Static stretching while muscles are warm helps maintain range of motion
Gradual heart rate reduction Avoids sudden drops in blood pressure that cause faintness
Promotes relaxation Gentle activity and stretching help the body transition to a resting state

⚠️ Consequences of Skipping Warm Up or Cool Down

Skip Warm Up Skip Cool Down
Higher risk of muscle tears and strains Blood pooling in legs causing dizziness
Reduced performance due to cold muscles Lactic acid accumulation and prolonged soreness
Slower reaction times Increased DOMS the following day
Stiff joints with limited range of motion Stiffness and reduced flexibility over time
Poor mental readiness and focus Slower recovery before next session

🔬 Lactic Acid and Recovery

Lactic Acid Removal Process:
During intense exercise → Lactic acid accumulates in muscles → Causes fatigue and burning sensation
During cool down → Maintained blood flow transports lactic acid to liver → Liver converts lactic acid back to pyruvic acid → Can be used for energy or converted to glycogen
Oxygen debt repayment → Extra oxygen consumed post-exercise (EPOC) supports this process
EPOC (Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption): The elevated oxygen intake after exercise that helps repay the oxygen debt and supports the breakdown of lactic acid. An active cool down supports EPOC more effectively than complete rest.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Name the four phases of a warm up in the correct order.

Q2: Explain how a pulse raiser reduces the risk of injury during exercise.

Q3: Why is dynamic stretching preferred over static stretching in a warm up?

Q4: Describe the role of the cool down in removing lactic acid from the body.

Q5: Explain what is meant by blood pooling and how a cool down prevents it.

Q6: How does mental preparation as part of a warm up benefit a competitive performer?

✅ Answers

  1. Pulse raiser, stretching, skill practice, mental preparation.
  2. The pulse raiser gradually increases heart rate and body temperature. Warmer muscles are more pliable and elastic, reducing the risk of tears and strains. Increased blood flow delivers more oxygen to muscles and synovial fluid is released to lubricate joints.
  3. Dynamic stretching involves moving muscles through their range of motion, mimicking the movements of the upcoming activity. This actively prepares the neuromuscular system. Static stretching can actually reduce muscle power output and is better suited to cool downs.
  4. The cool down maintains an elevated heart rate and continued blood flow. This circulating blood transports lactic acid from the muscles to the liver, where it is converted back to pyruvic acid and then either used for energy or converted to glycogen for storage.
  5. Blood pooling occurs when exercise stops suddenly and blood collects in the lower extremities due to gravity, as the muscle pump is no longer active. This reduces blood return to the heart and brain, causing dizziness or fainting. A cool down maintains gentle muscular contraction, keeping the muscle pump active and blood returning to the heart.
  6. Mental preparation helps the performer focus on the task, improves concentration, builds confidence through visualisation, and ensures the performer is psychologically ready to compete at their best. It can also help manage pre-competition anxiety.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always name all four phases of the warm up in order - examiners look for this specific sequence
  • Remember: warm up = dynamic stretching, cool down = static stretching
  • When explaining benefits, link the physical change to the performance outcome (e.g. "increased temperature → more pliable muscles → reduced injury risk")
  • Use the term "lactic acid removal" not "lactic acid removal through sweating" - it is removed via blood circulation to the liver
  • Know the difference between EPOC and oxygen debt - they are related but distinct concepts
  • When asked about cool down benefits, always mention preventing blood pooling and reducing DOMS

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often say "stretching prevents injury" without explaining how. Wrong: "Stretching stops you getting injured." Correct: "Stretching increases muscle temperature and pliability, which reduces the risk of muscle tears and strains during vigorous activity."

Students often confuse dynamic and static stretching. Wrong: "Hold stretches for 30 seconds in a warm up." Correct: "Use dynamic stretching in the warm up (moving through range of motion) and static stretching in the cool down (holding stretches for 15-30 seconds)."

Students often think lactic acid is removed through sweating. Wrong: "Sweating removes lactic acid from the body." Correct: "Lactic acid is transported by the blood to the liver where it is converted back to pyruvic acid."

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think fitness and health are the same thing. Wrong: Fitness and health are the same thing Correct: Health is a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being (WHO). Fitness is the ability to meet the demands of the environment. You can be fit but unhealthy (e.g. a marathon runner with flu).

Students often think static stretching is the best warm-up. Wrong: Static stretching is the best warm-up Correct: Current evidence suggests dynamic stretching is more effective in warm-ups as it mimics sporting movements. Static stretching may actually reduce power output if held too long before activity.

Students often think no pain, no gain is a good training principle. Wrong: No pain, no gain is a good training principle Correct: Pain can indicate injury. Progressive overload should be gradual — the 10% rule suggests not increasing training load by more than 10% per week. Ignoring pain leads to overuse injuries.
