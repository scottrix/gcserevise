P26: Black Body Radiation

Higher

Black body radiation, absorption and emission of radiation, the relationship between temperature and peak emission wavelength, and Earth's radiation balance.

What is a Black Body?

A perfect black body is an object that absorbs all the radiation incident upon it. No radiation is reflected or transmitted. A perfect black body is also the best possible emitter of radiation.

  • A perfect black body absorbs all wavelengths of EM radiation that fall on it
  • Since no radiation is reflected or transmitted, it appears completely black at low temperatures
  • A perfect black body is the best possible emitter because good absorbers are also good emitters
  • Stars can be approximated as black bodies

No real object is a perfect black body, but some materials come close. Black velvet absorbs about 99% of visible light. A small hole in a heated cavity behaves almost exactly like a perfect black body.

Absorption and Emission of Radiation

All objects continually absorb and emit infrared radiation. The temperature of an object depends on the balance between the radiation it absorbs and the radiation it emits.

  • If an object absorbs more radiation than it emits, its temperature increases
  • If an object emits more radiation than it absorbs, its temperature decreases
  • If an object absorbs radiation at the same rate as it emits, its temperature stays constant (thermal equilibrium)

The type and intensity of radiation an object emits depends on its temperature. As temperature increases:

  • The total power of radiation emitted across all wavelengths increases
  • The peak wavelength of the emitted radiation decreases (shifts towards shorter wavelengths)
  • The colour of a heated object changes from red to orange to yellow to white as temperature increases

Temperature and Peak Wavelength

As the temperature of a black body increases, the peak wavelength of the emitted radiation becomes shorter. This is known as Wien's displacement law.

  • A cool object (e.g. a human body at 37 C) emits mainly long-wavelength infrared
  • A hotter object (e.g. a hot poker at 800 C) emits visible light as well as infrared, appearing red
  • At even higher temperatures (e.g. the Sun at 5800 K), the peak emission is in the visible range
  • At extremely high temperatures (e.g. hot blue stars), peak emission shifts towards ultraviolet

Black Body Radiation Curves

A black body radiation curve shows how the intensity of radiation varies with wavelength. The curve has a characteristic shape with a peak at the wavelength of maximum emission.

  • At higher temperatures, the entire curve is higher (more intense radiation at all wavelengths)
  • At higher temperatures, the peak shifts to shorter wavelengths
  • The area under the curve represents the total power emitted per unit surface area
  • At room temperature, the peak is in the infrared region and the object does not glow visibly

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

Total Power Emitted

P = sigma x A x T to the power 4

P = total power emitted (W)
sigma = Stefan-Boltzmann constant (5.67 x 10^-8 W/m2K4)
A = surface area of the object (m2)
T = absolute temperature (K)

The total power radiated by a black body is proportional to the fourth power of its absolute temperature. A small increase in temperature causes a large increase in the power radiated.

Worked Example

Calculate the total power radiated by a sphere of radius 0.1 m at a temperature of 1000 K.

Solution:

Surface area A = 4 pi r squared = 4 x 3.14 x 0.01 = 0.126 m2

P = sigma x A x T^4

P = 5.67 x 10^-8 x 0.126 x (1000)^4

P = 5.67 x 10^-8 x 0.126 x 10^12

P = 5.67 x 0.126 x 10^4 = 7144 W

Earth's Radiation Balance

The temperature of the Earth depends on the balance between the radiation it receives from the Sun and the radiation it emits back into space.

  • The Sun emits short-wavelength radiation (visible light and UV) which passes through the atmosphere
  • The Earth absorbs this radiation and warms up
  • The Earth emits long-wavelength infrared radiation back towards space
  • Some of this infrared radiation is absorbed by greenhouse gases in the atmosphere (CO2, methane, water vapour)
  • The atmosphere then re-radiates some of this energy back towards the Earth's surface (the greenhouse effect)

If the balance is disturbed (e.g. by increasing greenhouse gas concentrations), the Earth's average temperature changes. More greenhouse gases mean more infrared is absorbed and re-emitted back to the surface, causing global warming.

Practice Questions

1. Define what is meant by a perfect black body. [2 marks]

A perfect black body is an object that absorbs all the radiation incident upon it. No radiation is reflected or transmitted. It is also the best possible emitter of radiation.

2. Explain why a hot star appears blue-white while a cooler star appears red. [3 marks]

Hotter objects emit radiation with a shorter peak wavelength. A very hot star has its peak emission in the ultraviolet or blue end of the visible spectrum, so it appears blue-white. A cooler star has its peak emission at longer wavelengths in the red part of the visible spectrum.

3. Describe how the black body radiation curve changes when the temperature of an object increases. [3 marks]

As temperature increases, the intensity of radiation at all wavelengths increases (the entire curve is higher). The peak of the curve shifts to a shorter wavelength. The total area under the curve (total power emitted) increases.

4. Explain how increasing the concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere affects the Earth's temperature. [3 marks]

Greenhouse gases absorb the long-wavelength infrared radiation emitted by the Earth. They then re-emit some of this radiation back towards the Earth's surface. With higher concentrations of greenhouse gases, more infrared is trapped and re-emitted, so the Earth's surface temperature increases.

5. An object absorbs 200 W of radiation and emits 150 W. Will its temperature increase, decrease, or stay the same? Explain your answer. [2 marks]

Its temperature will increase because it is absorbing more radiation (200 W) than it is emitting (150 W). The net energy gain causes the temperature to rise until absorption and emission are balanced.

6. The Sun emits radiation with a peak wavelength in the visible region. If the Sun's temperature were to decrease, what would happen to the peak wavelength and the total power emitted? [2 marks]

The peak wavelength would increase (shift towards longer wavelengths, into the infrared) and the total power emitted would decrease because power is proportional to T to the power 4.
