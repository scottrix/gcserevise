P1: Poetry Terminology

Foundation Higher All Boards AO2

Key terms for analysing poetry - the essential vocabulary for discussing poems.

📜 Structure Terms

Stanza

A group of lines forming a section of a poem (also called a "verse").

  • Poems are divided into stanzas like prose is divided into paragraphs
  • Stanza breaks often signal shifts in idea, tone, or time
  • Number of lines per stanza can be significant
Stanza Types
Name Lines Common Use
Couplet 2 lines Emphasis, completion
Tercet 3 lines Movement, progression
Quatrain 4 lines Most common, stable
Quintain 5 lines Extended thought
Sestet 6 lines Elaboration
Octave 8 lines First part of sonnet

Enjambment

When a sentence or phrase runs over the end of a line without punctuation.

Effect of Enjambment

"I cannot rest from travel: I will drink
Life to the lees"

  • Creates forward momentum
  • Links ideas across lines
  • Reflects ongoing action or thought
  • Natural, conversational flow

Caesura

A pause within a line of poetry, usually marked by punctuation.

Effect of Caesura

"To be, or not to be: that is the question."

  • Creates pause and emphasis
  • Slows the pace
  • Adds weight to what follows
  • Reflects hesitation or decision

Line Length

Length Effect
Long lines Slows pace, allows development, more formal
Short lines Fast pace, emphatic, punchy, intense
Varied line length Creates rhythm variation, reflects emotional shifts

🎵 Sound Terms

Rhyme

Type Definition Example
End rhyme Rhyme at line endings cat/hat
Internal rhyme Rhyme within lines "Once upon a midnight dreary"
Eye rhyme Looks like it should rhyme, doesn't love/move
Half/slant rhyme Partial rhyme, similar sounds soul/all

Rhyme Schemes

Pattern of rhymes in a poem (labelled ABAB, AABB, etc.)

Common Rhyme Schemes
  • AABB - Couplet rhyme (paired lines)
  • ABAB - Alternate rhyme (interlocking)
  • ABBA - Enclosed rhyme (bracketing)
  • ABABCDCD - Ballad stanza pattern

Alliteration

Repetition of consonant sounds at the start of words.

"The solitary silent single star"
  • Creates rhythm and music
  • Links words together
  • Emphasises key words
  • Can create harsh or soft effects

Sibilance

Repetition of 's' sounds - creates hissing effect.

  • Soft, seductive effect
  • Or sinister, snake-like quality

Assonance

Repetition of vowel sounds within words.

  • Creates internal music
  • Links words across lines
Assonance Example

"The rain in Spain falls mainly on the plain"
Long 'a' sound creates musical quality.

Onomatopoeia

Words that sound like what they describe.

  • bang, crash, whisper, buzz, hiss

📐 Meter

What is Meter?

The rhythmic structure of a line - pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables.

Meter Pattern Effect
Iambic da-DUM (unstressed-stressed) Natural speech rhythm
Trochaic DA-dum (stressed-unstressed) Marching, insistent, supernatural
Anapestic da-da-DUM Gallop, forward momentum
Dactylic DA-da-dum Falling rhythm, melancholic

Number of Feet

Name Feet per Line
Monometer 1
Dimeter 2
Trimeter 3
Tetrameter 4
Pentameter 5 (most common)
Hexameter 6

Iambic Pentameter

Five iambs per line (da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM da-DUM)

Example

"Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"
shall I com-PARE thee TO a SUM-mer's DAY

Most common meter in English poetry - Shakespeare, Milton, Wordsworth.

Free Verse

No regular meter or rhyme - more like natural speech.

  • Allows flexibility and spontaneity
  • Modern form (Whitman, Eliot, contemporary poets)
  • Structure comes from line breaks, not meter

📝 Poetic Forms

Sonnet

14-line poem, usually in iambic pentameter.

Type Structure Features
Shakespearean 3 quatrains + 1 couplet (ABAB CDCD EFEF GG) Problem in quatrains, resolution in couplet
Petrarchan Octave + sestet (ABBAABBA CDECDE) Turn (volta) between octave and sestet
Why write a sonnet? Traditionally about love. Using sonnet form signals: this is serious, this is about love, this connects to poetic tradition.

Ballad

Narrative poem that tells a story, often tragic.

  • Regular rhyme and meter
  • Quatrains with ABCB rhyme
  • Simple language
  • Oral tradition - meant to be sung

Dramatic Monologue

Poem spoken by a character, revealing their personality.

  • Single speaker throughout
  • Implied listener
  • Reveals character through speech

Ode

Long poem celebrating or meditating on a subject.

  • Formal, elevated language
  • Complex stanza structures

Elegy

Poem of mourning, usually for someone who has died.

  • Reflective, melancholic tone
  • Often moves from grief to consolation

🎨 Language in Poetry

Imagery

Language that creates pictures in the reader's mind.

Type Definition Example
Visual Sight images "Golden daffodils"
Auditory Sound images "The clash of swords"
Olfactory Smell images "The scent of roses"
Tactile Touch images "Rough bark"
Gustatory Taste images "Sweet as honey"

Extended Metaphor

A metaphor developed throughout a poem, not just one line.

Example

"I'm a riddle in nine syllables" - entire poem explores pregnancy through extended metaphor of being "fat", "fine", "melon", "red fruit".

Personification

Giving human qualities to non-human things.

  • Makes abstract concepts concrete
  • Creates emotional connection

Symbolism

An object that represents a larger idea.

  • Rose = love
  • Dove = peace
  • Cross = faith

✍️ Using Terminology

Don't Just Name It

Golden Rule: Naming a technique scores zero marks. You must explain its EFFECT.
Good vs Poor Analysis

Poor: "The poet uses enjambment."

Good: "The enjambment between lines 3 and 4 creates a sense of ongoing movement, reflecting the river's continuous flow."

Useful Effect Words

  • Creates, suggests, implies, emphasises, highlights, reinforces
  • Echoes, mirrors, reflects, contrasts with
  • Builds, develops, intensifies, slows, accelerates

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What is enjambment and what effect can it create?

Q2: What's the difference between a caesura and an end-stopped line?

Q3: What are the features of a Shakespearean sonnet?

Q4: What is the effect of sibilance?

✅ Answers

  1. Enjambment is when a sentence runs over the end of a line without punctuation. Effects: creates forward momentum, links ideas, reflects continuous action or thought, sounds natural and conversational.
  2. Caesura is a pause WITHIN a line (usually with punctuation). End-stopped lines have punctuation AT the end. Caesura slows within the line; end-stopped lines complete the thought before the next.
  3. Shakespearean sonnet: 14 lines, iambic pentameter, ABAB CDCD EFEF GG rhyme scheme. Three quatrains present problem; final couplet provides resolution or twist.
  4. Sibilance (repeated 's' sounds) can create: soft, seductive effect; sinister, snake-like quality; whispering or hushing sound; emphasis on certain words through the hissing sound.
