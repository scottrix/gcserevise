P2: Comparing Poems

Foundation Higher All Boards AO1 AO2 AO3

How to compare poems effectively - identifying similarities and differences.

📋 Understanding Comparison Questions

What You're Asked to Do

Comparison questions ask you to:

  • Analyse both poems
  • Identify similarities AND differences
  • Discuss methods, themes, and contexts
  • Show how poets present ideas differently
Key Point: Don't just write about one poem then the other. Weave them together - compare as you go.

What to Compare

Element What to Look For
Themes What ideas do both poems explore? How differently?
Methods Structure, language, form - how do poets achieve effects?
Tone Attitude of speaker - similar or contrasting?
Context Historical/social background - how does it shape meaning?
Perspective Who's speaking? First person? Omniscient?

🔍 Approaches to Comparison

Method 1: Alternating (Recommended)

Move between poems in each paragraph:

Alternating Structure

Paragraph 1 (Theme X):

  • Point about Poem A
  • Point about Poem B - compare/contrast

Paragraph 2 (Method Y):

  • Point about Poem A
  • Point about Poem B - compare/contrast

Method 2: Integrated

Discuss both poems simultaneously in every sentence:

Integrated Sentence

"While Poet A uses natural imagery to convey love, Poet B employs urban metaphors, suggesting different views of relationships."

Method 3: Whole-Then-Whole (Avoid)

Writing about Poem A completely, then Poem B - this scores lower because it doesn't compare.

Why Alternating Works: Examiners can see you're comparing throughout. It's easier to track similarities and differences. You cover both poems equally.

📊 What to Compare: Themes

Identifying Shared Themes

Most comparison questions pair poems with related themes. Start by identifying what connects them:

Poem Pair Theme Questions to Ask
Love Is it romantic, familial, platonic? Happy or tragic? Idealised or realistic?
War Is it glorified or condemned? Focus on soldiers, civilians, or aftermath?
Nature Is nature beautiful or threatening? What's the human relationship to nature?
Identity How is identity defined? Through memory, place, relationships?
Power Who has power? Is it used justly or corruptly? How is it presented?
Theme Comparison Example

"Ozymandias" and "London" both explore power and corruption.

  • Similarity: Both show power as destructive and ultimately futile
  • Difference: "Ozymandias" shows ancient, fallen power; "London" shows contemporary, systemic oppression

🎨 What to Compare: Methods

Form and Structure

Compare how poems are shaped:

Element Poem A Poem B
Form Sonnet (strict, traditional) Free verse (flexible, modern)
Stanza structure Regular quatrains (orderly) Irregular stanzas (chaotic)
Rhyme Regular rhyme (controlled) No rhyme (conversational)
Enjambment Limited (contained emotion) Frequent (overflowing feeling)

Language Techniques

Compare specific methods:

Comparing Language

Poem A: "The river runs like a silver thread" (simile, natural imagery)

Poem B: "The city pulses with electric veins" (metaphor, urban imagery)

Comparison: Both use imagery of flowing movement, but Poem A connects to nature while Poem B uses industrial metaphor. This reflects different attitudes to progress.

🎭 What to Compare: Tone

Identifying Tone

Tone Definition Example Words
Angry Furious, condemning "Damn you", "rotten", "curse"
Melancholic Sad, reflective, mournful "Lost", "faded", "empty"
Celebratory Joyful, praising "Beautiful", "radiant", "glorious"
Ironic Saying opposite of meaning "Wonderful" meaning terrible
Detached Clinical, observational "They", "one observes", factual

Tone Shifts

Track how tone changes within poems:

Comparing Tone Shifts
  • Poem A: Begins angry, ends resigned (acceptance)
  • Poem B: Begins hopeful, ends despairing (defeat)
  • Comparison: Both poems show emotional journey, but Poem A moves toward peace while Poem B moves toward greater pain.

🌍 What to Compare: Context

Historical Context

When were poems written? What was happening?

Period Context Effect on Poetry
Romantic (late 1700s-1800s) Industrial Revolution Nature vs industry, individual emotion
Victorian (1837-1901) Science vs religion Faith, doubt, social issues
World War I (1914-18) Mass warfare Anti-war, disillusionment
Modern/Contemporary Post-war, multiculturalism Identity, fragmentation

Personal Context

Poet's biography and experiences:

Personal Context Comparison
  • Poet A: Upper-class, wrote from comfort about war
  • Poet B: Working-class soldier, wrote from trenches
  • Comparison: Different perspectives based on lived experience. Poet A idealises; Poet B witnesses.

🔗 Comparative Connectives

Similarities

Use these words to show agreement:

  • Similarly, Likewise, In the same way
  • Both poems...
  • Like [Poet A], [Poet B]...
  • Equally, Also

Differences

Use these words to show contrast:

  • However, In contrast, Whereas
  • While [Poem A]..., [Poem B]...
  • Unlike [Poet A], [Poet B]...
  • On the other hand, Conversely
Using Connectives

"Both poets use nature imagery to convey love. However, while Rossetti uses soft, gentle images of flowers, Barrett Browning employs grander natural metaphors of sun and stars, suggesting a more elevated view of romantic love."

✍️ Writing a Comparison Essay

Structure

Introduction:
Name both poems, poets, and themes. State your comparison focus.

3-4 Paragraphs:
Each paragraph compares ONE element across BOTH poems.

Conclusion:
Summarise key similarities and differences. Evaluate significance.

Sample Paragraph

Model Comparison Paragraph

Both poets explore the theme of lost love, but use contrasting imagery to convey different attitudes. In "When You Are Old," Yeats employs gentle imagery of "soft look" and "shadows deep," creating a melancholic, romantic tone that idealises the lost relationship. Similarly, Duffy in "Havisham" writes about lost love, but uses violent imagery of "dark green pebbles" and "ropes that on the back of my hands I could strangle with." While Yeats's poem mourns what was lost with tenderness, Duffy's speaker seethes with destructive rage. This contrast reflects their different contexts: Yeats wrote as a rejected suitor, expressing regret; Duffy writes from Miss Havisham's perspective, capturing her frozen moment of betrayal. Both poets use first-person perspective, but Yeats addresses the beloved directly, whereas Duffy's speaker is isolated in her trauma.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

Mistake Fix
Writing about poems separately Weave them together in each paragraph
Listing similarities, ignoring differences Balance both - differences often more interesting
Comparing only content, not methods Include language, structure, form in comparison
Forgetting context Link poems to when/why they were written
Saying "this poem is better" Don't evaluate quality - analyse methods

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What's the difference between alternating and integrated comparison?

Q2: Name three elements you should compare between poems.

Q3: Why should you include both similarities and differences?

Q4: Give an example of a comparative connective for similarity and one for difference.

✅ Answers

  1. Alternating: discuss one aspect, then compare both poems on it, then move to next aspect. Integrated: discuss both poems in every sentence. Alternating is clearer; integrated shows stronger comparison skills.
  2. Elements to compare: themes, methods (language/form/structure), tone, context, perspective, setting, imagery.
  3. Both are required for a full comparison. Similarities show shared understanding; differences show nuanced analysis. A comparison that only lists similarities is incomplete.
  4. Similarity: "Similarly", "Both", "Likewise", "In the same way". Difference: "However", "In contrast", "Whereas", "Unlike", "On the other hand".
