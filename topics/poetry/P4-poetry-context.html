P4: Poetry Context

Foundation Higher All Boards AO3

Understanding context in poetry - historical period, poet's biography, social background.

📋 Why Context Matters

Context helps you understand:

  • Why the poet wrote the poem
  • What influenced their perspective
  • How original audiences would have understood it
  • Deeper meanings beyond surface reading
AO3 Weighting: Context is worth 15-25% of marks. Always link context to specific analysis, never just list facts.

📜 Historical Period

Romantic Era (late 1700s-1800s)

Key features:

  • Industrial Revolution: Urbanisation, pollution, loss of rural life
  • Nature worship: Nature as spiritual, pure, threatened
  • Individual emotion: Personal feeling valued over reason
  • Revolution: French Revolution influenced ideas about freedom
Romantic Poets

Wordsworth, Blake, Shelley, Keats, Byron - wrote about nature, emotion, and social injustice.

Victorian Era (1837-1901)

Key features:

  • Science vs Religion: Darwin's evolution challenged faith
  • Social reform: Poverty, child labour, class inequality
  • Empire: British expansion and its effects
  • Gender roles: Women's rights emerging

World War I (1914-1918)

Key features:

  • Mass warfare: First industrialised war, unprecedented casualties
  • Lost generation: Young men decimated
  • Disillusionment: Patriotic myths shattered by reality
  • Trauma: Shell shock, survivors' guilt
War Poets

Owen, Sassoon, Rosenberg - wrote from trenches, challenged glorification of war.

Modern/Contemporary (Post-1945)

Key features:

  • Multiculturalism: Immigration, identity, belonging
  • Gender equality: Feminism, changing roles
  • Fragmentation: Loss of certainty, multiple perspectives
  • Personal voice: Confessional poetry, autobiography

👤 Poet's Biography

Why Biography Matters

A poet's life experiences shape their work:

Poet Key Life Events Influence on Poetry
Wilfred Owen Trench warfare, shell shock Anti-war poetry, graphic realism
Sylvia Plath Depression, suicide attempts Confessional poetry, intense emotion
William Blake Working-class London, visionary Social criticism, mystical imagery
Caution: Don't reduce poems to autobiography. Use biography to illuminate, not define meaning.

🌍 Social Context

Class and Society

Consider:

  • What class was the poet? How does this affect perspective?
  • What social issues does the poem address?
  • Who is the intended audience?
Class in Poetry

Upper-class poets (e.g., Brooke) often wrote romanticised war poetry. Working-class poets (e.g., Rosenburg) wrote from direct experience. Class shapes perspective.

Gender Context

Consider:

  • When was this written? What were women's roles?
  • Is the poet challenging or reinforcing gender norms?
  • How might female experience differ from male?

Race and Identity

Consider:

  • Is the poet from a marginalised group?
  • How does identity shape the poem's perspective?
  • What experiences of racism, displacement, belonging?

📚 Literary Movement

Poetic Traditions

Movement Key Features Representative Poets
Romanticism Nature, emotion, individual Wordsworth, Keats, Shelley
Victorian Formal structure, moral purpose Tennyson, Browning, Arnold
Modernism Fragmentation, experimentation Eliot, Pound, H.D.
Confessional Personal experience, taboo subjects Plath, Sexton, Lowell

Response to Tradition

Ask: Is this poem following a tradition or challenging it?

Example

A modern poet writing a sonnet about war (not love) is using traditional form for contemporary, subversive purpose. This contrast creates meaning.

🔗 Anthology Context

Understanding the Anthology

Your anthology is organised around themes. Each poem relates to the theme differently:

Anthology Theme Key Poems
AQA Power and Conflict Power, its abuse, conflict Ozymandias, My Last Duchess, War Photographer
AQA Love and Relationships Love in various forms Porphyria's Lover, Sonnet 29, Neutral Tones
Edexcel Time and Place Landscape, identity, memory Various location-based poems

Why Anthology Context Matters

  • Poems are selected to show different perspectives
  • Theme shapes interpretation
  • Comparisons are expected within anthology

✍️ Using Context Effectively

The Golden Rule

Never bolt context on: "This poem was written in 1917 during WW1. Owen shows..."
Integrate context: "Owen's graphic imagery of 'gargling blood' reflects the brutal reality of trench warfare, challenging the patriotic lies told to families at home."

Useful Phrases

  • "Written during [period], this reflects..."
  • "The poet's experience of [X] shapes..."
  • "For contemporary audiences, this would have..."
  • "This challenges the [movement] tradition by..."

⚠️ Common Mistakes

Mistake Fix
Bolting on context facts Link every context point to a quotation
Over-biographising Poem isn't just autobiography - analyse the text
Forgetting audience Consider how original vs modern audiences respond
Generic context Be specific: not just "Victorian times" but "Victorian fears about science vs religion"

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What is the difference between historical context and biographical context?

Q2: Why were Romantic poets interested in nature?

Q3: How does World War I context affect war poetry?

Q4: What's the difference between bolting on context and integrating it?

✅ Answers

  1. Historical context = the time period, events, social conditions when written. Biographical context = the poet's personal life, experiences, background. Both shape the poem but in different ways.
  2. Romantic poets wrote during the Industrial Revolution, which threatened rural life and nature. They saw nature as spiritual, pure, and worth preserving against industrial destruction.
  3. WWI was the first industrialised mass war. Poets like Owen wrote from trenches, challenging glorification. The context of unprecedented casualties, shell shock, and government propaganda shaped anti-war poetry.
  4. Bolting on: listing context facts without connecting ("This was written in 1917"). Integrating: linking context to specific analysis ("Owen's imagery reflects the brutal reality of trench warfare").
