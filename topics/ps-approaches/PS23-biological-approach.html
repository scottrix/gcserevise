The Biological Approach

The biological approach to psychology: how genes, the nervous system and biochemistry influence behaviour. The nature side of the nature-nurture debate and evaluation of biological explanations.

Key Fact: The biological approach assumes all behaviour has a physical cause in the brain, nervous system, genes and biochemistry. Behaviour can be explained in terms of biological structures and processes.
Key Fact: Genetic basis of behaviour: genes inherited from parents influence characteristics. Twin studies compare MZ (identical, 100% genes shared) with DZ (fraternal, 50% shared) twins. If MZ concordance is higher, genetic influence is likely. Example: McGuffin et al. found 37% MZ vs 15% DZ concordance for depression.
Key Fact: Evolution and behaviour: behaviours that enhanced survival and reproduction were naturally selected. Evolutionary explanations account for: fight-or-flight (adaptive for physical threats), attachment (infants who stayed close to caregivers survived), phobias (fear of snakes/spiders was adaptive).
Key Fact: Neurochemistry: neurotransmitters influence mood and behaviour. Serotonin (mood, aggression), dopamine (reward, motivation), adrenaline (fight-or-flight). Imbalances are linked to mental health conditions.
Key Fact: Brain structure: different brain regions have different functions (localisation). Damage to specific areas causes specific deficits (Broca's aphasia, anterograde amnesia). The brain's structure determines behaviour.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the key assumptions of the biological approach and explain how genes influence behaviour.

Q2: Evaluate the biological approach, considering its strengths and limitations.

Q3: Discuss whether behaviour is determined by biology, referring to the nature-nurture debate.

✅ Answers

  1. The biological approach assumes behaviour is caused by biological processes: genes (inherited predispositions), neurochemistry (neurotransmitter activity), brain structure (localised functions), and evolution (naturally selected behaviours). Genes influence behaviour through heredity - twin studies show higher MZ than DZ concordance for many traits, suggesting genetic influence.
  2. Strengths: scientific (uses objective methods like brain scanning, drug trials, twin studies), practical applications (drug treatments based on neurochemistry), has predictive power (identifies risk factors). Limitations: reductionist (reduces complex behaviour to genes and chemicals), deterministic (implies free will is an illusion), ignores environmental influences (cannot explain why MZ twins differ), ethical issues (genetic research raises concerns about labelling and discrimination).
  3. Nature argument: behaviour is determined by biology (genes, brain, neurochemistry). Supported by: twin studies, evolution, brain damage case studies. Nurture argument: behaviour is shaped by experience. Supported by: cultural differences, learning theory, plasticity. Conclusion: both contribute - the diathesis-stress model shows genetic vulnerability interacts with environmental triggers. Neither nature nor nurture alone fully explains behaviour.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Biological approach: behaviour = GENES + BRAIN + NEUROCHEMISTRY + EVOLUTION.
  • MZ twins share 100% genes. DZ share 50%. Higher MZ concordance = genetic influence.
  • Strengths = scientific + practical treatments. Weaknesses = reductionist + deterministic + ignores environment.

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating the biological approach, always consider: is it reductionist? Is it deterministic? Does it ignore important environmental factors? Balance the scientific credibility against the limitations of reducing complex behaviour to biology.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Our behaviour is entirely determined by our genes and brain chemistry, so we cannot change how we act. Correct: The biological approach is biologically deterministic - it assumes behaviour is caused by internal biological factors beyond our control. However, this ignores: environmental influences (upbringing, culture, learning), cognitive factors (our thoughts and decisions), and neuroplasticity (the brain changes in response to experience). Gene expression depends on environmental triggers (epigenetics). While biology creates predispositions, it does not determine behaviour absolutely - people can and do change through therapy, learning, and personal choice.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the biological approach as an explanation for human behaviour.

A grade 9 response will: outline key assumptions (genes, neurochemistry, brain structure, evolution); present supporting evidence (twin studies, brain scanning, drug effectiveness); evaluate strengths (scientific, practical applications, testable); evaluate limitations (reductionist, deterministic, ignores environment, cannot explain MZ twin differences); consider the interactionist alternative (diathesis-stress, biopsychosocial); conclude: the biological approach provides valuable scientific insights and effective treatments but is incomplete alone - behaviour is best understood through the interaction of biological, psychological and social factors.

Detailed Notes

Key Studies and Theories in The Biological Approach

The Biological Approach is a core topic in GCSE Psychology that requires understanding of key studies, theories and research methods. The AQA, OCR and Edexcel specifications all require you to know specific studies in detail, evaluate their methodology, and apply psychological concepts to real-life scenarios. When studying the biological approach, focus on: the aims, methods, results and conclusions of key studies; the strengths and weaknesses of the research methods used; and how psychological understanding of the biological approach applies to everyday life.

Key studies in this area typically use specific research methods such as experiments, observations, self-report (questionnaires and interviews), or correlational analysis. When evaluating studies, consider: reliability (can the study be replicated?); validity (does it measure what it claims to measure?); ethics (were participants protected from harm?); generalisability (can the findings be applied beyond the study sample?); and usefulness (does the research have practical applications?).

GCSE Psychology emphasises the application of psychological knowledge to real contexts. You must be able to explain how understanding of the biological approach can be used to improve people’s lives, influence policy, or solve practical problems.

GCSE Example: Key Studies and Theories in The Biological Approach

When writing about a study related to the biological approach, use the AMRC format: Aim (what was the study trying to find out?), Method (how was it conducted — design, sample, procedure?), Results (what were the key findings?), Conclusions (what do the results mean?).

Evaluating Research in The Biological Approach

Evaluation is a crucial skill in GCSE Psychology. When evaluating studies related to the biological approach, use the GRAVE method: Generalisability (can the findings be applied to the wider population?), Reliability (are the results consistent if the study is repeated?), Applications (how can the findings be used in real life?), Validity (does the study measure what it claims to measure?), and Ethics (were participants treated ethically?).

Research methods commonly used in the biological approach research include: laboratory experiments (high control but low ecological validity), field experiments (more realistic but less control), natural experiments (no manipulation of variables), case studies (in-depth but not generalisable), questionnaires (large samples but social desirability bias), and observations (natural behaviour but ethical concerns). Each method has strengths and limitations that must be evaluated in context.

Ethical issues in the biological approach research include: informed consent (participants must agree to take part), debriefing (explaining the true purpose after the study), confidentiality (protecting participants’ identities), protection from harm (no physical or psychological damage), and right to withdraw (participants can leave at any time). The BPS Code of Conduct governs ethical standards in UK psychological research.

GCSE Example: Evaluating Research in The Biological Approach

When evaluating Milgram’s obedience study, you might note: low generalisability (all male, American sample); high reliability (standardised procedure with 65% obedience rate); useful applications (understanding wartime atrocities); low ecological validity (artificial laboratory setting); and serious ethical concerns (deception, psychological distress).

Applying The Biological Approach to Real Life

GCSE Psychology requires you to apply psychological understanding of the biological approach to everyday situations. Consider how the theories and studies you have learned about explain real behaviour: How does the biological approach affect people in schools, workplaces, families and communities? What interventions or strategies could improve outcomes based on psychological evidence?

When applying the biological approach to real-life scenarios, use the following structure: identify the relevant psychological concept, explain how it applies to the scenario, support your explanation with evidence from a named study, and evaluate the strength of the application (are there limitations or alternative explanations?).

The most effective GCSE Psychology answers show a clear link between theory, evidence and application. Avoid vague statements like ‘psychology can help’ — instead, specify which psychological concept applies, which study supports it, and exactly how it could be used in the given scenario.

GCSE Example: Applying The Biological Approach to Real Life

A strong application answer: ‘Understanding of the biological approach could help in schools by [specific application]. This is supported by [named study] which found that [specific finding]. This means that [specific practical recommendation]. However, a limitation is [specific weakness of applying this research].’
