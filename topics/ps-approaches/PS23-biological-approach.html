The Biological Approach

The biological approach to psychology: how genes, the nervous system and biochemistry influence behaviour. The nature side of the nature-nurture debate and evaluation of biological explanations.

Key Fact: The biological approach assumes all behaviour has a physical cause in the brain, nervous system, genes and biochemistry. Behaviour can be explained in terms of biological structures and processes.
Key Fact: Genetic basis of behaviour: genes inherited from parents influence characteristics. Twin studies compare MZ (identical, 100% genes shared) with DZ (fraternal, 50% shared) twins. If MZ concordance is higher, genetic influence is likely. Example: McGuffin et al. found 37% MZ vs 15% DZ concordance for depression.
Key Fact: Evolution and behaviour: behaviours that enhanced survival and reproduction were naturally selected. Evolutionary explanations account for: fight-or-flight (adaptive for physical threats), attachment (infants who stayed close to caregivers survived), phobias (fear of snakes/spiders was adaptive).
Key Fact: Neurochemistry: neurotransmitters influence mood and behaviour. Serotonin (mood, aggression), dopamine (reward, motivation), adrenaline (fight-or-flight). Imbalances are linked to mental health conditions.
Key Fact: Brain structure: different brain regions have different functions (localisation). Damage to specific areas causes specific deficits (Broca's aphasia, anterograde amnesia). The brain's structure determines behaviour.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the key assumptions of the biological approach and explain how genes influence behaviour.

Q2: Evaluate the biological approach, considering its strengths and limitations.

Q3: Discuss whether behaviour is determined by biology, referring to the nature-nurture debate.

✅ Answers

  1. The biological approach assumes behaviour is caused by biological processes: genes (inherited predispositions), neurochemistry (neurotransmitter activity), brain structure (localised functions), and evolution (naturally selected behaviours). Genes influence behaviour through heredity - twin studies show higher MZ than DZ concordance for many traits, suggesting genetic influence.
  2. Strengths: scientific (uses objective methods like brain scanning, drug trials, twin studies), practical applications (drug treatments based on neurochemistry), has predictive power (identifies risk factors). Limitations: reductionist (reduces complex behaviour to genes and chemicals), deterministic (implies free will is an illusion), ignores environmental influences (cannot explain why MZ twins differ), ethical issues (genetic research raises concerns about labelling and discrimination).
  3. Nature argument: behaviour is determined by biology (genes, brain, neurochemistry). Supported by: twin studies, evolution, brain damage case studies. Nurture argument: behaviour is shaped by experience. Supported by: cultural differences, learning theory, plasticity. Conclusion: both contribute - the diathesis-stress model shows genetic vulnerability interacts with environmental triggers. Neither nature nor nurture alone fully explains behaviour.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Biological approach: behaviour = GENES + BRAIN + NEUROCHEMISTRY + EVOLUTION.
  • MZ twins share 100% genes. DZ share 50%. Higher MZ concordance = genetic influence.
  • Strengths = scientific + practical treatments. Weaknesses = reductionist + deterministic + ignores environment.

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating the biological approach, always consider: is it reductionist? Is it deterministic? Does it ignore important environmental factors? Balance the scientific credibility against the limitations of reducing complex behaviour to biology.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Our behaviour is entirely determined by our genes and brain chemistry, so we cannot change how we act. Correct: The biological approach is biologically deterministic - it assumes behaviour is caused by internal biological factors beyond our control. However, this ignores: environmental influences (upbringing, culture, learning), cognitive factors (our thoughts and decisions), and neuroplasticity (the brain changes in response to experience). Gene expression depends on environmental triggers (epigenetics). While biology creates predispositions, it does not determine behaviour absolutely - people can and do change through therapy, learning, and personal choice.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the biological approach as an explanation for human behaviour.

A grade 9 response will: outline key assumptions (genes, neurochemistry, brain structure, evolution); present supporting evidence (twin studies, brain scanning, drug effectiveness); evaluate strengths (scientific, practical applications, testable); evaluate limitations (reductionist, deterministic, ignores environment, cannot explain MZ twin differences); consider the interactionist alternative (diathesis-stress, biopsychosocial); conclude: the biological approach provides valuable scientific insights and effective treatments but is incomplete alone - behaviour is best understood through the interaction of biological, psychological and social factors.
