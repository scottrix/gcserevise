The Cognitive Approach

The cognitive approach: how mental processes influence behaviour. The role of schemas, cognitive biases, and the computer metaphor. Evaluation and comparison with the biological approach.

Key Fact: The cognitive approach focuses on internal mental processes: perception, memory, thinking, problem-solving, attention, and language. It treats the mind like a computer: input (sensory information) -> processing (thinking) -> output (behaviour).
Key Fact: Schemas: mental frameworks that organise knowledge and expectations. They help us process information quickly but can lead to biases and errors. Schemas develop through experience and affect how we interpret new information (e.g. Bartlett's 'War of the Ghosts' study showed participants distorted a story to fit their cultural schemas).
Key Fact: Cognitive biases: systematic errors in thinking that affect decisions and judgements. Confirmation bias: seeking information that confirms existing beliefs. Anchoring: relying too heavily on the first piece of information. Hindsight bias: 'I knew it all along' after an event. These biases are studied in forensic psychology and economics.
Key Fact: The cognitive approach and mental health: Beck's negative triad (negative views of self, world, future) maintains depression. Ellis's ABC model: Activating event -> Beliefs -> Consequences. Irrational beliefs cause emotional problems. CBT directly applies cognitive principles to treat mental health conditions.
Key Fact: Cognitive neuroscience: combines cognitive psychology with brain scanning (fMRI, EEG) to identify which brain areas are involved in specific mental processes. This bridges the gap between the cognitive and biological approaches.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the key assumptions of the cognitive approach and explain how schemas influence behaviour.

Q2: Evaluate the cognitive approach, comparing it with the biological approach.

Q3: Explain how cognitive biases affect decision-making, giving examples.

✅ Answers

  1. The cognitive approach assumes behaviour is shaped by internal mental processes (thinking, memory, perception). The mind works like a computer: input -> processing -> output. Schemas organise knowledge and help process information quickly, but can lead to distortion (Bartlett's study showed cultural schemas altered recall). Cognitive biases (confirmation bias, anchoring, hindsight bias) affect decisions systematically.
  2. Cognitive strengths: scientific (uses lab experiments, brain scanning), practical applications (CBT is highly effective), explains internal processes (not just observable behaviour). Limitations: machine reductionism (the mind is more than a computer), ignores biological factors (emotion, hormones), relies on inference (we cannot directly observe mental processes). Compared to biological: cognitive explains thinking, biological explains brain processes; both are needed. Cognitive neuroscience combines both.
  3. Confirmation bias: we notice and remember information that supports our beliefs, ignoring contradictory evidence. Example: if you believe left-handed people are creative, you notice creative left-handers but ignore creative right-handers. Anchoring: first information disproportionately influences judgements. Example: a shop shows a high 'was' price next to a lower price, making the lower price seem like a bargain. Hindsight bias: after an event, we believe we 'knew it would happen'.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Cognitive approach: mind = INFORMATION PROCESSOR. Input -> Processing -> Output.
  • Schemas = mental frameworks. They HELP (quick processing) but can DISTORT (bias, stereotyping).
  • Cognitive strengths = scientific + CBT. Limitations = machine reductionism + ignores biology + based on inference.

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating the cognitive approach, consider: is it too mechanistic? Does it ignore emotion and biology? Are its methods scientific enough? Compare with the biological approach to show how they complement each other.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The cognitive approach is more scientific than other approaches because it uses experiments and brain scanning. Correct: While the cognitive approach uses scientific methods, it relies heavily on inference - we cannot directly observe mental processes like thoughts or schemas. We can only infer them from behaviour and brain activity. This is less direct than biological measures (e.g. neurotransmitter levels). However, cognitive neuroscience is improving this by combining cognitive models with brain scanning, providing more objective evidence for mental processes.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the cognitive approach as an explanation for human behaviour, comparing it with the biological approach.

A grade 9 response will: outline cognitive assumptions (internal mental processes, information processing, schemas); present strengths (scientific methods, effective applications like CBT, explains internal processes); present limitations (machine reductionism, ignores emotion and biology, relies on inference); compare with biological (cognitive explains thinking, biological explains brain, both are needed); conclude: the cognitive approach has transformed psychology by studying internal mental processes and developing effective therapies, but it is incomplete alone. Cognitive neuroscience combines the strengths of both approaches for a more complete understanding.

Detailed Notes

Key Studies and Theories in The Cognitive Approach

The Cognitive Approach is a core topic in GCSE Psychology that requires understanding of key studies, theories and research methods. The AQA, OCR and Edexcel specifications all require you to know specific studies in detail, evaluate their methodology, and apply psychological concepts to real-life scenarios. When studying the cognitive approach, focus on: the aims, methods, results and conclusions of key studies; the strengths and weaknesses of the research methods used; and how psychological understanding of the cognitive approach applies to everyday life.

Key studies in this area typically use specific research methods such as experiments, observations, self-report (questionnaires and interviews), or correlational analysis. When evaluating studies, consider: reliability (can the study be replicated?); validity (does it measure what it claims to measure?); ethics (were participants protected from harm?); generalisability (can the findings be applied beyond the study sample?); and usefulness (does the research have practical applications?).

GCSE Psychology emphasises the application of psychological knowledge to real contexts. You must be able to explain how understanding of the cognitive approach can be used to improve people’s lives, influence policy, or solve practical problems.

GCSE Example: Key Studies and Theories in The Cognitive Approach

When writing about a study related to the cognitive approach, use the AMRC format: Aim (what was the study trying to find out?), Method (how was it conducted — design, sample, procedure?), Results (what were the key findings?), Conclusions (what do the results mean?).

Evaluating Research in The Cognitive Approach

Evaluation is a crucial skill in GCSE Psychology. When evaluating studies related to the cognitive approach, use the GRAVE method: Generalisability (can the findings be applied to the wider population?), Reliability (are the results consistent if the study is repeated?), Applications (how can the findings be used in real life?), Validity (does the study measure what it claims to measure?), and Ethics (were participants treated ethically?).

Research methods commonly used in the cognitive approach research include: laboratory experiments (high control but low ecological validity), field experiments (more realistic but less control), natural experiments (no manipulation of variables), case studies (in-depth but not generalisable), questionnaires (large samples but social desirability bias), and observations (natural behaviour but ethical concerns). Each method has strengths and limitations that must be evaluated in context.

Ethical issues in the cognitive approach research include: informed consent (participants must agree to take part), debriefing (explaining the true purpose after the study), confidentiality (protecting participants’ identities), protection from harm (no physical or psychological damage), and right to withdraw (participants can leave at any time). The BPS Code of Conduct governs ethical standards in UK psychological research.

GCSE Example: Evaluating Research in The Cognitive Approach

When evaluating Milgram’s obedience study, you might note: low generalisability (all male, American sample); high reliability (standardised procedure with 65% obedience rate); useful applications (understanding wartime atrocities); low ecological validity (artificial laboratory setting); and serious ethical concerns (deception, psychological distress).

Applying The Cognitive Approach to Real Life

GCSE Psychology requires you to apply psychological understanding of the cognitive approach to everyday situations. Consider how the theories and studies you have learned about explain real behaviour: How does the cognitive approach affect people in schools, workplaces, families and communities? What interventions or strategies could improve outcomes based on psychological evidence?

When applying the cognitive approach to real-life scenarios, use the following structure: identify the relevant psychological concept, explain how it applies to the scenario, support your explanation with evidence from a named study, and evaluate the strength of the application (are there limitations or alternative explanations?).

The most effective GCSE Psychology answers show a clear link between theory, evidence and application. Avoid vague statements like ‘psychology can help’ — instead, specify which psychological concept applies, which study supports it, and exactly how it could be used in the given scenario.

GCSE Example: Applying The Cognitive Approach to Real Life

A strong application answer: ‘Understanding of the cognitive approach could help in schools by [specific application]. This is supported by [named study] which found that [specific finding]. This means that [specific practical recommendation]. However, a limitation is [specific weakness of applying this research].’
