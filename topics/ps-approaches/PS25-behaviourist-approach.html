The Behaviourist & Social Learning Approach

The behaviourist approach: how we learn through conditioning (classical and operant) and observation (social learning theory). Bandura, Pavlov and Skinner. Evaluation of behaviourism.

Key Fact: Classical conditioning (Pavlov): learning by association. A neutral stimulus becomes associated with an unconditioned stimulus, producing a conditioned response. Pavlov's dogs: bell (NS) paired with food (UCS) -> salivation (UCR). After conditioning: bell alone (CS) -> salivation (CR). Explains phobias and emotional responses.
Key Fact: Operant conditioning (Skinner): learning by consequences. Positive reinforcement: adding something pleasant to increase behaviour (reward). Negative reinforcement: removing something unpleasant to increase behaviour (taking painkillers removes pain). Punishment: adding something unpleasant to decrease behaviour. Skinner's Box: rats pressed a lever for food (positive reinforcement).
Key Fact: Social Learning Theory (Bandura): we learn by observing others. Key processes: attention (notice the behaviour), retention (remember it), reproduction (ability to copy it), motivation (want to copy it - influenced by vicarious reinforcement). Bandura's Bobo doll study: children who saw an aggressive model were significantly more aggressive.
Key Fact: Mediational processes in SLT distinguish it from pure behaviourism: SLT acknowledges cognitive processes (attention, retention, motivation) mediate between stimulus and response. This makes SLT more comprehensive than traditional behaviourism.
Key Fact: Applications: classical conditioning explains phobias and underpins systematic desensitisation. Operant conditioning explains token economies in institutions. SLT explains how aggression and gender roles are learned from media and role models.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe classical conditioning and operant conditioning, using examples from psychological research.

Q2: Explain Bandura's Social Learning Theory and evaluate how well it accounts for observational learning.

Q3: Compare the behaviourist approach with the cognitive approach, evaluating their relative contributions.

✅ Answers

  1. Classical conditioning (Pavlov): learning by association. NS paired with UCS produces CR. Explains phobias (Little Albert - white rat paired with loud noise = fear of rat and similar objects). Operant conditioning (Skinner): learning by consequences. Positive reinforcement increases behaviour (rewards); punishment decreases it. Explains maintained behaviours (addiction, token economies).
  2. SLT: we learn by observing models. Process: Attention -> Retention -> Reproduction -> Motivation. Bandura's Bobo doll: children who observed aggressive models imitated aggression (67% of boys in aggressive condition vs 0% in control). Evaluation: explains learning without direct experience; includes cognitive processes; supported by research. Limitations: lab study (low ecological validity), Bobo doll is designed to be hit (demand characteristics), doesn't fully explain why some children don't imitate.
  3. Behaviourism: observable behaviour only, learning through conditioning, environmental determinism. Cognitive: internal mental processes, information processing, schemas. Behaviourism strengths: scientific (observable), practical applications (systematic desensitisation, token economies). Limitations: ignores thinking, machine-like, environmental determinism. Cognitive strengths: explains thinking, CBT applications. Limitations: ignores learning from environment. Both contribute: SLT bridges the gap by including cognitive mediational processes.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Classical conditioning = ASSOCIATION (Pavlov's dogs). Operant conditioning = CONSEQUENCES (Skinner's rats).
  • SLT process: ARRM - Attention, Retention, Reproduction, Motivation. We learn by OBSERVING, not just doing.
  • Behaviourism = observable behaviour only. Cognitive = mental processes. SLT bridges both.

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating behaviourism, always consider: is it too reductionist? Does it ignore cognition? Is it environmentally deterministic? Compare with the cognitive approach and SLT to show the development from strict behaviourism to cognitive-social approaches.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Behaviourism fully explains human behaviour because everything we do is learned through conditioning. Correct: While conditioning explains many learned behaviours, it cannot account for: innate behaviours (reflexes, universal emotions), cognitive processes (problem-solving, decision-making), biological predispositions (Seligman's preparedness theory - we are biologically prepared to fear some things more than others), and spontaneous behaviours that occur without any apparent reinforcement. The cognitive approach and biological approach both show that behaviourism provides an incomplete picture of human behaviour.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the contribution of the behaviourist approach to our understanding of human behaviour.

A grade 9 response will: describe classical and operant conditioning with evidence (Pavlov, Skinner); describe SLT with evidence (Bandura); evaluate behaviourism strengths (scientific, practical applications - systematic desensitisation, token economies); evaluate limitations (reductionist, ignores cognition, environmental determinism, ignores biology); evaluate SLT as an improvement (includes cognitive mediational processes); conclude: behaviourism made psychology scientific and produced effective treatments, but its exclusion of mental processes and biology is a significant limitation. SLT and the cognitive approach have built on behaviourism by incorporating cognitive processes, providing a more complete account of human learning.
