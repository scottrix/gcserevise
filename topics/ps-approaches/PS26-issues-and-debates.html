PS26: Issues & Debates in Psychology

Foundation Higher AQA 8182, OCR J203, Edexcel 1PS0

Key debates and issues: nature vs nurture, free will vs determinism, reductionism vs holism, idiographic vs nomothetic, ethnocentrism, and gender bias in psychological research.

Issues & Debates in Psychology

Key debates and issues: nature vs nurture, free will vs determinism, reductionism vs holism, idiographic vs nomothetic, ethnocentrism, and gender bias in psychological research.

Key Fact: Nature vs nurture: nature argues behaviour is determined by inherited genes and biology; nurture argues behaviour is determined by experience and environment. Modern view: interactionism - both contribute. Epigenetics shows environmental factors can switch genes on/off, demonstrating that nature and nurture interact.
Key Fact: Free will vs determinism: free will argues people have conscious control over their choices; determinism argues behaviour is caused by factors beyond our control. Biological determinism (genes/neurochemistry), environmental determinism (conditioning), and psychic determinism (unconscious drives). Soft determinism (Bandura): some freedom within determined constraints.
Key Fact: Reductionism vs holism: reductionism explains behaviour by breaking it down into simpler components (biological reductionism: behaviour = brain chemistry). Holism argues the whole is greater than the sum of parts - behaviour must be understood as an integrated system. Reductionism is scientific but oversimplifies; holism is comprehensive but harder to test.
Key Fact: Ethnocentrism: judging other cultures by the standards of your own. Much research is WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialised, Rich, Democratic) and may not generalise. Example: Asch's conformity findings reflect 1950s America; collectivist cultures show higher conformity.
Key Fact: Gender bias: research that treats one gender as the norm (usually male = norm, female = deviation). Alpha bias: exaggerating differences between genders. Beta bias: minimising or ignoring differences. Examples: Kohlberg's moral development theory was based on male participants only; Milgram's study used only male participants but generalised to all people.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the nature-nurture debate and evaluate whether behaviour is determined by nature or nurture.

Q2: Describe the difference between free will and determinism, giving examples of each from psychological research.

Q3: Evaluate whether psychological research suffers from ethnocentric and gender bias, and explain how this can be addressed.

✅ Answers

  1. Nature: behaviour is biologically determined (genes, brain, evolution). Evidence: twin studies, universal behaviours. Nurture: behaviour is environmentally determined (learning, culture, upbringing). Evidence: cultural differences, conditioning. Modern interactionism: both contribute - genes provide predispositions, environment triggers or suppresses them. Epigenetics shows environment can alter gene expression.
  2. Free will: people make conscious choices about their behaviour. Supported by: subjective experience of choice, effectiveness of therapies that assume personal agency (CBT). Determinism: behaviour is caused by prior factors. Biological determinism (genes -> behaviour), environmental determinism (conditioning -> behaviour), psychic determinism (unconscious drives). Soft determinism (Bandura): behaviour is influenced but not rigidly determined - we have conscious control within constraints.
  3. Ethnocentric bias: Western research dominates and may not generalise (Asch's conformity findings vary by culture; WEIRD samples are not representative). Gender bias: male participants overrepresented (Milgram used only males, Kohlberg's moral stages based on males). Addressing bias: use diverse samples, conduct cross-cultural research, include both genders, be aware of androcentrism (male-centred perspective), and report limitations of sample generalisability.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Nature = biology. Nurture = experience. Modern answer: BOTH (interactionism, epigenetics).
  • Free will = we choose. Determinism = behaviour is caused. Soft determinism = influenced but not rigidly fixed.
  • Reductionism = break it down (scientific but oversimplified). Holism = see the whole picture (comprehensive but harder to test).

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When discussing issues and debates, always: take a balanced position, use specific examples from research, and acknowledge that most debates are not either/or but exist on a continuum. The interactionist position is usually best supported by evidence.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: If behaviour is determined by biology and environment, then people cannot be held responsible for their actions. Correct: While determinism suggests behaviour has causes, this does not mean people lack all responsibility. Soft determinism (Bandura) argues we have conscious choice within constraints. The legal system recognises degrees of responsibility (diminished responsibility for mental illness). Free will may be limited rather than absent. Furthermore, understanding the causes of behaviour can INCREASE our ability to change it (e.g. CBT teaches people to challenge determinist thoughts). Determinism explains causes; it doesn't eliminate the value of personal agency.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether the nature-nurture debate has been resolved by modern psychology.

A grade 9 response will: define nature and nurture with evidence for each; explain why the debate is a false dichotomy (interactionism); present epigenetics as evidence for interaction (environment switches genes on/off); discuss the diathesis-stress model (genetic vulnerability + environmental triggers); evaluate using specific examples (depression: genetic predisposition + life events; phobias: biological preparedness + conditioning); conclude: the nature-nurture debate is largely resolved in favour of interactionism - both contribute to all behaviour. The question is no longer whether nature or nurture matters, but HOW they interact. Epigenetics provides the mechanism for this interaction, showing that the old nature-vs-nurture framework is outdated.

Detailed Notes

Key Studies and Theories in Issues & Debates in Psychology

Issues & Debates in Psychology is a core topic in GCSE Psychology that requires understanding of key studies, theories and research methods. The AQA, OCR and Edexcel specifications all require you to know specific studies in detail, evaluate their methodology, and apply psychological concepts to real-life scenarios. When studying issues & debates in psychology, focus on: the aims, methods, results and conclusions of key studies; the strengths and weaknesses of the research methods used; and how psychological understanding of issues & debates in psychology applies to everyday life.

Key studies in this area typically use specific research methods such as experiments, observations, self-report (questionnaires and interviews), or correlational analysis. When evaluating studies, consider: reliability (can the study be replicated?); validity (does it measure what it claims to measure?); ethics (were participants protected from harm?); generalisability (can the findings be applied beyond the study sample?); and usefulness (does the research have practical applications?).

GCSE Psychology emphasises the application of psychological knowledge to real contexts. You must be able to explain how understanding of issues & debates in psychology can be used to improve people’s lives, influence policy, or solve practical problems.

GCSE Example: Key Studies and Theories in Issues & Debates in Psychology

When writing about a study related to issues & debates in psychology, use the AMRC format: Aim (what was the study trying to find out?), Method (how was it conducted — design, sample, procedure?), Results (what were the key findings?), Conclusions (what do the results mean?).

Evaluating Research in Issues & Debates in Psychology

Evaluation is a crucial skill in GCSE Psychology. When evaluating studies related to issues & debates in psychology, use the GRAVE method: Generalisability (can the findings be applied to the wider population?), Reliability (are the results consistent if the study is repeated?), Applications (how can the findings be used in real life?), Validity (does the study measure what it claims to measure?), and Ethics (were participants treated ethically?).

Research methods commonly used in issues & debates in psychology research include: laboratory experiments (high control but low ecological validity), field experiments (more realistic but less control), natural experiments (no manipulation of variables), case studies (in-depth but not generalisable), questionnaires (large samples but social desirability bias), and observations (natural behaviour but ethical concerns). Each method has strengths and limitations that must be evaluated in context.

Ethical issues in issues & debates in psychology research include: informed consent (participants must agree to take part), debriefing (explaining the true purpose after the study), confidentiality (protecting participants’ identities), protection from harm (no physical or psychological damage), and right to withdraw (participants can leave at any time). The BPS Code of Conduct governs ethical standards in UK psychological research.

GCSE Example: Evaluating Research in Issues & Debates in Psychology

When evaluating Milgram’s obedience study, you might note: low generalisability (all male, American sample); high reliability (standardised procedure with 65% obedience rate); useful applications (understanding wartime atrocities); low ecological validity (artificial laboratory setting); and serious ethical concerns (deception, psychological distress).

Applying Issues & Debates in Psychology to Real Life

GCSE Psychology requires you to apply psychological understanding of issues & debates in psychology to everyday situations. Consider how the theories and studies you have learned about explain real behaviour: How does issues & debates in psychology affect people in schools, workplaces, families and communities? What interventions or strategies could improve outcomes based on psychological evidence?

When applying issues & debates in psychology to real-life scenarios, use the following structure: identify the relevant psychological concept, explain how it applies to the scenario, support your explanation with evidence from a named study, and evaluate the strength of the application (are there limitations or alternative explanations?).

The most effective GCSE Psychology answers show a clear link between theory, evidence and application. Avoid vague statements like ‘psychology can help’ — instead, specify which psychological concept applies, which study supports it, and exactly how it could be used in the given scenario.

GCSE Example: Applying Issues & Debates in Psychology to Real Life

A strong application answer: ‘Understanding of issues & debates in psychology could help in schools by [specific application]. This is supported by [named study] which found that [specific finding]. This means that [specific practical recommendation]. However, a limitation is [specific weakness of applying this research].’
