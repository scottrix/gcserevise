Perception

How we interpret sensory information: visual illusions, Gregory's constructivist theory, Gibson's direct theory, and factors affecting perception including culture, emotion and motivation.

Key Fact: Perception is the process of interpreting sensory information to make sense of the world. Sensation is detecting stimuli; perception is interpreting them - they are distinct processes.
Key Fact: Gregory's constructivist theory: perception uses past experience and expectations to interpret sensory input (top-down processing). Visual illusions support this - we misinterpret because we apply assumptions (e.g. the Muller-Lyer illusion).
Key Fact: Gibson's direct theory: perception is entirely determined by sensory input from the environment (bottom-up processing). We perceive affordances - what the environment offers us (e.g. a chair affords sitting). No past experience needed.
Key Fact: Factors affecting perception: culture (Hudson's study showed Western and non-Western people perceive depth cues differently), emotion (anxious people perceive threats more readily), motivation (hungry people are more likely to perceive food-related images).
Key Fact: Visual illusions reveal how perception can be distorted: the Ames Room exploits size constancy; the Kanizsa Triangle shows we perceive shapes that don't physically exist (illusory contours).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Compare Gregory's constructivist theory with Gibson's direct theory of perception.

Q2: Explain how culture can affect perception, using research evidence.

Q3: Evaluate what visual illusions tell us about the nature of perception.

✅ Answers

  1. Gregory: perception is constructed using past experience and expectations (top-down). Gibson: perception is directly picked up from the environment (bottom-up). Gregory explains illusions and ambiguous figures but struggles with infant perception. Gibson explains accurate perception in novel situations but cannot explain illusions.
  2. Hudson (1960) found that Western participants easily perceived depth in 2D pictures using perspective cues, while non-Western participants (who had less exposure to 2D representations) found it harder. This suggests that some perceptual skills are learned through cultural experience.
  3. Illusions show perception is not a direct copy of reality - the brain interprets and sometimes misinterprets information. This supports Gregory's top-down processing view. However, not all illusions are explained by past experience; some (like the Kanizsa Triangle) may involve innate perceptual processes, supporting Gibson's view partially.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Sensation = detecting. Perception = interpreting. They are different processes.
  • Gregory = top-down (experience-based). Gibson = bottom-up (environment-based).
  • Always name a SPECIFIC illusion (Muller-Lyer, Ames Room) when discussing perception.

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating perception theories, consider whether perception is more influenced by nature (Gibson - innate) or nurture (Gregory - learned). Support your argument with specific studies and acknowledge that both processes likely contribute.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Perception is entirely determined by the information available in the environment, so visual illusions cannot exist. Correct: Visual illusions clearly demonstrate that perception is not simply a direct read-out of environmental information - the brain must be interpreting sensory input using assumptions and expectations. Even Gibson acknowledged that illusions exist; he argued they are exceptions caused by unusual environments that trick normally reliable perceptual systems.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether perception is primarily a top-down or bottom-up process.

A grade 9 response will: outline Gregory's top-down theory with evidence (illusions, ambiguous figures, past experience); outline Gibson's bottom-up theory with evidence (affordances, accurate perception in novel situations); evaluate both; conclude: perception involves both processes - bottom-up provides raw data, top-down refines interpretation. Interactionist approaches best explain the evidence.
