Cognitive Development

How thinking develops: Piaget's stage theory, the role of schemas, assimilation and accommodation, and the development of conservation. Evaluating Piaget and alternative views from Vygotsky.

Key Fact: Piaget proposed that children's thinking develops through four stages: sensorimotor (0-2), pre-operational (2-7), concrete operational (7-11), formal operational (11+). Each stage represents a qualitative change in thinking.
Key Fact: Schemas are mental frameworks that organise knowledge. Assimilation: fitting new information into existing schemas. Accommodation: modifying schemas when new information doesn't fit. Equilibration drives development - disequilibrium motivates accommodation.
Key Fact: Conservation: understanding that quantity stays the same despite changes in appearance. Pre-operational children fail conservation tasks (e.g. pouring liquid into a taller, narrower glass). Concrete operational children succeed.
Key Fact: Egocentrism: pre-operational children struggle to see things from another's perspective. Piaget's Three Mountains Task showed children could not describe the view from a doll's position.
Key Fact: Vygotsky's sociocultural theory: cognitive development occurs through social interaction. The Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) is the gap between what a child can do alone and what they can do with help (scaffolding). Language is crucial for thinking.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe Piaget's stages of cognitive development and explain the key features of each.

Q2: Explain the difference between assimilation and accommodation, using examples.

Q3: Evaluate Piaget's theory of cognitive development, comparing it with Vygotsky's sociocultural theory.

✅ Answers

  1. Sensorimotor: object permanence develops, learning through senses and actions. Pre-operational: egocentrism, lack of conservation, symbolic play. Concrete operational: conservation, classification, less egocentrism. Formal operational: abstract reasoning, hypothetical thinking.
  2. Assimilation: a child who knows 'dog' calls a sheep a 'dog' - fitting new info into an existing schema. Accommodation: the child learns a sheep is different and creates a new schema - modifying understanding. Both processes work together to build knowledge.
  3. Piaget strengths: highly influential, supported by conservation and egocentrism studies, practical applications in education (discovery learning). Limitations: underestimates children's abilities (Baillargeon showed object permanence earlier), stages are too rigid, ignores social/cultural factors. Vygotsky addresses social learning and the ZPD but is less specific about stages.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Assimilation = fitting INTO existing schema. Accommodation = CHANGING the schema.
  • Pre-operational children CANNOT conserve. Concrete operational children CAN.
  • Vygotsky emphasises SOCIAL interaction. Piaget emphasises INDIVIDUAL discovery.

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating developmental theories, consider: are the stages universal? Are children more capable than Piaget claimed? Does culture affect development? Use GRAVE to evaluate supporting studies.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Piaget's stages mean that children cannot be taught concepts before they are developmentally ready, so teaching conservation to a 4-year-old is impossible. Correct: While Piaget argued that children must reach the appropriate stage before understanding concepts, research shows that with scaffolding (Vygotsky), children can learn concepts slightly beyond their current stage. Targeted teaching within the ZPD can accelerate understanding. Piaget's stages are a useful guide but not an absolute barrier to learning.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether Piaget's theory or Vygotsky's theory provides a better explanation of cognitive development.

A grade 9 response will: outline Piaget's stage theory with evidence (conservation, egocentrism); outline Vygotsky's sociocultural theory (ZPD, scaffolding, language); evaluate Piaget (supported but underestimates children, ignores culture); evaluate Vygotsky (emphasises social learning but lacks clear stages); conclude: both theories contribute - Piaget provides the structural framework, Vygotsky explains how social interaction drives progress through those stages.
