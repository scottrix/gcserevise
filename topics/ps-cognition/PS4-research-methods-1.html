Research Methods (Part 1)

The scientific method in psychology: experiments (lab, field, natural, quasi), observations, correlations, and the key features of research design including variables, controls and sampling.

Key Fact: Experiments test cause-and-effect by manipulating the independent variable (IV) and measuring the dependent variable (DV) while controlling extraneous variables. Types: lab (high control, low ecological validity), field (natural setting, lower control), natural (IV not manipulated), quasi (pre-existing groups).
Key Fact: Observations record behaviour without manipulation. Naturalistic: in a natural setting with no intervention. Controlled: some variables regulated. Covert: participants unaware. Overt: participants aware. Participant: researcher joins the group. Non-participant: researcher observes from outside.
Key Fact: Correlations measure the relationship between two variables without establishing causation. Positive correlation: both variables increase together. Negative: one increases as the other decreases. Zero: no relationship. Correlation does NOT mean causation.
Key Fact: Sampling methods: random (everyone has equal chance), systematic (every nth person), stratified (proportional representation of subgroups), opportunity (whoever is available), volunteer (self-selected). Each has strengths and limitations.
Key Fact: Key terms: operationalisation (defining variables precisely so they can be measured), reliability (consistency of results), validity (measuring what was intended), ecological validity (how well findings generalise to real life).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the different types of experiment used in psychological research and evaluate their strengths and weaknesses.

Q2: Explain why correlation does not equal causation, using an example from psychology.

Q3: Compare random sampling with opportunity sampling, evaluating the strengths and limitations of each.

✅ Answers

  1. Lab: high control (can establish cause-effect), replicable, but low ecological validity and demand characteristics. Field: natural setting, higher ecological validity, but less control and harder to replicate. Natural: IV not manipulated (ethical advantage), but no random allocation so causation harder to establish. Quasi: allows study of existing groups, but cannot randomly allocate.
  2. A correlation between ice cream sales and drowning incidents does not mean ice cream causes drowning - the third variable (hot weather) causes both. In psychology, a correlation between self-esteem and academic achievement does not prove one causes the other.
  3. Random sampling: every member of the target population has an equal chance - reduces bias, representative. But impractical for large populations. Opportunity sampling: convenient, quick, cheap. But biased (unrepresentative). Random is more representative but harder to achieve; opportunity is practical but less rigorous.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Lab experiment = high control, low ecological validity. Field = low control, high ecological validity.
  • Correlation does NOT equal causation - always mention the third variable problem.
  • Operationalise your variables: don't just measure 'memory', measure 'number of words recalled from a list of 20'.

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating research methods, consider: control vs realism (lab vs field), generalisability (sampling), ethics (deception, consent), and practical issues (time, cost, access). Always weigh strengths against limitations for the specific research question.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Lab experiments are always the best method because they have the most control over variables. Correct: While lab experiments have high internal validity due to control, they often lack ecological validity - participants behave artificially. For some research questions (e.g. how people behave in emergencies), field experiments or observations are more appropriate despite lower control. The best method depends on the research question.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the use of experiments and observations in psychological research, considering when each method is most appropriate.

A grade 9 response will: describe experiments (lab, field, natural, quasi) and observations (naturalistic, controlled); evaluate experiments (cause-effect but artificial); evaluate observations (natural behaviour but no control); conclude: experiments are best for testing causal relationships; observations are best for studying natural behaviour. Researchers should choose the method that best fits their research question.

Detailed Notes

Key Studies and Theories in Research Methods (Part 1)

Research Methods (Part 1) is a core topic in GCSE Psychology that requires understanding of key studies, theories and research methods. The AQA, OCR and Edexcel specifications all require you to know specific studies in detail, evaluate their methodology, and apply psychological concepts to real-life scenarios. When studying research methods (part 1), focus on: the aims, methods, results and conclusions of key studies; the strengths and weaknesses of the research methods used; and how psychological understanding of research methods (part 1) applies to everyday life.

Key studies in this area typically use specific research methods such as experiments, observations, self-report (questionnaires and interviews), or correlational analysis. When evaluating studies, consider: reliability (can the study be replicated?); validity (does it measure what it claims to measure?); ethics (were participants protected from harm?); generalisability (can the findings be applied beyond the study sample?); and usefulness (does the research have practical applications?).

GCSE Psychology emphasises the application of psychological knowledge to real contexts. You must be able to explain how understanding of research methods (part 1) can be used to improve people’s lives, influence policy, or solve practical problems.

GCSE Example: Key Studies and Theories in Research Methods (Part 1)

When writing about a study related to research methods (part 1), use the AMRC format: Aim (what was the study trying to find out?), Method (how was it conducted — design, sample, procedure?), Results (what were the key findings?), Conclusions (what do the results mean?).

Evaluating Research in Research Methods (Part 1)

Evaluation is a crucial skill in GCSE Psychology. When evaluating studies related to research methods (part 1), use the GRAVE method: Generalisability (can the findings be applied to the wider population?), Reliability (are the results consistent if the study is repeated?), Applications (how can the findings be used in real life?), Validity (does the study measure what it claims to measure?), and Ethics (were participants treated ethically?).

Research methods commonly used in research methods (part 1) research include: laboratory experiments (high control but low ecological validity), field experiments (more realistic but less control), natural experiments (no manipulation of variables), case studies (in-depth but not generalisable), questionnaires (large samples but social desirability bias), and observations (natural behaviour but ethical concerns). Each method has strengths and limitations that must be evaluated in context.

Ethical issues in research methods (part 1) research include: informed consent (participants must agree to take part), debriefing (explaining the true purpose after the study), confidentiality (protecting participants’ identities), protection from harm (no physical or psychological damage), and right to withdraw (participants can leave at any time). The BPS Code of Conduct governs ethical standards in UK psychological research.

GCSE Example: Evaluating Research in Research Methods (Part 1)

When evaluating Milgram’s obedience study, you might note: low generalisability (all male, American sample); high reliability (standardised procedure with 65% obedience rate); useful applications (understanding wartime atrocities); low ecological validity (artificial laboratory setting); and serious ethical concerns (deception, psychological distress).

Applying Research Methods (Part 1) to Real Life

GCSE Psychology requires you to apply psychological understanding of research methods (part 1) to everyday situations. Consider how the theories and studies you have learned about explain real behaviour: How does research methods (part 1) affect people in schools, workplaces, families and communities? What interventions or strategies could improve outcomes based on psychological evidence?

When applying research methods (part 1) to real-life scenarios, use the following structure: identify the relevant psychological concept, explain how it applies to the scenario, support your explanation with evidence from a named study, and evaluate the strength of the application (are there limitations or alternative explanations?).

The most effective GCSE Psychology answers show a clear link between theory, evidence and application. Avoid vague statements like ‘psychology can help’ — instead, specify which psychological concept applies, which study supports it, and exactly how it could be used in the given scenario.

GCSE Example: Applying Research Methods (Part 1) to Real Life

A strong application answer: ‘Understanding of research methods (part 1) could help in schools by [specific application]. This is supported by [named study] which found that [specific finding]. This means that [specific practical recommendation]. However, a limitation is [specific weakness of applying this research].’
