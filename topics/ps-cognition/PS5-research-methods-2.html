Research Methods (Part 2)

Data analysis and ethics in psychology: quantitative and qualitative data, measures of central tendency, measures of dispersion, data presentation, ethical issues and the BPS code of conduct.

Key Fact: Quantitative data: numerical, can be statistically analysed. Qualitative data: descriptive, words rather than numbers. Primary data: collected by the researcher. Secondary data: collected by someone else for a different purpose.
Key Fact: Measures of central tendency: Mean (average - affected by outliers), Median (middle value - not affected by outliers), Mode (most common - useful for categorical data). Measures of dispersion: Range (highest minus lowest - simple but affected by outliers), Standard deviation (average distance from the mean - more precise but complex).
Key Fact: Data presentation: bar charts (discrete/categorical data), histograms (continuous data, no gaps between bars), scatter graphs (correlations), tables (organised raw data). Choose the presentation method that best represents the data type.
Key Fact: Ethical issues: informed consent (participants must agree to take part), deception (misleading participants - only acceptable if justified and debriefed), protection from harm (physical and psychological), privacy (right to confidentiality), right to withdraw (participants can leave at any time).
Key Fact: The BPS Code of Conduct sets ethical guidelines for UK psychologists: respect, competence, responsibility, integrity. Ethics committees must approve research before it is conducted. Debriefing is essential when deception has been used.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between quantitative and qualitative data, giving one strength and one limitation of each.

Q2: Describe three ethical issues that psychologists must consider when conducting research.

Q3: A study finds mean = 15, median = 12, mode = 10. Explain what these results suggest about the distribution of the data.

✅ Answers

  1. Quantitative: numerical, easy to analyse and compare, objective. But lacks depth and context. Qualitative: rich, detailed, captures experiences. But harder to analyse, subjective. Both types are valuable - mixed methods provide a more complete picture.
  2. Informed consent: participants must know what they are agreeing to - without it, their autonomy is violated. Deception: misleading participants undermines trust - only justified if the research question cannot be addressed otherwise. Protection from harm: researchers must minimise risks.
  3. Mean > median > mode suggests a positively skewed distribution - a few high scores are pulling the mean upwards. The median (12) is a better measure of central tendency here because it is less affected by the extreme high scores than the mean (15).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Mean = average (affected by outliers). Median = middle (not affected by outliers). Mode = most common.
  • Informed consent + Right to withdraw + Debriefing = the ethical minimum for any study.
  • Bar chart = categorical data (gaps between bars). Histogram = continuous data (no gaps).

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating data, consider: is the mean appropriate or is it distorted by outliers? Is qualitative data too subjective? Is quantitative data too simplistic? Always justify your choice of statistical measure based on the data distribution.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: If a study has ethical issues, it should not be conducted under any circumstances. Correct: Some ethical issues can be managed rather than eliminated. Deception can be addressed through thorough debriefing. Lack of informed consent can be partially addressed through prior general consent or retrospective consent. The key is to minimise harm and ensure participants are fully informed as soon as possible.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the importance of ethical guidelines in psychological research, considering whether they are too strict.

A grade 9 response will: explain key ethical principles (consent, deception, protection, withdrawal); argue they are necessary (Milgram's study caused extreme stress); argue they may be too strict (some important research could not be conducted, deception sometimes necessary for valid results); conclude: ethical guidelines are essential but should be proportionate - researchers should seek the least deceptive approach while still being able to investigate important questions.

Detailed Notes

Key Studies and Theories in Research Methods (Part 2)

Research Methods (Part 2) is a core topic in GCSE Psychology that requires understanding of key studies, theories and research methods. The AQA, OCR and Edexcel specifications all require you to know specific studies in detail, evaluate their methodology, and apply psychological concepts to real-life scenarios. When studying research methods (part 2), focus on: the aims, methods, results and conclusions of key studies; the strengths and weaknesses of the research methods used; and how psychological understanding of research methods (part 2) applies to everyday life.

Key studies in this area typically use specific research methods such as experiments, observations, self-report (questionnaires and interviews), or correlational analysis. When evaluating studies, consider: reliability (can the study be replicated?); validity (does it measure what it claims to measure?); ethics (were participants protected from harm?); generalisability (can the findings be applied beyond the study sample?); and usefulness (does the research have practical applications?).

GCSE Psychology emphasises the application of psychological knowledge to real contexts. You must be able to explain how understanding of research methods (part 2) can be used to improve people’s lives, influence policy, or solve practical problems.

GCSE Example: Key Studies and Theories in Research Methods (Part 2)

When writing about a study related to research methods (part 2), use the AMRC format: Aim (what was the study trying to find out?), Method (how was it conducted — design, sample, procedure?), Results (what were the key findings?), Conclusions (what do the results mean?).

Evaluating Research in Research Methods (Part 2)

Evaluation is a crucial skill in GCSE Psychology. When evaluating studies related to research methods (part 2), use the GRAVE method: Generalisability (can the findings be applied to the wider population?), Reliability (are the results consistent if the study is repeated?), Applications (how can the findings be used in real life?), Validity (does the study measure what it claims to measure?), and Ethics (were participants treated ethically?).

Research methods commonly used in research methods (part 2) research include: laboratory experiments (high control but low ecological validity), field experiments (more realistic but less control), natural experiments (no manipulation of variables), case studies (in-depth but not generalisable), questionnaires (large samples but social desirability bias), and observations (natural behaviour but ethical concerns). Each method has strengths and limitations that must be evaluated in context.

Ethical issues in research methods (part 2) research include: informed consent (participants must agree to take part), debriefing (explaining the true purpose after the study), confidentiality (protecting participants’ identities), protection from harm (no physical or psychological damage), and right to withdraw (participants can leave at any time). The BPS Code of Conduct governs ethical standards in UK psychological research.

GCSE Example: Evaluating Research in Research Methods (Part 2)

When evaluating Milgram’s obedience study, you might note: low generalisability (all male, American sample); high reliability (standardised procedure with 65% obedience rate); useful applications (understanding wartime atrocities); low ecological validity (artificial laboratory setting); and serious ethical concerns (deception, psychological distress).

Applying Research Methods (Part 2) to Real Life

GCSE Psychology requires you to apply psychological understanding of research methods (part 2) to everyday situations. Consider how the theories and studies you have learned about explain real behaviour: How does research methods (part 2) affect people in schools, workplaces, families and communities? What interventions or strategies could improve outcomes based on psychological evidence?

When applying research methods (part 2) to real-life scenarios, use the following structure: identify the relevant psychological concept, explain how it applies to the scenario, support your explanation with evidence from a named study, and evaluate the strength of the application (are there limitations or alternative explanations?).

The most effective GCSE Psychology answers show a clear link between theory, evidence and application. Avoid vague statements like ‘psychology can help’ — instead, specify which psychological concept applies, which study supports it, and exactly how it could be used in the given scenario.

GCSE Example: Applying Research Methods (Part 2) to Real Life

A strong application answer: ‘Understanding of research methods (part 2) could help in schools by [specific application]. This is supported by [named study] which found that [specific finding]. This means that [specific practical recommendation]. However, a limitation is [specific weakness of applying this research].’

Additional Practice Questions

Q: Describe one study related to research methods (part 2) and evaluate its methodology using the GRAVE method.

A: A key study in research methods (part 2) investigated [aim] using [method] with [sample]. The results showed [key finding], leading to the conclusion that [interpretation]. Evaluation: Generalisability — limited by [sample issue]; Reliability — strengthened by [standardisation]; Applications — useful for [practical application]; Validity — limited by [validity concern]; Ethics — problematic because [ethical issue]. Overall, the study makes a valuable contribution but has limitations that should be considered when applying the findings.

Q: Explain how psychological understanding of research methods (part 2) could be applied to improve a real-life situation.

A: Understanding of research methods (part 2) can be applied to improve [specific situation]. Research by [named psychologist] found that [key finding], which suggests that [practical recommendation]. For example, in a school context, this understanding could lead to [specific intervention] which would benefit people by [specific improvement]. However, individual differences mean that not everyone will respond the same way, so a personalised approach may be needed.
