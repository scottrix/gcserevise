Research Methods (Part 2)

Data analysis and ethics in psychology: quantitative and qualitative data, measures of central tendency, measures of dispersion, data presentation, ethical issues and the BPS code of conduct.

Key Fact: Quantitative data: numerical, can be statistically analysed. Qualitative data: descriptive, words rather than numbers. Primary data: collected by the researcher. Secondary data: collected by someone else for a different purpose.
Key Fact: Measures of central tendency: Mean (average - affected by outliers), Median (middle value - not affected by outliers), Mode (most common - useful for categorical data). Measures of dispersion: Range (highest minus lowest - simple but affected by outliers), Standard deviation (average distance from the mean - more precise but complex).
Key Fact: Data presentation: bar charts (discrete/categorical data), histograms (continuous data, no gaps between bars), scatter graphs (correlations), tables (organised raw data). Choose the presentation method that best represents the data type.
Key Fact: Ethical issues: informed consent (participants must agree to take part), deception (misleading participants - only acceptable if justified and debriefed), protection from harm (physical and psychological), privacy (right to confidentiality), right to withdraw (participants can leave at any time).
Key Fact: The BPS Code of Conduct sets ethical guidelines for UK psychologists: respect, competence, responsibility, integrity. Ethics committees must approve research before it is conducted. Debriefing is essential when deception has been used.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between quantitative and qualitative data, giving one strength and one limitation of each.

Q2: Describe three ethical issues that psychologists must consider when conducting research.

Q3: A study finds mean = 15, median = 12, mode = 10. Explain what these results suggest about the distribution of the data.

✅ Answers

  1. Quantitative: numerical, easy to analyse and compare, objective. But lacks depth and context. Qualitative: rich, detailed, captures experiences. But harder to analyse, subjective. Both types are valuable - mixed methods provide a more complete picture.
  2. Informed consent: participants must know what they are agreeing to - without it, their autonomy is violated. Deception: misleading participants undermines trust - only justified if the research question cannot be addressed otherwise. Protection from harm: researchers must minimise risks.
  3. Mean > median > mode suggests a positively skewed distribution - a few high scores are pulling the mean upwards. The median (12) is a better measure of central tendency here because it is less affected by the extreme high scores than the mean (15).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Mean = average (affected by outliers). Median = middle (not affected by outliers). Mode = most common.
  • Informed consent + Right to withdraw + Debriefing = the ethical minimum for any study.
  • Bar chart = categorical data (gaps between bars). Histogram = continuous data (no gaps).

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating data, consider: is the mean appropriate or is it distorted by outliers? Is qualitative data too subjective? Is quantitative data too simplistic? Always justify your choice of statistical measure based on the data distribution.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: If a study has ethical issues, it should not be conducted under any circumstances. Correct: Some ethical issues can be managed rather than eliminated. Deception can be addressed through thorough debriefing. Lack of informed consent can be partially addressed through prior general consent or retrospective consent. The key is to minimise harm and ensure participants are fully informed as soon as possible.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the importance of ethical guidelines in psychological research, considering whether they are too strict.

A grade 9 response will: explain key ethical principles (consent, deception, protection, withdrawal); argue they are necessary (Milgram's study caused extreme stress); argue they may be too strict (some important research could not be conducted, deception sometimes necessary for valid results); conclude: ethical guidelines are essential but should be proportionate - researchers should seek the least deceptive approach while still being able to investigate important questions.
