Animal vs Human Communication

How animal communication differs from human language: features of human language, animal communication systems, and whether animals can learn language (Washoe, Kanzi, Pepperberg).

Key Fact: Human language has unique features (Hockett's design features): semanticity (words have meaning), generativity (can create infinite new sentences), displacement (can talk about past/future/abstract), arbitrariness (no connection between word and referent), and cultural transmission (learned from others).
Key Fact: Animal communication: bees use waggle dances to communicate food location (shows displacement and semanticity but not generativity). Vervet monkeys use distinct alarm calls for different predators (shows semanticity but limited generativity). Birdsongs are largely innate with some learned elements.
Key Fact: Washoe the chimp: taught over 100 signs in ASL, combined signs creatively (e.g. 'water bird' for swan). However, critics argued Washoe's signing was operant conditioning rather than true language - she didn't use syntax consistently.
Key Fact: Kanzi the bonobo: learned lexigram symbols through observation (not explicit training). Kanzi understood spoken English and could follow complex instructions. Showed some understanding of word order, suggesting rudimentary syntax.
Key Fact: Pepperberg's Alex the parrot: learned over 100 English words, could label objects by colour, shape and material, and understood concepts of same/different, bigger/smaller. Showed cognitive abilities beyond simple mimicry but did not use generative syntax.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the key features that distinguish human language from animal communication systems.

Q2: Evaluate whether apes can learn language, referring to studies of Washoe and Kanzi.

Q3: Discuss whether the difference between human and animal communication is qualitative or quantitative.

✅ Answers

  1. Human language has: semanticity (meaning), generativity (infinite combinations), displacement (past/future), arbitrariness (no word-object connection), cultural transmission. Most animal communication lacks generativity and displacement - bees show some displacement but cannot create new messages.
  2. Washoe: learned 100+ ASL signs and combined them creatively. Suggests apes can learn aspects of language. However: Terrace argued Washoe's combinations were imitation, not syntax; the Gardners were accused of over-interpreting signs; apes don't develop language naturally. Kanzi: learned through observation, understood spoken English, showed some syntax. More convincing but still limited compared to human children.
  3. Qualitative difference: humans have unique features (generativity, syntax) that animals lack entirely - a difference in kind. Quantitative difference: animals show some language abilities (semanticity, limited displacement, basic combinations) - a difference in degree. Evidence supports a qualitative difference in generativity and syntax, but animals demonstrate many of the building blocks of language.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Human language = generativity + displacement + syntax. Animal communication = limited, fixed signals.
  • Washoe = chimp learned ASL. Kanzi = bonobo learned lexigrams. Alex = parrot learned English words.
  • When evaluating animal language studies, consider: is it true language or operant conditioning? Are researchers over-interpreting?

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating animal language research, consider: how do we define language? Are the criteria fair? Is it possible that animals communicate in ways we don't recognise? Use specific studies as evidence.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Animals cannot communicate at all - only humans have communication systems. Correct: Animals have sophisticated communication systems - bees communicate food locations through dances, vervet monkeys have distinct alarm calls, and many species use complex vocalisations. The key difference is that human language is generative (infinite new sentences) and uses displacement (talking about past/future), while most animal communication consists of a finite set of signals for specific situations.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether animals can be said to use language, referring to research evidence.

A grade 9 response will: define language using Hockett's features; describe animal communication (bees, vervets); evaluate ape language studies (Washoe, Kanzi, Alex); argue for qualitative difference (generativity and syntax are uniquely human); argue for quantitative similarity (animals share many building blocks); conclude: animals communicate effectively but do not use language as humans do - the difference is primarily qualitative (humans have generativity and syntax), though some animals demonstrate impressive precursors to language when raised in human-like environments.
