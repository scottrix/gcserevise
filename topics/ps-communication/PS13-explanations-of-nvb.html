PS13: Explanations of Non-Verbal Behaviour

Explanations of Non-Verbal Behaviour

Why we use non-verbal behaviour: evolutionary explanations, the role of culture, and explanations of how NVC develops. Yuki's study, biological vs cultural influences.

Key Fact: Evolutionary explanations: NVC evolved because it helped survival. Darwin argued that facial expressions evolved from useful actions (e.g. baring teeth in anger originally prepared for biting). Universal expressions suggest an evolutionary origin shared across cultures.
Key Fact: Innate explanations: NVC is biologically programmed. Evidence: Ekman's universal facial expressions; blind individuals show similar facial expressions despite never seeing them (Matsumoto & Hwang); infants smile and cry without learning.
Key Fact: Cultural explanations: NVC is learned through socialisation. Evidence: display rules vary across cultures; specific gestures (emblems) have different meanings in different cultures; proxemics vary by culture (Latin American cultures use less space than Northern European).
Key Fact: Yuki's study (2007): compared how American and Japanese participants interpreted emotional expressions using eyes vs mouths. Japanese participants relied more on eyes (cultural emphasis on reading subtle eye cues in a collectivist culture), while Americans relied more on mouths. This demonstrates that NVC interpretation is culturally shaped.
Key Fact: Interactionist view: NVC has innate foundations (universal expressions) but is shaped by cultural learning (display rules, gestures, proxemics). The evolutionary basis provides a starting point; culture refines and modifies expression.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Compare evolutionary and cultural explanations of non-verbal behaviour.

Q2: Describe Yuki's study on cultural differences in interpreting facial expressions and evaluate its methodology.

Q3: Discuss whether non-verbal behaviour is primarily innate or learned.

✅ Answers

  1. Evolutionary: NVC evolved for survival (e.g. expressions signal intent, eye contact signals threat). Supported by universality and blind individuals showing expressions. Cultural: NVC is learned through socialisation. Supported by cultural differences in display rules, gestures, and proxemics. Both contribute - NVC has an innate basis modified by cultural learning.
  2. Yuki (2007): American and Japanese participants judged emotions from eyes vs mouths. Americans relied more on mouths (expressive, open culture); Japanese relied more on eyes (subtle cues matter in collectivist culture). Evaluation: controlled methodology, but used static images rather than real interaction; samples were university students (limited generalisability); demonstrates cultural shaping of NVC interpretation.
  3. Innate evidence: universal facial expressions (Ekman); blind individuals show same expressions; infants express emotions without learning. Learned evidence: display rules vary; gestures differ by culture; Yuki's study shows interpretation is cultural. Conclusion: NVC is primarily innate in its basic forms but extensively shaped by cultural learning. An interactionist position best explains the evidence.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Evolutionary = NVC evolved for survival. Cultural = NVC is learned through socialisation.
  • Innate evidence: universal expressions + blind people + infants. Cultural evidence: display rules + gestures + proxemics.
  • Yuki: Japanese read EYES. Americans read MOUTHS. Culture shapes interpretation.

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating NVC explanations, always consider both nature and nurture. No single explanation fully accounts for the evidence - the interactionist position (innate basis + cultural modification) is most supported.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Facial expressions are entirely learned, which is why they differ across all cultures. Correct: While display rules and specific gestures differ across cultures, the six basic facial expressions (happiness, sadness, anger, fear, surprise, disgust) are recognised universally, including in remote tribes with minimal Western contact (Ekman's Fore tribe study). This strongly suggests a biological foundation. Culture modifies HOW and WHEN expressions are displayed, but the underlying expressions are innate.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether non-verbal behaviour is better explained by evolutionary or cultural factors.

A grade 9 response will: outline evolutionary explanations (universal expressions, survival value, blind individuals); outline cultural explanations (display rules, gestures, proxemics, Yuki's study); evaluate evolutionary (strong universality evidence but cannot explain cultural variation); evaluate cultural (explains variation but cannot explain universal expressions in blind individuals); conclude: NVC has innate evolutionary foundations that are shaped by cultural learning - neither explanation alone is sufficient. The interactionist position provides the most complete account.

Detailed Notes

Key Studies and Theories in Explanations of Non-Verbal Behaviour

Explanations of Non-Verbal Behaviour is a core topic in GCSE Psychology that requires understanding of key studies, theories and research methods. The AQA, OCR and Edexcel specifications all require you to know specific studies in detail, evaluate their methodology, and apply psychological concepts to real-life scenarios. When studying explanations of non-verbal behaviour, focus on: the aims, methods, results and conclusions of key studies; the strengths and weaknesses of the research methods used; and how psychological understanding of explanations of non-verbal behaviour applies to everyday life.

Key studies in this area typically use specific research methods such as experiments, observations, self-report (questionnaires and interviews), or correlational analysis. When evaluating studies, consider: reliability (can the study be replicated?); validity (does it measure what it claims to measure?); ethics (were participants protected from harm?); generalisability (can the findings be applied beyond the study sample?); and usefulness (does the research have practical applications?).

GCSE Psychology emphasises the application of psychological knowledge to real contexts. You must be able to explain how understanding of explanations of non-verbal behaviour can be used to improve people’s lives, influence policy, or solve practical problems.

GCSE Example: Key Studies and Theories in Explanations of Non-Verbal Behaviour

When writing about a study related to explanations of non-verbal behaviour, use the AMRC format: Aim (what was the study trying to find out?), Method (how was it conducted — design, sample, procedure?), Results (what were the key findings?), Conclusions (what do the results mean?).

Evaluating Research in Explanations of Non-Verbal Behaviour

Evaluation is a crucial skill in GCSE Psychology. When evaluating studies related to explanations of non-verbal behaviour, use the GRAVE method: Generalisability (can the findings be applied to the wider population?), Reliability (are the results consistent if the study is repeated?), Applications (how can the findings be used in real life?), Validity (does the study measure what it claims to measure?), and Ethics (were participants treated ethically?).

Research methods commonly used in explanations of non-verbal behaviour research include: laboratory experiments (high control but low ecological validity), field experiments (more realistic but less control), natural experiments (no manipulation of variables), case studies (in-depth but not generalisable), questionnaires (large samples but social desirability bias), and observations (natural behaviour but ethical concerns). Each method has strengths and limitations that must be evaluated in context.

Ethical issues in explanations of non-verbal behaviour research include: informed consent (participants must agree to take part), debriefing (explaining the true purpose after the study), confidentiality (protecting participants’ identities), protection from harm (no physical or psychological damage), and right to withdraw (participants can leave at any time). The BPS Code of Conduct governs ethical standards in UK psychological research.

GCSE Example: Evaluating Research in Explanations of Non-Verbal Behaviour

When evaluating Milgram’s obedience study, you might note: low generalisability (all male, American sample); high reliability (standardised procedure with 65% obedience rate); useful applications (understanding wartime atrocities); low ecological validity (artificial laboratory setting); and serious ethical concerns (deception, psychological distress).

Applying Explanations of Non-Verbal Behaviour to Real Life

GCSE Psychology requires you to apply psychological understanding of explanations of non-verbal behaviour to everyday situations. Consider how the theories and studies you have learned about explain real behaviour: How does explanations of non-verbal behaviour affect people in schools, workplaces, families and communities? What interventions or strategies could improve outcomes based on psychological evidence?

When applying explanations of non-verbal behaviour to real-life scenarios, use the following structure: identify the relevant psychological concept, explain how it applies to the scenario, support your explanation with evidence from a named study, and evaluate the strength of the application (are there limitations or alternative explanations?).

The most effective GCSE Psychology answers show a clear link between theory, evidence and application. Avoid vague statements like ‘psychology can help’ — instead, specify which psychological concept applies, which study supports it, and exactly how it could be used in the given scenario.

GCSE Example: Applying Explanations of Non-Verbal Behaviour to Real Life

A strong application answer: ‘Understanding of explanations of non-verbal behaviour could help in schools by [specific application]. This is supported by [named study] which found that [specific finding]. This means that [specific practical recommendation]. However, a limitation is [specific weakness of applying this research].’
