Mental Health: An Introduction

Understanding mental health: the difference between mental health and mental illness, the medical model, the biopsychosocial model, and cultural issues in diagnosing mental health problems.

Key Fact: Mental health: a state of wellbeing where a person can cope with normal stresses, work productively, and contribute to their community (WHO definition). Mental health exists on a continuum - it is not simply 'healthy' or 'ill'.
Key Fact: Mental illness: a condition that affects thinking, feeling or behaviour, causing significant distress. The medical model views mental illness as a disease with biological causes (chemical imbalances, genetics, brain damage) treatable with medication.
Key Fact: The biopsychosocial model (Engel): mental illness results from biological factors (genes, neurotransmitters), psychological factors (cognitions, trauma, learned behaviour), and social factors (poverty, discrimination, social isolation). This is more comprehensive than the medical model alone.
Key Fact: Cultural issues in diagnosis: different cultures express and interpret symptoms differently. Culture-bound syndromes (e.g. ataque de nervios in Latin American cultures) are not recognised in Western diagnostic systems. Ethnocentric bias means Western diagnostic criteria may not apply globally.
Key Fact: Stigma and mental health: stigma (negative stereotypes) prevents people seeking help. The medical model can reduce blame (it's an illness, not a personal failing) but can also increase stigma (labelled as 'mentally ill'). Anti-stigma campaigns (e.g. Time to Change) aim to normalise mental health problems.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Compare the medical model with the biopsychosocial model of mental illness.

Q2: Explain how cultural factors can affect the diagnosis of mental health problems.

Q3: Evaluate whether the medical model is the best approach to understanding mental illness.

✅ Answers

  1. Medical model: mental illness is a disease with biological causes (genetics, neurotransmitters, brain abnormalities). Treatment: medication (drugs). Biopsychosocial model: mental illness results from biological, psychological and social factors interacting. Treatment: combination of medication, therapy and social support. The biopsychosocial model is more comprehensive but harder to implement.
  2. Cultural factors: symptoms vary by culture (e.g. depression expressed as physical pain in some Asian cultures); culture-bound syndromes don't fit Western diagnoses; diagnostic criteria (DSM-5, ICD-11) are Western-developed and may not generalise; language barriers affect diagnosis; stigma varies across cultures. Result: people from minority cultures may be misdiagnosed or under-diagnosed.
  3. Medical model strengths: scientific basis (brain chemistry evidence), effective drug treatments (SSRIs for depression), reduces blame (illness not personal failure). Limitations: reductionist (ignores social and psychological causes), over-reliance on medication (side effects, doesn't address root causes), deterministic (implies individuals cannot change). The biopsychosocial model is more comprehensive but the medical model provides effective short-term treatment.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Medical model = BIOLOGICAL causes only (drugs treat it). Biopsychosocial = BIO + PSYCHO + SOCIAL (combined treatment).
  • Culture affects diagnosis: different cultures express symptoms differently. Western diagnostic systems may not generalise.
  • Mental health is a CONTINUUM, not a simple healthy/ill divide.

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating mental health models, consider: is the model reductionist? Does it lead to effective treatment? Does it account for individual and cultural differences? Always use a biopsychosocial approach for comprehensive evaluation.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Mental illness is simply a chemical imbalance in the brain, so medication alone is the best treatment. Correct: While neurotransmitter imbalances are associated with many mental health conditions, the relationship is correlational not necessarily causal. The diathesis-stress model suggests biological vulnerability interacts with psychological and social factors. Medication alone treats symptoms but doesn't address underlying causes (e.g. trauma, social isolation, unhelpful thinking patterns). Combined treatment (medication + therapy + social support) is generally more effective than medication alone.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether the medical model or the biopsychosocial model provides a better understanding of mental health problems.

A grade 9 response will: define both models; present medical model strengths (scientific evidence, effective drug treatments, reduces blame) and limitations (reductionist, ignores social/psychological factors, over-relies on medication); present biopsychosocial strengths (comprehensive, considers multiple causes, combined treatment) and limitations (harder to implement, less precise, research is less controlled); conclude: the medical model is useful for acute treatment and biological understanding, but the biopsychosocial model provides a more complete explanation. For effective long-term treatment, an integrated approach using both models is best.

Key Studies and Theories in Mental Health: An Introduction

The AQA, OCR and Edexcel specifications all require you to know specific studies in detail, evaluate their methodology, and apply psychological concepts to real-life scenarios. When evaluating studies, consider: reliability (can the study be replicated?); validity (does it measure what it claims to measure?); ethics (were participants protected from harm?); generalisability (can the findings be applied beyond the study sample?); and usefulness (does the research have practical applications?).

Evaluating Research in Mental Health: An Introduction

Evaluation is a crucial skill in GCSE Psychology. When evaluating studies related to mental health: an introduction, use the GRAVE method: Generalisability (can the findings be applied to the wider population?), Reliability (are the results consistent if the study is repeated?), Applications (how can the findings be used in real life?), Validity (does the study measure what it claims to measure?), and Ethics (were participants treated ethically?).

Research methods commonly used in mental health: an introduction research include: laboratory experiments (high control but low ecological validity), field experiments (more realistic but less control), natural experiments (no manipulation of variables), case studies (in-depth but not generalisable), questionnaires (large samples but social desirability bias), and observations (natural behaviour but ethical concerns). Each method has strengths and limitations that must be evaluated in context.

Ethical issues in mental health: an introduction research include: informed consent (participants must agree to take part), debriefing (explaining the true purpose after the study), confidentiality (protecting participants’ identities), protection from harm (no physical or psychological damage), and right to withdraw (participants can leave at any time). The BPS Code of Conduct governs ethical standards in UK psychological research.

GCSE Example: Evaluating Research in Mental Health: An Introduction

When evaluating Milgram’s obedience study, you might note: low generalisability (all male, American sample); high reliability (standardised procedure with 65% obedience rate); useful applications (understanding wartime atrocities); low ecological validity (artificial laboratory setting); and serious ethical concerns (deception, psychological distress).
