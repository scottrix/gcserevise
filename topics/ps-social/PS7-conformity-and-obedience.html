Conformity & Obedience in Depth

Explanations for conformity and obedience: normative and informational social influence, agentic state and legitimacy of authority. How minority influence creates social change.

Key Fact: Normative social influence (NSI): conforming to be accepted and avoid rejection - leads to compliance. Driven by the desire for social approval.
Key Fact: Informational social influence (ISI): conforming because we believe the group is correct - leads to internalisation. Most likely in ambiguous or novel situations.
Key Fact: Agentic State theory (Milgram): people obey because they enter an 'agentic state' - they see themselves as instruments carrying out another's wishes, not responsible for their actions. Binding factors (politeness, fear of disruption) keep people in the agentic state.
Key Fact: Legitimacy of authority: we obey people we perceive as having legitimate power (uniform, institutional context). When authority is perceived as illegitimate, obedience decreases (Milgram's run-down office: 48% vs 65% at Yale).
Key Fact: Minority influence creates social change through: consistency, commitment, and flexibility. Process: minority -> consistency -> cognitive conflict -> augmentation -> snowball effect -> social crypto-amnesia.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between normative and informational social influence.

Q2: Describe Milgram's agentic state theory and evaluate its usefulness for understanding obedience.

Q3: Explain how minority influence can lead to social change.

✅ Answers

  1. NSI: driven by desire to be liked/accepted, leads to compliance, occurs when the correct answer is obvious. ISI: driven by desire to be correct, leads to internalisation, occurs in ambiguous situations.
  2. The agentic state explains how ordinary people commit harmful acts - they feel they are 'just following orders'. Supported by Milgram's variations. Limitations: doesn't explain why some people resist; may excuse harmful behaviour.
  3. Social change through minority influence: (1) Minority is consistent - creates cognitive conflict. (2) Minority shows commitment - augmentation principle. (3) Minority is flexible - avoids seeming dogmatic. (4) Snowball effect builds. (5) Social crypto-amnesia. Example: the suffragette movement.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • NSI = want to be LIKED. ISI = want to be RIGHT.
  • Agentic state = 'I'm just following orders.' Autonomous state = 'I'm making my own choices.'
  • Minority influence needs: Consistency + Commitment + Flexibility = CCF.

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating theories of social influence, always link to real-world examples: the Holocaust (obedience), civil rights movements (minority influence), and modern social media campaigns (normative influence).

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: People who obey authority are simply cruel or weak-willed. Correct: Milgram's research showed that ordinary, well-adjusted people obeyed harmful instructions - 65% delivered maximum shocks. This demonstrates that obedience is a situational phenomenon, not a personality trait. The agentic state explains that people obey because they enter a different psychological state, not because they are cruel.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate whether minority influence or majority influence is more powerful in creating social change.

A grade 9 response will: explain majority influence (normative and informational) - powerful through social approval; explain minority influence (consistency, commitment, flexibility) - creates change through cognitive conflict; compare: majority influence is immediate but may only produce compliance; minority influence is slower but creates deeper internalisation; conclude: minority influence is more effective for lasting social change because it produces genuine belief change.
