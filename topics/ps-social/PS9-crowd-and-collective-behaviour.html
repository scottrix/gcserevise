Crowd & Collective Behaviour

How groups behave: deindividuation, social identity theory, in-group and out-group behaviour, prejudice and discrimination, and how crowds can become aggressive or prosocial.

Key Fact: Deindividuation: losing your sense of personal identity in a crowd, which can lead to behaviour you would not normally do. Anonymity (e.g. uniforms, masks) reduces self-awareness and increases conformity to group norms.
Key Fact: Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner): we derive part of our identity from group membership. Process: social categorisation -> social identification -> social comparison. This can lead to in-group favouritism and out-group discrimination.
Key Fact: Tajfel's minimal group paradigm: even when groups are assigned arbitrarily (e.g. preference for Klee vs Kandinsky), participants favoured their in-group. This shows that even meaningless group distinctions create bias.
Key Fact: Prejudice: a negative attitude towards a group based on stereotypes. Discrimination: unfair treatment based on group membership. Causes: social identity, scapegoating, media representations, and authoritarian personality.
Key Fact: Reducing prejudice: intergroup contact (Allport's contact hypothesis), superordinate goals (Sherif's Robbers Cave experiment), and education about stereotyping.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain how deindividuation can lead to both antisocial and prosocial behaviour in crowds.

Q2: Describe Tajfel's minimal group paradigm and evaluate what it tells us about prejudice.

Q3: Evaluate methods for reducing prejudice, referring to psychological research.

✅ Answers

  1. Deindividuation reduces personal accountability - people follow group norms. In an antisocial crowd (riot), anonymity leads to aggression because the group norm is destructive. In a prosocial crowd (charity run), anonymity leads to helpfulness because the group norm is supportive.
  2. Tajfel showed that even arbitrary group divisions create in-group favouritism. This demonstrates that prejudice arises from the basic cognitive process of categorisation. Limitation: the paradigm is artificial - real-world prejudice involves history, power, and material conflict.
  3. Contact hypothesis: prejudice reduces when groups interact under equal status, cooperation, and shared goals. Sherif's Robbers Cave: hostile groups reconciled when they worked together on superordinate goals. Evaluation: contact works under optimal conditions but may backfire if groups compete.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Deindividuation = losing personal identity in a crowd. Behaviour follows GROUP NORMS (good or bad).
  • Social Identity Theory: Categorise -> Identify -> Compare. 'Us vs Them' thinking is automatic.
  • Reduce prejudice: contact + superordinate goals + equal status + education.

📝 Exam Technique

Psychology Exam Tips:
When evaluating crowd behaviour and prejudice, consider: lab vs real-world, cultural factors, and practical applications (how can findings reduce discrimination in schools and workplaces?).

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Crowds always make people behave more aggressively than they would alone. Correct: Deindividuation theory shows that crowds lead people to follow group norms - which can be aggressive or prosocial. Johnson & Downing (1979) found that participants in KKK-style uniforms gave more shocks (antisocial norm), but those in nurses' uniforms gave fewer shocks (prosocial norm). The crowd amplifies the existing group norm.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that prejudice is an inevitable consequence of group membership.

A grade 9 response will: explain SIT (categorisation creates in-group favouritism automatically); support with Tajfel's minimal group paradigm; evaluate (SIT shows bias is basic to group membership); counter-argument (prejudice levels vary across cultures and time, contact reduces prejudice, superordinate goals create cooperation); conclude: while the cognitive processes are basic, the expression of prejudice is not inevitable - it depends on social context and whether conditions promote intergroup contact.
