R1: Retrieval & Literal Comprehension

Foundation Higher All Boards AO1

Finding and extracting explicit information from texts - what the text directly states.

📋 What is Retrieval?

Definition: Retrieval means finding information that is directly stated in the text. You don't need to guess or interpret - the answer is right there in black and white.

Retrieval questions ask you to:

  • Find specific details mentioned in the text
  • List things the writer has directly stated
  • Locate information about characters, settings, or events
  • Identify facts, statistics, or examples
Golden Rule:
If you have to guess, it's probably NOT retrieval.
The answer is always EXPLICITLY stated in the text.

📝 Types of Retrieval Questions

1. "List Four Things" Questions (AQA Paper 1 Q1)

These questions ask you to list specific details from a text. They're worth 4 marks - 1 mark for each correct point.

Example Question

Look in detail at lines 1-10. List four things you learn about the narrator's surroundings.

You should write your answer as a list, using full sentences or bullet points.

2. True/False Questions (AQA Paper 2 Q1)

Circle the correct option - the answer is directly stated in the text.

Example Question

Which of these statements is TRUE? Shade the correct circle.

  • A) The writer visited London in 1905
  • B) The writer was born in Manchester
  • C) The writer published three novels
  • D) The writer never married

3. Summary Questions (AQA Paper 2 Q2)

Summarise the differences or similarities between two texts.

Example Question

Use details from both sources to write a summary of the differences between the two writers' experiences.

🔍 How to Find Information

Strategy 1: Scanning

Quickly move your eyes over the text to locate specific keywords related to the question.

  • Look for names, dates, places, or specific nouns
  • Don't read every word - just look for keywords
  • Once you find the relevant section, slow down and read carefully

Strategy 2: Skimming

Read quickly to get the gist of each paragraph before looking for specific details.

  • Read the first and last sentences of each paragraph
  • Get an overview of the whole text
  • Then return to relevant sections for closer reading

Strategy 3: Line References

If the question gives line numbers, read those lines carefully first.

  • Check the lines before and after for context
  • Don't rely on memory - always check the text
  • Highlight or underline relevant information

✏️ How to Write Your Answer

For "List Four Things" Questions:

Top Tips:

  • Write in your own words where possible (but keep close to the text)
  • You can use bullet points or numbered lists
  • Each point should be a complete idea
  • Don't repeat the same point in different words
Good Example

The narrator is in a dark, dusty attic.

There are boxes stacked against the walls.

A single window lets in dim light.

The air smells of old books and mothballs.

Bad Example (Too Vague)

It's dark.

There's stuff there.

A window.

Smells.

For Summary Questions:

  • Use synthesising language: "While Source A... Source B..."
  • Focus on DIFFERENCES or SIMILARITIES as asked
  • Use short, relevant quotations to support points
  • Don't analyse language - just report what's there

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Using inference when retrieval is asked for You're guessing instead of finding what's stated Find the exact information in the text
Quoting too much You lose marks for copying whole sections Select only the relevant words
Not answering all parts of the question You might miss easy marks Check you've listed the right number of points
Repeating the same point You won't get marks for saying the same thing twice Make each point different
Analysing language when asked to retrieve That's a different question type Just find and state the facts

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AQA Paper 1 Question 1 (4 marks)

  • Level 4: 4 marks - Four valid points identified
  • Level 3: 3 marks - Three valid points identified
  • Level 2: 2 marks - Two valid points identified
  • Level 1: 1 mark - One valid point identified
Examiner's Tip: This is one of the easiest questions on the paper. Don't overthink it - just find four things and write them down. Aim to complete this in about 5 minutes.

AQA Paper 2 Question 2 (8 marks)

  • Requires synthesis of both texts
  • Show clear understanding of differences
  • Use relevant quotations from both sources
  • Don't analyse language - focus on content

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: When answering a "List four things" question, should you use your own words or copy the text exactly?

Q2: What's the difference between retrieval and inference?

Q3: How long should you spend on AQA Paper 1 Q1?

Q4: What two strategies can you use to quickly find information in a text?

✅ Answers

  1. Either is acceptable, but it's better to use your own words while staying close to the text's meaning.
  2. Retrieval finds information directly stated in the text. Inference reads between the lines to interpret implied meanings.
  3. About 5 minutes - it's worth only 4 marks.
  4. Scanning (looking for keywords) and skimming (reading quickly for gist).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Read the question carefully and check line references
  • Don't analyse language - just find facts
  • Use bullet points for clarity
  • Check you have the right number of points
  • Keep answers concise - don't waffle
  • This is usually the easiest question - get it done quickly!

📝 Exam Technique

Retrieval Exam Strategy: This is the easiest question on the paper - don't overcomplicate it. For AQA Paper 1 Q1 (4 marks), spend no more than 5 minutes. Read the question carefully, note any line references, scan for keywords, then write four clear points. For AQA Paper 2 Q1 (true/false), go back to the text and verify each option before choosing. Examiners mark this question generously - they want to give you full marks. Just find the information and state it clearly.

Time management: Q1 (Paper 1) = 5 minutes for 4 marks. Q1 (Paper 2) = 2 minutes for 4 marks. Q2 (Paper 2) = 10 minutes for 8 marks.

What examiners look for: Accurate information taken directly from the text, clear expression of each point as a complete idea, and four distinct points that don't repeat the same detail in different words.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often try to infer when the question asks for retrieval, reading between the lines instead of finding what is directly stated. Wrong: "The character probably feels lonely because she lives alone." Correct: "The character lives alone in a 'small flat on the outskirts of town.'"

Students often copy huge chunks of text instead of selecting the key detail. Wrong: Quoting an entire paragraph to make one point. Correct: Extracting the specific detail and rephrasing in your own words.

Students often make the same point twice using different wording. Wrong: "It was raining heavily" and "There was a downpour." Correct: Making each point about a different detail from the text.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

4 marks: Look in detail at lines 1-8. List four things you learn about the setting from this part of the source.

The setting is a remote coastal village, described as "perched on the edge of the cliff" which suggests it is dangerously close to the sea below. The houses are old and weather-worn, with "whitewashed walls peeling in the salt wind," indicating they have long been exposed to harsh coastal conditions. There is a single narrow road leading into the village, implying it is isolated and difficult to reach. The harbour below contains only "a handful of fishing boats," revealing that fishing is a key but small-scale industry in the community.

Mark scheme: 1 mark for each valid point identified from the specified lines. Points must be distinct and accurately reflect information stated in the text. Own words or close paraphrase accepted.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 requires you to "identify and interpret explicit information and ideas." For retrieval, the key word is explicit - the information must be directly stated, not implied. At grade 5, students can find relevant details but may present them vaguely or repeat the same point. At grade 9, students demonstrate precise selection of information, expressing each point as a fully developed idea in their own words, and ensuring every point is distinct. The difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is often clarity and precision: grade 9 responses show the examiner that the student has truly understood what the text states, rather than just copied fragments without comprehension.
