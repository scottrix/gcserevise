R2: Inference & Deduction

Foundation Higher All Boards AO1

Reading between the lines - understanding what is suggested or implied, not directly stated.

📋 What is Inference?

Definition: Inference means using evidence from the text AND your own reasoning to work out meanings that are NOT directly stated. You "read between the lines."

When you infer, you:

  • Use clues from the text (words, phrases, actions, descriptions)
  • Apply your knowledge and experience
  • Draw logical conclusions about characters, feelings, settings, or themes
  • Make sensible guesses that are supported by evidence
The Inference Formula:
Text Evidence + Your Knowledge = Inference
(Always support your inference with a quotation!)

🔍 Retrieval vs Inference

Retrieval Inference
Information is directly stated Information is suggested or implied
You FIND the answer in the text You WORK OUT the answer using clues
"The boy was wearing a red coat" "The boy shivered and pulled his coat tighter" → He was cold
One correct answer Multiple valid interpretations (with evidence)
AO1 - Identify and interpret explicit information AO1 - Identify and interpret implicit information
Compare These Sentences

Retrieval: "Sarah slammed the door and stormed upstairs."

Question: What did Sarah do?

Answer: She slammed the door and went upstairs. (Directly stated)


Inference: "Sarah slammed the door and stormed upstairs."

Question: How is Sarah feeling?

Answer: Sarah is angry or upset. We know this because she "slammed" the door and "stormed" - both words suggest aggressive, emotional movement.

📖 How to Make Inferences

Step 1: Identify What You're Being Asked

Inference questions often ask about:

  • Feelings and emotions: "How does the character feel?"
  • Atmosphere and mood: "What is the atmosphere in this scene?"
  • Character traits: "What kind of person is...?"
  • Relationships: "What is the relationship between...?"
  • Themes and ideas: "What does the writer suggest about...?"

Step 2: Find Evidence

  • Look for specific words, actions, or descriptions
  • Note dialogue and how characters speak
  • Consider what is NOT said (omissions matter)
  • Think about connotations of word choices

Step 3: Draw Your Conclusion

  • What does this evidence suggest?
  • What do you know about similar situations?
  • What is the most logical interpretation?

Step 4: Write Your Answer

  • State your inference clearly
  • Support it with a quotation
  • Explain HOW the evidence supports your inference

✏️ Writing Inference Answers

PEE Structure:
Point - State your inference
Evidence - Quote from the text
Explain - Show how the evidence proves your point
Example

Text: "Mia checked her phone again. Still nothing. She paced across the room, glancing at the clock every few seconds."


Question: What does this suggest about Mia's state of mind?


Answer:

(Point) Mia appears anxious and impatient.

(Evidence) She "checked her phone again" and "glanced at the clock every few seconds."

(Explanation) The repetition of checking suggests she's waiting for something important, and constantly looking at the clock shows she is impatient and worried about time passing.

🎭 Types of Inference

1. Emotional Inference

Working out how a character feels based on their actions, words, or body language.

Example

"His hands trembled as he reached for the letter."

Inference: He is nervous, scared, or anxious about what the letter contains. The trembling hands are a physical sign of strong emotion.

2. Character Inference

Understanding a character's personality, motivations, or traits.

Example

"Even though she was exhausted, Mrs. Chen stopped to help the elderly man with his groceries."

Inference: Mrs. Chen is kind, compassionate, and selfless. She prioritises helping others even when she's tired.

3. Atmosphere Inference

Understanding the mood or feeling created in a scene.

Example

"The house stood silent at the end of the lane, its windows dark, its garden overgrown with weeds."

Inference: The atmosphere is eerie, abandoned, or melancholic. The "silent" house and "dark" windows suggest emptiness, while "overgrown" indicates neglect.

4. Relationship Inference

Understanding how characters relate to each other.

Example

"James couldn't even look at his brother. 'You know what you did,' he muttered."

Inference: There is tension and conflict between the brothers. James "couldn't even look" suggests deep hurt or anger, and "you know what you did" implies betrayal or wrongdoing.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Making claims without evidence: Always back up your inference with a quotation
  • Being too vague: "She's upset" - be more specific: "She's disappointed and hurt"
  • Confusing retrieval with inference: If it's directly stated, you're not inferring
  • Over-interpreting: Stick to what the text reasonably suggests - don't make wild guesses
  • Using weak evidence: Choose the strongest quotations that support your point

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Read this sentence: "Tom stared at the empty space where his bike had been, his fists clenched at his sides."

What emotion is Tom likely feeling, and what evidence supports this?

Q2: What's the difference between retrieval and inference?

Q3: "The classroom fell silent as Mrs. Roberts entered. Even the usually chatty students straightened in their seats."

What can you infer about Mrs. Roberts?

Q4: Write a PEE paragraph inferring the atmosphere of this setting: "Thick fog rolled across the empty streets. Every shop was closed, every window dark."

✅ Answers

  1. Tom is likely feeling anger or frustration. Evidence: "his fists clenched" suggests tension and anger; "stared at the empty space" shows disbelief or shock at the theft.
  2. Retrieval finds information directly stated in the text. Inference uses clues and reasoning to work out meanings that are implied but not directly stated.
  3. Mrs. Roberts appears to be a strict or intimidating teacher. The "classroom fell silent" and students "straightened in their seats" suggest they respect and possibly fear her authority.
  4. (Point) The atmosphere is eerie and abandoned. (Evidence) "Thick fog rolled" and "empty streets" create a sense of isolation, while "every shop was closed, every window dark" suggests emptiness and absence of life. (Explanation) The combination of fog (obscuring vision) and darkness suggests mystery and unease.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always support inferences with quotations
  • Use PEE structure for clear, structured answers
  • Focus on words with strong connotations
  • Be specific - avoid vague emotional words
  • Consider the wider context, not just isolated words
  • Trust your instincts but verify with evidence
