R3: Language Analysis

Foundation Higher All Boards AO2

Analysing how writers use language to create effects and influence readers.

📋 What is Language Analysis?

Definition: Language analysis examines how writers choose and use words, phrases and literary devices to create specific effects on the reader.

Language analysis involves:

  • Identifying language devices and techniques
  • Explaining how these create effects
  • Linking techniques to the writer's purpose
  • Considering the impact on the reader
Key Formula:
Identify → Explain effect → Link to purpose/reader

📝 Key Language Devices

Figurative Language

Metaphor

Direct comparison where one thing is said to be another.

"The city was a beast, hungry and restless."

Simile

Comparison using 'like' or 'as'.

"Her voice was as sharp as broken glass."

Personification

Giving human qualities to non-human things.

"The wind howled through the empty streets."

Sound Devices

Alliteration: Repetition of consonant sounds for emphasis or musicality.
"The slippery snake slithered silently."
Onomatopoeia: Words that sound like what they describe.
"Crash, bang, hiss, whisper."
Sibilance: Repeated 's' sounds creating a hissing effect.
"The soft, silky snake slid through the grass."

Word Choice

  • Semantic field: Groups of words related to a particular topic or theme
  • Emotive language: Words designed to evoke emotional responses
  • Sensory language: Appeals to the five senses (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell)
  • Connotations: Associations and feelings words evoke beyond their literal meaning

🔍 How to Analyse Language

The PEEL Method

Point - Make a clear point about the language used
Evidence - Quote the specific words or phrase
Explain - Explain the effect created
Link - Link to the writer's purpose or reader's response
Example PEEL Paragraph

Point: The writer uses a powerful metaphor to convey the character's isolation.

Evidence: She describes herself as "a ghost haunting her own life."

Explain: The metaphor suggests she feels invisible and disconnected from reality, as if she no longer belongs in the world of the living.

Link: This emphasises the devastating impact of her grief on the reader, making us empathise with her sense of loss.

What to Avoid

Mistake Correction
Just naming devices Always explain the effect
Using long quotations Embed short, relevant quotes
Saying "this makes the reader want to read on" Be specific about the emotional response
Writing "this is effective" Explain WHY and HOW it's effective

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Feature spotting: Listing devices without explaining effects
  • Generic comments: Saying "it draws the reader in" without specifics
  • Over-quoting: Using whole sentences when a few words would suffice
  • Ignoring context: Not considering why the writer chose this technique
  • Vague terminology: Using "powerful" or "effective" without explanation

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AQA Paper 1 Question 2 (8 marks)

  • Level 4 (7-8 marks): Detailed analysis with clear explanation of effects
  • Level 3 (5-6 marks): Clear analysis with some explanation of effects
  • Level 2 (3-4 marks): Some understanding with limited explanation
  • Level 1 (1-2 marks): Simple identification with minimal explanation
Examiner's Tip: Focus on 2-3 language features in depth rather than listing many superficially. Always link to effect on the reader.

AQA Paper 2 Question 3 (12 marks)

  • Same skills but applied to non-fiction texts
  • Consider writer's viewpoint and perspective
  • Look for persuasive and rhetorical devices

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What is the difference between a metaphor and a simile?

Q2: What does PEEL stand for?

Q3: Why should you avoid writing "this makes the reader want to read on"?

Q4: What is emotive language and why do writers use it?

✅ Answers

  1. A metaphor directly says something IS something else, while a simile compares using "like" or "as".
  2. Point, Evidence, Explain, Link - a structure for writing analytical paragraphs.
  3. It's too vague and generic - you need to explain the specific emotional or intellectual response created.
  4. Emotive language uses words designed to provoke emotional responses, used to manipulate the reader's feelings about a topic.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Use precise terminology but focus on effects
  • Embed short quotations into your sentences
  • Consider the writer's purpose and context
  • Focus on depth over breadth - analyse fewer features in more detail
  • Use phrases like "this suggests", "this conveys", "this implies"
  • Always link back to the question focus

📝 Exam Technique

Language Analysis Exam Strategy: Focus on 2-3 language features in real depth rather than listing many devices superficially. For AQA Paper 1 Q2 (8 marks), spend 10 minutes. For Paper 2 Q3 (12 marks), spend 15 minutes. Always follow: identify the device → quote it → explain the specific effect → link to the writer's purpose. Examiners reward depth and precision over breadth. Never just name a device - always explain what it does to the reader.

Time management: Paper 1 Q2 = 10 minutes for 8 marks. Paper 2 Q3 = 15 minutes for 12 marks. Aim for 2-3 fully developed PEEL paragraphs.

What examiners look for: Precise subject terminology used accurately, short embedded quotations, detailed explanation of effects on the reader, and clear links between the language choice and the writer's purpose. Top responses explore connotations and layers of meaning.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often "feature spot" - naming devices without explaining their effect. Wrong: "The writer uses a metaphor to describe the city." Correct: "The metaphor 'the city was a beast' dehumanises the urban environment, presenting it as predatory and threatening, as though it could consume the individual."

Students often use vague reader-response phrases that say nothing specific. Wrong: "This makes the reader want to read on" or "This is effective." Correct: "This creates a sense of unease in the reader, as the familiar landscape becomes threatening."

Students often analyse quotations that are too long, losing focus on the key words. Wrong: Quoting a full sentence and making a general comment about it. Correct: Zooming into a single word like 'festering' and exploring its connotations of decay and infection.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

8 marks: Look in detail at lines 1-9. How does the writer use language here to describe the storm? You should include the writer's choice of words and phrases.

The writer personifies the storm as a violent, predatory force. The verb "prowled" suggests the storm is like a wild animal hunting its prey, creating a sense of danger that is deliberate and malicious rather than random. This personification is extended through the phrase "clawed at the rooftops," where the verb "clawed" has connotations of savage, desperate aggression, as though the storm is trying to tear the houses apart. The alliterative "furious fingers" reinforces this imagery of a hostile entity physically attacking the town, while the fricative 'f' sounds mimic the howling wind, making the reader almost hear the storm's rage. Together, these choices present nature not as indifferent but as actively malevolent, positioning the reader to feel the vulnerability of the people below.

Mark scheme: Level 4 (7-8 marks) requires perceptive analysis with detailed explanation of effects. Must use precise terminology, embed short quotations, and explore connotations and layers of meaning.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO2 asks you to "explain, comment on and analyse how writers use language and structure to achieve effects and influence readers." For language analysis, the focus is on word-level choices. At grade 5, students identify devices and offer basic explanations of effect ("the metaphor shows the storm is dangerous"). At grade 9, students explore the connotations and implications of specific word choices, consider alternative interpretations, and trace patterns across the extract (such as a semantic field of violence). The crucial leap is from describing what a device does to exploring how it works on the reader - moving from "the writer uses a simile" to "the simile transforms the reader's perception, forcing us to see the familiar as threatening."
