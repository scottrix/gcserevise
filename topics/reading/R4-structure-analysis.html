R4: Structure Analysis

Foundation Higher All Boards AO2

Analysing how writers organise and structure their texts for effect.

📋 What is Structure Analysis?

Definition: Structure analysis examines how a text is organised - the order of ideas, paragraphing, sentence variety, and narrative techniques used to shape meaning.

Structure analysis involves:

  • Examining how the text begins, develops and ends
  • Analysing paragraph organisation and length
  • Looking at sentence variety and length
  • Considering shifts in focus, time or perspective
Remember:
Language = WHAT words are used
Structure = HOW the text is organised

📝 Key Structural Features

Whole Text Structure

Openings: How does the text begin? What effect does the opening have?
- In media res (starting in the middle of the action)
- Setting the scene
- Creating mystery or intrigue
Endings: How does the text conclude? What is the final impression?
- Resolution or closure
- Cliffhanger or ambiguity
- Reflective circular ending
Narrative Perspective: Who is telling the story?
- First person (I, we) - personal, subjective
- Third person (he, she, they) - more detached
- Omniscient narrator - knows everything

Paragraph Level

  • Paragraph length: Short paragraphs for impact, long for detail
  • Topic sentences: Opening sentences that introduce the main idea
  • Focus shifts: Changes in subject, time, place or perspective
  • Repetition: Ideas or phrases that recur throughout

Sentence Level

Sentence Length Effects

Short sentences: Create tension, drama, urgency

"She stopped. Listened. Nothing."

Long sentences: Build detail, create flow, slow pace

Sentence Types

Simple: One clause - clear, direct, emphatic

Compound: Two or more main clauses - balanced, connected

Complex: Main + subordinate clause - layered, detailed

🔍 How to Analyse Structure

Questions to Ask

  • Where does the writer's attention focus at the beginning? How does this change?
  • Are there shifts in time, place, or perspective?
  • How does paragraph length affect pacing?
  • What information is revealed and when?
  • How does the ending connect to the beginning?

Structural Analysis Framework

Beginning: How does the text start? What does the writer focus on?
Middle: How does it develop? What shifts occur?
End: How does it conclude? What's the final effect?
Example Analysis

The text begins with a close focus on the narrator's immediate surroundings, creating claustrophobia. The writer then zooms out to the wider landscape, suggesting freedom beyond. This structural shift mirrors the narrator's emotional journey from trapped to liberated.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Correction
Analysing language instead of structure Focus on organisation, not word choice
Listing structural features without effect Always explain the effect of structural choices
Ignoring the whole text structure Consider beginning, middle and end
Forgetting sentence-level analysis Comment on sentence variety and length
Not using structural terminology Use terms like "focus", "shift", "perspective"

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AQA Paper 1 Question 3 (8 marks)

  • Level 4 (7-8 marks): Detailed analysis of structural features with clear effects
  • Level 3 (5-6 marks): Clear analysis with some explanation
  • Level 2 (3-4 marks): Some understanding of structure
  • Level 1 (1-2 marks): Simple or irrelevant comment
Examiner's Tip: This question often causes confusion with language analysis. Focus on WHERE things are placed and WHY - not what words mean. Think about the "journey" through the text.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What is the difference between language and structure analysis?

Q2: Why might a writer use a short paragraph?

Q3: What does "in media res" mean?

Q4: How can sentence length affect the pacing of a text?

✅ Answers

  1. Language analysis looks at word choice and literary devices; structure analysis looks at how the text is organised and sequenced.
  2. To create impact, drama, tension, or to draw attention to a key moment or idea.
  3. Starting in the middle of the action - a narrative technique that creates immediate engagement.
  4. Short sentences speed up pacing and create tension; long sentences slow pacing and allow for detailed description.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Trace the reader's journey through the text
  • Comment on beginnings, middles and endings
  • Look for shifts in focus, time, or perspective
  • Use structural terminology: focus, shift, perspective, sequence
  • Consider paragraph and sentence length
  • Don't analyse language - that's a different question!
