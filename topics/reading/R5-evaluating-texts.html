R5: Evaluating Texts

Foundation Higher All Boards AO4

Critically evaluating texts and supporting opinions with textual evidence.

📋 What is Evaluation?

Definition: Evaluation means making judgements about a text and supporting your opinions with evidence. You assess how successfully the writer achieves their purpose.

Evaluation involves:

  • Making a judgement about the text's effectiveness
  • Supporting your view with specific evidence
  • Considering how the text affects the reader
  • Analysing the writer's methods and choices
Key Principle:
There's no "right" opinion - but you must support your view with evidence from the text.

📝 Types of Evaluation Questions

1. "To What Extent..." Questions

Example Question

To what extent do you agree that the writer creates a sense of danger?

You should write about the writer's methods and support your ideas with quotations.

2. Statement Evaluation

Example Question

"The writer makes the reader feel sorry for the main character."

To what extent do you agree? Support your answer with detailed references to the text.

3. Overall Effectiveness

Example Question

How successfully does the writer convey their viewpoint?

🔍 How to Evaluate a Text

Step-by-Step Approach

Step 1: Decide your overall opinion - agree, disagree, or partially agree
Step 2: Find evidence that supports your view
Step 3: Analyse how the writer achieves the effect
Step 4: Consider alternative viewpoints if appropriate
Step 5: Reach a conclusion

Structuring Your Response

  • Start with a clear statement of your opinion
  • Use PEEL paragraphs to develop your argument
  • Include detailed analysis of methods
  • Use quotations to support your points
  • End with a reasoned conclusion
Example Evaluation

I largely agree that the writer creates a sense of danger. The opening establishes threat immediately through the metaphor "the shadows stalked him," personifying darkness as a predator. This is reinforced by short, punchy sentences like "He ran. Faster. Harder." which accelerate the pace and mirror his panic. However, the writer also creates moments of false security, such as the "deceptive calm" of the moonlit clearing, which actually heighten tension by suggesting danger could emerge at any moment.

📊 Key Skills for Evaluation

Using Evidence

  • Select relevant, specific quotations
  • Embed quotes naturally into your writing
  • Explain how the evidence supports your judgement
  • Use quotations throughout your response

Analysing Methods

  • Comment on language choices and effects
  • Consider structural techniques
  • Discuss tone and style
  • Link methods to the overall impact

Expressing Opinions

Phrase Type Examples
Agreement I agree that... / This effectively creates... / The writer successfully...
Partial agreement To some extent... / While... I also think... / However, this could also be seen as...
Disagreement I disagree because... / This is not convincing because... / The effect is actually...

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Correction
Just retelling the story Focus on evaluating, not summarising
Not giving an opinion Make a clear judgement
Lack of evidence Support every point with quotations
Only discussing language Consider structure and form too
Being too vague Use specific examples and analyse effects

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AQA Paper 1 Question 4 (20 marks)

  • Level 4 (17-20 marks): Perceptive evaluation with detailed analysis
  • Level 3 (13-16 marks): Clear evaluation with clear analysis
  • Level 2 (9-12 marks): Some evaluation with some comment
  • Level 1 (5-8 marks): Simple comment with limited support
  • Level 0 (0-4 marks): Nothing relevant or response not linked to text
Examiner's Tip: This is worth 20 marks - the most on the paper. Spend about 20-25 minutes on it. Make sure you give a clear opinion and back it up with detailed analysis of the text.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What does "evaluate" mean in the context of English Language?

Q2: How should you structure an evaluation response?

Q3: Why is it important to use evidence in evaluation?

Q4: What phrases can you use to show partial agreement?

✅ Answers

  1. To make a judgement about how effective or successful a text is, supported by evidence and analysis.
  2. Start with your opinion, use PEEL paragraphs with evidence and analysis, consider alternative views, and reach a conclusion.
  3. Evidence proves your opinion is based on the text rather than just personal preference - it shows you can support your ideas.
  4. "To some extent...", "While I agree that..., I also think...", "However, this could also be seen as..."

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Start by deciding your overall opinion
  • Make your judgement clear from the start
  • Use "I agree/disagree" language confidently
  • Support every point with specific evidence
  • Analyse language AND structure where relevant
  • Consider both sides for a balanced argument
  • This is a high-mark question - manage your time well

📝 Exam Technique

Evaluation Exam Strategy: This is AQA Paper 1 Q4 - the highest-mark question at 20 marks. Spend 20-25 minutes on it. Start by deciding your position: agree, disagree, or partially agree. Then build your argument with 3-4 PEEL paragraphs, each one analysing a different method the writer uses. Include a counter-argument or concession to show balanced thinking. End with a clear conclusion. Examiners reward confident, well-supported judgements - don't sit on the fence, but do show you've considered the text fully.

Time management: Q4 = 20-25 minutes for 20 marks. This is the most valuable question on the paper - protect your time for it.

What examiners look for: A clear evaluative judgement (not just description), detailed analysis of the writer's methods (language AND structure), precise embedded quotations, and a sustained argument that builds towards a conclusion. Top responses show perceptive, personal engagement with the text.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often retell the story instead of evaluating it. Wrong: "First the character goes to the house, then she looks through the window, then she goes inside." Correct: "The writer effectively builds tension by delaying the character's entry to the house, making the reader share her apprehension."

Students often give an opinion without analysing the writer's methods. Wrong: "I agree the writer creates fear because it is very scary." Correct: "I agree the writer creates fear. The use of short, fragmented sentences such as 'Footsteps. Closer. Now.' accelerates the pace, replicating the character's racing heartbeat."

Students often only discuss language, ignoring structure and form. Wrong: Only writing about similes, metaphors, and word choices. Correct: Also commenting on paragraph shifts, sentence variety, narrative perspective, and how the text's structure contributes to the overall effect.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

20 marks: "This extract effectively creates a sense of danger and fear." To what extent do you agree? In your response, you should consider the writer's methods and support your ideas with quotations from the text.

I strongly agree that the writer creates a powerful sense of danger and fear. The extract opens with the narrator walking "along the cliff path, where the edge crumbled into darkness," and this juxtaposition of the unstable "crumbled" edge with the unknown "darkness" below immediately establishes both physical peril and psychological dread. The writer's use of personification is particularly effective in sustaining this threat: the wind is described as "pushing" at the narrator's back, as though an unseen force is actively trying to send them over the edge. This transforms a natural phenomenon into something predatory and deliberate, making the reader feel the environment itself is hostile.

Structurally, the writer escalates fear through increasingly short sentences as the extract progresses. The early paragraphs are flowing and descriptive, but as the narrator moves further along the path, the sentences fragment: "The path narrowed. Stones fell. No railing now." This structural shift accelerates the reading pace, reproducing the narrator's quickening heartbeat and building visceral tension. The final paragraph's return to a longer, reflective sentence - "She stood at last on solid ground, the sea roaring its frustration far below" - provides a controlled release of tension, but the personification of the sea "roaring its frustration" suggests the danger has not been defeated, merely escaped, leaving the reader with lingering unease.

However, the writer also introduces moments of deceptive calm that arguably intensify the fear. The description of "the moonlight casting a silver path across the water" is beautiful, yet its placement between two passages of danger creates a false sense of security, making the subsequent return to threat more shocking. This structural ebb and flow means the reader never fully relaxes, which is perhaps the most sophisticated way the writer sustains fear throughout.

Mark scheme: Level 4 (17-20 marks) requires perceptive evaluation with detailed, exploratory analysis of methods. Must analyse language AND structure, use precise quotations, show balanced judgement, and demonstrate sophisticated understanding of how the text works on the reader.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO4 requires you to "evaluate texts critically and support this with appropriate textual references." This is the most demanding reading AO. At grade 5, students make a judgement and support it with some evidence, but analysis is general and methods are mentioned without exploration. At grade 9, students demonstrate perceptive evaluation: their judgements are nuanced (not simply "I agree"), their analysis explores how specific methods create precise effects, and they consider the text holistically, weighing different elements against each other. The leap from grade 5 to grade 9 is the move from "the writer is effective because they use good description" to "the writer's restraint is more powerful than explicit horror - by withholding the full revelation, they force the reader's imagination to supply the threat, making the fear deeply personal and therefore more unsettling."
