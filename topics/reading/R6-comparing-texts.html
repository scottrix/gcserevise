R6: Comparing Texts

Foundation Higher All Boards AO3

Comparing texts and identifying similarities and differences in perspectives and methods.

📋 What is Text Comparison?

Definition: Comparison involves identifying similarities and differences between texts, considering perspectives, methods, and how writers convey their viewpoints.

Comparison involves:

  • Identifying each writer's perspective or viewpoint
  • Comparing how these perspectives are similar or different
  • Analysing the methods each writer uses
  • Using evidence from both texts
Key Focus:
Compare PERSPECTIVES + Compare METHODS

📝 What to Compare

1. Perspectives and Viewpoints

  • What is each writer's stance on the topic?
  • Are their opinions similar or different?
  • How strongly does each writer feel?
  • Is there bias or objectivity?
Example

Source A: Argues that social media has a positive impact on young people's communication.

Source B: Claims social media isolates young people and harms real-world relationships.

These perspectives directly oppose each other.

2. Methods

  • What language devices does each writer use?
  • How do they structure their arguments?
  • What tone does each writer adopt?
  • How do they appeal to the reader?
Comparing Methods

While Source A uses statistics and expert opinion to build credibility, Source B relies on emotive language and personal anecdote to create sympathy.

3. Tone and Style

Tone Effect
Formal Authoritative, serious, credible
Informal Relatable, conversational, accessible
Emotive Passionate, persuasive, moving
Objective Balanced, factual, neutral

🔍 How to Structure a Comparison

Method 1: Integrated Approach

Write about both texts in each paragraph, using comparative connectives to link them.
Example Integrated Paragraph

Both writers express strong opinions about climate change, but their perspectives differ significantly. Source A presents a scientific perspective, arguing that "immediate action is essential," while Source B adopts a more sceptical stance, suggesting that "the evidence remains inconclusive." While Source A uses authoritative language like "undeniable" and "urgent," Source B employs rhetorical questions like "Can we really be certain?" to create doubt.

Method 2: Point-by-Point

  • Make a point about Text A
  • Compare with Text B
  • Use comparative language
  • Repeat for each comparison point

Comparative Connectives

Similarity Difference
Similarly However
Likewise In contrast
Both Whereas
Equally On the other hand
In the same way Unlike

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Correction
Writing about texts separately Use integrated approach - compare throughout
Describing content without comparing Focus on similarities and differences
Only comparing content, not methods Compare both perspectives AND methods
Using only one text for evidence Quote from BOTH texts equally
Not using comparative language Use connectives: similarly, however, whereas

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AQA Paper 2 Question 4 (16 marks)

  • Level 4 (13-16 marks): Perceptive comparison with detailed analysis
  • Level 3 (9-12 marks): Clear comparison with clear analysis
  • Level 2 (6-8 marks): Some comparison with some comment
  • Level 1 (3-5 marks): Simple comparison with limited support
Examiner's Tip: You must compare BOTH perspectives AND methods. Use evidence from both texts equally. Spend about 20-25 minutes on this question.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What are the two main things you should compare between texts?

Q2: What is the difference between an integrated approach and writing about texts separately?

Q3: Name three comparative connectives for showing differences.

Q4: Why should you use evidence from both texts?

✅ Answers

  1. Perspectives (viewpoints) and methods (how they convey those viewpoints).
  2. Integrated approach compares within each paragraph using connectives; separate approach writes about each text individually without comparing.
  3. However, in contrast, whereas, on the other hand, unlike.
  4. To show you can analyse both texts and make valid comparisons - it proves your comparison is based on evidence.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Read both texts and identify each writer's viewpoint
  • Use comparative connectives throughout
  • Write about both texts in each paragraph
  • Compare methods as well as perspectives
  • Quote from both texts equally
  • Look for similarities AND differences
  • Don't analyse language in depth - focus on comparison

📝 Exam Technique

Comparison Exam Strategy: This is AQA Paper 2 Q4 (16 marks) - spend 20-25 minutes. You must compare BOTH perspectives AND methods. Use an integrated approach: write about both texts in every paragraph, using comparative connectives (however, similarly, whereas, in contrast). Read both texts first and annotate them side by side. Identify each writer's viewpoint, then find 2-3 points of comparison. Examiners reward responses that weave comparison throughout, not those that write about each text separately.

Time management: Q4 = 20-25 minutes for 16 marks. This is a high-value question - protect time for it and aim for 3-4 integrated comparison paragraphs.

What examiners look for: Sustained comparison throughout (not separate sections), analysis of both perspectives AND methods, balanced use of evidence from both texts, and comparative connectives used consistently. Top responses show perceptive insights about why the writers differ.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often write about each text in separate paragraphs, treating them as two mini-essays. Wrong: A paragraph about Source A, then a paragraph about Source B with no links between them. Correct: "While Source A presents city life as vibrant and exciting through the metaphor of 'a symphony of noise,' Source B rejects this romanticism, describing the city as 'a concrete cage' - the contrast in metaphors reveals fundamentally opposed perspectives."

Students often compare content only, ignoring the writers' methods. Wrong: "Source A says the city is good. Source B says the city is bad." Correct: "Source A persuades through sensory celebration, while Source B uses emotive language of imprisonment to condemn urban life."

Students often use evidence from only one text. Wrong: Quoting extensively from Source A but barely referencing Source B. Correct: Balancing quotations from both texts within each comparison point.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

16 marks: Compare how the two writers convey their different perspectives about living in the city. In your response, you should compare their different perspectives and their methods.

Both writers present contrasting perspectives on city life, and they employ markedly different methods to convey their views. Source A celebrates urban living, presenting the city as a place of energy and possibility. The writer's perspective is one of enthusiasm, conveyed through a semantic field of vitality: words like "pulsing," "alive," and "buzzing" create an image of the city as a living organism that invigorates its inhabitants. In contrast, Source B presents a deeply negative perspective, depicting the city as oppressive and dehumanising. Where Source A uses organic imagery, Source B employs a semantic field of confinement: "trapped," "caged," and "crushed" suggest the city strips away individual freedom.

The writers also differ in their structural approaches. Source A opens with a panoramic view of the city from above, establishing a sense of awe and wonder before zooming into human detail - a structure that mirrors the writer's argument that the city offers both grandeur and intimacy. Source B, however, begins at street level, "wedged between tower blocks," trapping the reader in a claustrophobic perspective from the outset. This structural contrast reveals the fundamental difference in their worldviews: Source A sees the city from a position of freedom, Source B from one of entrapment.

Their methods of persuasion also diverge. Source A appeals to the reader's sense of aspiration through direct address: "You can be anyone here." This inclusive pronoun invites the reader to share the optimistic vision. Source B, however, uses rhetorical questions - "Is this really living?" - to challenge the reader's assumptions and provoke doubt, adopting a more confrontational tone that refuses to let the reader remain comfortable.

Mark scheme: Level 4 (13-16 marks) requires perceptive comparison of both perspectives and methods. Must use integrated approach, quote from both texts, analyse language and structural methods, and use comparative connectives throughout.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO3 requires you to "compare writers' ideas and perspectives, as well as how these are conveyed." This means you must address both content (what they think) and method (how they convey it). At grade 5, students make some comparisons but tend to write about texts separately, or compare content without considering methods. At grade 9, students integrate comparison seamlessly, moving fluently between texts within each paragraph. They perceive why perspectives differ (perhaps due to context, audience, or purpose) and explore how methods reveal attitude. The leap from grade 5 to 9 is moving from "Source A thinks X and Source B thinks Y" to "the writers' diametrically opposed perspectives are shaped by their different relationships with the city - Source A views it as an outsider seduced by its spectacle, while Source B writes from the exhausted experience of a resident."
