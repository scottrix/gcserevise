RS32: Gender Equality and Prejudice

All Boards

The roles of men and women; gender equality; gender prejudice and discrimination including examples

⚖️ Gender Equality and Prejudice

Key Fact: Both Christianity and Islam teach that men and women are equal in the sight of God, but both traditions have historically assigned different roles to men and women. This tension is a key GCSE RS topic.

✝️ Christian Teachings

  • "There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus" (Galatians 3:28) — all equal before God
  • Traditional: Men and women have different roles — men lead the church and family; women raise children and support husbands. The Catholic Church only ordains men as priests.
  • Egalitarian: Men and women should have equal roles in church and society. Many Protestant denominations now ordain women as ministers and bishops (e.g. Church of England since 1994).
  • Christianity has been at the forefront of campaigns for gender equality — the suffragette movement, equal pay, anti-discrimination laws

☪️ Muslim Teachings

  • "Whoever does righteous deeds, whether male or female, and is a believer — they will enter Paradise" (Surah 4:124) — spiritual equality
  • Islam gave women property rights, divorce rights and inheritance rights 1,400 years ago — revolutionary at the time
  • However, many Muslim-majority societies restrict women's roles today
  • Men and women have equal spiritual status but different social roles in traditional Islam
  • Some Muslims argue for full gender equality; others believe different roles are divinely ordained
  • The hijab (head covering) is seen by some as oppressive and by others as liberating — a woman's choice to dress modestly

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain contrasting religious views on gender equality.

✅ Answers

  1. Traditional Christians and Muslims believe men and women have equal spiritual worth but different social roles — men lead the church/family while women nurture children. The Catholic Church only ordains men, and traditional Islam assigns different family roles. However, egalitarian Christians (e.g. Church of England) now ordain women as bishops, and many Muslims argue that Islam's original teachings on women's rights were progressive but have been distorted by culture. The debate centres on whether different roles are divinely ordained or culturally conditioned.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Theme A questions: You must explain contrasting beliefs using Christianity AND Islam (or another religion). Consider non-religious views (humanist, atheist). Address the three required contrasts: contraception, premarital sex, same-sex relationships. Always refer to specific teachings and sources of authority.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often oversimplify religious views on relationships. Wrong: "All Christians oppose same-sex relationships." Correct: "While the Catholic Church teaches homosexual acts are wrong, many Protestant denominations (e.g. Quakers, United Reformed Church) accept and bless same-sex relationships. Views vary within and between traditions."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that the roles of men and women; gender equality; gender prejudice and discrimination including examples.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
