RS43: Good, Evil and the Causes of Crime

All Boards

Good and evil intentions and actions; reasons for crime: poverty, mental health, greed, opposition to unjust law

⚖️ Good, Evil and the Causes of Crime

Key Fact: GCSE RS requires you to understand why people commit crimes and how religion explains good and evil. Both Christianity and Islam teach that humans have free will and are responsible for their choices.

✝️ Christian Teachings

  • God created humans with free will — people choose to do good or evil
  • Original sin (the Fall in Genesis 3) means all humans have a tendency to sin
  • Causes of crime: poverty, greed, addiction, mental health, peer pressure, lack of moral education
  • Christians should hate the sin but love the sinner — people who commit crimes still have human dignity
  • The Parable of the Sheep and Goats (Matthew 25) teaches that how we treat the vulnerable matters
  • Some crime is caused by injustice — Christians should work to remove the conditions that lead to crime

☪️ Muslim Teachings

  • Allah gave humans free will — people choose whether to follow Allah's guidance or not
  • Iblis (Satan) tempts humans to do evil — but humans are still responsible for their choices
  • Islam teaches that society should be just — poverty and inequality that drive people to crime must be addressed
  • Zakah and sadaqah exist to reduce poverty, a major cause of crime
  • The Qur'an teaches: "Whoever does righteous deeds — it is for the benefit of his own soul; and whoever does evil — it is to its detriment" (Surah 45:15)
  • Crime is a failure to follow Sharia (Islamic law) and Allah's guidance

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two religious teachings about the causes of crime.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Christianity teaches that crime results from free will and original sin — humans choose to do evil rather than good. However, Christians also recognise social causes of crime such as poverty, inequality and lack of opportunity. The Parable of the Sheep and Goats teaches that society has a duty to care for the vulnerable, which could reduce crime. 2) Islam teaches that crime results from choosing not to follow Allah's guidance. However, Islam also addresses social causes — zakah and sadaqah exist to reduce poverty, a major driver of crime. A just society that follows Sharia would minimise the conditions that lead to crime.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Theme E questions: You must explain contrasting beliefs using Christianity AND Islam. Address the three required contrasts: corporal punishment, death penalty, forgiveness. Know the three aims of punishment (retribution, deterrence, reformation). Consider ethical arguments from both utilitarian and sanctity of life perspectives.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse retribution with revenge. Wrong: "Retribution means getting revenge on criminals." Correct: "Retribution is punishment that is proportionate to the crime, based on justice and the principle that wrongdoers deserve punishment. Revenge is personal and emotional, often disproportionate. Retribution is a principle of justice; revenge is not."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that good and evil intentions and actions; reasons for crime: poverty, mental health, greed, opposition to unjust law.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
