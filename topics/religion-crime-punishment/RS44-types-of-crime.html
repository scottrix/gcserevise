RS44: Types of Crime

All Boards

Hate crimes, theft and murder; views about people who break the law for different reasons

🔍 Types of Crime

Key Fact: GCSE RS focuses on three types of crime: hate crimes, theft and murder. You need to understand religious responses to each type and whether people who break the law for moral reasons should be punished.

📋 Key Types of Crime

TypeDescriptionReligious Response
Hate crimesCrimes motivated by prejudice against race, religion, sexuality or disabilityBoth Christianity and Islam condemn prejudice — all humans are equal before God. Hate crimes violate the command to love your neighbour.
TheftTaking property that belongs to someone elseBoth traditions forbid theft (7th/8th Commandment; Qur'an). However, stealing from necessity (e.g. to feed a starving family) raises moral questions.
MurderThe unlawful killing of another personBoth Christianity and Islam consider murder a grave sin. "You shall not murder" (Exodus 20:13); "Whoever kills a soul... it is as if he had slain all mankind" (Surah 5:32).

🤔 Breaking the Law for Moral Reasons

  • Should someone who breaks the law for a good reason be punished?
  • Examples: hiding Jews during the Holocaust (illegal but morally right); civil disobedience for racial justice (Martin Luther King)
  • Christians may follow conscience over law — "We must obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29)
  • Some laws are unjust and should be challenged — but through peaceful means where possible
  • Muslims are required to follow just laws but may resist oppressive rulers who contradict Sharia

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two religious responses to hate crime.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Christianity teaches that all humans are made in God's image (Genesis 1:27) and are equal before God — "There is neither Jew nor Gentile... for you are all one in Christ Jesus" (Galatians 3:28). Hate crimes contradict Jesus' command to "love your neighbour" and are always wrong. 2) Islam teaches that all humans are equal before Allah — "We created you from a single pair" (Surah 4:1). Racism and prejudice are forbidden, and the Qur'an celebrates human diversity as a sign of Allah's creativity. Hate crimes violate Islamic teaching on the dignity of every person.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Theme E questions: You must explain contrasting beliefs using Christianity AND Islam. Address the three required contrasts: corporal punishment, death penalty, forgiveness. Know the three aims of punishment (retribution, deterrence, reformation). Consider ethical arguments from both utilitarian and sanctity of life perspectives.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse retribution with revenge. Wrong: "Retribution means getting revenge on criminals." Correct: "Retribution is punishment that is proportionate to the crime, based on justice and the principle that wrongdoers deserve punishment. Revenge is personal and emotional, often disproportionate. Retribution is a principle of justice; revenge is not."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that hate crimes, theft and murder; views about people who break the law for different reasons.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
