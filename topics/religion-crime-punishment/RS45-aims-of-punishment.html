RS45: Aims of Punishment

All Boards

Retribution, deterrence and reformation; religious views on the purpose of punishment

⚖️ Aims of Punishment

Key Fact: There are three main aims of punishment: retribution (punishment because the offender deserves it), deterrence (putting people off committing crimes) and reformation/rehabilitation (helping the offender change their behaviour).

📋 The Three Aims Explained

AimMeaningExampleReligious View
RetributionPunishment proportionate to the crime; the offender deserves itA longer sentence for a more serious crimeBoth traditions accept retribution as justice — but it must be proportionate, not revenge
DeterrencePunishment that discourages the offender and others from reoffendingSevere penalties to warn othersAccepted if it protects society, but harsh punishment purely to frighten is controversial
ReformationPunishment that helps the offender change and rejoin societyEducation, training, therapy in prisonStrongly supported — both traditions believe people can repent and change

✝️ Christian Views

  • Jesus taught forgiveness and redemption — "I have not come to call the righteous, but sinners" (Mark 2:17)
  • Reformation should be the primary aim — Christians believe in the possibility of change through God's grace
  • The Parable of the Prodigal Son shows God welcomes back those who repent
  • Retribution is accepted but must be just — "An eye for an eye" was replaced by Jesus with "Turn the other cheek"
  • Some Christians support harsher punishment for serious crimes to protect society

☪️ Muslim Views

  • Islam accepts all three aims but balances them differently
  • Sharia law includes specific punishments (hudud) for serious crimes — these serve as both retribution and deterrence
  • However, reformation is also valued — the Qur'an teaches repentance and forgiveness
  • "Whoever forgives and makes reconciliation, his reward is with Allah" (Surah 42:40)
  • In practice, many Muslim-majority countries focus on rehabilitation for lesser crimes

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two aims of punishment and a religious view on each.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Retribution — punishment that is proportionate to the crime because the offender deserves it. Christianity accepts retribution as justice but warns against revenge; Jesus replaced "an eye for an eye" with "turn the other cheek." 2) Reformation — punishment that helps the offender change and rejoin society. Islam values reformation because the Qur'an teaches repentance and forgiveness — "Whoever forgives and makes reconciliation, his reward is with Allah" (Surah 42:40). Both traditions believe people can change through God's grace.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Theme E questions: You must explain contrasting beliefs using Christianity AND Islam. Address the three required contrasts: corporal punishment, death penalty, forgiveness. Know the three aims of punishment (retribution, deterrence, reformation). Consider ethical arguments from both utilitarian and sanctity of life perspectives.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse retribution with revenge. Wrong: "Retribution means getting revenge on criminals." Correct: "Retribution is punishment that is proportionate to the crime, based on justice and the principle that wrongdoers deserve punishment. Revenge is personal and emotional, often disproportionate. Retribution is a principle of justice; revenge is not."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that retribution, deterrence and reformation; religious views on the purpose of punishment.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
