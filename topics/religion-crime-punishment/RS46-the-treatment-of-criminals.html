RS46: The Treatment of Criminals

All Boards

Prison, corporal punishment and community service; religious perspectives on treatment of offenders

🔒 The Treatment of Criminals

Key Fact: GCSE RS requires you to evaluate different methods of treating criminals — prison, corporal punishment and community service — from Christian and Muslim perspectives.

📋 Methods of Treatment

MethodDescriptionChristian ViewMuslim View
PrisonRemoving freedom as punishmentAccepted as necessary for public safety; should include rehabilitation. Concerns about overcrowding and recidivism.Accepted but should focus on reform. Some Muslim countries use prison alongside Sharia punishments.
Corporal punishmentPhysical punishment (e.g. flogging)Generally rejected in Western Christianity as incompatible with Jesus' teaching on love and forgivenessSome Muslim countries practise corporal punishment under Sharia (e.g. for alcohol consumption). Controversial even within Islam.
Community serviceUnpaid work in the communityWidely supported — allows reform, gives back to society, and avoids the negatives of prisonGenerally supported as a form of restorative justice — the offender makes amends to the community

🔍 Key Religious Principles

  • Human dignity — even criminals are made in God's image; punishment must not degrade
  • Fairness — punishment must be proportionate and just
  • Reform — both traditions believe in the possibility of change
  • Protection — society must be protected from dangerous offenders
  • Christians generally favour rehabilitation over retribution; Islam balances both

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two religious views on the treatment of criminals.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Christianity generally supports prison for public safety but emphasises rehabilitation — Christians believe in the possibility of change through God's grace (Parable of the Prodigal Son). Corporal punishment is widely rejected as incompatible with Jesus' teaching on love. 2) Islam accepts prison but also includes Sharia punishments like corporal punishment for specific crimes. Community service is supported by both traditions as restorative justice. Both religions teach that criminals retain human dignity and punishment should aim at reform, not just suffering.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Theme E questions: You must explain contrasting beliefs using Christianity AND Islam. Address the three required contrasts: corporal punishment, death penalty, forgiveness. Know the three aims of punishment (retribution, deterrence, reformation). Consider ethical arguments from both utilitarian and sanctity of life perspectives.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse retribution with revenge. Wrong: "Retribution means getting revenge on criminals." Correct: "Retribution is punishment that is proportionate to the crime, based on justice and the principle that wrongdoers deserve punishment. Revenge is personal and emotional, often disproportionate. Retribution is a principle of justice; revenge is not."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that prison, corporal punishment and community service; religious perspectives on treatment of offenders.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
