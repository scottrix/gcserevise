RS37: Euthanasia and the Afterlife

All Boards

Beliefs about euthanasia; beliefs about death and an afterlife; their impact on the value of human life

⚕️ Euthanasia and the Afterlife

Key Fact: Euthanasia means "good death" — it is the deliberate ending of a person's life to end suffering. It is illegal in the UK. Religious views are shaped by beliefs about the sanctity of life and the afterlife.

📋 Types of Euthanasia

TypeDescription
VoluntaryThe person asks for their life to be ended
Non-voluntaryThe person cannot give consent (e.g. in a coma)
InvoluntaryThe person's life is ended against their will (murder)
ActiveA positive act to end life (e.g. lethal injection)
PassiveWithdrawing treatment that is keeping someone alive

✝️ Christian Teachings

  • Most Christians oppose euthanasia — life is sacred and God-given; only God should end it
  • "The Lord gives and the Lord takes away" (Job 1:21)
  • Suffering can have meaning — Jesus suffered on the cross; Christians are called to carry their cross
  • The Hospice Movement, founded by Dame Cicely Saunders (a Christian), provides palliative care as an alternative to euthanasia
  • Some Christians support voluntary euthanasia in extreme cases, emphasising compassion and quality of life
  • Belief in the afterlife means death is not the end — there is hope beyond suffering

☪️ Muslim Teachings

  • Islam forbids euthanasia — life is a gift from Allah and only Allah can take it
  • "Do not take life, which Allah has made sacred" (Surah 17:33)
  • Suffering is a test from Allah and can bring spiritual reward — patience (sabr) is a virtue
  • However, Muslims do not have to prolong suffering artificially — withholding futile treatment is allowed
  • Belief in akhirah means suffering in this life is temporary and will be rewarded in the afterlife

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two religious views on euthanasia.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Catholics oppose euthanasia because life is sacred and God-given — only God should end it. Job 1:21 states "The Lord gives and the Lord takes away." Suffering can have spiritual meaning, and the hospice movement offers palliative care as an alternative. 2) Islam forbids euthanasia because life belongs to Allah and only he can take it (Surah 17:33). Suffering is a test that can bring spiritual reward through patience (sabr). However, both traditions allow the withholding of futile treatment — there is no requirement to prolong suffering artificially.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Theme B questions: You must explain contrasting beliefs using Christianity AND Islam (or another religion). Address the three required contrasts: abortion, euthanasia, animal experimentation. Consider scientific perspectives (Big Bang, evolution) alongside religious views. Use the sanctity of life and quality of life as key evaluative concepts.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse sanctity of life with quality of life. Wrong: "Sanctity of life means life should have good quality." Correct: "Sanctity of life means all life is sacred because it is God-given and has intrinsic value. Quality of life refers to the standard of health, comfort and happiness. They are different principles that can lead to different ethical conclusions."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that beliefs about euthanasia; beliefs about death and an afterlife; their impact on the value of human life.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
