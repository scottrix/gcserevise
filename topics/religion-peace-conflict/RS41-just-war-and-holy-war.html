RS41: Just War and Holy War

All Boards

The just war theory and its criteria; holy war; contrasting Christian and Muslim perspectives

⚔️ Just War and Holy War

Key Fact: A just war is a war that meets specific moral criteria. A holy war is fought for religious reasons or on God's command. They are different concepts — a war can be one without being the other.

📋 Just War Theory (Christian)

Developed by St Augustine and St Thomas Aquinas, the Just War criteria are:

  • Legitimate authority — declared by a proper government, not individuals
  • Just cause — to defend against attack, protect the innocent, or restore rights
  • Right intention — to achieve peace and justice, not revenge or gain
  • Last resort — all peaceful alternatives exhausted
  • Proportionality — the good achieved must outweigh the harm
  • Reasonable chance of success — fighting a hopeless war is wasteful of life

Conduct during war must also be just — no targeting civilians, no torture, humane treatment of prisoners.

☪️ Muslim Teachings on War

  • Islam has similar criteria to Just War theory — war must be in self-defence, declared by legitimate authority, as a last resort, and proportionate
  • The concept of jihad includes the possibility of armed struggle under strict conditions
  • Some historical Muslim wars have been called "holy wars" — but the Qur'an does not use this term
  • Forced conversion is explicitly forbidden: "There is no compulsion in religion" (Surah 2:256)
  • Rules of war: no harming civilians, no destroying crops or places of worship, fair treatment of prisoners

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the Just War theory and how it compares to Islamic teachings on war.

✅ Answers

  1. Just War theory (developed by Augustine and Aquinas) states war is only justified if it meets six criteria: legitimate authority, just cause, right intention, last resort, proportionality and reasonable chance of success. Islamic teachings are remarkably similar — war must be in self-defence, declared by legitimate authority, proportionate and a last resort. Both traditions forbid targeting civilians and require humane treatment of prisoners. The key difference is that Islam explicitly frames war within the concept of jihad (striving for Allah), while Just War theory is based on natural law and reason rather than divine command.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

📝 Exam Technique

RS Exam Tips:
For Theme D questions: You must explain contrasting beliefs using Christianity AND Islam. Address the three required contrasts: violence, weapons of mass destruction, pacifism. Know the just war theory criteria in detail. Explain both greater and lesser jihad. Consider the role of religious peace-making in the contemporary world.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse just war with holy war. Wrong: "Just war and holy war mean the same thing." Correct: "A just war must meet criteria including legitimate authority, just cause, last resort and proportionality. A holy war is fought for religious reasons or on God's command. They are distinct concepts — a war could be one without being the other."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

12 marks: Evaluate the view that the just war theory and its criteria; holy war; contrasting christian and muslim perspectives.

A strong 12-mark answer will: (1) Present a religious viewpoint with specific teaching/scripture; (2) Present a contrasting religious or non-religious viewpoint; (3) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (4) Reach a justified conclusion. For top marks, show awareness of diversity within traditions, use precise terminology, and demonstrate independent critical thinking rather than just rehearsing arguments.

Mark scheme: AO1 (knowledge and understanding) = 6 marks; AO2 (analysis and evaluation) = 6 marks

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE Religious Studies tests two AOs equally: AO1 (Knowledge and Understanding, 50%) — demonstrate knowledge of beliefs, teachings and practices; use specialist vocabulary; refer to sources of wisdom and authority. AO2 (Analysis and Evaluation, 50%) — analyse and evaluate aspects of religion and belief; consider contrasting views; construct well-reasoned and balanced arguments; reach justified conclusions. For grade 9, demonstrate nuanced understanding of diversity within traditions, use precise scriptural references, and show independent critical evaluation. The key difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of evaluation — not just describing views but critically analysing their strengths and weaknesses.
