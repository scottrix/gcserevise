RS42: Nuclear Weapons and Pacifism

All Boards

Nuclear weapons and deterrence; weapons of mass destruction; pacifism; religious peace-making

☢️ Nuclear Weapons and Pacifism

Key Fact: Nuclear weapons can destroy entire cities and kill millions. Their existence raises serious moral questions for religious believers about whether they can ever be justified.

✝️ Christian Views

  • Pacifists (e.g. Quakers): All weapons of mass destruction are wrong — nuclear weapons can never be used justly because they are inherently disproportionate and kill civilians
  • Deterrence: Some Christians support possessing nuclear weapons as a deterrent — the threat prevents war. However, actually using them would almost certainly violate Just War criteria
  • Nuclear disarmament: Many Christian groups campaign for nuclear disarmament, arguing that trust in weapons of mass destruction contradicts trust in God
  • Pope Francis has called for the total elimination of nuclear weapons, rejecting even deterrence

☪️ Muslim Views

  • Islam forbids weapons that kill indiscriminately — nuclear weapons harm non-combatants and destroy the environment
  • Most Muslim scholars argue nuclear weapons violate the proportionality rule of lesser jihad
  • Some Muslim-majority countries (Pakistan) possess nuclear weapons as a deterrent
  • The Islamic principle of avoiding harm (la darar wa la dirar) would prohibit using nuclear weapons
  • Muslims generally support nuclear disarmament as consistent with Islamic peace teachings

☮️ Pacifism

  • Pacifism rejects ALL violence and war — peace must be achieved through non-violent means
  • Christian pacifists point to Jesus' teaching: "Love your enemies" (Matthew 5:44)
  • Quakers, Mennonites and some other denominations are traditionally pacifist
  • Muslim pacifists are rarer but some Sufi traditions emphasise inner peace and non-violence
  • Critics argue pacifism allows evil to triumph when aggressors are not stopped

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two religious views on nuclear weapons.

✅ Answers

  1. 1) Christian pacifists (e.g. Quakers) reject nuclear weapons entirely — they can never be used justly because they are inherently disproportionate and kill civilians indiscriminately, violating Just War criteria. 2) Most Muslim scholars argue nuclear weapons violate the proportionality rule of lesser jihad and the principle of avoiding indiscriminate harm. Using them would kill non-combatants and destroy the environment, which Islam forbids. However, some argue that possessing them as a deterrent may be acceptable, similar to the Christian deterrence argument.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always refer to specific sources of authority (scripture, sacred texts, religious leaders)
  • Explain contrasting beliefs — different views within and between traditions
  • Use key terminology precisely (e.g. tawhid, atonement, sanctity of life)
  • For 12-mark evaluation questions, consider both sides before reaching a justified conclusion
  • Link beliefs to their impact on individuals, communities and society
  • Acknowledge diversity — no religious tradition has a single, uniform view

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse just war with holy war. Wrong: "Just war and holy war mean the same thing." Correct: "A just war must meet criteria including legitimate authority, just cause, last resort and proportionality. A holy war is fought for religious reasons or on God's command. They are distinct concepts — a war could be one without being the other."

📖 Detailed Notes:

💡 Christian and Muslim Perspectives on

For GCSE Religious Studies, understanding requires examining both Christian and Muslim perspectives, as well as non-religious viewpoints. Christianity teaches that all issues of moral concern should be guided by the principles of love (agape), justice and the sanctity of life. Key biblical teachings include the Great Commandment to love God and love your neighbour (Mark 12:30–31) and the Golden Rule: “Do to others as you would have them do to you” (Luke 6:31). Islam approaches moral issues through the guidance of the Qur’an and the Sunnah (the Prophet’s example), seeking to apply the principles of tawhid (the oneness of God), adl (justice) and rahma (mercy) to contemporary questions. The Qur’an states: “Allah commands justice, the doing of good and liberality to relatives, and He forbids all shameful deeds, injustice and rebellion” (Surah 16:90). Both traditions require believers to consider not only their own interests but the welfare of the wider community and the principles of their faith.

In the UK, raises important questions about the relationship between religious belief, law and individual freedom. Britain is a multicultural, multi-faith society where people of different religions and none must coexist and find common ground on ethical issues. The Equality Act 2010 protects freedom of religion while also prohibiting discrimination. For GCSE, the key skill is to present contrasting views fairly, using specific religious teachings and sources of authority, and to reach a justified conclusion. This means acknowledging the diversity within each tradition — not all Christians or Muslims agree on every issue — and considering non-religious perspectives such as humanism and secularism.

🔍 Diversity of Viewpoints Within Religious Traditions

Both Christianity and Islam contain a diversity of viewpoints on . Within Christianity, Catholics, Anglicans and Protestants may hold different positions based on their interpretation of the Bible and the weight they give to tradition, reason and experience. Within Islam, Sunni and Shi’a scholars may reach different conclusions based on their respective legal traditions and the guidance of their religious authorities. It is a common mistake to assume that all Christians or all Muslims hold the same view on every issue — in reality, both traditions contain liberals, conservatives and moderates who interpret scripture and tradition differently. For GCSE, the mark scheme specifically rewards students who show awareness of this diversity and who present contrasting views within as well as between traditions.

The relationship between religious belief and contemporary British values is also important. Britain is a liberal democracy that values individual freedom, equality and the rule of law. Some religious teachings on may appear to conflict with these values, creating tension that both religious and secular people must navigate. However, many religious believers argue that their faith’s core values (justice, compassion, human dignity) are fully compatible with British values, and that apparent conflicts are the result of misunderstanding or misinterpretation rather than genuine incompatibility. The challenge for GCSE students is to engage with this complexity thoughtfully and honestly.

⚖️ Applying Religious Teachings to

When applying religious teachings to , Christians may refer to specific biblical passages, the teachings of Jesus, the guidance of church leaders and the principles of Christian ethics such as the sanctity of life, love of neighbour and stewardship. Muslims may refer to specific Qur’anic verses, the Hadith (sayings of the Prophet), the guidance of scholars (ulama) and the principles of Islamic ethics such as tawhid, justice, compassion and the public interest (maslaha). Both traditions use a process of reasoning to apply ancient teachings to modern situations — this is called ‘situation ethics’ in Christianity and ‘ijtihad’ (independent reasoning) in Islam. The challenge is to remain faithful to core principles while responding sensitively to new circumstances.

For GCSE exam success, students should aim to: (1) Present specific religious teachings with accurate references to scripture and authority; (2) Show awareness of diversity within and between traditions; (3) Consider non-religious perspectives (humanist, atheist, secular); (4) Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each position; (5) Reach a justified conclusion that demonstrates independent critical thinking. The best answers do not simply describe what Christians and Muslims believe — they evaluate those beliefs, considering whether they are coherent, relevant and applicable to the real-world situations that involves. For AO2 (analysis and evaluation), the key is critical engagement, not just description.

UK Context: British examiners specifically look for the use of specific scriptural references (e.g. Surah numbers, Bible chapter and verse) and named scholars or traditions. Vague references to ‘what Christians believe’ or ‘what Muslims think’ without specific evidence will not achieve the highest marks.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain two contrasting religious views on an issue related to .

Q: Evaluate whether religious teachings are still relevant to debates about in modern Britain.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. Christianity and Islam often share similar core principles (sanctity of life, justice, compassion) but may apply them differently to . Within Christianity, Catholics tend to take absolutist positions based on natural law and the Catechism, while Anglicans and Protestants may take more situational or consequentialist approaches. Within Islam, the majority scholarly opinion often provides clear guidance, but progressive and conservative scholars may disagree on how to apply traditional teachings to modern British contexts. The key contrast for GCSE is between traditions that prioritise fixed moral rules and those that emphasise the specific circumstances of each case.
  2. On one hand, religious teachings provide a moral framework based on centuries of reflection and wisdom, offering perspectives that prioritise human dignity, justice and the common good over individual self-interest. Many British people — both religious and non-religious — draw on religious values when forming ethical opinions. On the other hand, Britain is increasingly secular, and some argue that religious teachings are outdated or biased by the historical contexts in which they were formed. A balanced view is that religious perspectives deserve a place in public debate alongside secular viewpoints, but that no single religious tradition should dictate law or policy in a pluralistic society. The principle of religious freedom means that believers can follow their conscience, while the principle of equality means that the law should treat all citizens fairly regardless of their faith.
