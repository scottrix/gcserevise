Roman Government & Politics

Understanding the structure of Roman government, from the Republic's system of consuls, Senate, and popular assemblies, to the Empire's concentration of power in the emperor.

Key Fact: The Roman Republic (509-27 BC) was governed by elected magistrates, the Senate, and popular assemblies, with a system of checks and balances.
Key Fact: Two consuls were elected annually: they commanded the army, presided over the Senate, and could veto each other. In emergencies, a dictator could be appointed for six months.
Key Fact: The Senate was the most powerful body in the Republic: 300 (later 600) former magistrates who advised consuls, controlled foreign policy, and managed state finances.
Key Fact: Tribunes of the Plebs were elected to protect plebeian rights: they could veto Senate decisions (intercessio) and propose laws. Their person was sacrosanct (inviolable).
Key Fact: The Republic's constitution balanced three elements: the monarchical (consuls), the aristocratic (Senate), and the democratic (assemblies), as described by Polybius.
Key Fact: The fall of the Republic: the Gracchi, Marius, Sulla, Pompey, and Caesar all challenged the traditional system, leading to civil wars and Caesar's dictatorship.
Key Fact: Augustus established the principate (27 BC): he held the titles princeps (first citizen) and Augustus, concentrating power while maintaining Republican forms.
Key Fact: Under the Empire, the Senate still met but had little real power. The emperor controlled the army, appointed officials, and made law through edicts.
Key Fact: The cursus honorum (course of offices) was the sequence of political offices: quaestor, aedile, praetor, consul. Age requirements and intervals between offices were established by law.
Key Fact: Roman citizenship was highly valued: it provided legal rights (provocatio — appeal against magistrate's coercion) and the right to vote. It was gradually extended to all Italians and eventually to all free inhabitants of the Empire.

✅ Answers

  1. Two consuls were elected annually. They commanded the army, presided over the Senate, proposed laws, and served as supreme judges. Each could veto the other's decisions, preventing any one person from gaining too much power.
  2. The Senate was the most powerful political body: 300 former magistrates who advised consuls, controlled foreign policy and state finances, assigned provinces to magistrates, and debated all major issues. Their authority (auctoritas) was immense though technically only advisory.
  3. Tribunes could veto (intercessio) any magistrate's decision or Senate decree, propose laws to the plebeian assembly, and convene the plebs. Their person was sacrosanct — harming them was a capital offence.
  4. Augustus kept Republican forms (Senate, consuls, tribunes) but concentrated real power: he held consulships, tribunician power, proconsular imperium over provinces with armies, and the title princeps and Augustus. He controlled the army and succession.
  5. The sequential course of political offices: quaestor (financial officer, age 30+), aedile (public works and games), praetor (judicial and military), consul (supreme authority, age 42+). A two-year interval between offices was required.
  6. Senatus Populusque Romanus — the Senate and People of Rome. It represented the partnership between the Senate and the popular assemblies in government, and appeared on public buildings, coins, and standards.
  7. Multiple factors: the Gracchi's reforms exposed social tensions; military commanders (Marius, Sulla) used armies for personal power; the Senate became corrupt and self-interested; and civil wars between ambitious generals (Pompey vs Caesar) destroyed the constitutional balance.
  8. Legal rights including provocatio (appeal against a magistrate's coercion), the right to vote in assemblies, the right to stand for office, the right to make legal contracts, and protection from summary execution.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always distinguish between the Republic and the Empire — their systems of government were fundamentally different.
  • Use the Latin terms: consul, senatus, tribunus, intercessio, princeps, auctoritas — they show precise understanding.
  • For the Republic, emphasise the system of checks and balances: no one branch could dominate.
  • For Augustus, explain how he maintained Republican forms while concentrating power — this is the key to understanding the principate.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ The Roman Republic was a democracy like modern ones. ✓ The Republic had democratic elements (popular assemblies) but was dominated by the aristocratic Senate. Wealth and social class determined political power, and most offices were restricted to the elite.

✗ The Senate made laws in the Roman Republic. ✓ The Senate's formal role was advisory (its decrees were called senatus consulta — advice). Laws were passed by the popular assemblies. However, the Senate's auctoritas (authority) meant its advice was almost always followed.

✗ Augustus abolished the Republic entirely. ✓ Augustus maintained all Republican institutions (Senate, consuls, tribunes, assemblies) but controlled them through his personal power. The transition was subtle: power shifted from institutions to one man, but the forms remained.

✗ All Romans could vote equally in the assemblies. ✓ Voting was organised by wealth and tribe. The comitia centuriata gave the wealthiest citizens disproportionate voting power, and the system was designed to favour the elite rather than provide equal representation.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Compare the government of the Roman Republic with the government of the Roman Empire under Augustus. (8 marks)

The Roman Republic and the Augustan Empire had fundamentally different distributions of power despite sharing many institutional forms. In the Republic, power was distributed across multiple bodies: two annually elected consuls held executive authority but could veto each other; the Senate of 300 former magistrates controlled foreign policy, finances, and provincial assignments; and the popular assemblies elected magistrates and passed laws. Tribunes of the plebs protected common citizens through their veto power (intercessio). This system of checks and balances prevented any one individual from dominating, as Polybius observed. Under Augustus, by contrast, all meaningful power was concentrated in one person. Augustus held consulships annually, was granted tribunician power (tribunicia potestas) for life, and held proconsular imperium over provinces containing the majority of Rome's legions. The Senate still met and was consulted, but Augustus controlled who could become a senator, directed foreign policy, and commanded the army. The popular assemblies continued to exist but their electoral role was largely ceremonial. The fundamental difference was the location of sovereignty: in the Republic, power derived from the offices and institutions; under the Empire, all power ultimately derived from the emperor. However, Augustus was careful to preserve Republican forms: he used the title princeps (first citizen) rather than rex (king), respected the Senate's dignity, and avoided overt displays of monarchical power. This combination of personal supremacy with Republican appearance was the essence of the principate — a system that was monarchy in practice but not in name.
