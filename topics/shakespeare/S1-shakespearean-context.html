S1: Shakespearean Context

Foundation Higher All Boards AO3

Understanding Shakespeare's theatre, era and conventions - the world that shaped his plays.

🏛️ Elizabethan and Jacobean England

Two Monarchs, Two Eras

Shakespeare wrote under two monarchs:

Monarch Reign Key Features
Elizabeth I 1558-1603 Elizabethan era - stability, Renaissance, national pride
James I 1603-1625 Jacobean era - union of crowns, witchcraft obsession, political tension
Key Context: "Macbeth" was written in 1606, early in James I's reign. The play reflects James's interests: witchcraft, kingship, and the dangers of treason.

The Elizabethan Worldview

Elizabethans believed in an ordered universe:

  • Great Chain of Being - strict hierarchy from God to angels to humans to animals. Kings were divinely appointed.
  • Divine Right of Kings - monarchs answered only to God. Killing a king was a sin against God.
  • Order vs Chaos - when natural order was disrupted (regicide), chaos followed (storms, unnatural events).
In "Macbeth"

When Duncan is murdered, nature rebels: horses eat each other, owls kill falcons, earthquakes shake the earth. This reflects the Elizabethan belief that killing a king disrupts the entire natural order.

🎭 The Globe Theatre

The Playhouse

Shakespeare's plays were performed at the Globe Theatre (built 1599):

  • Open-air - daylight performances, no electric lighting
  • Thrust stage - audience on three sides, creating intimacy
  • No scenery - setting created through language
  • Minimal props - imagination filled gaps
  • Live music - sound effects and atmosphere

The Audience

Section Who Price
Groundlings (the Yard) Common people, standing 1 penny
The Galleries Middle class, seated 2-3 pennies
Lords' Rooms Nobility, best view Expensive
Theatre was for everyone: Shakespeare wrote for all classes - high drama for nobility, jokes and action for groundlings. Plays needed to engage diverse audiences.

Theatrical Conventions

  • Male actors only - women didn't perform; boys played female roles
  • Colourful costumes - indicated character type and status
  • No artificial lighting - most plays performed in afternoon
  • Minimal sets - language created time, place, atmosphere

👑 Kingship and Politics

The Divine Right of Kings

James I strongly believed in divine right:

  • Kings appointed by God - to question them was to question God
  • Treason was the worst crime - punished by horrific execution
  • Stability depended on obedience to the monarch
"Macbeth" and Kingship

Shakespeare shows the consequences of regicide: Scotland descends into chaos, Macbeth becomes a tyrant, and ultimately divine order is restored when Malcolm (the rightful heir) takes the throne. This reinforces James's belief in divine right.

The Gunpowder Plot (1605)

Just one year before "Macbeth" was written:

  • Catholic conspirators attempted to blow up Parliament and kill James I
  • Guy Fawkes discovered with gunpowder
  • Traitors executed brutally - hung, drawn, quartered
  • National trauma and anti-Catholic sentiment
Relevance: "Macbeth" explores treason, regicide, and the betrayal of a king by a trusted subject. The Gunpowder Plot was fresh in the audience's minds.

🧙 Witches and the Supernatural

Belief in Witchcraft

Jacobean England genuinely feared witches:

  • Witches blamed for crop failures, illness, bad luck
  • Witches seen as agents of the Devil
  • James I wrote "Daemonologie" (1597) - a guide to identifying witches
  • Thousands executed for witchcraft
The Witches in "Macbeth"

Shakespeare's witches would have terrified his audience. They: are clearly evil and supernatural, speak in prophetic riddles, manipulate Macbeth, and represent temptation and evil. Opening with witches establishes the play's dark, supernatural tone.

The Supernatural World

Elizabethans believed in:

  • Ghosts - spirits of the dead, especially those wronged
  • Demons - could possess or tempt humans
  • Prophecy - fortune-telling was taken seriously
  • Omens - natural events could predict the future

⚔️ Gender and Society

Elizabethan Gender Roles

Men Women
Public sphere - politics, business, war Private sphere - home, children
Authority figures - fathers, husbands, kings Subordinate - obey father, then husband
Valued for strength, courage, reason Valued for chastity, obedience, silence
Could inherit property and titles Property passed through them, not to them

Lady Macbeth Subverts Gender Roles

"Unsex me here"

Lady Macbeth asks spirits to "unsex me here" and "take my milk for gall." She rejects femininity to gain masculine cruelty. This would have shocked Elizabethan audiences - a woman taking masculine power through evil means.

Macbeth's Masculinity

Lady Macbeth manipulates Macbeth by attacking his masculinity:

  • "When you durst do it, then you were a man"
  • She associates cruelty with masculinity
  • Macbeth proves his "manhood" through increasingly violent acts

📖 Literary Context

The Renaissance

Shakespeare wrote during the English Renaissance:

  • Revival of classical learning - Greek and Roman texts
  • Humanism - focus on human nature and potential
  • New forms - sonnets, tragedies, comedies
  • Exploration of complex characters and psychology

Tragedy Tradition

Shakespeare drew on classical tragedy:

  • Tragic hero - noble but flawed
  • Hamartia - fatal flaw (Macbeth's ambition)
  • Peripeteia - reversal of fortune
  • Anagnorisis - moment of recognition
  • Catharsis - audience purges emotions

Sources

Shakespeare borrowed plots from:

  • Holinshed's Chronicles - history of Britain, source for "Macbeth"
  • Plutarch's Lives - Roman history
  • Italian novellas - romances and comedies
  • Existing plays - adapted and improved

✍️ Using Context in Essays

How to Integrate Context

Golden Rule: Link context to specific moments in the play. Don't just dump historical facts - explain how context deepens understanding of the text.
Good Context Integration

When Macbeth sees Banquo's ghost, his guilt manifests as a visible apparition that only he can see. This would have resonated with James I's audience, who believed that the souls of murdered men could return to haunt their killers. The ghost represents divine justice - Banquo's spirit cannot rest until Macbeth is punished.

Useful Phrases

  • "For a Jacobean audience, this would have..."
  • "Shakespeare's contemporary audience believed..."
  • "This reflects Elizabethan beliefs about..."
  • "James I's interest in X influenced..."

⚠️ Common Mistakes

Mistake How to Fix
Saying "Shakespeare's time" vaguely Be specific: Elizabethan, Jacobean, 1606
Listing context facts without linking Connect every fact to a quotation or moment
Forgetting James I context for "Macbeth" Remember: written specifically for James
Ignoring theatrical context Consider how plays were performed

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What is the Divine Right of Kings and how does it affect "Macbeth"?

Q2: Why did Shakespeare include witches in "Macbeth"?

Q3: How did the Globe Theatre shape Shakespeare's writing?

Q4: What was the Gunpowder Plot and why is it relevant?

✅ Answers

  1. Divine Right of Kings meant monarchs were appointed by God. Killing a king was a sin against divine order. In "Macbeth," regicide triggers chaos in nature and leads to Macbeth's downfall, reinforcing this belief.
  2. James I was obsessed with witchcraft and wrote a book about identifying witches. Including witches appealed to James, established the play's evil atmosphere, and reflected genuine Jacobean fears.
  3. The Globe had no sets or artificial lighting, so Shakespeare used language to create setting and atmosphere. The thrust stage meant audience surrounded actors, creating intimacy. All-male casts meant boys played women.
  4. The Gunpowder Plot (1605) was a failed Catholic attempt to blow up Parliament and kill James I. "Macbeth" (1606) explores treason and regicide by a trusted subject, resonating with audiences still shaken by the plot.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know the difference between Elizabethan and Jacobean periods
  • Link context to specific quotations
  • Remember James I's influence on "Macbeth"
  • Consider theatrical conditions (Globe, all-male cast)
  • Understand beliefs: divine right, witches, Great Chain of Being
  • Use context to explain audience reactions
