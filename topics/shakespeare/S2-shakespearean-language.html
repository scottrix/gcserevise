S2: Shakespearean Language

Foundation Higher All Boards AO2

Analysing Shakespeare's distinctive language - verse, prose, imagery, and wordplay.

📝 Verse and Prose

Iambic Pentameter

Shakespeare's most common verse form:

  • 10 syllables per line
  • Alternating unstressed/stressed beats (da-DUM da-DUM)
  • Mimics natural speech rhythm
  • Associated with nobility and serious moments
Iambic Pentameter in "Macbeth"

"So foul and fair a day I have not seen"

so FOUL and FAIR a DAY i HAVE not SEEN

Perfect iambic pentameter - Macbeth's first line echoes the witches.

Blank Verse

Unrhymed iambic pentameter - Shakespeare's default for serious drama:

  • Flexible - can be interrupted, fragmented
  • Allows natural speech within poetic structure
  • Breaking the rhythm signals emotional disturbance
Broken Verse

After murdering Duncan, Macbeth's speech becomes fragmented:

"I have done the deed. Didst thou not hear a noise?"

The caesura (break) shows his panic. Normal rhythm broken by guilt.

Prose

Non-poetic speech - no meter or rhyme:

When Used Examples
Lower-class characters Porter in "Macbeth", servants
Comic moments Drunken porter scene
Madness or mental breakdown Ophelia in "Hamlet"
Letters or documents Macbeth's letter to Lady Macbeth

🎭 Soliloquies

What is a Soliloquy?

A speech where a character alone on stage speaks their thoughts aloud:

  • Audience hears inner thoughts
  • Reveals true feelings, plans, conflicts
  • Creates intimacy with audience
  • No other characters can hear
Why Soliloquies Matter: They give direct access to a character's psychology. In "Macbeth," soliloquies show his internal conflict, ambition, guilt, and eventual nihilism.

Famous Soliloquies in "Macbeth"

"Is this a dagger which I see before me" (Act 2, Scene 1)

Macbeth hallucinates a dagger leading him to Duncan. Shows:

  • Mental turmoil before the murder
  • Guilt and fear already present
  • Supernatural visions
  • Decision to act ("I go, and it is done")
"Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow" (Act 5, Scene 5)

Macbeth's response to Lady Macbeth's death. Shows:

  • Complete emotional numbness
  • Nihilism - life is meaningless
  • "A tale told by an idiot... signifying nothing"
  • All passion spent - only survival remains

💬 Asides

What is an Aside?

A brief comment that other characters on stage don't hear:

  • Short, revealing moments
  • Shares secrets with audience
  • Shows true feelings while pretending otherwise
Aside in "Macbeth"

"Stars, hide your fires! Let not light see my black and deep desires."

Macbeth speaks this while Duncan and others are present. Shows his secret ambition and awareness that it's wrong. The audience becomes complicit in his dark thoughts.

🎨 Imagery in Shakespeare

Key Image Clusters in "Macbeth"

Image Meaning Examples

Extended Metaphors

Clothing Imagery

Macbeth's kingship is repeatedly compared to ill-fitting clothes:

  • "Why do you dress me in borrowed robes?" (Act 1)
  • "Like a giant's robe upon a dwarfish thief" (Act 5)

Effect: Shows Macbeth is not suited for kingship - he stole it, doesn't belong in it.

🔤 Rhetorical Devices

Repetition

Shakespeare uses repetition for emphasis:

Examples in "Macbeth"
  • "Sleep no more! Macbeth does murder sleep" - emphasises eternal guilt
  • "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow" - meaningless repetition of days
  • "Double, double toil and trouble" - witches' chant

Antithesis

Contrasting ideas in parallel structure:

"Fair is foul, and foul is fair"

The witches' paradox establishes:

  • Theme of appearance vs reality
  • Moral confusion - good and evil inverted
  • Macbeth's world is turned upside down

Oxymoron

Two contradictory terms together:

Examples
  • "Dagger of the mind" - physical weapon vs mental hallucination
  • "Hurly-burly" - chaos of battle
  • "Good things of day begin to droop" - natural vs unnatural

🧩 Wordplay and Puns

Double Meanings

Shakespeare loved words with multiple meanings:

"Kind" in "Macbeth"

When Macbeth says Duncan is "too full o'th'milk of human kindness", "kind" meant:

  • Compassionate, generous (modern meaning)
  • Natural, proper to one's nature (Elizabethan meaning)

Macbeth thinks Duncan is too soft, but also that he's too natural - not ruthless enough.

Ambiguity

The Witches' Prophecies

"None of woman born shall harm Macbeth"

  • Macbeth interprets: No one can kill me (everyone is born of woman)
  • Reality: Macduff was "from his mother's womb untimely ripped" (C-section)

Effect: Prophecies are ambiguous - Macbeth's downfall comes from misinterpretation.

📊 Language and Character

Language Reveals Character

Character Language Features What It Shows
Macbeth (early) Noble language, complex sentences Respected warrior, controlled
Macbeth (late) Fragmented, monosyllabic, nihilistic Mental deterioration, emptiness
Lady Macbeth (early) Imperatives, harsh consonants, violent imagery Dominant, cruel, powerful
Lady Macbeth (late) Fragmented, repetitive, childlike Guilt destroyed her mind
Witches Rhyming couplets, trochaic meter, paradox Supernatural, evil, disorder

Language Shifts

Track language changes: How a character speaks at the beginning vs the end shows their development (or deterioration).

✍️ Analysing Shakespeare's Language

Step-by-Step Approach

  1. Identify the technique - verse/prose, soliloquy, imagery, etc.
  2. Quote specific words - not whole speeches
  3. Analyse the effect - what does it show about character/theme?
  4. Consider context - why would this affect Shakespeare's audience?
Model Analysis

Shakespeare uses the metaphor of clothing to show Macbeth's unsuitability for kingship. When Angus says Macbeth's title hangs loose about him "like a giant's robe upon a dwarfish thief," the clothing imagery emphasises that Macbeth has stolen the crown and cannot fill it properly. The comparison to a "dwarf" suggests Macbeth is morally and physically diminished by his crimes, while "thief" reminds the audience that his power is illegitimate. For James I's court, this would reinforce the Divine Right of Kings - Macbeth's ill-fitting reign proves he was never meant to rule.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

Mistake Fix
Quoting too much Focus on specific words, not whole speeches
Not identifying verse vs prose Always check: Is this verse or prose? Why?
Forgetting soliloquies reveal truth Soliloquies = character's real thoughts
Ignoring imagery patterns Track recurring images (blood, darkness)

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What is iambic pentameter and why does Shakespeare use it?

Q2: What's the difference between a soliloquy and an aside?

Q3: What does the blood imagery represent in "Macbeth"?

Q4: Why do the witches speak in rhyming couplets?

✅ Answers

  1. Iambic pentameter is 10-syllable verse with alternating stressed/unstressed beats (da-DUM da-DUM). Shakespeare uses it for nobility and serious moments - it elevates language, creates rhythm, and signals importance. Breaking the rhythm shows emotional disturbance.
  2. A soliloquy is a long speech with the character alone, revealing inner thoughts. An aside is a brief comment that other characters don't hear. Soliloquies = deep psychological insight; asides = quick revelations.
  3. Blood represents guilt that cannot be washed away. Macbeth asks if "Neptune's ocean" can wash the blood, but it will "the multitudinous seas incarnadine." Lady Macbeth sleepwalks, trying to wash imaginary blood. Blood stains represent permanent moral corruption.
  4. Witches speak in rhyming couplets and trochaic meter (opposite of iambic) to sound supernatural, unsettling, and different from noble characters. Their chanting quality associates them with spells and evil. This would have frightened Shakespeare's audience.
