S4: Shakespeare Extract Analysis

Foundation Higher All Boards AO1 AO2

How to analyse Shakespeare extracts effectively - close reading, quotation selection, and linking to the whole play.

📋 Understanding Extract Questions

What to Expect

Shakespeare questions typically give you:

  • A short extract (about 30-40 lines)
  • Question asking you to analyse the extract
  • Instruction to link to the whole play
  • Usually worth 30 marks (20 for extract, 10 for whole play)
Key Point: You MUST discuss BOTH the extract AND the whole play. Ignoring either will cost you marks.

Common Question Types

Focus Example Question
Character "How does Shakespeare present Macbeth in this extract?"
Theme "Explore how Shakespeare presents ambition in this extract"
Relationship "How does Shakespeare present the relationship between Macbeth and Lady Macbeth?"
Language/Technique "How does Shakespeare use language to create tension?"

🔍 Close Reading Strategy

Step 1: Read Twice

First reading: Get the gist - what's happening? Who's speaking?

Second reading: Look for techniques - language, structure, form

Step 2: Annotate

Mark up the extract with:

  • Key quotations - specific words to analyse
  • Techniques - imagery, metaphor, structure
  • Effects - what does it make the audience feel/think?
  • Links - connections to other parts of the play

Step 3: Plan Your Points

Identify 3-4 key quotations to analyse in depth. Don't try to cover everything - go deep on specific moments.

📝 Selecting Quotations

What Makes a Good Quotation?

  • Rich in language techniques
  • Reveals character or theme
  • Connects to wider play
  • Short enough to analyse word by word
Good vs Poor Quotations
Good Quotation Why
"Stars, hide your fires" Imagery, imperative, symbolism, links to whole play
"fair is foul" Paradox, theme of appearance vs reality, establishes witches
"dagger of the mind" Metaphor, oxymoron, psychological insight

Poor: "He kills the king" - too vague, no language to analyse

Embedding Quotations

Don't just drop quotes in - embed them in your analysis:

Embedding Quotations

Poor: This shows Macbeth is ambitious. "I have no spur to prick the sides of my intent." He wants to be king.

Good: Macbeth acknowledges his "vaulting ambition" as his only motivation, noting he has "no spur to prick the sides of my intent." The metaphor of horse-riding suggests he is forcing himself forward without proper justification.

🔬 Language Analysis in Extracts

Zoom In on Words

Analyse specific words, not whole sentences:

Word-Level Analysis

Extract: "Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood / Clean from my hand?"

  • "Neptune's ocean" - hyperbole, Roman god of sea, classical reference for educated audience
  • "blood" - metonymy for guilt, visual image of stain
  • "wash" - verb of cleansing, but impossible here
  • "Clean" - ironic, nothing is clean anymore

Track Image Patterns

Connect imagery in extract to the whole play:

Blood Imagery

In the extract, Macbeth sees blood on his hands. This connects to:

  • Lady Macbeth: "A little water clears us of this deed"
  • Later: Lady Macbeth sleepwalks, trying to wash imaginary blood
  • Final scene: Macduff enters with Macbeth's head - blood comes full circle

🎭 Structure Analysis in Extracts

Look at the Speech Structure

Feature What to Look For Example
Verse vs prose Why this form? Prose = lower status or madness
Line length Broken lines = emotional disturbance Fragmented after murder
Soliloquy Direct access to thoughts "Is this a dagger"
Interruption Power dynamics Lady Macbeth interrupts Macbeth

Beginning and End of Extract

The extract starts and ends where it does for a reason. Ask:

  • Why start HERE? What's the dramatic situation?
  • Why end HERE? What tension or resolution?
  • What's the journey within the extract?

🔗 Linking to the Whole Play

Why Linking Matters

About 1/3 of marks are for whole-play knowledge. You need to show:

  • Understanding of where extract fits in the play
  • Character development before/after extract
  • Themes explored beyond this moment

How to Link Effectively

The Bridge Method: After analysing the extract, add a paragraph that connects to the wider play. Use phrases like:
  • "This moment echoes..."
  • "Earlier in the play..."
  • "This contrasts with..."
  • "Later, we see..."
Effective Linking

Extract analysis: Macbeth's use of the imperative "Stars, hide your fires" reveals his desire for darkness to conceal his "black and deep desires."

Link to whole play: This moment establishes Macbeth's awareness of his own evil, which contrasts sharply with his later bravado. As the play progresses, Macbeth embraces darkness fully - ordering Banquo's murder by night, and later declaring he has "almost forgotten the taste of fears." His initial desire to hide his ambitions evolves into a complete moral descent, culminating in his nihilistic recognition that life is "a tale told by an idiot."

Whole-Play Points to Know

For each major character and theme, memorise 2-3 key moments outside the extract:

Topic Key Moments
Macbeth's ambition Prophecy, "dagger" soliloquy, "tomorrow" speech
Lady Macbeth "Unsex me", drugging guards, sleepwalking scene
Guilt Blood on hands, Banquo's ghost, sleepwalking
Kingship Duncan's virtues, Macbeth's tyranny, Malcolm's restoration

✍️ Structuring Your Essay

Recommended Structure

Introduction (2-3 sentences):
Identify the focus, place the extract in the play, state your argument.

Extract Analysis (3-4 paragraphs):
Each paragraph: Point → Quote → Analyse → Effect

Whole-Play Links (1-2 paragraphs):
Connect to earlier/later scenes, track development, contrast

Conclusion (2-3 sentences):
Sum up how extract reveals character/theme, Shakespeare's purpose

Time Management

  • 5 mins: Read and annotate extract
  • 30 mins: Write essay (20 mins extract, 10 mins whole play)
  • 5 mins: Check and add missing links

📊 Mark Scheme

What Examiners Want

AO Focus Weight
AO1 Understanding, critical style, personal response ~40%
AO2 Language, form, structure analysis ~40%
AO3 Context (historical, social, literary) ~20%

Top Level Responses

  • Analyse specific words and techniques
  • Show clear understanding of effects
  • Link extract to whole play consistently
  • Use context where relevant
  • Maintain critical, analytical style

⚠️ Common Mistakes

Mistake Fix
Not linking to whole play Always add a paragraph connecting to other scenes
Retelling the story Analyse language and effect, don't just describe
Quoting too much Focus on specific words, analyse in depth
Feature spotting Always explain the EFFECT of techniques
Ignoring structure/form Consider verse, soliloquy, pacing, not just language

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What should you look for in a close reading of an extract?

Q2: How do you embed a quotation effectively?

Q3: What's the difference between extract analysis and whole-play linking?

Q4: How much time should you spend on the extract vs whole play?

✅ Answers

  1. Look for: key quotations rich in language techniques; structural features (verse/prose, soliloquy); effects on audience; connections to themes and whole play.
  2. Embed by integrating the quotation into your sentence structure. Instead of "He says 'quote'. This shows...", write "Macbeth's use of 'quote' reveals..." - make the quotation part of your analysis.
  3. Extract analysis focuses on the specific lines given: word choice, imagery, structure within that passage. Whole-play linking connects to scenes before/after, tracking character development and themes across the entire play.
  4. For a 30-mark question: roughly 20 marks for extract analysis (2/3 of essay), 10 marks for whole-play links (1/3 of essay). Time: 20 mins extract, 10 mins whole play.
