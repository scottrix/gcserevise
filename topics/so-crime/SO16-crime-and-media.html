Crime, Media & Public Perception

The media's role in crime: moral panics, deviancy amplification, and how media coverage affects public fear and crime policy.

Key Fact: Media representations of crime: the media over-represent violent and sexual crime (12% actual but 60-70% media coverage). The media under-represent corporate and white-collar crime. This distortion creates a false public perception.
Key Fact: Moral panics (Cohen): a condition where the media defines a group as a threat to societal values. Cohen's mods and rockers study: media exaggerated minor beach fights, creating a moral panic that labelled them as 'folk devils'.
Key Fact: Deviancy amplification spiral: media exaggeration -> increased public concern -> increased policing -> more arrests -> more media coverage -> further exaggeration. The spiral makes the 'problem' appear bigger than it really is.
Key Fact: Fear of crime: media coverage increases fear, especially among vulnerable groups (elderly, women). However, those who fear crime most are least likely to be victims. Young men are the most likely victims but fear crime least.
Key Fact: Media and crime policy: moral panics influence government policy. The 'war on drugs' was partly driven by media moral panics; ASBOs were introduced partly due to media campaigns.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe Cohen's concept of moral panics and evaluate its usefulness.

Q2: Explain how the media distorts the public's perception of crime.

Q3: Evaluate whether media coverage of crime causes more harm than the crime itself.

✅ Answers

  1. Cohen: moral panics occur when the media defines a group as a threat. Mods and rockers: media exaggerated violence, labelled them as folk devils, and the deviancy amplification spiral created more deviance. Evaluation: useful but some panics may reflect genuine concern.
  2. The media distorts crime by: over-representing violent crime (12% actual, 60-70% media), under-representing corporate crime, using emotionally charged language. Result: public overestimates violent crime and underestimates white-collar crime.
  3. Media harm: creates fear, diverts resources, drives punitive policy. Crime harm: direct damage to victims. Evaluation: both cause significant harm, but media distortion can be more harmful because it misdirects resources from effective prevention.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Cohen: moral panic = media defines group as THREAT. Folk devils + deviancy amplification spiral.
  • Media over-represents VIOLENT crime (12% actual, 60-70% media). Creates FEAR.
  • Deviancy amplification: media -> concern -> policing -> arrests -> media -> MORE DEVIANCE.

Sociology Exam Tips:

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The media simply reports what happens in relation to crime, so its coverage is accurate. Correct: Research consistently shows media coverage is distorted: violent crime is over-represented by 5-6 times; white-collar crime is almost entirely absent; news values drive selection. The media actively constructs a particular version of crime that serves commercial and ideological purposes.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that the media creates rather than merely reflects public concern about crime.

A grade 9 response will: describe media distortions; explain Cohen's moral panic theory with the mods and rockers study; present evidence that media creates concern; present counter-argument; conclude: the media both reflects and amplifies public concern, but the amplification effect is significant - moral panics demonstrate that media coverage can transform minor issues into major social problems.

📖 Detailed Notes: Crime and Media

💡 Media Representations of Crime

The media do not simply reflect the reality of crime — they construct it through selection, emphasis and presentation. News values determine which crimes are reported: violent crime, crimes involving celebrities, and crimes with dramatic or shocking elements receive disproportionate coverage, while everyday crimes such as burglary and shoplifting receive less attention. This creates a distorted picture: the media over-represent violent and sexual crime (which account for a small minority of all offences) and under-represent property crime and white-collar crime (which are far more common and costly). Surette’s research found that media representations of crime follow a ‘law of opposites’ — the crimes most commonly shown in the media are the least common in reality, and the crimes most common in reality are the least shown in the media. This distortion matters because it shapes public perceptions: people who rely on media for crime information tend to overestimate the level of violent crime and fear victimisation more than is statistically justified.

Moral panics occur when the media identifies a group or activity as a threat to societal values and presents it in a sensationalised, exaggerated way. Stanley Cohen’s classic study of the 1960s mods and rockers demonstrated how media coverage distorted the scale and severity of youth disturbances, creating a ‘folk devil’ and a moral panic that demanded a social control response. Other examples include the panic over mugging in the 1970s (Hall et al.), video nasties in the 1980s, and concerns about knife crime and gang culture in recent decades. Moral panics often target groups already marginalised by society — young working-class men, ethnic minorities and immigrant communities — reinforcing existing prejudices and justifying increased social control.

UK Context: The UK media’s coverage of knife crime illustrates how media representation can distort reality. While knife crime is a serious issue, particularly in London, media coverage often gives the impression that it is far more widespread than statistics suggest. In 2023, there were approximately 50,000 offences involving a knife or sharp instrument in England and Wales — a significant number, but representing only about 1% of all recorded crime.

🔍 Media Effects on Crime and Fear of Crime

The media may influence crime in several ways. Bandura’s social learning theory suggests that people (especially young people) may imitate violent behaviour they see in the media. The ‘deviancy amplification spiral’ (Wilkins) describes how media coverage of deviant behaviour leads to increased public concern, which leads to more intensive policing, which leads to more arrests and more media coverage, creating a self-reinforcing cycle. This was evident in the mods and rockers panic and in more recent coverage of drug use and gang activity. The concept of ‘copycat crime’ suggests that media coverage of a particular crime may inspire others to commit similar offences — there is evidence that this happens with mass shootings and suicide.

The fear of crime is itself a significant social problem. The CSEW consistently shows that a significant proportion of the public fears crime, particularly violent crime, despite falling crime rates since the mid-1990s. Women and older people tend to fear crime most, even though young men are statistically the most likely victims. Gerbner’s ‘cultivation theory’ argues that heavy television viewers develop an exaggerated fear of crime because TV over-represents violence and danger, creating a ‘mean world syndrome’. In the UK, tabloid newspaper readers tend to have a greater fear of crime than broadsheet readers, suggesting that the style and content of media consumption affects perceptions. Social media has added a new dimension: viral videos of violent incidents, neighbourhood crime alerts and online rumour-spreading can amplify fear of crime rapidly within communities.

UK Context: The murder of Sarah Everard in 2021, and its extensive media coverage, highlighted both the reality and the fear of violence against women in the UK. While the case was genuinely horrific, media coverage also sparked a wider conversation about how women routinely modify their behaviour to avoid harassment and assault — a form of fear of crime that affects daily life but is often invisible in official statistics.

⚖️ New Media, Cybercrime and the Future of Crime

The internet and digital technology have created new forms of crime. Cybercrime includes hacking, identity theft, online fraud, cyberbullying, online grooming, revenge porn and the distribution of illegal content. The National Crime Agency estimates that cybercrime costs the UK economy approximately £27 billion per year, making it one of the most significant crime threats. However, cybercrime is underreported because many victims are embarrassed, do not know how to report it, or believe that nothing can be done. Social media platforms are used to facilitate crimes ranging from drug dealing to harassment to the radicalisation of young people. The ‘dark web’ enables anonymous criminal transactions including the sale of drugs, weapons and stolen data.

The media landscape is changing rapidly, and these changes affect both crime and our understanding of it. Social media enables ‘citizen journalism’ — ordinary people can report crimes, share footage and mobilise campaigns (as with the Black Lives Matter movement). However, social media also enables misinformation, online abuse and the rapid spread of moral panics. The concept of ‘trial by social media’ describes cases where individuals are publicly shamed or accused online before any legal process has taken place, potentially violating the principle of innocent until proven guilty. Streaming services and online platforms have also raised questions about the representation of crime — true crime documentaries and podcasts have become hugely popular, potentially glamourising criminals and trivialising victims. The challenge for sociologists is to understand how new media technologies are reshaping both the reality of crime and our perceptions of it.

UK Context: The UK’s Online Safety Act (2023) was introduced to address online harms including cyberbullying, revenge porn and terrorist content. However, critics argue that the legislation is difficult to enforce across international borders, that it risks restricting free speech, and that technology companies are not doing enough to protect users.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Evaluate the view that media representations of crime create a false picture of the reality of crime in the UK.

Q: Explain how a moral panic develops, using a UK example, and evaluate whether the concept is still useful for understanding media coverage of crime.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. Media representations clearly distort the reality of crime: violent and sexual crime are over-represented, while white-collar crime and corporate crime are under-represented. Surette’s ‘law of opposites’ shows that the crimes most commonly featured in the media are the least common in reality. This distortion creates public fear disproportionate to actual risk. However, left realists argue that media coverage sometimes reflects genuine public concern about real problems — knife crime in London is a serious issue, and media coverage can prompt necessary policy responses. Additionally, social media means that the public is no longer a passive audience: people can access official statistics, challenge media narratives and share their own experiences. A balanced view is that while mainstream media creates a distorted picture, the increasing availability of information means that informed citizens can develop a more accurate understanding of crime.
  2. A moral panic develops in stages (Cohen): first, a group or behaviour is defined as a threat; second, the media portrays the group in a stylised and stereotypical way, creating a ‘folk devil’; third, the public, media and authorities respond with increased concern and social control; fourth, the moral panic leads to changes in law or policing. The 1960s mods and rockers panic is the classic example. More recently, the UK media’s coverage of ‘acid attacks’ in London followed a similar pattern. The concept remains useful because it highlights how media coverage can distort reality, target marginalised groups and drive disproportionate policy responses. However, critics argue that the concept may overstate media power (audiences are now more active and critical), that it assumes a passive public, and that some media panics reflect genuine social problems rather than irrational overreactions.
