Crime, Media & Public Perception

The media's role in crime: moral panics, deviancy amplification, and how media coverage affects public fear and crime policy.

Key Fact: Media representations of crime: the media over-represent violent and sexual crime (12% actual but 60-70% media coverage). The media under-represent corporate and white-collar crime. This distortion creates a false public perception.
Key Fact: Moral panics (Cohen): a condition where the media defines a group as a threat to societal values. Cohen's mods and rockers study: media exaggerated minor beach fights, creating a moral panic that labelled them as 'folk devils'.
Key Fact: Deviancy amplification spiral: media exaggeration -> increased public concern -> increased policing -> more arrests -> more media coverage -> further exaggeration. The spiral makes the 'problem' appear bigger than it really is.
Key Fact: Fear of crime: media coverage increases fear, especially among vulnerable groups (elderly, women). However, those who fear crime most are least likely to be victims. Young men are the most likely victims but fear crime least.
Key Fact: Media and crime policy: moral panics influence government policy. The 'war on drugs' was partly driven by media moral panics; ASBOs were introduced partly due to media campaigns.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe Cohen's concept of moral panics and evaluate its usefulness.

Q2: Explain how the media distorts the public's perception of crime.

Q3: Evaluate whether media coverage of crime causes more harm than the crime itself.

✅ Answers

  1. Cohen: moral panics occur when the media defines a group as a threat. Mods and rockers: media exaggerated violence, labelled them as folk devils, and the deviancy amplification spiral created more deviance. Evaluation: useful but some panics may reflect genuine concern.
  2. The media distorts crime by: over-representing violent crime (12% actual, 60-70% media), under-representing corporate crime, using emotionally charged language. Result: public overestimates violent crime and underestimates white-collar crime.
  3. Media harm: creates fear, diverts resources, drives punitive policy. Crime harm: direct damage to victims. Evaluation: both cause significant harm, but media distortion can be more harmful because it misdirects resources from effective prevention.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Cohen: moral panic = media defines group as THREAT. Folk devils + deviancy amplification spiral.
  • Media over-represents VIOLENT crime (12% actual, 60-70% media). Creates FEAR.
  • Deviancy amplification: media -> concern -> policing -> arrests -> media -> MORE DEVIANCE.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing crime and media, always use Cohen's moral panic framework and explain the deviancy amplification spiral.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The media simply reports what happens in relation to crime, so its coverage is accurate. Correct: Research consistently shows media coverage is distorted: violent crime is over-represented by 5-6 times; white-collar crime is almost entirely absent; news values drive selection. The media actively constructs a particular version of crime that serves commercial and ideological purposes.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that the media creates rather than merely reflects public concern about crime.

A grade 9 response will: describe media distortions; explain Cohen's moral panic theory with the mods and rockers study; present evidence that media creates concern; present counter-argument; conclude: the media both reflects and amplifies public concern, but the amplification effect is significant - moral panics demonstrate that media coverage can transform minor issues into major social problems.
