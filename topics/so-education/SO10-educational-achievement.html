Educational Achievement & Inequality

Why some groups achieve more than others: the impact of social class, gender and ethnicity on educational achievement. In-school and external factors that create inequality.

Key Fact: Social class and achievement: working-class students consistently achieve less well than middle-class students. External factors: material deprivation (poorer housing, less study space, no internet), cultural deprivation (Bernstein: restricted code vs elaborated code), cultural capital (Bourdieu: middle-class knowledge, tastes and values give advantage in school). Internal factors: streaming and labelling (Becker: teachers have an 'ideal pupil' image that is middle-class).
Key Fact: Gender and achievement: girls now outperform boys at every level (61% of girls vs 50% of boys achieve 5+ GCSEs A*-C). Reasons for girls' improvement: feminism (changed aspirations), changing family patterns (more women-headed households), employment opportunities (more jobs for women), GCSE coursework favoured girls. Reasons for boys' underachievement: laddish subcultures (Epstein: working-class boys see academic work as 'unmasculine'), male role model deficit (few male primary teachers).
Key Fact: Ethnicity and achievement: Chinese and Indian students achieve above the national average; Bangladeshi, Pakistani and Black Caribbean students achieve below. External factors: language barriers (English as additional language), material deprivation (higher poverty rates among some ethnic groups), family structure (more lone-parent families among Black Caribbean). Internal factors: teacher labelling (Gillborn and Youdell: teachers have lower expectations of Black students), institutional racism (policies that inadvertently disadvantage ethnic minorities), ethnocentric curriculum.
Key Fact: Labelling and self-fulfilling prophecy: Rosenthal and Jacobson's Pygmalion study showed that teacher expectations influenced student performance. Students labelled as 'bright' improved; those labelled as 'slow' declined. Becker: teachers judge students against the 'ideal pupil' (middle-class, compliant, motivated).
Key Fact: Streaming and setting: students are placed in ability groups. Hargreaves: streaming creates anti-school subcultures in lower streams (students reject school values because the school has rejected them). Gillborn and Youdell: the 'educational triage' - schools categorise students as those who will pass anyway, those with potential to improve, and hopeless cases - working-class and Black students are disproportionately placed in the 'hopeless' category.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain how social class affects educational achievement, using external and internal factors.

Q2: Evaluate why girls now outperform boys in education.

Q3: Assess the relative importance of external and internal factors in creating ethnic differences in educational achievement.

✅ Answers

  1. External factors: material deprivation (poverty, poor housing, less resources), cultural deprivation (less parental support, restricted language code - Bernstein), cultural capital (Bourdieu: middle-class advantage). Internal factors: labelling (Becker's ideal pupil is middle-class), streaming (working-class placed in lower streams), self-fulfilling prophecy (teacher expectations shape outcomes). Both are important but internal factors may be more significant because even affluent working-class students underachieve.
  2. Girls outperform boys because: feminism raised aspirations; GCSE coursework suited girls' learning style; more female role models; changing family patterns give girls higher expectations. Boys underachieve because: laddish subcultures reject academic work; fewer male role models; literacy is seen as feminine. However: gender gap is narrowing; boys still dominate some subjects (maths, physics); the 'crisis of masculinity' may explain boys' disengagement.
  3. External: poverty (Bangladeshi and Pakistani groups have highest poverty rates), language barriers, family structure. Internal: teacher labelling (Gillborn: teachers have lower expectations of Black students), institutional racism (policies and practices that disadvantage ethnic minorities), ethnocentric curriculum (focuses on White British culture). Evaluation: external factors (especially poverty) are significant, but even when class is controlled, some ethnic differences persist, suggesting internal factors (racism, labelling) also matter. Both are important and interact.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Class: external (material + cultural deprivation) + internal (labelling, streaming). Both matter.
  • Gender: girls NOW outperform boys. Causes: feminism, coursework, aspirations. Boys: laddish culture, role model deficit.
  • Ethnicity: external (poverty, language) + internal (labelling, institutional racism, ethnocentric curriculum). Both interact.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing educational inequality, always: distinguish external from internal factors, use named studies (Bourdieu, Bernstein, Becker, Gillborn), and evaluate which factors are most important for each group (class, gender, ethnicity).

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Differences in educational achievement are due to natural differences in intelligence between social groups. Correct: Sociological research consistently shows that achievement differences are caused by social factors, not innate intelligence: cultural capital gives middle-class children advantages; labelling and streaming disadvantage working-class and ethnic minority students; teacher expectations create self-fulfilling prophecies; material deprivation (poverty, poor housing) affects learning; and institutional racism disadvantages some ethnic groups. The fact that achievement patterns change over time (e.g. girls now outperform boys) proves social factors, not natural ability, drive differences.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the relative importance of internal and external factors in explaining social class differences in educational achievement.

A grade 9 response will: describe external factors (material deprivation, cultural deprivation, cultural capital - Bourdieu, Bernstein); describe internal factors (labelling, streaming, self-fulfilling prophecy - Becker, Hargreaves); evaluate: external factors explain why working-class children start school behind; internal factors explain why the gap WIDENS during school; interaction: cultural capital leads to labelling which creates a self-fulfilling prophecy; conclude: both are important and interact, but internal factors may be more significant because they actively reproduce inequality during the school process - schools could address internal factors through anti-labelling training and mixed-ability teaching.
