School Processes & the Hidden Curriculum

How school processes affect achievement: labelling, streaming, setting, the hidden curriculum, pupil subcultures, and how these create or reinforce inequality.

Key Fact: Labelling: teachers attach labels to students based on assumptions about ability, behaviour and background. Becker: teachers have an 'ideal pupil' image (middle-class, well-behaved, motivated) and judge students against it. Raynor: teachers labelled students as 'bright' or 'difficult' based on appearance and behaviour, not ability. Labels become self-fulfilling prophecies (Rosenthal & Jacobson: Pygmalion in the Classroom).
Key Fact: Streaming and setting: ability grouping within schools. Streaming: broad ability groups across all subjects. Setting: ability groups within individual subjects. Ball found that working-class and ethnic minority students are over-represented in lower streams. Once placed in a lower stream, it is very difficult to move up - streams become self-perpetuating.
Key Fact: Pupil subcultures: pro-school subculture (accept school values, work hard, aim for qualifications - typically in higher streams) and anti-school subculture (reject school values, disrupt lessons, seek status through deviance - typically in lower streams). Hargreaves: streaming creates anti-school subcultures because lower-stream students suffer a loss of self-esteem and seek status through alternative means.
Key Fact: Lacey's concepts: differentiation (teachers label and categorise students as 'bright' or 'thick') and polarisation (students respond by moving towards pro-school or anti-school subcultures). Woods identified more responses: ingratiation (trying to please teachers), ritualism (going through the motions), retreatism (withdrawing), rebellion (active opposition), and colonisation (minimum effort to avoid trouble).
Key Fact: The hidden curriculum reinforces inequality: it teaches compliance and obedience (particularly to working-class students who will fill low-status jobs), competition (which benefits those who are already advantaged), and gender/ethnic stereotypes through curriculum content and teacher expectations.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain how labelling and streaming can affect students' educational achievement.

Q2: Describe how pupil subcultures develop in response to streaming, using Hargreaves' and Lacey's research.

Q3: Evaluate whether school processes are more important than external factors in explaining educational inequality.

✅ Answers

  1. Labelling: teachers label students based on class/gender/ethnicity (Becker: ideal pupil is middle-class). Labels become self-fulfilling prophecies (Rosenthal & Jacobson). Streaming: working-class and ethnic minority students are placed in lower streams (Ball). Lower streams = less qualified teachers, less challenging work, lower expectations. The cycle: label -> lower stream -> low self-esteem -> anti-school subculture -> underachievement.
  2. Hargreaves: students in lower streams suffer status frustration (loss of self-esteem from being labelled failures) and form anti-school subcultures to gain status through non-academic means (troublemaking, rejecting school values). Lacey: differentiation (labelling by ability) leads to polarisation (students move to pro- or anti-school extremes). This explains why streaming WIDENS the achievement gap rather than narrowing it.
  3. School processes (labelling, streaming, subcultures) are important because they actively create inequality during schooling. External factors (poverty, cultural capital) set the starting point but internal factors determine the trajectory. However: external factors create the conditions for labelling (middle-class children are labelled positively because they fit the ideal pupil); school processes interact with external factors; and some students resist labels. Conclusion: both are important, but internal factors are more significant because they represent missed opportunities - schools COULD reduce inequality but instead often reproduce it.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Labelling -> self-fulfilling prophecy -> streaming -> status frustration -> anti-school subculture -> underachievement. This is the PROCESS of inequality.
  • Pro-school subculture = accept school values (higher streams). Anti-school subculture = reject school values (lower streams). Hargreaves and Lacey.
  • Differentiation (label by ability) + Polarisation (move to extremes) = streaming widens the gap.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing school processes, always: show the chain from labelling to achievement outcomes, name specific studies (Becker, Hargreaves, Lacey, Ball), and evaluate whether school processes create inequality or merely reflect external differences.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Streaming helps all students because they learn at their own pace with students of similar ability. Correct: While streaming may seem logical, research shows it WIDENS inequality: working-class and ethnic minority students are disproportionately placed in lower streams (Ball); lower-stream students receive less challenging work and lower expectations; streaming creates anti-school subcultures (Hargreaves); students rarely move between streams, making placement self-perpetuating. Mixed-ability teaching with differentiated support is a fairer alternative because it avoids the self-fulfilling prophecy of labelling and streaming.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that school processes are more important than home background in explaining working-class underachievement.

A grade 9 response will: describe home background factors (material deprivation, cultural capital, Bernstein's codes); describe school processes (labelling, streaming, self-fulfilling prophecy, subcultures); argue school processes are more important (they actively CREATE inequality during school - even working-class children who start well are pulled down by labelling); argue home background matters (sets the starting point, affects teacher perceptions); conclude: both interact - home background creates conditions for labelling, but school processes determine whether the gap narrows or widens. Schools have the power to reduce inequality through anti-labelling policies and mixed-ability teaching, making internal factors more significant as a potential lever for change.
