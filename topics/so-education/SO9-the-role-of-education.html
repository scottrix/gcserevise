SO9: The Role of Education

Foundation Higher AQA 8192, OCR J205

The Role of Education

Sociological perspectives on education: Functionalist, Marxist, Feminist and Interactionist views of the role and function of the education system in society.

Key Fact: Functionalist view: education serves vital functions for society. Durkheim: education transmits society's norms and values (social solidarity) and teaches specialist skills for the division of labour. Parsons: education is the main agent of secondary socialisation and a bridge between family and wider society; it promotes meritocracy (everyone has equal opportunity to succeed based on ability and effort).
Key Fact: Marxist view: education serves capitalism. Althusser: education is an Ideological State Apparatus (ISA) that transmits ruling class ideology, making inequality seem natural and inevitable. Bowles and Gintis: the correspondence principle - schools mirror the workplace through the hidden curriculum (hierarchy, competition, obedience to authority, punctuality) preparing workers for capitalist exploitation.
Key Fact: Feminist view: education is patriarchal. Historically: girls were steered towards domestic subjects and away from science/maths. Kelly: the curriculum is male-dominated (textbooks feature men, examples from male experiences). Modern challenge: despite girls outperforming boys, women still face a glass ceiling in careers and are underrepresented in STEM subjects.
Key Fact: The hidden curriculum: the unwritten, unofficial lessons that schools teach - obedience to authority, competition, conformity, acceptance of hierarchy, punctuality. Functionalists see this as positive (teaches social norms); Marxists see it as negative (prepares workers for exploitation).
Key Fact: Vocational education and training: provides work-based skills. Functionalists argue it meets the economy's need for skilled workers. Marxists argue it creates a compliant, low-paid workforce and reproduces class inequality (working-class students are steered towards vocational courses, middle-class towards academic).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Compare Functionalist and Marxist views of the role of education.

Q2: Explain what the hidden curriculum is and evaluate its importance in education.

Q3: Evaluate whether the education system is meritocratic.

✅ Answers

  1. Functionalism: education transmits norms (Durkheim), allocates roles by merit (Parsons), provides social mobility. Marxism: education transmits ruling class ideology (Althusser), corresponds to workplace hierarchy (Bowles & Gintis), reproduces class inequality. Key difference: Functionalism sees education serving everyone; Marxism sees it serving the ruling class.
  2. The hidden curriculum teaches: obedience (follow rules without questioning), hierarchy (teachers have authority over pupils), competition (grades, rankings), punctuality and attendance. Functionalists: this socialises young people for society. Marxists: this produces compliant workers for capitalism. Evaluation: the hidden curriculum exists but its effects vary - some students resist and question authority (e.g. student protests).
  3. Meritocracy means equal opportunity based on ability and effort. Evidence for: comprehensive schools aim to give all children the same education; exam results are (mostly) blind-marked. Evidence against: private schools give huge advantages (7% of pupils, 74% of judges); cultural capital (Bourdieu) gives middle-class children an advantage; streaming and labelling disadvantage working-class and minority ethnic students. Conclusion: the education system CLAIMS to be meritocratic but structural inequalities undermine this claim.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Functionalism: education = SOCIAL SOLIDARITY + MERITOCRACY. Marxism: education = IDEOLOGICAL CONTROL + CORRESPONDENCE PRINCIPLE.
  • Hidden curriculum: obedience + hierarchy + competition + punctuality. Functionalists say POSITIVE. Marxists say NEGATIVE.
  • Meritocracy: claimed but NOT fully achieved - class, gender and ethnicity affect outcomes.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When evaluating the role of education, always compare perspectives, use named sociologists and concepts, and provide evidence for whether education serves all groups equally or reproduces inequality.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The education system gives everyone an equal chance to succeed regardless of their background. Correct: While the education system claims to be meritocratic, evidence shows significant inequalities: private school pupils are disproportionately represented in top universities and professions; middle-class children benefit from cultural capital (Bourdieu); working-class and some ethnic minority students are disadvantaged by streaming and labelling; and the hidden curriculum reproduces class, gender and ethnic inequalities. Equal opportunity in principle does not mean equal opportunity in practice.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that the education system serves the interests of the ruling class.

A grade 9 response will: present Marxist evidence (Althusser's ISA, Bowles & Gintis' correspondence principle, hidden curriculum, cultural capital); present Functionalist counter-evidence (education provides social solidarity and skills, enables some social mobility); evaluate using evidence on class inequality in achievement (working-class underachievement); conclude: the education system does serve ruling class interests by reproducing class inequality, but it also provides genuine opportunities for some working-class students. The system is not purely ideological - it has dual functions: maintaining inequality AND enabling some social mobility. The question is whether the balance favours reproduction or mobility.
