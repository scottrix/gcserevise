SO7: Marriage, Divorce & Demography

Marriage, Divorce & Demography

Changing patterns of marriage, divorce and cohabitation. Demographic trends: birth rates, death rates, ageing population, and their social implications.

Key Fact: Marriage patterns: the marriage rate in the UK has fallen to its lowest level since records began. First marriages have declined; the average age of first marriage has risen (30 for women, 32 for men). Reasons: secularisation (less religious pressure), changing attitudes (cohabitation is now socially acceptable), women's economic independence (no longer need to marry for financial security).
Key Fact: Divorce: the divorce rate increased dramatically after the 1969 Divorce Reform Act (made 'irretrievable breakdown' sufficient grounds). Currently about 42% of marriages end in divorce. Reasons: easier divorce laws, less stigma, women's financial independence, rising expectations of marriage (Giddens: confluent love - relationships only survive while both partners are satisfied).
Key Fact: Cohabitation: living together without marriage. Has increased significantly - 20% of couples now cohabit. Some see it as a trial marriage; others see it as an alternative to marriage. Cohabiting couples have fewer legal rights than married couples. About half of cohabitations lead to marriage.
Key Fact: Birth rates: UK total fertility rate is about 1.6 children per woman (below replacement level of 2.1). Reasons: contraception, women's careers, later marriage, cost of raising children, changing values. Death rates: have fallen due to medical advances, better nutrition, improved sanitation. Life expectancy: 81 years (UK average).
Key Fact: Ageing population: the proportion of people over 65 is increasing (currently 19% in the UK). Causes: falling birth rates, longer life expectancy. Implications: increased pension and healthcare costs, a smaller working-age population supporting more dependants, but also positive contributions (grandparents providing childcare, voluntary work).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain why the divorce rate has increased in the UK since the 1960s.

Q2: Describe the demographic changes affecting UK families and assess their social implications.

Q3: Evaluate whether the decline of marriage is a problem for society.

✅ Answers

  1. Divorce increased because: (1) legal changes - 1969 Divorce Reform Act made divorce easier; (2) secularisation - less religious opposition; (3) less stigma - divorce is now socially acceptable; (4) women's economic independence - women can afford to leave unhappy marriages; (5) rising expectations - Giddens' confluent love means people expect personal fulfilment; (6) media normalisation - divorce is common in media portrayals.
  2. Key changes: falling birth rate (1.6 children per woman, below replacement), rising life expectancy (81 years), ageing population (19% over 65), declining marriage, rising cohabitation and divorce. Implications: smaller families mean more individual attention but also fewer siblings; ageing population creates pressure on pensions and healthcare; declining marriage reflects individualism but may reduce stability.
  3. New Right: yes - declining marriage causes family breakdown, fatherless families, and social problems. Feminists: no - declining marriage reflects women's liberation from patriarchal institutions. Postmodernists: no - people are exercising choice and creating diverse relationships. Evaluation: marriage decline is only a problem if it leads to negative outcomes; evidence is mixed - many cohabiting and lone-parent families function well.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Divorce rate increased: 1969 Act + secularisation + less stigma + women's independence + higher expectations.
  • UK fertility rate = 1.6 (below replacement 2.1). Ageing population = 19% over 65. Implications: pensions, healthcare, dependency ratio.
  • Declining marriage: New Right = problem (instability). Feminism = progress (liberation). Postmodernism = choice (diversity).

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing demographic trends, always use specific statistics (divorce rate, fertility rate, life expectancy) and explain the social causes AND consequences of changes. Link to perspectives for evaluation.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The increase in divorce shows that families are breaking down and society is in decline. Correct: While the divorce rate has increased, this does not necessarily mean families are 'breaking down': many divorced people remarry or form new stable relationships; the increase partly reflects legal changes (1969 Act) making divorce accessible; rising divorce may reflect higher expectations of relationships rather than declining commitment; and the vast majority of children still live with at least one parent. Divorce rates have actually fallen slightly since 2004, suggesting the trend may be stabilising.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the impact of changing patterns of marriage and divorce on society.

A grade 9 response will: describe changes (declining marriage, rising divorce and cohabitation) with statistics; explain causes (legal changes, secularisation, women's independence, rising expectations); evaluate impacts on individuals (freedom vs instability), children (most cope well but some struggle), and society (diversity vs decline debate); use perspectives (New Right, Feminism, Postmodernism); conclude: changing patterns reflect greater individual freedom and equality rather than social decline, but adequate support systems (childcare, welfare, legal protection for cohabiting couples) are needed to ensure all family forms can thrive.
