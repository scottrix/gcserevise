The Role of the Family in Society

Sociological perspectives on the family: Functionalist, Marxist, Feminist and Interactionist views of what the family does for society and individuals.

Key Fact: Functionalist view: the family serves essential functions for society. Murdock (1949): the nuclear family is universal and performs four functions - sexual (regulates sexual behaviour), reproductive (produces next generation), economic (pools resources), educational (socialises children). Parsons: the family serves two 'irreducible' functions - primary socialisation of children and stabilisation of adult personalities (warm bath theory).
Key Fact: Marxist view: the family serves capitalism, not society as a whole. Zaretsky (1976): the family consumes capitalist products (TVs, cars, processed food) and provides unpaid domestic labour (women's housework reproduces the next generation of workers at no cost to the state). The family is a 'unit of consumption' and an 'ideological conditioning device' that supports capitalism.
Key Fact: Feminist view: the family is patriarchal and oppresses women. Delphy and Leonard: women do most unpaid domestic labour, enabling men to work while the family benefits men economically. Radical feminists: the family is the root of patriarchy - domestic violence, sexual violence and emotional abuse are normalised within families. Firestone: the family must be abolished to achieve gender equality.
Key Fact: Interactionist view: the family is created through everyday interactions. Berger and Kellner: marriage involves a 'negotiated reality' where couples construct shared meanings and roles through daily interaction. The family is not a fixed structure but an ongoing accomplishment.
Key Fact: Personal Life perspective (Smart): we should understand families from the perspective of those involved, not impose theoretical frameworks. People define their own families (including pets, friends as 'family'). This perspective values lived experience over abstract theory.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Compare Functionalist and Marxist views of the role of the family.

Q2: Evaluate the Feminist view that the family oppresses women.

Q3: Assess which perspective provides the most useful understanding of the family in modern society.

✅ Answers

  1. Functionalism: the family benefits society (socialisation, emotional support). Marxism: the family benefits capitalism (producing workers, consuming products, ideological control). Key difference: Functionalism sees the family as serving everyone; Marxism sees it as serving the ruling class. Functionalism ignores exploitation; Marxism ignores the positive emotional aspects of family life.
  2. Feminist evidence: women do most housework (dual burden); domestic violence affects 1 in 4 women; the family normalises women's economic dependence; the divorce rate shows women want to escape oppressive marriages. Counter-arguments: many women choose family life; family structures are becoming more equal (symmetrical family); men also experience domestic violence; not all families are patriarchal. Conclusion: the family CAN oppress women but this is not inevitable - changing gender roles are creating more equal families.
  3. Functionalism: explains social order but ignores conflict and exploitation. Marxism: explains exploitation but ignores the emotional value of family. Feminism: explains gender inequality but may neglect other inequalities and positive family experiences. Personal Life perspective: values lived experience but lacks critical analysis. Conclusion: no single perspective is sufficient - each reveals different aspects. A multi-perspective approach combining structural analysis with lived experience is most useful.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Functionalism: family benefits EVERYONE (functions). Marxism: family benefits CAPITALISM (exploitation). Feminism: family benefits MEN (patriarchy).
  • Murdock: 4 functions (sexual, reproductive, economic, educational). Parsons: 2 functions (socialisation, stabilisation).
  • Zaretsky: family = unit of consumption. Delphy & Leonard: family = patriarchal exploitation. Smart: ask people what family means to THEM.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When evaluating the family, always: compare at least two perspectives, use named sociologists, provide evidence for and against each view, and consider whether the family serves different functions for different groups (men vs women, rich vs poor).

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The nuclear family benefits everyone equally by performing essential functions for society. Correct: While Functionalists argue the nuclear family serves universal functions, this ignores: who benefits and who loses. Women do most domestic labour (Oakley), the family consumes capitalist products (Zaretsky), and domestic violence is hidden behind the 'private' family (feminists). The nuclear family may serve functions for society but these functions are not equally distributed - men, capitalists and the state may benefit more than women and children.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that the family is a beneficial institution for society.

A grade 9 response will: present Functionalist evidence (Murdock's four functions, Parsons' socialisation and stabilisation); present Marxist critique (family serves capitalism not people - Zaretsky); present Feminist critique (family oppresses women - Delphy and Leonard, domestic violence); evaluate whether benefits are equally distributed; conclude: the family IS beneficial in providing socialisation and emotional support, but these benefits are unequally distributed. The family serves men better than women, and capitalism better than workers. Making families more equal requires challenging patriarchal and capitalist structures within the family.
