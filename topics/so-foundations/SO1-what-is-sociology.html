SO1: What Is Sociology?

Foundation Higher AQA 8192, OCR J205

What Is Sociology?

The study of society: how sociology differs from common sense, the sociological imagination, and how social structures and processes shape individual behaviour and life chances.

Key Fact: Sociology is the systematic study of society, social institutions, and social relationships. It investigates how society shapes human behaviour, attitudes and life chances. C. Wright Mills called this the 'sociological imagination' - seeing the link between personal troubles and public issues.
Key Fact: Sociology vs common sense: common sense is based on everyday assumptions; sociology uses systematic research methods and evidence. Example: common sense says crime is rising; official statistics show it has fallen since the 1990s. Sociology tests claims against evidence.
Key Fact: Social structures are the organised patterns of relationships and institutions that shape society: family, education, religion, economy, government. They constrain individual behaviour but are also maintained by human action (structure vs agency debate).
Key Fact: Key perspectives overview: Functionalism (society as a system of interdependent parts), Marxism (society shaped by class conflict over economic resources), Feminism (society shaped by gender inequality), Interactionism (society created through everyday social interactions).
Key Fact: The sociological imagination links 'personal troubles' (individual problems like unemployment) to 'public issues' (social problems like economic recession). One person losing their job is a personal trouble; mass unemployment is a public issue requiring sociological explanation.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between sociology and common sense, using examples.

Q2: Describe what C. Wright Mills meant by the sociological imagination.

Q3: Evaluate whether individual behaviour is shaped more by social structures or personal agency.

✅ Answers

  1. Sociology uses systematic research and evidence; common sense relies on untested assumptions. Example: common sense says 'criminals are born'; sociology shows crime is influenced by poverty, socialisation and labelling. Sociology challenges taken-for-granted assumptions.
  2. The sociological imagination sees the connection between personal experiences and wider social forces. Example: divorce may feel like a personal failure, but rising divorce rates reflect social changes (changing gender roles, easier divorce laws, declining stigma). Mills argued we need this imagination to understand society.
  3. Structure: social institutions (family, education, economy) shape our choices - working-class children are less likely to attend university. Agency: individuals make choices within constraints - some working-class children do succeed. Evaluation: structuration theory (Giddens) argues structure and agency are two sides of the same coin - structures enable and constrain agency; agency reproduces and transforms structures.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Sociology = systematic, evidence-based study. Common sense = untested everyday assumptions.
  • Personal troubles + public issues = sociological imagination (C. Wright Mills).
  • Functionalism = consensus. Marxism = conflict. Feminism = gender inequality. Interactionism = meaning-making.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When writing sociology essays, always: define key terms, use sociological perspectives (Functionalism, Marxism, Feminism, Interactionism), support arguments with evidence and named studies, and evaluate strengths and limitations of each perspective.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Sociology is just common sense because anyone can understand how society works. Correct: While sociology studies everyday life, it goes beyond common sense by: using systematic research methods (not just assumptions), testing ideas against evidence, revealing patterns that are not obvious (e.g. hidden curricula in schools), and challenging taken-for-granted beliefs. Common sense told us the Earth was flat; systematic investigation proved otherwise. Similarly, sociology reveals social patterns that common sense misses.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that sociology is no different from common sense.

A grade 9 response will: define sociology and common sense; argue they overlap (both make claims about society); argue they differ fundamentally (sociology uses evidence, challenges assumptions, reveals hidden patterns); give examples where common sense is wrong (crime rates, educational achievement, gender roles); conclude: while sociology and common sense both describe society, sociology is fundamentally different because it is systematic, evidence-based, and challenges assumptions that common sense accepts uncritically.
