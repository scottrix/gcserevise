Key Sociological Perspectives

The main sociological perspectives: Functionalism, Marxism, Feminism, Interactionism, and Postmodernism. Their key ideas, thinkers and how they explain society differently.

Key Fact: Functionalism (Durkheim, Parsons): society is a system of interdependent parts working together to maintain stability. Each institution has a function: family socialises children, education teaches skills, economy provides material needs. Society is based on value consensus (shared norms and values).
Key Fact: Marxism (Marx, Engels): society is divided by class conflict between the bourgeoisie (owners of production) and proletariat (workers). The ruling class controls the economy and ideology (institutions like education and media maintain ruling class dominance). Capitalism exploits workers and creates false consciousness (workers accept a system that exploits them).
Key Fact: Feminism (Oakley, Barrett): society is patriarchal (male-dominated). Liberal feminism seeks reform through legislation. Marxist feminism sees patriarchy and capitalism as intertwined. Radical feminism sees patriarchy as the primary form of oppression, present in all institutions. Difference feminism recognises different women experience oppression differently.
Key Fact: Interactionism (Becker, Goffman): society is created through everyday social interactions and meanings. Labelling theory: people are labelled and act according to those labels (Becker: 'deviance is not a quality of the act but of the response of others'). Goffman's dramaturgical model: social life is like a performance on a stage.
Key Fact: Postmodernism (Lyotard, Baudrillard): there is no single truth or grand narrative. Society is diverse, fragmented and constantly changing. Media and technology create hyperreality (simulations that replace reality). Traditional perspectives are outdated because society has changed beyond what they can explain.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Compare Functionalism and Marxism as sociological perspectives.

Q2: Explain how Feminism challenges traditional sociological perspectives.

Q3: Evaluate whether Interactionism provides a more valid view of society than Functionalism.

✅ Answers

  1. Functionalism: consensus-based, society as a system, institutions serve functions, stability is normal. Marxism: conflict-based, society divided by class, institutions serve ruling class, conflict is normal. Functionalism emphasises agreement; Marxism emphasises exploitation. Both are structural (focus on large-scale social structures) but reach opposite conclusions about whether society works harmoniously or exploitatively.
  2. Feminism challenges Functionalism (shows institutions like family serve men, not everyone), Marxism (shows class is not the only inequality - gender matters too), and Interactionism (micro-level interactions reflect patriarchal power, not just individual meanings). Feminism has transformed sociology by making gender a central category of analysis, not an afterthought.
  3. Interactionism explains how individuals create society through everyday interactions (valid for understanding labelling, identity, roles). Functionalism explains large-scale social patterns (valid for understanding institutional functions). Interactionism strength: accounts for individual experience. Weakness: cannot explain large-scale structures. Functionalism strength: explains social order. Weakness: ignores conflict and individual agency. Both are needed for a complete picture.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Functionalism = CONSENSUS (society works together). Marxism = CONFLICT (society is exploitative).
  • Marxism: bourgeoisie vs proletariat, false consciousness, ideological control. Feminism: patriarchy, gender inequality across institutions.
  • Interactionism = micro (individual interactions). Functionalism/Marxism = macro (social structures). Both levels are needed.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When comparing perspectives, always: identify what they agree/disagree about, use specific thinkers and concepts, evaluate using evidence, and consider whether perspectives complement or contradict each other.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Functionalism is no longer relevant because it cannot explain conflict and change in modern society. Correct: While Functionalism has been criticised for ignoring conflict, it still provides useful insights: institutions DO have functions (education DOES socialise and teach skills); value consensus DOES exist (shared norms enable cooperation); and social order IS a real phenomenon that needs explaining. The best approach is to combine Functionalism's insights about social order with Marxism's insights about conflict and Feminism's insights about gender inequality.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the usefulness of sociological perspectives for understanding modern society.

A grade 9 response will: outline each perspective with key concepts and thinkers; evaluate Functionalism (explains order but ignores conflict); evaluate Marxism (explains inequality but overemphasises class); evaluate Feminism (highlights gender but may neglect other inequalities); evaluate Interactionism (explains micro-level but misses macro structures); conclude: no single perspective fully explains society - each reveals different aspects. A multi-perspective approach combining insights from all perspectives provides the most comprehensive understanding.
