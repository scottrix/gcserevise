Key Concepts in Sociology

Essential sociological concepts: socialisation, norms, values, roles, status, culture, identity, and how these shape behaviour and social order.

Key Fact: Socialisation is the process through which individuals learn the norms, values and culture of their society. Primary socialisation (0-5 years): family teaches basic norms, language, and values. Secondary socialisation (5+ years): school, peers, media and religion reinforce and extend social learning.
Key Fact: Norms are social rules that guide behaviour (e.g. queuing, shaking hands). Values are broad beliefs about what is good and desirable (e.g. democracy, equality, achievement). Norms are specific; values are general. Norms enforce values through sanctions (positive: praise; negative: disapproval).
Key Fact: Status: a social position within a group. Ascribed status: given at birth (gender, ethnicity, royal family). Achieved status: earned through effort (qualifications, career). Role: the behaviour expected of someone with a particular status (teachers are expected to teach, mark work, and maintain discipline).
Key Fact: Culture: the way of life of a particular society, including norms, values, language, beliefs and material objects. Subculture: a smaller group within society with distinct norms and values (e.g. youth subcultures, religious groups). Cultural diversity: different cultures coexisting within one society.
Key Fact: Identity: how we see ourselves and how others see us. Social identity: based on group membership (gender, ethnicity, class). Personal identity: based on individual characteristics. Identity is shaped by socialisation, social structures and personal agency.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between primary and secondary socialisation, with examples.

Q2: Describe the relationship between norms, values and sanctions.

Q3: Evaluate whether socialisation determines our identity or whether we have free choice.

✅ Answers

  1. Primary socialisation: family teaches basic norms (saying please/thank you), language, right/wrong, and core values (honesty, respect). Secondary socialisation: school teaches punctuality, obedience and competition (the hidden curriculum); peers teach group norms and identity; media teaches values and consumer behaviour; religion teaches moral codes.
  2. Values are broad beliefs (e.g. 'education is important'). Norms are specific rules that put values into practice (e.g. 'attend school every day', 'complete homework'). Sanctions enforce norms: positive sanctions reward conformity (praise for good work), negative sanctions punish deviance (detention for being late). Formal sanctions are official (laws, school rules); informal sanctions are unofficial (gossip, dirty looks).
  3. Socialisation shapes identity: gender socialisation teaches 'masculine' and 'feminine' behaviour; class socialisation teaches aspirations and tastes; ethnic socialisation teaches cultural traditions. However, individuals also have agency: they can accept, modify or reject socialised norms (e.g. choosing non-traditional gender roles). Evaluation: socialisation is powerful but not deterministic - people resist and reinterpret social messages.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Primary = family (0-5). Secondary = school, peers, media (5+). Both shape identity.
  • Values = broad beliefs. Norms = specific rules. Sanctions = rewards/punishments that enforce norms.
  • Ascribed status = given (birth). Achieved status = earned (effort). Modern societies value achieved status.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing socialisation, always: distinguish primary from secondary, name specific agents (family, school, peers, media), and give concrete examples of norms and values learned through each agent.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Socialisation completely determines our behaviour, leaving no room for free will. Correct: While socialisation strongly influences behaviour, individuals are not passive recipients. People can: resist socialisation (rebellious teenagers reject parental values), reinterpret norms (feminists challenge traditional gender roles), and choose between competing social messages (different agents teach different values). The structure-agency debate shows that socialisation sets patterns but individuals have the capacity to make choices within those constraints.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the importance of socialisation in shaping human behaviour.

A grade 9 response will: define socialisation and distinguish primary from secondary; describe key agents (family, school, peers, media) with examples of norms/values learned; argue socialisation is crucial (feral children show what happens without it); argue agency matters (people resist and reinterpret socialisation); conclude: socialisation provides the framework within which identity develops, but individuals actively construct their identity within this framework. Neither socialisation alone nor individual choice alone explains human behaviour.
