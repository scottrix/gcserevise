SO5: Types of Families & Households

Foundation Higher AQA 8192, OCR J205

Types of Families & Households

Different family types: nuclear, extended, reconstituted, lone parent, same-sex. How family structures have changed and the sociological explanations for changing family patterns.

Key Fact: Nuclear family: two generations (parents and children) living together. Murdock argued the nuclear family is universal and performs four essential functions: sexual, reproductive, economic and educational. Parsons called it the 'isolated nuclear family' - geographically and socially mobile for modern industrial society.
Key Fact: Extended family: three or more generations living together or nearby. Vertical extended: grandparents, parents, children. Horizontal extended: aunts, uncles, cousins. Common in pre-industrial and many non-Western societies; declining in the UK due to geographical mobility.
Key Fact: Reconstituted/step families: formed when adults with children from previous relationships form new partnerships. About 10% of UK families. Challenges: divided loyalties, relationships with absent parent, new family roles and boundaries.
Key Fact: Lone-parent families: one parent living with children. About 25% of UK families with children. 90% headed by women. Causes: divorce, separation, choice. Murray (New Right) argues lone parenthood creates welfare dependency; feminists argue it reflects women's increasing independence.
Key Fact: Same-sex families: couples of the same sex, with or without children. Increasingly recognised since civil partnerships (2005) and same-sex marriage (2014). Weeks argues same-sex families create 'families of choice' based on commitment rather than biology. Family diversity is now the norm rather than the exception.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe different types of families and explain how family structures have changed in the UK.

Q2: Evaluate the view that the nuclear family is the best family type for society.

Q3: Assess the causes and consequences of increasing family diversity.

✅ Answers

  1. Family types: nuclear (two-generation, most common), extended (multi-generation, declining), reconstituted (step-families, 10%), lone-parent (25%, mostly female-headed), same-sex (increasing since 2014 marriage equality). Changes: decline of nuclear, rise of cohabitation, more lone-parent and same-sex families, increase in childless couples, later marriage and childbearing.
  2. Functionalists argue the nuclear family is best: it performs essential functions (Murdock: sexual, reproductive, economic, educational) and is suited to industrial society (Parsons: geographically mobile). However: this ignores family diversity, assumes all nuclear families are functional (domestic violence, abuse), and pathologises other family types. Feminists argue the nuclear family oppresses women (unpaid domestic labour). Modern society accepts diverse family forms.
  3. Causes of diversity: secularisation (less religious influence on family life), changing gender roles (women's economic independence), easier divorce (1969 Divorce Reform Act), contraception (decoupling sex from reproduction), welfare state (financial support for lone parents). Consequences: greater individual freedom but also potential instability. New Right sees diversity as causing social problems; postmodernists celebrate diversity as choice.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Murdock: nuclear family is UNIVERSAL (4 functions: sexual, reproductive, economic, educational). Parsons: nuclear family suits INDUSTRIAL society.
  • Nuclear = 2 generations. Extended = 3+ generations. Reconstituted = step-family. Lone-parent = 1 parent. Same-sex = same-gender couple.
  • Family diversity is now the NORM. The nuclear family is no longer the only or even the majority family type.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing family types, always: define each type clearly, use statistics (25% lone-parent, 10% step), name specific sociologists (Murdock, Parsons, Weeks), and evaluate whether the nuclear family is still the 'norm' or just one type among many.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The nuclear family is the normal and best type of family for all societies. Correct: While the nuclear family has been the most common type in the UK, family diversity is now the norm: 25% of families with children are lone-parent, cohabitation has increased, same-sex families are legally recognised, and many people choose to live alone. The idea that the nuclear family is 'best' reflects a normative bias (New Right) rather than evidence. Different family types work well for different people - the quality of relationships matters more than the structure.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that family diversity has had a positive impact on society.

A grade 9 response will: define family diversity and describe changes (decline of nuclear, rise of cohabitation, lone-parent, same-sex); present positive impacts (individual freedom, women's independence, acceptance of difference, reduced stigma); present negative impacts (New Right: instability, welfare dependency, fatherless families); evaluate using evidence (most children in lone-parent families do well; diversity reflects choice not decline); conclude: family diversity is largely positive - it reflects greater individual freedom and equality, and there is no evidence that non-nuclear families are inherently worse. The quality of relationships matters more than family structure.
