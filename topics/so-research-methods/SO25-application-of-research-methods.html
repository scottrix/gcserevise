Application of Research Methods

Choosing methods for specific contexts: studying families, education, crime and stratification. Practical, ethical and theoretical considerations.

Key Fact: Studying families: sensitive topics (divorce, domestic violence). Questionnaires miss depth; interviews capture experiences but may distress; observations are intrusive. Official statistics provide marriage/divorce rates.
Key Fact: Studying education: access requires permission (gatekeepers). Questionnaires survey many quickly; interviews explore experiences; observations see classroom dynamics (Hawthorne effect); statistics show exam results; documents include Ofsted reports.
Key Fact: Studying crime: victim surveys capture unreported crime; self-report studies reveal hidden offending; observations study deviant subcultures (ethical issues); statistics are readily available but distorted; field experiments test discrimination.
Key Fact: Choosing methods: practical (time, money, access), ethical (consent, harm), and theoretical considerations (reliability vs validity, positivism vs interpretivism).
Key Fact: Triangulation: using multiple methods increases validity. Example: studying educational inequality by combining statistics (patterns), interviews (experiences), and observations (processes).

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Application of Research Methods, you must know:

  • Gatekeepers
  • Positivism
  • Interpretivism
  • Triangulation
  • Hawthorne effect
  • Mixed methods

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: A researcher wants to study the impact of divorce on children. Evaluate which method would be most appropriate.

Q2: Compare the practical and ethical issues of researching crime vs education.

Q3: Evaluate whether triangulation always produces better research than a single method.

✅ Answers

  1. Semi-structured interviews: allow children to express experiences, follow up on sensitive topics, build rapport. Ethical issues: consent from parents AND children, protection from distress.
  2. Crime: greater ethical issues (risk, illegal activity, deception). Education: access challenges (gatekeepers, parental consent). Both require sensitivity but crime faces greater ethical dilemmas.
  3. Triangulation strengths: overcomes individual method limitations, increases validity, provides complete picture. Weaknesses: more time/cost, may produce contradictory findings. Conclusion: generally preferable but not always practical.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Choose methods based on: PRACTICAL, ETHICAL, THEORETICAL factors.
  • Positivism = quantitative (reliable). Interpretivism = qualitative (meaningful). Both contribute.
  • Triangulation = combine methods. Quantitative shows WHAT, qualitative explains WHY.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When applying research methods, always: consider the specific research question, weigh practical/ethical/theoretical factors, and justify your choice.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The best research method is always the one that produces the most reliable and valid data. Correct: The 'best' method depends on constraints: time, budget, access, ethical concerns, and the research question. Reliability and validity are important but not the only considerations. A good researcher works within constraints.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the factors sociologists should consider when choosing a research method.

A grade 9 response will: describe practical, ethical and theoretical factors; apply to a specific context; evaluate trade-offs; conclude: no single factor determines choice - sociologists must balance all three. Triangulation provides the strongest evidence where practical.
