Application of Research Methods

Choosing methods for specific contexts: studying families, education, crime and stratification. Practical, ethical and theoretical considerations.

Key Fact: Studying families: sensitive topics (divorce, domestic violence). Questionnaires miss depth; interviews capture experiences but may distress; observations are intrusive. Official statistics provide marriage/divorce rates.
Key Fact: Studying education: access requires permission (gatekeepers). Questionnaires survey many quickly; interviews explore experiences; observations see classroom dynamics (Hawthorne effect); statistics show exam results; documents include Ofsted reports.
Key Fact: Studying crime: victim surveys capture unreported crime; self-report studies reveal hidden offending; observations study deviant subcultures (ethical issues); statistics are readily available but distorted; field experiments test discrimination.
Key Fact: Choosing methods: practical (time, money, access), ethical (consent, harm), and theoretical considerations (reliability vs validity, positivism vs interpretivism).
Key Fact: Triangulation: using multiple methods increases validity. Example: studying educational inequality by combining statistics (patterns), interviews (experiences), and observations (processes).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: A researcher wants to study the impact of divorce on children. Evaluate which method would be most appropriate.

Q2: Compare the practical and ethical issues of researching crime vs education.

Q3: Evaluate whether triangulation always produces better research than a single method.

✅ Answers

  1. Semi-structured interviews: allow children to express experiences, follow up on sensitive topics, build rapport. Ethical issues: consent from parents AND children, protection from distress.
  2. Crime: greater ethical issues (risk, illegal activity, deception). Education: access challenges (gatekeepers, parental consent). Both require sensitivity but crime faces greater ethical dilemmas.
  3. Triangulation strengths: overcomes individual method limitations, increases validity, provides complete picture. Weaknesses: more time/cost, may produce contradictory findings. Conclusion: generally preferable but not always practical.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Choose methods based on: PRACTICAL, ETHICAL, THEORETICAL factors.
  • Positivism = quantitative (reliable). Interpretivism = qualitative (meaningful). Both contribute.
  • Triangulation = combine methods. Quantitative shows WHAT, qualitative explains WHY.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When applying research methods, always: consider the specific research question, weigh practical/ethical/theoretical factors, and justify your choice.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: The best research method is always the one that produces the most reliable and valid data. Correct: The 'best' method depends on constraints: time, budget, access, ethical concerns, and the research question. Reliability and validity are important but not the only considerations. A good researcher works within constraints.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the factors sociologists should consider when choosing a research method.

A grade 9 response will: describe practical, ethical and theoretical factors; apply to a specific context; evaluate trade-offs; conclude: no single factor determines choice - sociologists must balance all three. Triangulation provides the strongest evidence where practical.

📖 Detailed Notes: Application of Research Methods

💡 Applying Methods to Education Research

When researching educational achievement, sociologists must choose methods that suit their research question and theoretical perspective. Positivist researchers studying patterns of achievement use official statistics from the Department for Education (DfE), which show that in 2023, 49.9% of pupils on free school meals achieved five good GCSEs compared to 72.6% of other pupils — a substantial attainment gap. Questionnaires can survey large numbers of students about their study habits, aspirations and home backgrounds. However, understanding why working-class students underachieve requires qualitative methods. Interviews with students can reveal how cultural capital (Bourdieu) affects their educational experience — middle-class students may feel more confident approaching teachers, while working-class students may feel that school is ‘not for them’.

Observation in schools can reveal the hidden processes that affect achievement. Rosenthal and Jacobson’s study demonstrated the self-fulfilling prophecy: teachers who were told (falsely) that certain students were ‘about to bloom’ treated them differently, and those students subsequently made greater progress. In the UK, Gillborn and Youdell found that teachers used the ‘A-to-C economy’ to ration their time, concentrating on borderline students at the expense of those predicted to fail — disproportionately affecting working-class and ethnic minority students. Ethnographic studies using participant observation in classrooms can reveal these processes in action, but access to schools is often difficult to obtain and the researcher’s presence may alter behaviour.

UK Context: A UK researcher studying why boys underachieve compared to girls at GCSE might combine DfE statistics (showing the gender gap), questionnaires (surveying attitudes to schoolwork across 1,000 students), interviews (exploring 20 boys’ experiences in depth), and classroom observation (watching how teachers interact with male and female students). This triangulation would provide both the statistical picture and the underlying explanations.

🔍 Applying Methods to Crime and Deviance Research

Researching crime presents unique methodological challenges because criminals are unlikely to cooperate with researchers, and much crime is hidden. The Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW) uses structured interviews with approximately 35,000 adults to estimate the true level of crime, including unreported offences. It reveals that only about 40% of crimes are reported to the police, meaning official statistics significantly underestimate crime. However, the CSEW has limitations: it excludes commercial crime, sexual offences were only added recently, and it relies on respondents’ honesty about their own criminal behaviour. Self-report studies ask people to confess to crimes they have committed, revealing that many people have broken the law without being caught. These studies suggest that crime is far more widespread than official statistics indicate, and that the typical criminal is not very different from the typical non-criminal.

Ethnographic research into crime and deviance often uses participant observation to access criminal subcultures. Patrick’s covert study of a Glasgow gang in the 1970s provided vivid insight into gang life, including violence, territorialism and the code of loyalty that governed members’ behaviour. However, such research raises serious ethical questions about deception and the risk of harm. More recently, cultural criminologists such as Ferrell have used auto-ethnography — researching their own experiences of deviant subcultures. Quantitative analysis of official statistics remains important for identifying patterns: ONS data shows that men are far more likely to commit violent crime than women, that young people commit more crime than older people, and that people from deprived areas have higher rates of both offending and victimisation.

UK Context: The 2023 CSEW showed that there were approximately 8.6 million incidents of crime against adults in England and Wales, but only 4.9 million offences were recorded by police. This ‘dark figure’ of unreported crime means that police statistics give an incomplete picture. The CSEW also reveals that some groups — particularly young men in deprived urban areas — are far more likely to be victims of crime than official statistics suggest.

⚖️ Practical, Ethical and Theoretical Choices in Applied Research

When applying research methods, sociologists must weigh practical, ethical and theoretical considerations. Practical issues include time, money, access and the characteristics of the target group. Researching powerful groups (e.g. corporate executives) is practically difficult because they can refuse access, while researching vulnerable groups (e.g. homeless people) requires careful ethical safeguards. Longitudinal studies are expensive and time-consuming but provide invaluable data on social change — the British Cohort Study has tracked people born in 1970 throughout their lives, revealing how childhood circumstances affect adult outcomes. Ethical issues constrain what researchers can do: deception must be minimised, vulnerable participants protected, and confidentiality maintained. Theoretical preferences shape method choice: positivists favour methods that produce reliable, generalisable data (surveys, experiments), while interpretivists favour methods that produce valid, in-depth data (interviews, observations).

Mixed methods research is increasingly common because it overcomes the limitations of single methods. The Education Endowment Foundation (EEF) in the UK uses randomised controlled trials (a quantitative method) to test whether educational interventions improve attainment, combined with qualitative process evaluation (interviews and observations) to understand how and why the intervention works or fails. This approach provides both the statistical evidence of effectiveness that policymakers need and the rich understanding of context that practitioners value. However, mixed methods research is complex and resource-intensive, requiring expertise in both quantitative and qualitative analysis.

UK Context: The EEF’s trial of a ‘philosophy for children’ programme used a randomised controlled trial with 3,000 pupils across 40 schools (quantitative) alongside observations of philosophy sessions and interviews with teachers and students (qualitative). The quantitative data showed the programme improved reading and maths scores, while the qualitative data explained that it developed children’s confidence in articulating ideas — insights the statistics alone could not provide.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain how a sociologist could use both quantitative and qualitative methods to investigate why some ethnic groups achieve lower GCSE results than others in the UK.

Q: Evaluate the strengths and limitations of using official statistics to study crime in the UK, comparing them with the Crime Survey for England and Wales.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. A sociologist could start by analysing DfE statistics to identify the attainment gap — for example, in 2023, only 38% of pupils from a Black Caribbean background achieved a strong pass in English and Maths, compared to 56% of white British pupils and 72% of Chinese pupils. This quantitative data reveals the pattern but not the causes. Qualitative interviews with 20–30 students from underachieving groups could explore their experiences: teacher expectations, peer pressure, cultural capital, and racism in school. Classroom observation could reveal how teachers interact differently with students from different ethnic backgrounds. Combining these methods (triangulation) provides both the statistical evidence of inequality and the lived experience that explains it, producing findings that are both reliable and valid.
  2. Official police statistics are reliable because they are collected using consistent procedures and are available annually for every police force area. They allow comparisons over time and between areas. However, they significantly underestimate crime because many offences go unreported (the dark figure of crime) and some reported crimes are not recorded. The CSEW addresses this by interviewing approximately 35,000 people about their experiences, capturing crimes not reported to police. The CSEW estimates that only about 40% of crime is reported. However, the CSEW has its own limitations: it excludes commercial crime, homicides, and crimes against under-16s; respondents may forget or conceal offences; and it cannot locate the geographical hotspots that police data can. A balanced view is that both sources are needed to build a complete picture of crime in the UK.
