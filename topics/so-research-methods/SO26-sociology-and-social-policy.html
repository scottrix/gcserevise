SO26: Sociology & Social Policy

Foundation Higher AQA 8192, OCR J205

How sociology influences social policy: evidence-based policy, the relationship between research and government policy, and debates about value-free sociology.

Sociology & Social Policy

How sociology influences social policy: evidence-based policy, the relationship between research and government policy, and debates about value-free sociology.

Key Fact: Social policy: government actions addressing social problems (education, welfare, crime, health). Sociology influences policy through evidence, evaluation and advocacy.
Key Fact: Evidence-based policy: using research evidence to design policies. Example: Sure Start was based on research showing early intervention improves outcomes. However, policies are also influenced by political ideology, economics and public opinion.
Key Fact: Positivist view: sociology should provide objective evidence for rational policymaking. Interpretivist view: sociology should give voice to lived experience to shape humane policy. Both contribute.
Key Fact: Value-free vs value-laden: Weber argued sociology should be objective, but values inevitably influence topic choice. Marx: the point is to change society. Modern view: complete value-freedom is impossible; transparency about values is essential.
Key Fact: Examples of sociology influencing policy: Beveridge's research led to the welfare state; Macpherson Report led to police reforms; Wilkinson and Pickett's inequality research influenced minimum wage debates.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain how sociological research can influence social policy, giving examples.

Q2: Compare positivist and interpretivist views on sociology and social policy.

Q3: Evaluate whether sociology should aim to influence social policy or remain value-free.

✅ Answers

  1. Sociology influences policy through: evidence (identifying problems), evaluation (assessing effectiveness), advocacy (campaigning for change). Examples: Beveridge (welfare state), Macpherson (police reform), inequality research (minimum wage).
  2. Positivists: provide objective evidence for rational policy. Interpretivists: give voice to lived experience. Both contribute: quantitative identifies problems; qualitative shapes solutions.
  3. Value-free: objectivity gives credibility; value-laden: all research is influenced by values anyway; sociology should pursue social justice. Conclusion: transparency about values is essential; research AND advocacy are not mutually exclusive.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Sociology -> evidence -> policy. Examples: Beveridge, Macpherson, inequality research.
  • Positivist = objective evidence. Interpretivist = lived experience. Both needed.
  • Value-free = credibility. Value-laden = honesty. TRANSPARENCY about values is the key.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing sociology and policy, always: give specific examples, consider political constraints, and evaluate value-freedom.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Sociological research always leads to better social policy because it provides objective evidence. Correct: While evidence can improve policy, barriers exist: political ideology overrides evidence; research may be contradictory; policies are constrained by economics and public opinion; researchers may be biased. Evidence-based policy is an ideal but policy is shaped by evidence, ideology, economics and public opinion.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that sociology should aim to change society rather than just study it.

A grade 9 response will: present case for studying (objectivity, credibility); present case for changing (Marx; research improves lives; value-free is impossible); evaluate: studying and changing are not mutually exclusive - rigorous research IS social change; conclude: sociology's primary role is rigorous research, but sociologists should engage with policy and debate. The distinction is a false dichotomy.

📖 Detailed Notes: Sociology and Social Policy

💡 How Sociology Influences Social Policy

Social policy refers to the plans and actions of government and other organisations that aim to address social problems and improve welfare. Sociological research can influence social policy by identifying problems, explaining their causes and evaluating potential solutions. For example, the Black Report (1980) used sociological evidence to show that health inequalities in the UK were largely caused by social and economic factors rather than individual behaviour. It found that the death rate for men in social class V (unskilled manual) was nearly twice that of men in social class I (professional). The report recommended policies to reduce poverty, improve housing and increase access to healthcare. However, the Thatcher government largely ignored its findings, illustrating that political ideology often determines whether sociological evidence is acted upon.

Different sociological perspectives have different views on the role of social policy. Functionalists argue that social policy should maintain social order and integration — the welfare state supports the smooth running of society. Marxists argue that social policy serves the interests of capitalism, for example by providing just enough welfare to prevent revolution while keeping a low-paid workforce available. Feminists argue that social policy has historically been based on a male breadwinner model, disadvantaging women — for example, the UK pension system was designed for men with continuous employment records, penalising women who took career breaks for childcare. New Right thinkers argue that generous welfare benefits create dependency and undermine the traditional family, pointing to the rise in lone-parent families as evidence. The coalition government’s austerity programme (2010–2015) reflected New Right thinking, cutting benefits to ‘incentivise’ work.

UK Context: The Marmot Review (2010), commissioned by the Labour government, updated the Black Report’s findings and showed that health inequalities had widened despite 30 years of policy interventions. It influenced the creation of Public Health England and policies aimed at reducing health inequalities, including the introduction of the sugar tax on soft drinks in 2018.

🔍 The Welfare State and Contemporary Debates

The UK welfare state was established in 1945 based on the Beveridge Report, which identified five ‘giant evils’: Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor and Idleness. The welfare state provides social security benefits, the NHS, free education, public housing and employment services. Functionalists see the welfare state as essential for social cohesion, providing a safety net that prevents extreme poverty and maintains social stability. The NHS, for example, ensures that healthcare is available to all regardless of income, which functionalists argue benefits society by maintaining a healthy workforce. However, New Right critics argue that the welfare state creates a ‘dependency culture’ where people rely on benefits rather than finding work. They point to the growth in incapacity benefit claims from 0.7 million in 1979 to 2.5 million by 2003 as evidence.

Current debates about welfare focus on the balance between support and incentives. Universal Credit, introduced from 2013, consolidated six working-age benefits into a single payment designed to ‘make work pay’ by tapering benefits gradually as earnings rise. However, critics argue that the five-week wait for initial payment, sanctions regime and single monthly payment have pushed many claimants into hardship and food bank use. The Trussell Trust reported that food bank use tripled between 2015 and 2023, with many referrals coming from people whose benefits had been delayed or sanctioned. Feminist sociologists highlight that Universal Credit is paid to one household member (usually the man), reducing women’s financial independence and potentially increasing their vulnerability to domestic abuse.

UK Context: The Joseph Rowntree Foundation’s annual Monitoring Poverty and Social Exclusion report provides detailed evidence on poverty in the UK. Their 2023 report found that 22% of the UK population lived in relative poverty, including 3.8 million children. This research has influenced policy debates on the two-child benefit cap, benefit levels and the adequacy of Universal Credit.

⚖️ Sociological Perspectives on Social Policy Effectiveness

Evaluating the effectiveness of social policy requires examining whether policies achieve their intended outcomes and whether they have unintended consequences. Sure Start, launched in 1999, was a flagship Labour policy based on sociological evidence that early intervention could reduce inequality. It provided children’s centres in deprived areas offering childcare, health services and parenting support. Evaluations found that Sure Start improved children’s social development and parenting skills in the most disadvantaged areas, but Conservative governments from 2010 cut funding by over 60%, closing more than 1,000 centres. Sociologists argue that this illustrates the political nature of social policy: evidence of effectiveness does not guarantee continued funding.

Education policy has been heavily influenced by sociological research, though not always in ways sociologists would endorse. Marketisation policies (league tables, academies, parental choice) were influenced by the New Right’s critique of comprehensive education as failing to raise standards. Sociologists such as Ball argued that marketisation increased inequality because middle-class parents used their cultural capital to navigate the system, securing places at the best schools for their children. Pupil Premium funding (introduced in 2011) gives schools extra money for each disadvantaged pupil, reflecting sociological evidence about the attainment gap. However, research by the EEF shows that many schools spend Pupil Premium on general school costs rather than targeted interventions for disadvantaged students, reducing its effectiveness.

UK Context: The government’s ‘Bedroom Tax’ (officially the Removal of the Spare Room Subsidy, 2013) reduced housing benefit for social housing tenants deemed to have a spare bedroom. The policy was intended to encourage people to move to smaller properties and reduce the housing benefit bill. Sociological research showed that in many areas, smaller properties were not available, so tenants were forced to cover the shortfall from other benefits, pushing them into poverty. The policy was widely criticised for penalising disabled people who needed a spare room for carers or medical equipment.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Evaluate the view that sociological research has had little real influence on social policy in the UK.

Q: Explain how feminist sociologists have criticised the welfare state and suggest how social policy could better reflect women’s needs.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. On one hand, there are clear examples of sociological research influencing policy: the Black Report and Marmot Review shaped health inequality policy, the Beveridge Report created the welfare state, and EEF research guides education spending. On the other hand, political ideology often overrides evidence — the Thatcher government ignored the Black Report, austerity continued despite evidence of its harm, and the Bedroom Tax was implemented despite research showing it would push people into poverty. Marxists argue that social policy always serves the interests of the powerful, so sociology can only achieve reform within the limits capitalism allows. A balanced view is that sociology influences policy when its findings align with the political priorities of the government in power, but is ignored when it challenges those priorities.
  2. Feminist sociologists argue that the welfare state was designed around a male breadwinner model, assuming women are financially dependent on men. For example, the UK pension system was built on continuous employment records, penalising women who take career breaks for childcare. Universal Credit pays benefits to one household member, usually the man, reducing women’s financial independence and increasing vulnerability to domestic abuse. Policies could better reflect women’s needs by: paying benefits individually rather than to households; providing affordable childcare to enable women to work; recognising caring work in pension calculations; and ensuring that domestic abuse victims can access benefits independently. The Nordic model, with individual taxation and generous childcare provision, is often cited as a more gender-equal approach to welfare policy.
