Social Stratification & Class

How society is stratified: social class, life chances, and how class affects access to resources, power and status.

Key Fact: Social stratification: the hierarchical arrangement of people into social strata based on wealth, income, status and power. In the UK, social class is the primary form of stratification.
Key Fact: Social class systems: the NS-SEC (8 classes from higher managerial to never worked) replaced the Registrar General's scale. Weber: class is based on market situation. Marx: class is based on relationship to means of production.
Key Fact: Life chances: class strongly affects health (working-class die 7-9 years earlier), education (middle-class children achieve higher), income (top 10% earn 12x more than bottom 10%), and housing (overcrowding 5x higher in lowest quintile).
Key Fact: The wealth gap: richest 10% hold 44% of total wealth; poorest 50% hold 9%. This gap has widened since the 1980s. Wilkinson and Pickett: more unequal societies have worse outcomes for everyone.
Key Fact: Poverty: relative poverty (below 60% of median) affects about 22% of the UK population. The poverty trap: people on benefits who work may be little better off due to lost benefits.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain what social stratification is and how social class affects life chances.

Q2: Describe the extent of wealth inequality in the UK and evaluate explanations for it.

Q3: Evaluate whether social class is still relevant in modern society.

✅ Answers

  1. Stratification divides society into layers based on wealth, power and status. Class dramatically affects life chances: health, education, income, housing. These inequalities persist across generations.
  2. Top 10% own 44% of wealth; bottom 50% own 9%. Marxist: capitalism concentrates wealth. Wilkinson and Pickett: inequality harms everyone. Some inequality may incentivise effort, but extreme inequality is harmful.
  3. Class still affects health, education, income, housing. Counter: class identities are weakening; social mobility exists. Conclusion: class is less culturally significant but its material impact remains powerful.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Stratification = structured INEQUALITY. Life chances vary dramatically by class.
  • Wealth gap: top 10% = 44%. Bottom 50% = 9%. WIDENING. Wilkinson & Pickett: inequality harms ALL.
  • Class STILL matters for life chances even if class identity is weakening.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing stratification, always: define the concept, use specific statistics on inequality, and evaluate whether class is still the most important form of stratification.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Social class no longer matters because anyone can succeed through talent and hard work. Correct: While social mobility exists, evidence shows class powerfully affects life chances: working-class people die 7-9 years earlier, achieve lower qualifications, earn less. Only 21% of children from the poorest fifth reach the top income quintile as adults. Talent and effort matter, but they operate within a stratified system that is far from level.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that social class is the most significant factor affecting life chances in modern Britain.

A grade 9 response will: define social class and life chances; present evidence that class affects health, education, income; compare with gender and ethnicity; consider intersectionality; conclude: social class is the most significant single factor but gender and ethnicity interact with class to compound disadvantage.
