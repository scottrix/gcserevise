Power, Authority & the State

How power is distributed: Weber's types of authority, the role of the state, and how power relations affect different social groups.

Key Fact: Power: the ability to make others do what you want. Lukes identified three dimensions: decision-making, agenda-setting, and ideological power.
Key Fact: Weber's three types of authority: Traditional (custom - monarchy), Charismatic (personal qualities - MLK), Rational-legal (rules and laws - elected officials). Most modern authority is rational-legal.
Key Fact: The state: institutions that govern (government, parliament, police, courts). Marxists: the state serves ruling class interests. Functionalists: the state serves collective interests. Pluralists: the state balances competing interests.
Key Fact: Power elites: Mills argued power is concentrated in a small group of corporate leaders, military chiefs and politicians. In the UK, the 'establishment' (Eton, Oxbridge) dominates politics, media and business.
Key Fact: Democracy and power: formal equality through elections but real power is unequal: wealthy fund parties, media owners shape opinion, corporations influence policy, and voter turnout has declined.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe Weber's three types of authority and evaluate their usefulness.

Q2: Compare Marxist and Functionalist views of the state.

Q3: Evaluate whether democracy ensures that power is equally distributed.

✅ Answers

  1. Traditional: custom (monarchy). Charismatic: personality (MLK). Rational-legal: rules (elected officials). Evaluation: useful but some leaders combine types. Charismatic brings change but is unstable; rational-legal is stable but may resist change.
  2. Marxist: state serves ruling class (protects property, maintains capitalist order). Functionalist: state serves everyone (maintains order, provides services). Evidence supports Marxist view but the state also provides welfare that benefits the working class.
  3. Democracy gives formal equality (one vote) but real power is unequal: wealth funds parties, media shapes opinion, lobbyists influence policy. Marxists: democracy is a sham. Pluralists: democracy works through competing groups. Conclusion: some distribution but significant inequalities remain.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Weber's 3 types: Traditional (custom), Charismatic (personality), Rational-legal (rules).
  • Marxist: state = tool of ruling class. Functionalist: state = serves everyone.
  • Democracy = formal equality but REAL power is unequal (money, media, lobbying).

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing power, always: distinguish power from authority, use Weber's typology, and evaluate whether power is democratically distributed.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: In a democracy, power is equally distributed because everyone has one vote. Correct: While everyone has one vote, real political power is very unequal: wealthy donors, media owners, corporate lobbyists, and establishment networks dominate. Lukes' third dimension shows the most powerful influence is shaping what people WANT. Voting equality is necessary but not sufficient for genuine democratic power.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that power in modern Britain is held by a small elite rather than democratically distributed.

A grade 9 response will: present elite theory evidence (Mills, establishment networks); present democratic counter-evidence (suffrage, free press, unions); evaluate: formal democracy exists but is undermined by economic inequality; conclude: real power is disproportionately held by a small elite. Reform could make democracy more genuine.

📖 Detailed Notes: Power and Authority

💡 Weber’s Types of Authority

Max Weber identified three types of authority that give people the right to exercise power over others. Traditional authority is based on custom and long-established practices — for example, the authority of the monarch in the UK, who holds the title of head of state by heredity rather than election. Charismatic authority is based on the personal qualities of a leader who inspires devotion and loyalty — historical examples include Mahatma Gandhi and Martin Luther King, while in the UK, political figures such as Winston Churchill and Tony Blair were often described as charismatic leaders. Legal-rational authority is based on written laws and rules, and on the official position a person holds rather than their personal qualities — for example, a police officer’s authority comes from their role and the law, not from their personality. In modern democratic societies, legal-rational authority is the dominant form: prime ministers, judges and civil servants all derive their authority from the laws and constitution rather than tradition or personal charisma.

Power is the ability to make others do what you want, even against their will. Lukes identified three dimensions of power: the first dimension is visible decision-making (who wins a vote in Parliament); the second dimension is agenda-setting (who decides which issues get discussed — powerful groups may keep controversial issues off the agenda); the third dimension is the manipulation of desires and beliefs (shaping what people think they want, so they do not even realise they are being controlled). For example, Marxist sociologists argue that the media and education system shape working-class people’s beliefs so that they accept capitalism as natural and inevitable, rather than questioning it. Feminist sociologists argue that patriarchy operates through all three dimensions: laws that disadvantage women (first dimension), male-dominated institutions that ignore women’s concerns (second dimension), and socialisation that teaches women to accept subordinate roles (third dimension).

UK Context: In the UK, the King has traditional authority as head of state, but real political power lies with the Prime Minister and Parliament, who exercise legal-rational authority. However, Lukes’ third dimension of power is relevant: the media, advertising and education shape public opinion, meaning that power often operates invisibly. For example, the revolving door between government and major corporations means that business interests significantly influence policy without most citizens being aware.

🔍 Power Relationships in the UK

Power in the UK is distributed unequally across social groups. Economic power is concentrated among the wealthy: the Sunday Times Rich List shows that a small number of individuals and families control vast resources. Political power is concentrated among the elite: despite only 7% of the population attending private school, 74% of judges, 71% of senior military officers and over 50% of the cabinet were privately educated (Sutton Trust). This raises questions about whether the UK is genuinely a democracy or an oligarchy — a society ruled by a small, privileged elite. The concept of the ‘establishment’ refers to the network of people who hold significant power across politics, business, media and the civil service, and who may share similar backgrounds and interests.

Corporate power has grown significantly in recent decades. Multinational corporations such as Amazon, Google and Facebook wield enormous economic and political influence, often operating across national borders in ways that individual governments struggle to regulate. In the UK, corporate lobbying of Parliament is a significant concern: corporations spend millions on lobbying to influence legislation in their favour. The influence of media owners is another dimension of power: Rupert Murdoch’s News International (owner of The Sun and The Times) has influenced UK politics for decades, with both Labour and Conservative leaders courting his support. Feminist sociologists highlight that power remains gendered: in 2023, only 35% of MPs and 31% of FTSE 100 board directors were women, despite women making up over half the population. Ethnic minorities are also underrepresented in positions of power, though this is slowly improving.

UK Context: The 2009 MPs’ expenses scandal revealed how those in power could exploit their positions for personal gain, while the 2021 Greensill scandal showed how former prime minister David Cameron used his political connections to lobby for a finance company, raising questions about the revolving door between government and business.

⚖️ The State and Power

The state exercises power through various mechanisms: the legal system (police, courts, prisons), the education system, the welfare system and the military. Functionalist sociologists see the state as a neutral body that serves the interests of society as a whole by maintaining order and providing essential services. Marxist sociologists see the state as an instrument of the ruling class: laws protect private property and the interests of capital, while the welfare state provides just enough support to prevent revolution. Althusser distinguished between the Repressive State Apparatus (police, army, prisons — which enforce control through force or threat of force) and the Ideological State Apparatus (education, media, religion — which enforce control through ideas and beliefs, making inequality seem natural and inevitable).

In the UK, the relationship between the state and citizens is governed by democratic processes and the rule of law. However, sociologists debate how genuinely democratic this relationship is. The UK is a representative democracy where citizens elect MPs to make decisions on their behalf, but voter turnout has declined — from 77% in 1992 to 60% in 2024. Some groups (young people, ethnic minorities, the working class) are significantly less likely to vote, meaning their interests may be underrepresented. The unelected House of Lords and the monarchy are also criticised as undemocratic. New Right thinkers argue that the state has become too powerful, interfering in areas of life that should be left to individuals and markets. They advocate for a smaller state with lower taxes and less regulation. In contrast, social democrats argue that an active state is essential to reduce inequality, protect the vulnerable and provide public services.

UK Context: The controversy over the Investigatory Powers Act (2016), dubbed the ‘Snooper’s Charter’, illustrates the tension between state power and individual liberty. The act gave security services unprecedented powers to monitor internet use, communications and browsing history. Supporters argued these powers were necessary to combat terrorism; civil liberties groups argued they represented an unacceptable expansion of state surveillance that threatened the right to privacy.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Explain Lukes’ three dimensions of power and evaluate whether the third dimension is the most important in modern Britain.

Q: Evaluate the view that the UK is still ruled by an elite rather than a genuine democracy.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. Lukes’ first dimension is visible decision-making — who wins in open contests such as parliamentary votes. The second dimension is agenda-setting — powerful groups can keep issues off the agenda entirely. The third dimension is ideological power — shaping what people believe and want, so they consent to their own subordination. The third dimension may be the most important in modern Britain because it operates invisibly through the media, education and advertising. For example, many working-class people vote for parties that favour the wealthy, having been persuaded that policies like tax cuts for the rich will ‘trickle down’ to benefit everyone. However, the first dimension remains important for visible policy decisions, and the second dimension is evident in how corporate interests keep certain issues off the political agenda. All three dimensions interact and reinforce each other.
  2. Evidence for elite rule includes: 74% of judges and over 50% of the cabinet were privately educated, despite only 7% of the population attending private schools; corporate lobbying influences legislation; media owners shape public opinion; voter turnout is low and declining; and the House of Lords is unelected. Marxist sociologists argue that the ruling class controls the state, regardless of which party is in power. However, evidence for genuine democracy includes: free and regular elections; universal suffrage; freedom of speech and press; the rule of law; and the ability of social movements to achieve change. A balanced view is that the UK has democratic structures, but power is exercised unequally — wealthy and well-connected groups have disproportionate influence, while the majority of citizens have limited ability to shape policy between elections.
