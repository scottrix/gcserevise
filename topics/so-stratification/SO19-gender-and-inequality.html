Gender & Inequality

Gender as a form of stratification: the gender pay gap, the glass ceiling, gender roles, and Feminist explanations of gender inequality.

Key Fact: The gender pay gap: women earn about 8% less per hour (2023). Causes: lower-paid occupations, part-time work, career breaks, glass ceiling. The gap is wider for older women and ethnic minority women.
Key Fact: The glass ceiling: invisible barriers preventing women from reaching top positions. Only 10% of FTSE 100 CEOs are women. The 'glass cliff': women more likely to be appointed during crises.
Key Fact: Gender roles: girls socialised into caring/nurturing, boys into active/independent. Oakley: gender roles are socially constructed. This affects subject choices, career choices, domestic roles.
Key Fact: Feminist perspectives: Liberal (reform through legislation), Marxist (capitalism + patriarchy), Radical (patriarchy is primary oppression), Difference (intersectionality of class, ethnicity, sexuality).
Key Fact: Progress: legislation, girls outperform boys in education. Remaining: 8% pay gap, glass ceiling, domestic violence (1 in 4 women), women do twice as much unpaid care work, 35% of MPs.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain why the gender pay gap exists and evaluate whether legislation can eliminate it.

Q2: Compare different Feminist perspectives on gender inequality.

Q3: Evaluate whether gender inequality has been significantly reduced in modern Britain.

✅ Answers

  1. The gap exists because: occupational segregation, part-time work, career breaks, glass ceiling, discrimination. Legislation has reduced but not eliminated it because laws address direct discrimination but not structural factors.
  2. Liberal: reform through law - effective but slow. Marxist: capitalism + patriarchy - need to overthrow both. Radical: patriarchy is root cause - need fundamental restructuring. Difference: women are not one group - intersectionality matters.
  3. Progress: girls outperform boys, equal pay legislation, more women in professional jobs. Remaining: 8% pay gap, glass ceiling, domestic violence, care work, under-representation. Conclusion: significant progress but full equality not achieved.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Gender pay gap = 8%. Causes: occupation, part-time, motherhood penalty, glass ceiling.
  • Glass ceiling = barrier to TOP jobs. Glass cliff = women appointed during CRISIS.
  • Progress YES. Full equality NO. Formal equality exists; substantive equality is incomplete.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When evaluating gender inequality, always: use specific statistics, distinguish formal from substantive equality, and consider intersectionality.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Gender inequality no longer exists because women have equal rights under the law. Correct: While legal equality exists, substantive inequality persists: 8% pay gap, only 10% of FTSE 100 CEOs are women, women do twice as much unpaid care work, domestic violence affects 1 in 4 women. Legal equality is necessary but not sufficient.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that gender inequality is the most significant form of inequality in modern Britain.

A grade 9 response will: present evidence for gender inequality; compare with class and ethnic inequality; consider intersectionality; conclude: gender inequality is significant but cannot be isolated from class and ethnic inequality. No single form is 'most significant' - they interact and compound each other.

📖 Detailed Notes: Gender and Inequality

💡 Gender Inequality in the UK Workplace

Gender inequality remains a significant feature of UK society despite decades of legislation and policy change. The gender pay gap — the difference between men’s and women’s average earnings — was 7.7% for full-time employees in 2023, meaning women effectively work for free from November each year compared to men. The gap is wider for part-time workers and for women over 40. The causes are complex: occupational segregation means women are concentrated in lower-paid sectors (healthcare, retail, education) and roles (administrative, caring), while men dominate higher-paid sectors (finance, engineering, technology). The ‘glass ceiling’ describes the invisible barrier that prevents women from reaching senior positions — in 2023, only 10% of FTSE 100 CEOs were female. The ‘motherhood penalty’ means that women who have children experience a significant and lasting reduction in earnings, while men who become fathers do not — in fact, some evidence suggests a ‘fatherhood bonus’.

The Equal Pay Act (1970) and the Equality Act (2010) made gender discrimination in employment illegal. Large employers are now required to publish their gender pay gap data annually, creating transparency and accountability. However, feminist sociologists argue that legislation alone cannot solve deep-rooted inequality. The lack of affordable childcare is a major barrier: in the UK, full-time childcare costs approximately £14,000 per year, more than in most European countries, forcing many women into part-time work or out of employment altogether. Flexible working arrangements, shared parental leave and subsidised childcare (as in Nordic countries) are policies that could help reduce the gender pay gap, but progress has been slow.

UK Context: The BBC’s own gender pay gap disclosure in 2017 revealed that the top-earning male presenter earned between £2.2 million and £2.25 million, while the top-earning female presenter earned between £400,000 and £450,000. This public disclosure led to significant pressure for change and demonstrated how transparency can drive action on gender inequality.

🔍 Feminist Perspectives on Gender Inequality

Liberal feminism focuses on achieving equality through legal reform and policy change. Liberal feminists campaigned successfully for the Equal Pay Act, the Sex Discrimination Act and the introduction of shared parental leave. They argue that incremental change through legislation and education is the most effective route to gender equality. However, Marxist and radical feminists criticise liberal feminism for failing to address the deeper structural causes of gender inequality. Liberal reforms have narrowed the gender pay gap but not eliminated it; they have increased women’s representation in professions but not ended occupational segregation; they have made discrimination illegal but not eradicated sexism.

Marxist feminism argues that gender inequality is rooted in capitalism: women’s unpaid domestic labour (cooking, cleaning, childcare) reproduces the workforce for free, enabling capitalists to profit without paying for the social reproduction of workers. Additionally, women form a ‘reserve army of labour’ — drawn into paid work during economic booms and pushed back into the home during downturns. Radical feminism argues that patriarchy — male domination — is the primary form of oppression, predating and operating independently of capitalism. Radical feminists highlight male violence against women (domestic abuse, sexual assault, harassment) as a key mechanism of patriarchal control. Intersectional feminism, developed by Kimberle Crenshaw, argues that gender inequality cannot be understood in isolation from class, ethnicity, sexuality and disability — a working-class Black woman experiences discrimination differently from a middle-class white woman.

UK Context: The #MeToo movement (2017 onwards) highlighted the pervasiveness of sexual harassment and assault against women, particularly in the workplace. In the UK, over 60% of women reported experiencing sexual harassment at work, according to a TUC survey. The movement led to changes in workplace policies and greater awareness, but critics note that it has had less impact on the structural inequalities (pay gap, caring responsibilities, occupational segregation) that limit women’s economic power.

⚖️ Gender and Domestic Life

The domestic division of labour refers to how housework and childcare are shared between partners. The ONS Time Use Survey shows that women in the UK still perform approximately 60% more unpaid domestic work than men, including cooking, cleaning, laundry and childcare. This ‘dual burden’ means that many women work a ‘double shift’ — a full day of paid employment followed by several hours of unpaid housework and caring. The concept of the ‘triple shift’ (Duncombe and Marsden) adds emotional work — women are expected to manage the emotional wellbeing of the family, supporting partners and children psychologically as well as practically. Feminist sociologists argue that this unequal division is maintained by socialisation, gender norms and the ‘gender pay gap’ that makes it economically rational for the lower-earning partner (usually the woman) to reduce paid work.

However, some sociologists argue that the domestic division of labour is becoming more equal. Younger couples, particularly those in cohabiting rather than married relationships, tend to share housework more equally. Gershuny’s study found that when women work full-time, men gradually increase their share of domestic work, though equality is rarely achieved. The concept of the ‘symmetrical family’ (Young and Willmott) suggests that middle-class families are moving towards greater equality in domestic roles. However, critics argue that this is largely an illusion: even when men do more housework, women still take responsibility for managing and organising it (Ann Oakley called this ‘the responsibility burden’). The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted these inequalities: during lockdowns, women’s unpaid work increased disproportionately, with mothers 1.5 times more likely than fathers to have left their jobs or reduced working hours to manage childcare.

UK Context: The 2023 ONS Time Use Survey found that women spent an average of 4 hours and 24 minutes per day on unpaid household work, compared to 2 hours and 36 minutes for men. The gap was even wider for childcare: women spent 1 hour 44 minutes per day on active childcare, compared to 56 minutes for men.

📊 Comparing Feminist Perspectives on Gender Inequality

Perspective Cause of Inequality Key Solution Key Criticism
Liberal feminism Discriminatory laws and attitudes Legal reform, education Does not address structural causes
Marxist feminism Capitalism exploits women’s unpaid labour Overthrow capitalism Underestimates patriarchy as independent cause
Radical feminism Patriarchy — male domination Separatism, challenge male violence Ignores class and ethnic differences among women
Intersectional feminism Overlapping oppressions (class, race, gender) Address multiple disadvantages simultaneously Can be difficult to form a unified movement
Difference feminism Women have different values from men Value femininity, reject patriarchal standards Risk of reinforcing gender stereotypes

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Evaluate the view that gender inequality in the UK has been largely eliminated.

Q: Explain how the domestic division of labour reflects gender inequality and evaluate whether it is becoming more equal.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. On one hand, significant progress has been made: the gender pay gap has narrowed from 17% in 1997 to 7.7% in 2023; women now outnumber men in higher education; legislation prohibits gender discrimination; and women have achieved visible positions of power. On the other hand, the pay gap persists, occupational segregation is significant, women still do 60% more unpaid domestic work, male violence against women remains widespread, and women are underrepresented in Parliament (35%) and senior business roles. Intersectional analysis shows that some women (working-class, ethnic minority, disabled) face compounded disadvantages. A balanced view is that formal equality has been largely achieved, but substantive equality — where gender no longer predicts life chances — remains a distant goal.
  2. The ONS Time Use Survey shows women do approximately 60% more unpaid domestic work than men, including a disproportionate share of cooking, cleaning, laundry and childcare. Duncombe and Marsden’s concept of the ‘triple shift’ highlights that women also perform emotional work, managing family relationships and wellbeing. This inequality is maintained by gender socialisation, cultural norms and the gender pay gap that makes it ‘economically rational’ for women to reduce paid work. Some evidence suggests change: Gershuny found that when women work full-time, men gradually increase their contribution; younger couples share more equally. However, critics argue the change is slow and incomplete: even when men do more, women still manage and organise the household. The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated that the domestic division of labour remains deeply unequal.
