Ethnicity & Inequality

Ethnicity as stratification: racial inequality in employment, education, crime and justice. Institutional racism and Islamophobia.

Key Fact: Ethnic inequality in employment: ethnic minorities earn less on average. Wood's CV study: identical CVs, 'White' names got 74% more callbacks. This proves discrimination operates in hiring.
Key Fact: Institutional racism (Macpherson Report, 1999): 'the collective failure of an organisation to provide an appropriate service to people because of their colour, culture or ethnic origin'. Found the Met Police was institutionally racist.
Key Fact: Ethnicity and crime: Black people are 9x more likely to be stopped and searched; 12% of prison vs 3% of population. Self-report studies show similar offending rates - the disparity reflects policing bias.
Key Fact: Islamophobia: prejudice against Muslims increased after 9/11 and 7/7. Muslims face employment discrimination, media stereotyping, and rising hate crime.
Key Fact: Anti-racist legislation (Race Relations Act 1976, Equality Act 2010) has improved formal equality but not eliminated discrimination.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain what institutional racism is and evaluate its usefulness.

Q2: Describe the evidence for ethnic inequality in employment and crime.

Q3: Evaluate whether legislation has effectively reduced ethnic inequality.

✅ Answers

  1. Institutional racism: collective failure of an organisation due to race (Macpherson). Found in police, education, CJS. Useful: explains systemic patterns without individual racist intent. Criticism: can be too vague.
  2. Employment: Wood's CV study proved discrimination (74% more callbacks for White names). Crime: Black people 9x more likely to be stopped; 12% of prison vs 3% of population. Causes: discrimination, institutional racism, socio-economic factors.
  3. Legislation has improved formal equality but discrimination persists (CV studies, stop and search). Legislation addresses individual discrimination but not structural inequality. Conclusion: necessary but not sufficient.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Institutional racism = SYSTEMIC bias (Macpherson/Stephen Lawrence). Not just individual prejudice.
  • Employment: CV study PROVES discrimination (White names = 74% more callbacks).
  • Legislation necessary but NOT sufficient: laws change faster than attitudes and structures.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing ethnic inequality, always: distinguish individual prejudice from institutional racism, use specific evidence, and evaluate legislation's effectiveness.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: Ethnic inequality is caused by cultural differences rather than discrimination. Correct: Wood's CV study proved identical qualifications received different responses based on ethnic names; stop and search data shows policing bias; the Macpherson Report identified institutional racism. Cultural explanations risk blaming the victim.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that institutional racism is the main cause of ethnic inequality in modern Britain.

A grade 9 response will: define institutional racism; present evidence (stop and search, CV studies); evaluate: it explains systemic patterns; counter-arguments: socio-economic factors also contribute; some ethnic groups achieve above average; conclude: institutional racism is significant but interacts with class and cultural factors. The most effective approach addresses all three.

📖 Detailed Notes: Ethnicity and Inequality

💡 Ethnic Inequalities in the UK

Ethnic inequality remains a significant feature of UK society. The Race Disparity Audit (2017), commissioned by Theresa May, revealed stark differences in outcomes across ethnic groups. In education, Chinese and Indian students consistently achieve the highest GCSE results, while Black Caribbean and Gypsy/Roma students achieve the lowest. In employment, the employment rate for people from a white British background (approximately 77%) is significantly higher than for people from Pakistani (58%), Bangladeshi (62%) or Black African (68%) backgrounds. Even when ethnic minority individuals have the same qualifications as white British people, they earn less on average — this ‘ethnic penalty’ suggests that discrimination, not just differences in human capital, explains part of the gap. In housing, ethnic minorities are more likely to live in overcrowded accommodation and less likely to own their own home, though Indian households are an exception with high rates of home ownership.

The Macpherson Report (1999), which followed the racist murder of Stephen Lawrence, found that the Metropolitan Police were ‘institutionally racist’ — meaning that their procedures, culture and practices disadvantaged ethnic minorities, even if individual officers were not personally racist. This concept has been applied to other institutions, including education (where Black students are more likely to be excluded from school) and the criminal justice system (where Black people are nine times more likely to be stopped and searched than white people). In 2020, the Black Lives Matter movement drew renewed attention to racial injustice, with protests across the UK highlighting disproportionality in police use of force, stop and search, and custodial deaths.

UK Context: In 2023, Black people in England and Wales were approximately nine times more likely to be stopped and searched by police than white people. The government argues this reflects patterns of crime, but critics argue it reflects racial profiling and discrimination. The Casey Review (2023) found that the Metropolitan Police remained institutionally racist, sexist and homophobic.

🔍 Sociological Explanations for Ethnic Inequality

Marxist sociologists argue that ethnic inequality serves the interests of capitalism. Racial divisions fragment the working class, preventing solidarity and making it harder for workers to unite against exploitation. Migrant workers and ethnic minorities often form a cheap, flexible labour force that capitalists can exploit, paying lower wages and offering poorer conditions. The concept of racialised class means that ethnicity and class interact: working-class ethnic minorities face both class exploitation and racial discrimination, compounding their disadvantage. The hostile environment policy (introduced under Theresa May as Home Secretary) and the Windrush scandal (2018) — where British citizens of Caribbean descent were wrongly detained, denied rights and deported — illustrate how state policies can reinforce ethnic inequality.

Weberian sociologists argue that ethnic minorities can form status groups with distinct cultures and identities, and that market inequalities interact with status inequalities to produce complex patterns. Some ethnic minority groups (particularly Indian and Chinese communities) have achieved economic success through social networks, mutual support and cultural emphasis on education, challenging the idea that ethnic minority status inevitably leads to disadvantage. The concept of institutional racism (Macpherson) explains how seemingly neutral policies and practices can produce racially unequal outcomes without overt prejudice — for example, school exclusion policies that disproportionately affect Black Caribbean boys, or recruitment practices that disadvantage candidates with non-British-sounding names.

UK Context: The Windrush scandal (2018) highlighted the impact of the ‘hostile environment’ policy on the Caribbean community in the UK. Hundreds of people who had arrived legally from the Caribbean as children in the 1950s and 1960s were wrongly classified as illegal immigrants, losing their jobs, homes, access to healthcare and in some cases being detained or deported.

⚖️ Identity, Culture and Ethnicity in Modern Britain

Ethnic identity is an important aspect of many people’s lives in the UK, but it is complex and evolving. The 2021 Census showed that 81.7% of the population of England and Wales identified as white British, down from 86% in 2011, reflecting growing diversity. Many second and third-generation ethnic minority people develop hybrid identities, combining elements of their heritage culture with British culture — for example, British Asian youth may speak a mix of English and heritage languages, eat both traditional and Western food, and participate in both mainstream and religious celebrations. Gilroy’s concept of the ‘Black Atlantic’ describes how Black British identity draws on connections across the African diaspora rather than being simply British or simply Caribbean/African.

Multiculturalism is the policy approach that encourages ethnic communities to maintain their distinct cultural identities while participating equally in wider society. The UK has generally followed a multicultural approach, supporting faith schools, funding ethnic community organisations and protecting religious freedom through equality legislation. However, multiculturalism has been criticised from different perspectives. Some argue it encourages segregation and undermines social cohesion, pointing to the 2001 riots in northern English towns as evidence that separate communities living side by side without interaction leads to tension. Others argue that multiculturalism does not go far enough — that it celebrates cultural diversity while failing to address the structural inequalities (in employment, housing, criminal justice) that ethnic minorities face regardless of their cultural practices.

UK Context: The 2021 Census revealed that London is the most ethnically diverse region in the UK, with only 37% of residents identifying as white British. In contrast, the North East of England was 93% white British. This regional variation has significant implications for social policy, service provision and community relations.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Evaluate the view that institutional racism is the main cause of ethnic inequality in the UK.

Q: Explain the concept of multiculturalism and evaluate whether it has been successful in the UK.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. Evidence for institutional racism includes: the Macpherson Report’s finding that the Metropolitan Police were institutionally racist; the Casey Review (2023) confirming this continued; Black people being nine times more likely to be stopped and searched; ethnic minorities earning less than white British people with the same qualifications; and the Windrush scandal demonstrating how policies can discriminate ethnically. However, other factors also contribute: language barriers, cultural differences, recent migration history, and socio-economic factors (many ethnic minorities live in deprived areas). Some groups (notably Indian and Chinese) achieve above-average results, suggesting that ethnicity alone does not determine outcomes. A balanced view is that institutional racism is a significant cause but interacts with class, culture, migration history and other factors.
  2. Multiculturalism is the policy of allowing and supporting different cultural communities to maintain their distinct identities within a shared society. In the UK, it has included funding for faith schools, support for community organisations, legal protection against discrimination, and recognition of minority religious festivals. Arguments that it has been successful include: the UK is generally a tolerant and diverse society; ethnic minorities participate in education and the economy; cultural diversity enriches British life. Arguments that it has failed include: the 2001 northern town riots showed communities living parallel lives; some multicultural policies may reinforce segregation; and it may not address structural inequalities. A balanced view is that multiculturalism has achieved cultural tolerance but not yet substantive equality.
