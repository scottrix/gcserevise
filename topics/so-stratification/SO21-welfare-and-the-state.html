Welfare, Poverty & the State

The welfare state and poverty: Beveridge, the development of welfare, debates about dependency culture, and poverty in modern Britain.

Key Fact: The welfare state (1945): based on Beveridge Report (1942) which identified five 'giant evils': Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor, Idleness. Key services: NHS, state education, social security, council housing.
Key Fact: Universal vs means-tested benefits: universal (available to all - NHS, state education) vs means-tested (only for those below a certain income - Universal Credit). Means-testing targets resources but creates the poverty trap.
Key Fact: New Right critique (Murray): welfare creates a 'dependency culture' where people rely on benefits. The 'underclass' is trapped in welfare dependency. Evaluation: 68% of families in poverty have at least one working adult; most benefit claimants are there temporarily.
Key Fact: Marxist view: welfare maintains capitalism (reserve army of labour, legitimises the system, controls the poor through conditionality and sanctions).
Key Fact: Poverty: relative poverty (below 60% of median) affects 22%. The Poverty and Social Exclusion survey found 14 million people cannot afford basic necessities. Causes: structural (unemployment, low wages, housing costs) vs individual (New Right: laziness, poor choices).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the key features of the welfare state and evaluate different views on its role.

Q2: Evaluate the New Right view that welfare creates a dependency culture.

Q3: Assess whether poverty is caused by structural factors or individual behaviour.

✅ Answers

  1. The welfare state provides NHS, education, social security, housing. Functionalists: safety net. Marxists: maintains capitalism. New Right: creates dependency. Feminists: supports women's caring role. Each perspective reveals different aspects.
  2. Murray: welfare removes incentive to work, creating an underclass. Evaluation: 68% of poor families have someone working (in-work poverty is the real problem); most benefit use is temporary; benefits are too low for comfortable dependency. The thesis stigmatises the poor.
  3. Structural: unemployment, low wages, inadequate benefits, housing costs. Individual: laziness, poor choices (New Right). Evidence: structural factors are far more significant - most poor people are in work or seeking work. Structural solutions (living wage, affordable housing) work better.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Beveridge's 5 evils: Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor, Idleness. Welfare = NHS + education + social security + housing.
  • New Right: DEPENDENCY CULTURE (Murray). Counter: 68% of poor families have someone WORKING.
  • Poverty: STRUCTURAL (low wages, unemployment) > INDIVIDUAL (poor choices). Structural solutions work better.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing welfare and poverty, always: distinguish structural from individual explanations and use evidence (in-work poverty statistics).

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: People are poor because they don't work hard enough and choose to rely on benefits. Correct: 68% of working-age families in poverty have at least one person in work. Poverty is primarily caused by low wages, high housing costs, inadequate benefits, and zero-hours contracts. The dependency narrative blames individuals while ignoring structural conditions. Most benefit claimants would prefer a living wage.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that the welfare state has failed to reduce poverty in modern Britain.

A grade 9 response will: describe the welfare state; present evidence it HAS reduced poverty (prevents destitution, NHS improves health); present evidence it HASN'T (22% in relative poverty, in-work poverty growing); explain why: structural inequality; conclude: the welfare state has prevented absolute poverty but not eliminated relative poverty because it addresses symptoms not causes. A living wage and affordable housing would reduce poverty more effectively.
