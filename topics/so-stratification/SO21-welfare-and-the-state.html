Welfare, Poverty & the State

The welfare state and poverty: Beveridge, the development of welfare, debates about dependency culture, and poverty in modern Britain.

Key Fact: The welfare state (1945): based on Beveridge Report (1942) which identified five 'giant evils': Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor, Idleness. Key services: NHS, state education, social security, council housing.
Key Fact: Universal vs means-tested benefits: universal (available to all - NHS, state education) vs means-tested (only for those below a certain income - Universal Credit). Means-testing targets resources but creates the poverty trap.
Key Fact: New Right critique (Murray): welfare creates a 'dependency culture' where people rely on benefits. The 'underclass' is trapped in welfare dependency. Evaluation: 68% of families in poverty have at least one working adult; most benefit claimants are there temporarily.
Key Fact: Marxist view: welfare maintains capitalism (reserve army of labour, legitimises the system, controls the poor through conditionality and sanctions).
Key Fact: Poverty: relative poverty (below 60% of median) affects 22%. The Poverty and Social Exclusion survey found 14 million people cannot afford basic necessities. Causes: structural (unemployment, low wages, housing costs) vs individual (New Right: laziness, poor choices).

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the key features of the welfare state and evaluate different views on its role.

Q2: Evaluate the New Right view that welfare creates a dependency culture.

Q3: Assess whether poverty is caused by structural factors or individual behaviour.

✅ Answers

  1. The welfare state provides NHS, education, social security, housing. Functionalists: safety net. Marxists: maintains capitalism. New Right: creates dependency. Feminists: supports women's caring role. Each perspective reveals different aspects.
  2. Murray: welfare removes incentive to work, creating an underclass. Evaluation: 68% of poor families have someone working (in-work poverty is the real problem); most benefit use is temporary; benefits are too low for comfortable dependency. The thesis stigmatises the poor.
  3. Structural: unemployment, low wages, inadequate benefits, housing costs. Individual: laziness, poor choices (New Right). Evidence: structural factors are far more significant - most poor people are in work or seeking work. Structural solutions (living wage, affordable housing) work better.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Beveridge's 5 evils: Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor, Idleness. Welfare = NHS + education + social security + housing.
  • New Right: DEPENDENCY CULTURE (Murray). Counter: 68% of poor families have someone WORKING.
  • Poverty: STRUCTURAL (low wages, unemployment) > INDIVIDUAL (poor choices). Structural solutions work better.

📝 Exam Technique

Sociology Exam Tips:
When discussing welfare and poverty, always: distinguish structural from individual explanations and use evidence (in-work poverty statistics).

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often make mistakes here. Wrong: People are poor because they don't work hard enough and choose to rely on benefits. Correct: 68% of working-age families in poverty have at least one person in work. Poverty is primarily caused by low wages, high housing costs, inadequate benefits, and zero-hours contracts. The dependency narrative blames individuals while ignoring structural conditions. Most benefit claimants would prefer a living wage.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Evaluate the view that the welfare state has failed to reduce poverty in modern Britain.

A grade 9 response will: describe the welfare state; present evidence it HAS reduced poverty (prevents destitution, NHS improves health); present evidence it HASN'T (22% in relative poverty, in-work poverty growing); explain why: structural inequality; conclude: the welfare state has prevented absolute poverty but not eliminated relative poverty because it addresses symptoms not causes. A living wage and affordable housing would reduce poverty more effectively.

📖 Detailed Notes: Welfare and the State

💡 The Development of the UK Welfare State

The modern UK welfare state was established after the Second World War, based on the Beveridge Report (1942). William Beveridge identified five ‘giant evils’ that needed to be defeated: Want (poverty), Disease (ill health), Ignorance (lack of education), Squalor (poor housing) and Idleness (unemployment). The post-war Labour government (1945–51) created the key institutions of the welfare state: the National Health Service (NHS, 1948), providing free healthcare for all; the National Insurance system, providing benefits for the sick, unemployed and retired; free secondary education for all children; and a major programme of council house building. The welfare state was based on three key principles: universalism (services available to all, not just the poor), collective responsibility (society as a whole pays through taxation), and cradle-to-grave support (from birth to death).

Functionalists argue that the welfare state serves essential functions for society: it maintains social cohesion by preventing extreme poverty, provides healthcare and education that benefit the economy, and socialises children into shared values through state education. Marxists argue that the welfare state serves the interests of capitalism by maintaining a healthy workforce, preventing the extreme poverty that would provoke revolution, and controlling the poor through conditionality and surveillance. Feminists argue that the welfare state was designed around a male breadwinner model, assuming women’s financial dependence on men, and that policies such as joint taxation and household-based benefits disadvantage women. The New Right argues that the welfare state creates dependency, undermines personal responsibility and discourages the traditional family.

UK Context: The creation of the NHS in 1948 transformed healthcare in the UK. Before the NHS, many working-class people could not afford to see a doctor, and health outcomes were heavily determined by class. The NHS provided free healthcare at the point of use, funded by taxation, significantly reducing health inequalities in the early decades.

🔍 Welfare Reform and Austerity

Since the 1980s, UK welfare policy has shifted from the universalist model towards greater targeting and conditionality. The Thatcher government (1979–90) introduced means-testing for some benefits, reduced the value of unemployment benefit and promoted the idea that welfare dependency was a social problem. New Labour (1997–2010) maintained the commitment to work as the best route out of poverty, introducing tax credits that supplemented low wages and setting targets to reduce child poverty (which fell from 34% in 1998 to 28% in 2010). However, the coalition and Conservative governments (2010–2024) introduced austerity — major cuts to public spending in response to the 2008 financial crisis. Welfare budgets were cut significantly, local government funding was reduced by 40%, and the benefits cap and bedroom tax were introduced.

Universal Credit, introduced from 2013, was the most significant welfare reform of the period, replacing six working-age benefits with a single monthly payment. It was intended to simplify the system and ‘make work pay’ by tapering benefits gradually as earnings rise. However, critics argue that the five-week wait for the first payment, the single monthly payment (which is difficult for budgeting), the sanctions regime (which can stop all payments for up to three years for minor infractions), and the digital-only application process have created significant hardship. The Trussell Trust reported that food bank use in areas where Universal Credit had been rolled out was twice as high as in areas where it had not. Feminist sociologists highlight that Universal Credit is paid to one household member (by default, the main earner, who is usually male), reducing women’s financial independence and potentially trapping them in abusive relationships.

UK Context: The United Nations Special Rapporteur on Extreme Poverty, Philip Alston, visited the UK in 2018 and concluded that austerity policies had ‘inflicted unnecessary misery’ on the poorest members of society. He found that 14 million people (one in five) lived in poverty, including 4 million children, and that the government’s policies had disproportionately affected women, ethnic minorities, children and disabled people.

⚖️ Debating the Future of Welfare

The future of the welfare state is one of the most contested issues in UK politics. Social democrats argue for expanded welfare provision: increasing benefit levels to reflect the true cost of living, investing in social housing, providing universal childcare, and reversing austerity cuts. They point to the Nordic model, where high taxation funds comprehensive welfare provision and produces lower inequality, better health outcomes and higher social mobility. The Joseph Rowntree Foundation argues that the UK needs to ‘unlock’ poverty through adequate benefits, affordable housing and good-quality jobs. The concept of Universal Basic Income (UBI) — paying all citizens a regular income regardless of employment — has gained attention as a potential simplification of the welfare system, with pilot schemes in Wales and Finland.

New Right and neoliberal thinkers continue to argue for a smaller welfare state, lower taxes and greater individual responsibility. They point to the cost of welfare (social protection accounts for approximately 40% of government spending) and argue that high taxes discourage enterprise and job creation. The argument that welfare creates dependency is central: New Right thinkers believe that generous benefits discourage work, particularly for men, and undermine the traditional nuclear family by making it economically possible for lone parents to live independently. However, evidence from countries with more generous welfare systems (e.g. Denmark, Sweden) shows that high welfare spending does not necessarily create dependency — employment rates in Nordic countries are actually higher than in the UK. This suggests that welfare design, not simply welfare spending, determines outcomes.

UK Context: Wales ran a Universal Basic Income pilot from 2022, providing £1,600 per month to over 500 care leavers for two years. Early findings showed improved mental health, greater financial stability and increased engagement with education and employment among participants.

❓ Additional Practice Questions

Q: Evaluate the impact of austerity on social inequality in the UK since 2010.

Q: Explain the arguments for and against Universal Basic Income as a replacement for the current welfare system.

✅ Additional Answers

  1. Austerity has had significant impacts on social inequality. The Institute for Fiscal Studies found that the poorest tenth of households lost an average of £2,800 per year from benefit cuts and reduced public services, while the richest tenth lost approximately £1,500. Women, ethnic minorities, disabled people and children have been disproportionately affected. Food bank use has risen dramatically, child poverty has increased, and the local government funding cuts (40% since 2010) have reduced services in the most deprived areas most severely. The UN Special Rapporteur concluded that austerity had inflicted ‘unnecessary misery’ on the poorest. However, supporters argue that austerity was necessary to reduce the budget deficit, that employment rates have risen, and that Universal Credit has simplified the benefits system.
  2. Arguments for UBI include: it eliminates poverty by guaranteeing a minimum income; it removes the stigma and complexity of means-tested benefits; it gives people the freedom to study, retrain or care for others without losing income; it reduces bureaucracy and administrative costs; and it may support entrepreneurship. Arguments against include: the cost — paying every adult in the UK £10,000 per year would cost approximately £500 billion; it may discourage work if set too high; it pays the same to the wealthy as to the poor; and it may not address the specific needs of disabled people and carers. A balanced view is that UBI has theoretical appeal but requires careful design and significant funding to work in practice.
