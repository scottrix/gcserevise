PE29: Engagement Patterns in Physical Activity and Sport

Foundation Higher All Boards Paper 2

Understanding the factors that affect participation in physical activity and sport, including gender, race, religion, culture, age, disability, family, peers and socio-economic status.

📊 Why Do Engagement Patterns Differ?

Engagement patterns: The ways in which different groups of people participate (or don't participate) in physical activity and sport. These patterns vary significantly based on personal, social and cultural factors.

Understanding engagement patterns is important because:

  • It helps identify barriers to participation for different groups
  • It allows organisations to design strategies to increase participation
  • It reveals inequalities that need to be addressed in sport
  • It helps explain why some groups are under-represented in certain sports
  • It supports the development of inclusive policies and programmes

🚻 Gender

Gender significantly affects participation levels and the types of sport people engage in.

Barriers for Females

  • Stereotyping: Traditional views that certain sports are "for boys" (e.g. rugby, football) or "for girls" (e.g. netball, gymnastics) discourage cross-gender participation
  • Media coverage: Women's sport receives far less media coverage than men's, reducing visibility and role models
  • Lack of role models: Fewer high-profile female athletes visible in the media
  • School provision: Historically, fewer opportunities and less funding for girls' sport in schools
  • Self-consciousness: Some girls avoid sport due to body image concerns, especially during teenage years
  • Childcare responsibilities: Women are more likely to have caring duties that limit time for sport

Strategies to Address Gender Barriers

  • Increased media coverage of women's sport (e.g. Women's Super League on TV)
  • Equal prize money at major events (e.g. Wimbledon)
  • Female role models and ambassadors (e.g. Leah Williamson, Dina Asher-Smith)
  • Girls-only sessions to build confidence without judgement
  • This Girl Can campaign to challenge stereotypes
Key Facts

Women's football participation in England increased significantly after the 2019 Women's World Cup and the Lionesses' Euro 2022 victory, demonstrating the impact of media coverage and role models on engagement patterns.

🌍 Race, Religion and Culture

Race, religion and cultural background can all influence participation in sport.

Race

  • Certain ethnic groups are under-represented in some sports (e.g. very few South Asian footballers in the Premier League)
  • Role models from the same ethnic background can increase participation
  • Racism in sport can deter participation from minority ethnic groups
  • Cultural expectations within some communities may prioritise academic study over sport

Religion

  • Ramadan: Muslim athletes fasting during Ramadan may struggle with energy levels, hydration and training schedules
  • Modesty: Some religions require modest dress, making activities like swimming problematic without appropriate facilities
  • Religious observance: Training or competition on holy days (e.g. Friday prayers, Saturday Sabbath) can conflict with sport schedules
  • Gender interaction: Some religions restrict mixed-gender activities, requiring single-sex provision

Culture

  • Some cultures value certain sports more than others (e.g. cricket in South Asian communities)
  • Cultural norms around gender roles may limit women's participation in some communities
  • Language barriers can prevent access to information about sporting opportunities
  • Family expectations may steer young people away from sport towards education
Sporting Examples

Ramadan: Muslim footballers such as Mohamed Salah observe Ramadan fasting during the Premier League season. Clubs adapt training schedules and nutrition plans around fasting periods, with pre-dawn meals (suhoor) and post-sunset meals (iftar) carefully planned.

Modesty: Some Muslim women wear a hijab or burkini for swimming, requiring facilities that permit religious dress. The FIFA ruling allowing hijabs in football has increased participation.

👴 Age

Age affects both the type and level of participation in physical activity and sport.

Young People

  • May lack motivation or interest in traditional sports
  • Competition from technology and social media for leisure time
  • Youth programmes like Sport England's "Satellite Clubs" aim to increase participation
  • PE is compulsory in schools but engagement varies
  • Drop-off in participation is common during teenage years, especially among girls

Older Adults

  • Physical limitations may reduce the range of suitable activities
  • Fear of injury can discourage participation
  • Masters/veterans competitions allow older adults to compete in age-group categories
  • Health benefits of exercise are particularly important for older adults
  • Activities like walking, swimming, bowls and cycling are popular with older age groups
  • Social aspects of sport become increasingly important for combating isolation
Age Group Common Barriers Strategies
Children (5-11) Lack of local facilities, cost, transport Primary school sport programmes, free taster sessions
Teenagers (12-18) Peer pressure, body image, competing interests Youth-focused sessions, social sport, StreetGames
Adults (19-50) Time pressures, work, family commitments Flexible timetables, lunchtime sessions, workplace sport
Older adults (50+) Physical limitations, fear of injury, isolation Masters events, adapted activities, walking groups

♿ Disability

People with disabilities face significant barriers to participation in sport, but opportunities are growing.

Barriers

  • Lack of accessible facilities (ramps, adapted changing rooms, pool hoists)
  • Limited availability of disability-specific sports programmes
  • Shortage of trained coaches who understand adaptive sport
  • Negative attitudes and low expectations from others
  • Cost of specialist equipment (e.g. racing wheelchairs)
  • Lack of information about available opportunities

Strategies to Increase Participation

  • Paralympic Games: Increased visibility and profile of disability sport
  • Classification systems: Ensure fair competition by grouping athletes by impairment type and severity
  • Adapted activities: Sitting volleyball, wheelchair basketball, goalball, boccia
  • Inclusive coaching: Training coaches to work with disabled performers
  • Facility improvements: Accessible buildings, equipment and transport
Key Facts

The London 2012 Paralympics significantly increased public awareness of disability sport. The Paralympic classification system groups athletes by the nature and severity of their impairment to ensure fair competition, e.g. T11-T13 for visual impairment in athletics.

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Family and Peers

Family and peer influences are among the most powerful factors affecting sports participation.

Family Influence

  • Socialisation: Children whose parents are active in sport are more likely to participate themselves
  • Financial support: Parents who pay for kit, equipment, club membership and transport enable participation
  • Emotional support: Encouragement and praise from parents builds confidence
  • Role modelling: Active parents provide a model for children to copy
  • Transport: Parents who drive children to training and competitions facilitate engagement

Peer Influence

  • Teenagers are heavily influenced by what their friends think and do
  • If friends participate in sport, an individual is more likely to join in
  • Peer pressure can be positive (encouraging participation) or negative (discouraging sport as "uncool")
  • Team sports provide social belonging that can motivate continued participation
Key Point: The process of socialisation means that children learn attitudes and behaviours from their family. A child raised in a sporty family is far more likely to participate in sport than one whose family has no interest in physical activity. This influence is especially strong in early childhood.

💰 Socio-Economic Status

Socio-economic factors have a major impact on the ability to participate in sport.

Barriers for Low-Income Groups

  • Cost: Club membership fees, equipment, kit, transport, and facility hire charges can be prohibitive
  • Access: Deprived areas often have fewer sports facilities and poorer-quality provision
  • Transport: Cannot afford to travel to better facilities in other areas
  • Time: Long or irregular working hours limit availability for sport
  • Priority: Feeding the family and paying bills take priority over leisure activities

Strategies to Address Economic Barriers

  • Subsidised or free access to facilities (e.g. free swimming for under-16s)
  • Community sport programmes in deprived areas
  • Equipment libraries and kit recycling schemes
  • Discounted membership for low-income families
  • Sport England funding targeted at areas of greatest need
Socio-Economic Factor Barrier Impact on Participation
Income Cannot afford fees, kit, transport Lower participation in fee-paying sports
Location Few facilities in deprived areas Reduced access to quality provision
Education Less awareness of sport benefits Lower prioritisation of physical activity
Employment Long/irregular working hours Less time available for sport
Housing No safe outdoor space nearby Fewer informal play opportunities

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain how gender stereotyping can limit participation in sport for both males and females.

Q2: Describe how religion can be a barrier to participation in sport and suggest how this barrier could be overcome.

Q3: Explain the term 'socialisation' and how family influences can affect a young person's participation in sport.

Q4: How does socio-economic status affect engagement in physical activity? Give three specific barriers.

Q5: Explain how the Paralympic Games and classification systems have helped increase participation in disability sport.

Q6: Suggest and explain three strategies that could increase teenage girls' participation in physical activity.

✅ Answers

  1. Gender stereotyping limits participation by creating expectations about which sports are appropriate for each gender. Girls may be discouraged from playing "masculine" sports like rugby or football, while boys may avoid "feminine" sports like dance or netball. This reduces the range of opportunities explored. Stereotyping also affects media coverage and funding, with women's sport receiving less investment and visibility, which limits role models and aspiration for girls.
  2. Religion can be a barrier through: (1) Fasting during Ramadan reduces energy and hydration for Muslim athletes, making training and competition difficult. (2) Modesty requirements may prevent participation in activities requiring revealing clothing, such as swimming. (3) Religious observance on holy days may conflict with training or competition schedules. Barriers can be overcome by: scheduling around fasting times, providing facilities that allow modest dress (e.g. burkinis for swimming), offering single-sex sessions, and respecting holy day commitments when planning fixtures.
  3. Socialisation is the process by which a child learns the norms, values and behaviours of their family and community. In sport, a child whose parents are active and enthusiastic about physical activity is likely to adopt similar attitudes through copying their behaviour and receiving encouragement. Parents who pay for club membership, provide transport and watch matches enable participation. Conversely, a child from a family with no interest in sport is less likely to see physical activity as normal or desirable.
  4. Socio-economic status affects engagement through: (1) Cost - low-income families cannot afford membership fees, specialist kit, equipment or transport to facilities, limiting access to many sports. (2) Access - deprived areas typically have fewer and poorer-quality sports facilities, reducing opportunities for local participation. (3) Time - people in low-paid or irregular employment may work long or unsociable hours, leaving less time available for sport compared to those with more flexible working conditions.
  5. The Paralympic Games have increased participation by raising the profile and visibility of disability sport, inspiring disabled people to take up sport, and challenging negative stereotypes about disability. Classification systems have helped by grouping athletes by the type and severity of their impairment, ensuring fair competition. This means athletes with different disabilities can compete meaningfully against others with similar limitations, which encourages participation by making competition achievable and fair.
  6. Three strategies: (1) Girls-only sessions - provide a non-judgemental environment where girls can participate without feeling self-conscious or intimidated, building confidence. (2) Female role models and ambassadors - using high-profile female athletes (e.g. Lionesses) to inspire girls and show that women's sport is valued. (3) Social media campaigns (e.g. This Girl Can) - challenge stereotypes about women in sport and promote the idea that sport is for everyone, regardless of body type or ability level.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always explain both the barrier AND a strategy to overcome it - examiners want both
  • Use specific examples for each factor (e.g. Ramadan for religion, not just "religious reasons")
  • Remember that barriers can be practical (cost, transport) or psychological (stereotyping, self-consciousness)
  • When discussing age, address both young people AND older adults
  • Classification in disability sport = fair competition by grouping similar impairments
  • Link socialisation to family influence - this is a key connection examiners look for

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often list barriers without suggesting solutions. Wrong: "Poor people can't afford sport." Correct: "Low-income families may not be able to afford club membership fees and kit. This can be addressed through subsidised access and equipment recycling schemes."

Students often confuse race and religion as factors. Wrong: "Race means you can't eat certain foods." Correct: "Religion may affect diet and fasting (e.g. Ramadan), while race relates to ethnic background which can influence cultural expectations and exposure to racism in sport."

📝 Exam Technique

PE Exam Tips:
For engagement patterns, cover ALL factors (gender, race, age, disability, socio-economics). For commercialisation, discuss impacts on ALL stakeholder groups (performers, sport, officials, spectators, sponsors). Evaluate positive AND negative impacts.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think commercialisation has only benefited sport. Wrong: Commercialisation has only benefited sport Correct: While commercialisation brings funding and media coverage, it can also lead to corruption, loss of tradition, pressure on athletes, and inequality between funded and unfunded sports.

Students often think technology in sport is always a good thing. Wrong: Technology in sport is always a good thing Correct: Technology can create unfair advantages (e.g. expensive swimsuits in 2008 Olympics), slow down games (VAR delays), and reduce the human element of officiating.

Students often think peds only affect the athlete who takes them. Wrong: PEDs only affect the athlete who takes them Correct: PEDs affect the whole sport: they create unfair competition, pressure others to dope to compete, damage the sport's reputation, and set dangerous examples for young athletes.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE PE tests four AOs: AO1 (Knowledge, 30%) — recall facts about Engagement Patterns in Physical Activity and Sport including definitions, classifications and specific examples; AO2 (Application, 30%) — apply knowledge to sporting contexts and training scenarios; AO3 (Analysis and Evaluation, 25%) — analyse data (graphs, tables), evaluate training methods or strategies, and justify recommendations; AO4 (Practical, 15%) — demonstrate relevant skills. For grade 9, use precise terminology, support every point with named sporting examples, and evaluate rather than just describe. The difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of application and depth of evaluation.
