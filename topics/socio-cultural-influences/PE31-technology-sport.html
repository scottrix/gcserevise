PE31: Technology in Sport

Foundation Higher All Boards Paper 2

Understanding the positive and negative impacts of technology in sport, including decision-making technology, performance analysis, equipment advances and their effects on each stakeholder group.

🔧 Technology in Modern Sport

Technology in sport: The use of scientific and digital innovations to improve officiating decisions, enhance performance, advance equipment design, and increase safety. Technology has transformed sport at every level, from grassroots to elite competition.

Technology impacts sport in four main areas:

  • Officiating and decision-making - Hawk-Eye, VAR, TMO
  • Performance analysis - data tracking, GPS, video analysis
  • Equipment advances - improved materials and design
  • Safety improvements - protective equipment and medical technology

✅ Positive Impacts of Technology

1. Decision-Making Technology

  • Hawk-Eye: Ball-tracking technology used in tennis, cricket and football to determine whether the ball was in/out, hit the stumps or crossed the line. Increases accuracy of decisions.
  • VAR (Video Assistant Referee): Used in football to review goals, penalties, red cards and mistaken identity. Reduces clear and obvious errors.
  • TMO (Television Match Official): Used in rugby to review tries and foul play. Ensures correct decisions in crucial moments.
  • GLT (Goal-Line Technology): Instantly determines whether the ball crossed the goal-line in football. Eliminates controversy over whether a goal was scored.
  • Third umpire: Cricket's video review system for run-outs, stumpings and boundaries.
Sporting Examples

Tennis: Hawk-Eye uses multiple cameras to create a 3D trajectory of the ball, allowing players to challenge line calls. Each player gets 3 incorrect challenges per set.

Cricket: The Decision Review System (DRS) uses Hawk-Eye, Hot Spot (infrared thermal imaging) and Snickometer (sound detection) to help the third umpire make accurate decisions.

Football: VAR reviews incidents and recommends corrections to the on-field referee. Goal-line technology (e.g. GoalControl) signals instantly when the ball crosses the line.

2. Performance Analysis

  • GPS tracking: Monitors distance covered, speed, acceleration and heart rate during training and matches. Helps coaches manage workload and prevent injury.
  • Video analysis: Coaches analyse technique frame by frame to identify areas for improvement. Software like Hudl allows tagging of specific actions.
  • Data analytics: Statistics on pass completion, shots, possession and more inform tactical decisions and player selection.
  • Wearable technology: Fitness trackers, smartwatches and biometric sensors provide real-time data on performance and recovery.

3. Equipment Advances

  • Carbon fibre: Lighter, stronger materials used in bicycles, tennis rackets and running shoes improve performance
  • Aerodynamic design: Drag-reducing suits in swimming and cycling give measurable advantages
  • Improved footwear: Vaporfly running shoes with carbon fibre plates have led to faster marathon times
  • Better surfaces: Advanced artificial pitches and athletic tracks improve consistency and performance

4. Safety Improvements

  • Concussion protocols: Head injury assessments using technology (e.g. King-Devick test) protect athletes from brain injury
  • Improved protective equipment: Helmets in cricket and cycling are lighter and more protective than ever
  • Medical imaging: MRI and ultrasound allow faster, more accurate diagnosis of injuries
  • Recovery technology: Cryotherapy chambers, compression therapy and hydrotherapy pools speed up rehabilitation

❌ Negative Impacts of Technology

1. Cost and Inequality

  • Elite technology is expensive and not available to all performers or clubs
  • Rich teams and nations gain an unfair advantage over those who cannot afford the technology
  • Creates a widening gap between well-funded professional sport and grassroots/amateur sport
  • Developing nations may be unable to compete at the highest level without access to advanced technology

2. Less Human Element

  • Technology removes the drama and debate that comes from human error in officiating
  • Some fans enjoy the controversy of marginal decisions - it is part of sport's appeal
  • Over-reliance on technology diminishes the authority and skill of officials
  • The spontaneous emotion of a goal or wicket is delayed while technology reviews the decision

3. Slower Game

  • VAR and TMO reviews can cause long delays that disrupt the flow of play
  • Football matches are frequently interrupted for VAR checks
  • Rugby matches can pause for several minutes while the TMO reviews footage
  • Spectators and players can become frustrated by lengthy stoppages

4. Cheating Potential

  • Technology can be exploited to gain an unfair advantage (e.g. mechanical doping in cycling)
  • Performance data could be hacked or stolen by rivals
  • Some athletes and teams may push the boundaries of what technology permits
  • Advances in equipment may amount to "technology doping" (e.g. super-suits in swimming, banned in 2010)

👥 Impacts on Each Stakeholder Group

Stakeholder Positive Impact Negative Impact
Performers Better equipment, performance analysis, injury prevention, fairer decisions Cost inequality, pressure from data tracking, less spontaneity in competition
Coaches Data-driven tactics, video analysis, GPS workload monitoring, improved training Over-reliance on data, cost of technology, information overload
Officials Technology supports correct decisions, reduces pressure on officials, fairer outcomes Undermined authority, increased scrutiny, slower game management
Spectators More accurate decisions, better TV experience with graphics, fairer sport Frustrating delays, loss of debate and controversy, expensive to access
Sport overall Higher standards, fairer outcomes, better safety, increased professionalism Inequality between rich and poor, rule changes for technology, integrity concerns

⚖️ Evaluating Technology in Sport

Balanced View: Technology has significantly improved accuracy, safety and performance in sport. However, it has also introduced new problems including cost inequality, delays and a diminished human element. The key challenge is finding the right balance between using technology to support sport and allowing it to dominate sport.
Evaluation Framework:
Does the technology improve fairness? → Hawk-Eye and GLT: YES
Does it slow down the sport? → VAR: YES, often significantly
Is it accessible to all? → GPS analysis: NO, only elite level
Does it change the nature of the sport? → Super-suits in swimming: YES, and were banned
Does it support or replace officials? → GLT supports; VAR sometimes replaces

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain two positive impacts of technology on officials in sport.

Q2: How can technology create inequality in sport? Give specific examples.

Q3: Describe how VAR works in football and evaluate its impact on the game.

Q4: Explain how performance analysis technology has changed coaching in elite sport.

Q5: Why were full-body polyurethane swimming suits banned in 2010? What does this tell us about the relationship between technology and sport?

Q6: Discuss the argument that technology is "ruining sport." Give points for and against.

✅ Answers

  1. Two positive impacts: (1) Decision-making technology such as Hawk-Eye and VAR supports officials in making correct decisions by providing video replay and ball-tracking evidence, reducing the pressure on them to get every call right in real-time. (2) Technology such as GLT (goal-line technology) gives instant, definitive answers on whether a goal was scored, removing the need for officials to make difficult judgement calls in crucial moments.
  2. Technology creates inequality because it is expensive and only available to well-funded teams and nations. For example, GPS tracking systems and performance analysis software cost thousands of pounds and are used by Premier League clubs but not by lower-league or grassroots teams. Richer nations can afford the best training technology, equipment and analysis, giving their athletes an advantage over developing nations who cannot access the same resources. This creates a performance gap based on wealth rather than talent.
  3. VAR (Video Assistant Referee) uses video technology to review four types of incident in football: goals, penalties, red cards and mistaken identity. The VAR team watches the game remotely and alerts the on-field referee to clear and obvious errors. The referee can then review the footage on a pitchside monitor or accept the VAR recommendation. Positive impacts: corrects obvious errors, ensures fairer outcomes, increases confidence in refereeing. Negative impacts: causes long delays, disrupts the flow of the game, can still be subjective, creates confusion over what constitutes a "clear and obvious error," and delays celebrations while checks are carried out.
  4. Performance analysis technology has changed coaching by: (1) GPS tracking provides data on distance covered, speed and heart rate, allowing coaches to manage player workload and prevent injury. (2) Video analysis software enables frame-by-frame technique review, identifying specific areas for improvement. (3) Data analytics inform tactical decisions - coaches can analyse opposition patterns and adjust strategies. (4) Wearable technology provides real-time feedback during training sessions. Coaches now make decisions based on objective data rather than just observation and experience.
  5. Full-body polyurethane swimming suits (e.g. LZR Racer by Speedo) were banned because they provided an unfair advantage through improved buoyancy and reduced drag. Over 40 world records were broken in 2009 alone, largely attributed to the suits rather than swimmer ability. This tells us that technology can go too far when it becomes the primary factor in performance rather than the athlete's skill and fitness. Governing bodies must regulate technology to ensure it supports fair competition rather than distorting it. FINA banned the suits, limiting swimsuits to textile materials and specific coverage.
  6. For: Technology slows down sport with lengthy reviews (VAR, TMO), reduces the human element and debate that makes sport exciting, creates cost inequality between rich and poor, undermines officials' authority, and can be exploited for cheating (mechanical doping). Against: Technology improves accuracy and fairness of decisions (Hawk-Eye, GLT), enhances safety through better protective equipment and medical technology, helps coaches and performers improve through data analysis, and provides a better experience for TV viewers. Technology itself is not the problem - it is how it is implemented and regulated that matters.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always give both positive AND negative impacts when discussing technology
  • Use specific named technologies (Hawk-Eye, VAR, GPS) not just "technology"
  • Address each stakeholder group separately - performers, officials, coaches, spectators
  • Know the difference between VAR (review system) and GLT (instant automated decision)
  • The swimming suit ban is a great example of technology going too far
  • Evaluate: don't just list impacts - weigh up whether technology is overall positive or negative

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse VAR and goal-line technology. Wrong: "VAR tells you if the ball crossed the line." Correct: "Goal-line technology (GLT) automatically and instantly determines if the ball crossed the line. VAR is a separate video review system used for goals, penalties, red cards and mistaken identity."

Students often say technology is "always good." Wrong: "Technology makes sport better because it helps everyone." Correct: "Technology can improve accuracy and safety, but it also creates cost inequality, slows down games, and can undermine the human element of sport. Both positive and negative impacts must be considered."

📝 Exam Technique

PE Exam Tips:
For engagement patterns, cover ALL factors (gender, race, age, disability, socio-economics). For commercialisation, discuss impacts on ALL stakeholder groups (performers, sport, officials, spectators, sponsors). Evaluate positive AND negative impacts.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think commercialisation has only benefited sport. Wrong: Commercialisation has only benefited sport Correct: While commercialisation brings funding and media coverage, it can also lead to corruption, loss of tradition, pressure on athletes, and inequality between funded and unfunded sports.

Students often think technology in sport is always a good thing. Wrong: Technology in sport is always a good thing Correct: Technology can create unfair advantages (e.g. expensive swimsuits in 2008 Olympics), slow down games (VAR delays), and reduce the human element of officiating.

Students often think peds only affect the athlete who takes them. Wrong: PEDs only affect the athlete who takes them Correct: PEDs affect the whole sport: they create unfair competition, pressure others to dope to compete, damage the sport's reputation, and set dangerous examples for young athletes.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE PE tests four AOs: AO1 (Knowledge, 30%) — recall facts about Technology in Sport including definitions, classifications and specific examples; AO2 (Application, 30%) — apply knowledge to sporting contexts and training scenarios; AO3 (Analysis and Evaluation, 25%) — analyse data (graphs, tables), evaluate training methods or strategies, and justify recommendations; AO4 (Practical, 15%) — demonstrate relevant skills. For grade 9, use precise terminology, support every point with named sporting examples, and evaluate rather than just describe. The difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of application and depth of evaluation.
