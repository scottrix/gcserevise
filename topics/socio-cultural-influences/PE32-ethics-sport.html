PE32: Ethics in Sport

Foundation Higher All Boards Paper 2

Understanding etiquette, sportsmanship, gamesmanship and the contract to compete in sport, with examples of each concept and their role in maintaining fair play.

⚖️ What are Ethics in Sport?

Ethics in sport: The moral principles and values that govern behaviour in sport. Ethics determine what is considered right and wrong, fair and unfair, and guide the conduct of performers, coaches, officials and spectators.

The four key concepts of ethics in sport are:

  • Etiquette - unwritten rules and conventions of behaviour
  • Sportsmanship - playing fairly and respecting opponents
  • Gamesmanship - bending the rules to gain an advantage without breaking them
  • Contract to compete - the agreement to abide by the rules and compete fairly

🤝 Etiquette

Etiquette: The unwritten rules, conventions and customs that govern behaviour in sport. Etiquette is about showing respect, courtesy and consideration for others. It is not enforced by officials but is expected by the sporting community.

Examples of Etiquette

  • Shaking hands with opponents before and after a match
  • Clapping a retiring batter off the cricket pitch
  • Not celebrating excessively when an opponent makes an error
  • Returning the ball politely to the opposition after a stoppage
  • Letting your opponent serve first in tennis as the receiver
  • Standing in silence during an opponent's concentration (golf, snooker)
  • Acknowledging a good shot by an opponent with a nod or applause
  • Not questioning the referee's decision verbally or through gesture
Sporting Examples

Tennis: Players shake hands at the net after every match, regardless of the result. The server waits for the receiver to be ready before serving. A player acknowledges a lucky net cord by raising their hand.

Golf: Players remain silent and still while others are playing their shot. A player calls a penalty on themselves even if nobody else saw the infringement. Players repair divots and ball marks on the green.

Cricket: The fielding team claps a batter who reaches a half-century or century. Batters walk when they know they have edged the ball to the wicket-keeper, even if the umpire hasn't given them out.

Etiquette vs Rules: Etiquette is NOT the same as the written rules of the sport. Rules are formally codified and enforced by officials with penalties for breaches. Etiquette is unwritten, self-policed and based on respect and tradition. Breaking etiquette has no formal punishment but damages a performer's reputation.

🏅 Sportsmanship

Sportsmanship: Behaviour that shows fairness, respect and generosity towards opponents. A sportsmanlike player plays within the spirit of the sport, not just the letter of the rules. Sportsmanship goes beyond what is required and reflects positive moral character.

Key Characteristics of Sportsmanship

  • Playing fairly and within the spirit of the sport
  • Respecting opponents, officials and teammates
  • Accepting defeat gracefully without making excuses
  • Winning with humility, not arrogance or excessive celebration
  • Being honest and not seeking to deceive officials
  • Putting the integrity of the sport above personal gain
Sporting Examples

Paolo Di Canio (football): When the Everton goalkeeper was injured, Di Canio caught the ball instead of scoring into an empty net, putting the opponent's welfare above a certain goal.

Rory McIlroy (golf): Calling a penalty on himself during a tournament when his ball moved slightly as he addressed it, even though no one else noticed.

Andy Murray (tennis): Telling the umpire that a ball was in when it was called out against him, correcting a decision in his opponent's favour.

🦊 Gamesmanship

Gamesmanship: The practice of bending the rules to gain an advantage without actually breaking them. Unlike cheating, gamesmanship stays within the letter of the law but violates the spirit of the sport. It is often seen as "cunning" or "streetwise" behaviour.

Examples of Gamesmanship

  • Time-wasting: Taking a long time over goal kicks, throw-ins or free kicks when leading
  • Feigning injury: Exaggerating contact to get an opponent booked or sent off
  • Strategic fouls: Committing a deliberate foul to stop a counter-attack (tactical foul)
  • Distracting opponents: Making noise during an opponent's serve or concentration
  • Questioning calls: Putting pressure on officials to influence future decisions
  • Deliberate delays: A cricketer taking time to set the field between deliveries
Sporting Examples

Football: A team that is 1-0 up in the 89th minute takes 30 seconds over each goal kick, slowly walks to collect the ball, and the goalkeeper pretends to be injured to waste more time. None of this breaks the rules directly, but it is against the spirit of the game.

Rugby: A player deliberately collapsing a maul to prevent the opposition from driving forward. The penalty is accepted as a "cost of doing business" - the advantage gained is worth more than the penalty conceded.

Tennis: A player challenges a call they know was correct simply to take a break and disrupt the opponent's rhythm.

📜 The Contract to Compete

Contract to Compete: The unwritten agreement between all participants in sport that everyone will compete within the agreed rules and give their best effort. By entering a competition, performers agree to abide by the rules, respect the officials' decisions, and compete fairly.

Key Principles of the Contract to Compete

  • Agreement to rules: By participating, you accept the rules of the sport and agree to follow them
  • Best effort: All participants are expected to try their hardest - not giving effort violates the contract
  • Respect for officials: Accepting officials' decisions even when you disagree
  • Fair competition: No cheating, doping or deliberate attempts to deceive
  • Mutual respect: Treating opponents as fellow competitors, not enemies
Contract to Compete:
By entering the competition, I agree to:
→ Follow the rules of the sport
→ Accept the decisions of officials
→ Give my best effort at all times
→ Respect my opponents and teammates
→ Compete fairly and without deception
If any party breaks this contract, the integrity of the competition is undermined.
When the Contract is Broken

Match-fixing: A player deliberately loses as part of a betting scam - breaks the contract by not giving best effort and competing unfairly.

Doping: An athlete uses performance-enhancing drugs - breaks the contract by gaining an unfair advantage that other competitors have not agreed to.

Tanking: A team deliberately loses to get an easier draw in the next round - breaks the contract by not giving best effort.

📊 Comparing the Four Concepts

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Define etiquette in sport and give two examples of etiquette in a named sport.

Q2: Explain the difference between sportsmanship and gamesmanship, using sporting examples.

Q3: What is the contract to compete and why is it important for fair competition?

Q4: A football team is winning 1-0 in the last 5 minutes and starts time-wasting. Is this gamesmanship or cheating? Explain your answer.

Q5: Describe two examples where a performer showed sportsmanship that went beyond the requirements of the rules.

Q6: How does gamesmanship undermine the contract to compete? Explain with reference to a specific example.

✅ Answers

  1. Etiquette in sport refers to the unwritten rules, conventions and customs that govern behaviour and show respect for others. It is not enforced by officials but is expected by the sporting community. Two examples in cricket: (1) The fielding team claps a batter who reaches a milestone (50 or 100 runs). (2) A batter "walks" when they know they have edged the ball, even if the umpire has not given them out.
  2. Sportsmanship is playing fairly and within the spirit of the sport, showing respect for opponents and the rules. Gamesmanship is bending the rules to gain an advantage without actually breaking them. Example of sportsmanship: A golfer calls a penalty on themselves when their ball moves, even though no one else saw it. Example of gamesmanship: A footballer takes excessive time over throw-ins and goal kicks when their team is winning, wasting time without technically breaking any rule. Sportsmanship upholds the spirit of the game; gamesmanship exploits loopholes.
  3. The contract to compete is the unwritten agreement between all participants that they will compete within the agreed rules, give their best effort, and respect officials' decisions. It is important because without this agreement, competition has no meaning - if some participants are cheating, not trying, or ignoring rules, the contest is not fair or genuine. The contract ensures that all participants are competing under the same conditions and that the result reflects genuine effort and ability.
  4. This is gamesmanship rather than cheating because the team is bending the rules rather than breaking them. Time-wasting exploits the rules (no specific time limit on restarts) rather than violating them directly. However, it is against the spirit of the sport and undermines the contract to compete because the team is not genuinely competing in the closing stages - they are manipulating the game clock rather than playing football. Some would argue that when time-wasting becomes excessive, it crosses into unsporting behaviour which officials can punish (e.g. yellow cards for time-wasting).
  5. Example 1: Paolo Di Canio in football caught the ball instead of scoring into an empty net when the Everton goalkeeper was injured. He put the opponent's welfare above his own team's advantage, which went far beyond what the rules required. Example 2: In golf, players regularly call penalties on themselves that no one else witnessed, such as when a ball moves slightly during address. The rules don't require self-reporting in all cases, but sportsmanship demands honesty.
  6. Gamesmanship undermines the contract to compete because it violates the spirit of the agreement even if it follows the letter. For example, when a tennis player deliberately challenges a call they know is correct simply to take a break and disrupt the opponent's momentum, they are using the rules (entitled to challenge) against their intended purpose (ensuring correct decisions). This breaks the contract because the player is not genuinely competing - they are manipulating the system. The opponent expects a contest based on skill and effort, not tactical exploitation of procedures.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know the clear definitions of all four terms - examiners test these directly
  • Etiquette = unwritten conventions; Sportsmanship = fair play beyond rules; Gamesmanship = bending rules; Contract = agreement to compete fairly
  • Always give named sporting examples for each concept
  • Gamesmanship is NOT cheating - it stays within the letter of the rules
  • The contract to compete is about mutual agreement - if one party breaks it, the competition is unfair
  • Be prepared to evaluate whether a specific behaviour is gamesmanship or cheating

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse gamesmanship with cheating. Wrong: "Gamesmanship is when a player breaks the rules to win." Correct: "Gamesmanship is bending the rules to gain an advantage WITHOUT breaking them. Cheating involves deliberately violating the rules. Time-wasting is gamesmanship because no specific rule is broken; deliberate handball is cheating because it violates the rules."

Students often say etiquette is "the rules." Wrong: "Etiquette means following the rules of the sport." Correct: "Etiquette refers to the unwritten conventions and customs of behaviour, such as shaking hands after a match. It is separate from the formal, written rules of the sport."

📝 Exam Technique

PE Exam Tips:
For engagement patterns, cover ALL factors (gender, race, age, disability, socio-economics). For commercialisation, discuss impacts on ALL stakeholder groups (performers, sport, officials, spectators, sponsors). Evaluate positive AND negative impacts.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think commercialisation has only benefited sport. Wrong: Commercialisation has only benefited sport Correct: While commercialisation brings funding and media coverage, it can also lead to corruption, loss of tradition, pressure on athletes, and inequality between funded and unfunded sports.

Students often think technology in sport is always a good thing. Wrong: Technology in sport is always a good thing Correct: Technology can create unfair advantages (e.g. expensive swimsuits in 2008 Olympics), slow down games (VAR delays), and reduce the human element of officiating.

Students often think peds only affect the athlete who takes them. Wrong: PEDs only affect the athlete who takes them Correct: PEDs affect the whole sport: they create unfair competition, pressure others to dope to compete, damage the sport's reputation, and set dangerous examples for young athletes.
