PE33: Performance Enhancing Drugs (PEDs)

Foundation Higher All Boards Paper 2

Understanding the different types of performance enhancing drugs including stimulants, narcotic analgesics, anabolic agents, peptide hormones, diuretics and beta blockers, plus blood doping and their advantages and disadvantages.

💊 Why Do Performers Take PEDs?

Performance Enhancing Drugs (PEDs): Substances used to improve sporting performance that are banned by the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA). Taking PEDs is considered cheating because it gives an unfair advantage over clean competitors.

Reasons performers may take PEDs:

  • To gain an unfair advantage over competitors
  • Pressure to perform at the highest level (financial, coach, sponsor)
  • To recover faster from injury or intense training
  • To build muscle mass, increase endurance or reduce pain
  • Because others in their sport are doing it
  • To meet the physical demands of their sport

⚡ Stimulants

Stimulants: Drugs that increase alertness, reduce fatigue and increase aggression and competitiveness. They speed up the activity of the central nervous system.
  • Increase heart rate and blood pressure
  • Reduce the perception of fatigue
  • Increase alertness, aggression and competitiveness
  • Suppress appetite
  • Examples: Amphetamines, caffeine (in high doses), ephedrine, cocaine

Sporting Advantages

  • Increased aggression for contact sports (boxing, rugby)
  • Greater alertness for quick reactions (sprinting, combat sports)
  • Reduced fatigue allowing harder training for longer
  • Improved focus and concentration under pressure

Side Effects

  • Increased heart rate and blood pressure - risk of heart attack
  • Insomnia and anxiety
  • Aggression and mood swings
  • Addiction and dependency
  • Dehydration and heat stroke risk
Sporting Example

A sprinter might take stimulants to increase alertness and reaction speed off the blocks, and to maintain aggression and focus throughout the race. A boxer might use stimulants to increase aggression and reduce the perception of pain during a fight.

💉 Narcotic Analgesics

Narcotic Analgesics: Painkilling drugs that mask the sensation of pain, allowing performers to continue training or competing despite injury. They work by blocking pain signals to the brain.
  • Mask pain from injuries, allowing continued performance
  • Enable performers to push beyond normal pain thresholds
  • Create a feeling of euphoria (wellbeing)
  • Examples: Morphine, methadone, codeine, heroin

Sporting Advantages

  • Performers can compete through injury without feeling pain
  • Can continue training when they would otherwise need to rest
  • Euphoric effect may reduce anxiety before competition

Side Effects

  • Masking pain can lead to worse injuries (performer continues on damaged tissue)
  • Highly addictive - risk of dependency
  • Drowsiness and loss of coordination
  • Nausea and constipation
  • Respiratory depression (slowed breathing) at high doses
Sporting Example

A rugby player with a shoulder injury might take narcotic analgesics to mask the pain and continue playing. This is dangerous because the injury could worsen significantly without the player realising, potentially causing permanent damage.

💪 Anabolic Agents (Anabolic Steroids)

Anabolic Agents: Drugs that mimic the effects of the male hormone testosterone, promoting muscle growth (hypertrophy) and increasing strength. They also speed up recovery from intense training.
  • Increase muscle mass and strength significantly
  • Speed up recovery from training and injury
  • Increase aggression and competitiveness
  • Allow more frequent and intense training sessions
  • Examples: Nandrolone, stanozolol, testosterone

Sporting Advantages

  • Greater muscle mass and strength for power events (weightlifting, sprinting)
  • Faster recovery allows more training volume
  • Increased aggression benefits contact sports
  • Improved power-to-weight ratio when combined with training

Side Effects

  • Liver damage and liver cancer
  • Increased risk of heart disease and high blood pressure
  • Infertility and reduced sperm count in males
  • Virilisation in females (deepened voice, facial hair, menstrual problems)
  • Acne, hair loss and mood swings
  • Stunted growth in adolescents
Sporting Example

A weightlifter might take anabolic steroids to increase muscle mass and strength, allowing them to lift heavier weights than they could achieve through training alone. Ben Johnson was stripped of his 100m Olympic gold medal in 1988 after testing positive for stanozolol, an anabolic steroid.

🧬 Peptide Hormones

Peptide Hormones: Naturally occurring substances that stimulate the production of other hormones in the body. In sport, they are used to increase red blood cell production (EPO) or promote muscle growth (HGH).

EPO (Erythropoietin)

  • Stimulates the bone marrow to produce more red blood cells
  • More red blood cells = more oxygen carried to muscles
  • Significantly improves aerobic endurance
  • Used in endurance sports (cycling, long-distance running)

HGH (Human Growth Hormone)

  • Stimulates growth, cell reproduction and muscle development
  • Promotes lean muscle mass and reduces body fat
  • Aids recovery from injury and intense training
  • Used in strength and power sports

Side Effects

  • EPO: Blood thickens (increased viscosity), increasing risk of blood clots, stroke and heart attack, especially during sleep when heart rate drops
  • HGH: Abnormal growth of organs (acromegaly), diabetes, joint pain, increased risk of cancer
Sporting Example

Cyclist Lance Armstrong admitted to using EPO throughout his Tour de France victories. EPO increased his red blood cell count, allowing his muscles to receive more oxygen during the gruelling mountain stages, giving him a significant endurance advantage over clean riders.

🚿 Diuretics

Diuretics: Drugs that increase urine production, causing rapid weight loss through fluid loss. They are also used as masking agents to dilute urine samples and make other banned substances harder to detect.
  • Remove water and salt from the body through increased urination
  • Enable rapid weight loss to make weight categories
  • Dilute urine to reduce the concentration of other PEDs
  • Examples: Furosemide, hydrochlorothiazide

Sporting Advantages

  • Make weight category in sports like boxing, wrestling and judo
  • Mask the presence of other banned substances in drug tests
  • Can provide a size advantage by competing in a lower weight class

Side Effects

  • Severe dehydration and electrolyte imbalance
  • Muscle cramps and weakness
  • Kidney damage
  • Low blood pressure and dizziness
  • Heart arrhythmias due to potassium loss
Sporting Example

A boxer who is slightly over the lightweight limit might take diuretics in the days before the weigh-in to lose water weight and make the category. After weighing in, they rehydrate and may be significantly heavier than the limit by fight time, giving them a size advantage over an opponent who made weight naturally.

💙 Beta Blockers

Beta Blockers: Drugs that block the effects of adrenaline, reducing heart rate and steadying the body. They calm the nervous system and reduce anxiety, tremors and the fight-or-flight response.
  • Lower heart rate and blood pressure
  • Reduce anxiety and nervous tension
  • Steady trembling hands and reduce muscle tremors
  • Examples: Propranolol, atenolol

Sporting Advantages

  • Steady heart rate for fine, precise skills (shooting, archery, darts)
  • Reduced anxiety in high-pressure situations
  • Elimination of hand tremors for accuracy-based sports
  • Calmed nerves before major competitions

Side Effects

  • Low blood pressure and dizziness
  • Fatigue and lethargy
  • Cold hands and feet
  • Slower recovery from exercise
  • Wheezing and breathing difficulties (dangerous for asthmatics)
Sporting Example

An archer might take beta blockers to slow their heart rate and steady their hand before a competition. A lower heart rate means less body movement between heartbeats, allowing a more precise aim. Beta blockers are banned in shooting, archery and snooker.

🩸 Blood Doping

Blood Doping: The practice of increasing the number of red blood cells in the bloodstream to enhance oxygen-carrying capacity. This can be done by transfusing previously stored blood (autologous transfusion) or by using EPO to stimulate red blood cell production.

How Blood Doping Works

  • Blood is drawn from the athlete weeks before competition
  • The body naturally replaces the removed red blood cells
  • The stored blood is reinfused before competition, increasing total red blood cell count
  • More red blood cells = more oxygen transported to working muscles
  • Significantly improves aerobic endurance performance

Sporting Advantages

  • Increased oxygen delivery to muscles
  • Improved VO₂ max and aerobic capacity
  • Delayed onset of fatigue in endurance events
  • Enhanced recovery between training sessions

Side Effects

  • Blood thickening increases risk of blood clots, stroke and heart attack
  • Risk of infection from transfusion process
  • Reactions if blood is not properly stored or matched
  • Increased strain on the heart

⚖️ Advantages and Disadvantages for Sport

For the performer: Advantages include improved performance, faster recovery, ability to compete through injury and potential for greater financial reward. Disadvantages include serious health risks, potential bans and loss of titles/records, reputational damage, and the psychological burden of cheating.
  • Advantages for sport: Higher performance levels may attract more spectators and media interest; pushes the boundaries of human performance
  • Disadvantages for sport: Undermines fair competition, breaks the contract to compete, damages the integrity and credibility of sport, discourages clean performers who feel they cannot compete fairly, sets a dangerous example for young athletes

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Name three types of performance enhancing drugs and explain the sporting advantage of each.

Q2: Explain how EPO and blood doping both improve endurance performance. What are the main health risks?

Q3: Why might a boxer use diuretics? Explain the advantage and the health risks.

Q4: Explain why anabolic steroids might appeal to a weightlifter but are dangerous for a teenage athlete.

Q5: Describe the difference between the effects of stimulants and beta blockers. Give a sport appropriate for each.

Q6: "Taking PEDs is a personal choice and should not be banned." To what extent do you agree? Give reasoned arguments.

✅ Answers

  1. Three types: (1) Stimulants - increase alertness, aggression and reaction speed, useful for sprinters or boxers. (2) Anabolic steroids - increase muscle mass and strength, useful for weightlifters or shot putters. (3) Beta blockers - reduce heart rate and anxiety, steady the hand, useful for archers or snooker players.
  2. EPO stimulates the bone marrow to produce more red blood cells, while blood doping involves reinfusing stored blood to increase red blood cell count. Both methods increase the number of red blood cells, which means more oxygen can be transported to working muscles. This delays fatigue and improves aerobic endurance in events like cycling and long-distance running. Health risks include blood thickening (increased viscosity), which raises the risk of blood clots, stroke and heart attack. Blood doping also carries infection risk from the transfusion process.
  3. A boxer might use diuretics to lose water weight rapidly before a weigh-in, allowing them to compete in a lower weight category. After weighing in, they rehydrate and may fight at a weight significantly above the limit, giving them a size and strength advantage. Health risks include severe dehydration, muscle cramps, kidney damage, electrolyte imbalance and heart arrhythmias from potassium loss.
  4. Anabolic steroids appeal to a weightlifter because they increase muscle mass and strength significantly, allowing heavier lifts and faster recovery from intense training sessions. However, they are particularly dangerous for teenage athletes because they can stunt growth by causing premature closure of the epiphyseal (growth) plates in bones, meaning the teenager may never reach their full adult height. They also cause hormonal disruption during a critical developmental period.
  5. Stimulants increase heart rate, alertness, aggression and reduce the perception of fatigue - they speed the body up. Appropriate sport: boxing or sprinting where aggression and fast reactions are needed. Beta blockers reduce heart rate, anxiety and muscle tremors - they calm the body down. Appropriate sport: archery or snooker where a steady hand and calm focus are needed. They have opposite effects on the nervous system.
  6. Disagree: PEDs give an unfair advantage over clean competitors, breaking the contract to compete. They damage the integrity and credibility of sport, discourage young athletes from competing fairly, and pose serious health risks that performers may feel pressured to accept. Sport should be about natural ability and training effort. Agree (limited): Some argue that if all performers had access, it would level the playing field, and that adults should make their own choices. However, this ignores the health risks, coercion pressure on clean athletes, and the fundamental purpose of sport as a test of human ability without artificial enhancement. Overall, PEDs should remain banned to protect fairness, health and sporting integrity.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Know all six drug types PLUS blood doping - their effects, advantages AND side effects
  • Match each PED to an appropriate sport - EPO = cycling, beta blockers = archery, anabolic steroids = weightlifting
  • Explain HOW each drug works, not just what it does
  • Diuretics have two uses: weight loss AND masking other drugs - know both
  • Blood doping and EPO have similar results but different methods - distinguish them
  • Always discuss both advantages and disadvantages when evaluating PEDs

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse EPO with anabolic steroids. Wrong: "EPO builds muscle mass." Correct: "EPO stimulates red blood cell production in the bone marrow, increasing oxygen-carrying capacity. Anabolic steroids build muscle mass and strength."

Students often forget diuretics' masking function. Wrong: "Diuretics are only used for weight loss." Correct: "Diuretics are used for two reasons: to lose weight rapidly for weight-category sports, AND to dilute urine samples to mask the presence of other banned substances."

📝 Exam Technique

PE Exam Tips:
For engagement patterns, cover ALL factors (gender, race, age, disability, socio-economics). For commercialisation, discuss impacts on ALL stakeholder groups (performers, sport, officials, spectators, sponsors). Evaluate positive AND negative impacts.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think commercialisation has only benefited sport. Wrong: Commercialisation has only benefited sport Correct: While commercialisation brings funding and media coverage, it can also lead to corruption, loss of tradition, pressure on athletes, and inequality between funded and unfunded sports.

Students often think technology in sport is always a good thing. Wrong: Technology in sport is always a good thing Correct: Technology can create unfair advantages (e.g. expensive swimsuits in 2008 Olympics), slow down games (VAR delays), and reduce the human element of officiating.

Students often think peds only affect the athlete who takes them. Wrong: PEDs only affect the athlete who takes them Correct: PEDs affect the whole sport: they create unfair competition, pressure others to dope to compete, damage the sport's reputation, and set dangerous examples for young athletes.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE PE tests four AOs: AO1 (Knowledge, 30%) — recall facts about Performance Enhancing Drugs (PEDs) including definitions, classifications and specific examples; AO2 (Application, 30%) — apply knowledge to sporting contexts and training scenarios; AO3 (Analysis and Evaluation, 25%) — analyse data (graphs, tables), evaluate training methods or strategies, and justify recommendations; AO4 (Practical, 15%) — demonstrate relevant skills. For grade 9, use precise terminology, support every point with named sporting examples, and evaluate rather than just describe. The difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of application and depth of evaluation.
