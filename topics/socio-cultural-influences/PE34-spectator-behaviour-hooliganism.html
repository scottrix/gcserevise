PE34: Spectator Behaviour and Hooliganism

Foundation Higher All Boards Paper 2

Understanding the positive and negative effects of spectators, the causes of hooliganism, and the strategies used to prevent and control spectator violence and disorder.

🏟️ The Role of Spectators in Sport

Spectators: People who watch sporting events, either at the venue in person or through media coverage. Spectators are a vital part of the sporting experience, providing atmosphere, revenue and motivation for performers.

Spectators can have both positive and negative effects on sport:

  • Positive: creating atmosphere, motivating performers, generating revenue
  • Negative: violence, hooliganism, abuse, property damage, disruption

✅ Positive Effects of Spectators

1. Atmosphere

  • Spectators create the atmosphere that makes live sport exciting and unique
  • Singing, chanting and organised support (e.g. football terrace culture)
  • The "12th man" effect where home crowds inspire their team to greater effort
  • Creates a memorable experience for performers and other spectators

2. Revenue

  • Ticket sales provide direct income for clubs and venues
  • Merchandise sales, food and drink revenue at venues
  • Large crowds attract sponsorship and media deals
  • Revenue funds facilities, player wages and grassroots development

3. Home Advantage

  • Teams consistently win more at home than away across most sports
  • Home crowd support can motivate performers and intimidate opponents
  • Familiarity with the venue and lack of travel also contribute
  • Officials may be subconsciously influenced by the home crowd

4. Motivation and Performance

  • Performers feed off the energy and support of the crowd
  • Home support can inspire players to perform beyond their normal level
  • A loud, supportive crowd can increase arousal to optimal levels
  • Crowd reaction can provide feedback on performance quality
Sporting Examples

Home advantage: In the Premier League, home teams consistently win approximately 45-50% of matches compared to 25-30% for away teams. Anfield's famous atmosphere on European nights is widely cited as giving Liverpool a significant home advantage.

Revenue: Manchester United generate over £100m per year from matchday revenue at Old Trafford, funding player transfers, facilities and community programmes.

❌ Negative Effects of Spectators

1. Violence and Disorder

  • Physical violence between rival fans before, during and after matches
  • Fighting inside and outside stadiums
  • Endangering the safety of other spectators, staff and performers
  • Can lead to serious injury and even death

2. Property Damage

  • Vandalism of stadiums, transport infrastructure and surrounding areas
  • Damage to seats, facilities and public property
  • Cost of repairs passed on to clubs, local authorities and taxpayers
  • Businesses near stadiums may suffer damage during disorder

3. Intimidation and Abuse

  • Verbal abuse directed at players, officials and other fans
  • Racist, homophobic and discriminatory chanting
  • Throwing objects onto the pitch (coins, flares, bottles)
  • Pitch invasions that endanger performers and officials

4. Financial and Reputational Costs

  • Clubs fined for spectator disorder
  • Stadium closures and matches played behind closed doors
  • Increased policing and stewarding costs
  • Damage to the reputation of the sport, club and community
  • Reduced attendance as families avoid hostile environments

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿 Hooliganism

Hooliganism: Disorderly, aggressive and violent behaviour by spectators at sporting events, most commonly associated with football. Hooliganism involves organised groups of fans who engage in fighting, vandalism and intimidation, often planning confrontations in advance.

Causes of Hooliganism

1. Rivalries

  • Long-standing rivalries between clubs or nations fuel hatred between fan groups
  • Local derbies (e.g. Manchester United vs Manchester City) intensify tensions
  • Historical incidents between rival fans create cycles of revenge
  • National rivalries (e.g. England vs Germany) can escalate into disorder

2. Alcohol and Drugs

  • Alcohol reduces inhibitions and increases aggression and risk-taking
  • Intoxicated spectators are more likely to engage in violent behaviour
  • Drugs such as cocaine are increasingly associated with hooligan culture
  • Pre-match drinking in pubs near stadiums is a common factor in disorder

3. Gang Culture

  • Organised "firms" or groups plan confrontations with rival gangs
  • Gang membership provides identity, belonging and status
  • Leaders organise violence through social media and encrypted messaging
  • Gang culture is linked to wider social issues of crime and deprivation

4. Other Contributing Factors

  • Media coverage that glorifies or sensationalises hooliganism
  • Social media making it easier to organise confrontations
  • Poor policing or inadequate stewarding at venues
  • Frustration and aggression that has nothing to do with sport but is expressed through it
  • Pack mentality - individuals behave in crowds as they never would alone
Historical Context

Football hooliganism in England peaked in the 1970s and 1980s. Notable tragedies include the Heysel Stadium disaster (1985) where 39 people died after Liverpool fans charged Juventus fans, leading to English clubs being banned from European competition for 5 years. The Hillsborough disaster (1989) where 97 Liverpool fans died led to the Taylor Report and the requirement for all-seater stadiums in the top two divisions.

🛡️ Strategies to Prevent Hooliganism

Various strategies have been implemented to reduce and prevent spectator disorder:

1. Segregation of Fans

  • Rival fans are kept in separate areas of the stadium
  • Dedicated entrances, exits and concourses for home and away supporters
  • Physical barriers between opposing fan sections
  • Separate travel routes to and from the stadium

2. CCTV and Surveillance

  • Extensive CCTV coverage inside and outside stadiums
  • Facial recognition technology to identify known troublemakers
  • Monitoring of social media for planned confrontations
  • Evidence used to prosecute and ban offenders

3. Banning Orders

  • Court orders that ban known hooligans from attending matches
  • Require individuals to surrender passports during international tournaments
  • Must report to police stations during match times
  • Breach of a banning order is a criminal offence with prison sentence

4. Alcohol Restrictions

  • Alcohol cannot be consumed in view of the pitch in English football stadiums
  • Alcohol sales restricted before and during matches
  • Some venues are entirely alcohol-free
  • Police can impose drinking exclusion zones around stadiums

5. Early Kick-Offs

  • High-risk matches scheduled for earlier times (12:30 or 13:30)
  • Reduces the time available for pre-match drinking
  • Lower alcohol consumption leads to reduced aggression
  • Less time for rival groups to organise confrontations

6. All-Seater Stadiums

  • The Taylor Report (1990) recommended all-seater stadiums for top divisions
  • Eliminates the "terraces" where crowds could surge and pressure build
  • Seated spectators are easier to identify and control
  • Significantly reduced crowd disorder and improved safety
  • Cameras identify troublemakers for prosecution

    ❓ Practice Questions

    Q1: Explain three positive effects of spectators on sport.

    Q2: Describe three causes of hooliganism and explain how each contributes to spectator disorder.

    Q3: Explain how banning orders work and evaluate their effectiveness in reducing hooliganism.

    Q4: Why are high-risk football matches sometimes given early kick-off times? Explain the reasoning behind this strategy.

    Q5: Describe three negative effects of spectator behaviour on sport and suggest how one of these could be addressed.

    Q6: Explain how the Taylor Report changed football stadiums in England and why these changes reduced hooliganism.

    ✅ Answers

    1. Three positive effects: (1) Atmosphere - spectators create excitement through singing, chanting and organised support, making the event memorable for performers and fans. (2) Revenue - ticket sales, merchandise and food/drink generate essential income for clubs and venues, funding facilities and development. (3) Home advantage - home crowd support motivates performers and can intimidate opponents, contributing to the statistical advantage that home teams enjoy in most sports.
    2. Three causes: (1) Rivalries - long-standing hatred between clubs (e.g. local derbies) fuels animosity between fan groups, leading to planned confrontations. (2) Alcohol - reduces inhibitions and increases aggression, making intoxicated spectators more likely to engage in violent behaviour. (3) Gang culture - organised firms provide identity and belonging; leaders plan violence through social media, and gang mentality encourages individuals to behave aggressively as part of a group.
    3. Banning orders are court-issued legal orders that prevent known hooligans from attending football matches. Individuals must surrender their passports during international tournaments and report to police stations during match times. Breaching a banning order is a criminal offence punishable by imprisonment. They are effective because they remove known troublemakers from the crowd, significantly reducing the risk of organised disorder. However, they are limited because they can only be applied to individuals who have already been identified and convicted, and new offenders may emerge. They work best in combination with other strategies like CCTV and segregation.
    4. High-risk matches are given early kick-off times (12:30 or 13:30) to reduce the time available for pre-match drinking. When matches kick off early, there is less opportunity for fans to consume large amounts of alcohol in pubs before the game, which reduces the aggression and loss of inhibition associated with intoxication. Early kick-offs also reduce the time available for rival groups to organise confrontations and travel to meeting points. This strategy is moderately effective as it reduces one of the key triggers for disorder.
    5. Three negative effects: (1) Violence between rival fans endangers safety and can cause serious injury. (2) Property damage to stadiums and surrounding areas costs money and resources. (3) Racist and discriminatory abuse towards players and officials creates a hostile environment. Property damage can be addressed through CCTV surveillance that identifies vandals for prosecution, combined with banning orders that remove repeat offenders from future events. The threat of identification and punishment acts as a deterrent.
    6. The Taylor Report (1990), commissioned after the Hillsborough disaster, recommended that all stadiums in the top two English divisions become all-seater. This eliminated standing terraces where crowd surging could occur and where crowd density was difficult to control. All-seater stadiums reduced hooliganism because: every spectator has an allocated seat, making identification easier; there is no standing area where crowds can gather and build group mentality; the controlled seating environment makes it harder for disorder to spread; and the overall atmosphere is more civilised, attracting families and discouraging those seeking confrontation.

    🎯 Exam Tips

    • Always balance positive and negative effects of spectators - examiners want both
    • Know three causes of hooliganism and at least three prevention strategies
    • Explain HOW each strategy works, not just what it is
    • Banning orders = legal orders, not just "being banned" - use the correct term
    • Alcohol restrictions: specific rule = no alcohol in view of the pitch in English football
    • The Taylor Report and all-seater stadiums are key historical context for football

    ⚠️ Common Errors

    Watch Out!

    Students often say alcohol is banned at football. Wrong: "Alcohol is banned at all football matches." Correct: "Alcohol cannot be consumed in view of the pitch at English football league matches, but it can be consumed in concourse areas. The restriction applies to the seating/playing area, not the entire venue."

    Students often describe hooliganism without explaining causes. Wrong: "Hooliganism is when fans fight." Correct: "Hooliganism is organised disorder and violence by spectators, caused by factors including rivalries, alcohol, gang culture and media influence. It is not spontaneous - it is often planned in advance through social media and encrypted messaging."

    ⚠️ Common Errors

    Watch Out!

    Students often think commercialisation has only benefited sport. Wrong: Commercialisation has only benefited sport Correct: While commercialisation brings funding and media coverage, it can also lead to corruption, loss of tradition, pressure on athletes, and inequality between funded and unfunded sports.

    Students often think technology in sport is always a good thing. Wrong: Technology in sport is always a good thing Correct: Technology can create unfair advantages (e.g. expensive swimsuits in 2008 Olympics), slow down games (VAR delays), and reduce the human element of officiating.

    Students often think peds only affect the athlete who takes them. Wrong: PEDs only affect the athlete who takes them Correct: PEDs affect the whole sport: they create unfair competition, pressure others to dope to compete, damage the sport's reputation, and set dangerous examples for young athletes.
