SPG1: Spelling Rules and Patterns

Foundation Higher All Boards AO6

Understanding and applying spelling rules - essential for accurate written communication.

📋 Why Spelling Matters

AO6 Requirement: Use a range of vocabulary and sentence structures for clarity, purpose and effect, with accurate spelling and punctuation. Spelling errors can cost you marks across all writing questions.

Good spelling:

  • Makes your writing clear and professional
  • Helps you achieve higher marks in AO6
  • Creates a positive impression on examiners
  • Is essential for subject-specific vocabulary

🔤 Key Spelling Rules

1. 'i Before e Except After c'

The Rule

Generally: i before e (believe, piece, field, chief)

After c: e before i (receive, deceive, ceiling, conceit)

Exceptions to remember: weird, seize, neither, either, foreign, height, their, heir

2. Doubling Consonants

Double the final consonant when adding -ing, -ed, -er to short vowel sounds:

  • run → running (short 'u' sound)
  • hop → hopped (short 'o' sound)
  • big → bigger (short 'i' sound)

Don't double if the vowel is long or there are two consonants:

  • walk → walking (long vowel)
  • jump → jumped (ends in two consonants)

3. Drop the 'e'

Drop the final 'e' before adding suffixes starting with a vowel:

  • hope + ing = hoping
  • write + er = writer
  • create + ion = creation

Keep the 'e' before suffixes starting with a consonant:

  • hope + ful = hopeful
  • care + less = careless

4. 'y' to 'i' Rule

Change 'y' to 'i' before suffixes (except those starting with 'i'):

  • happy + ness = happiness
  • beauty + ful = beautiful
  • carry + ed = carried

Keep 'y' before -ing:

  • carry + ing = carrying
  • worry + ing = worrying

🎯 Homophones to Master

Word 1 Word 2 Usage Tip
their there their = belonging to them; there = place
your you're your = belonging to you; you're = you are
its it's its = belonging to it; it's = it is
to too / two to = direction; too = also/excessively; two = number
where wear / were where = place; wear = clothing; were = past tense
of off of = belonging/relating; off = away from
then than then = time; than = comparison
affect effect affect = verb (to influence); effect = noun (result)

📝 Commonly Misspelt Words

High-Frequency Errors
  • accommodation - double c, double m
  • separate - there's 'a rat' in sep-a-rate
  • definitely - definitely has 'finite'
  • occurrence - double c, double r
  • necessary - one c, two s (like a shirt has one collar, two sleeves)
  • beginning - double n
  • disappear - dis + appear
  • environment - environ + ment
  • privilege - no 'd' in privilege
  • embarrass - double r, double s

🔧 Prefixes and Suffixes

Common Prefixes

  • un- - unhappy, unnecessary, unusual
  • dis- - disappear, disagree, disadvantage
  • mis- - misunderstand, mistake, mislead
  • pre- - prepare, predict, previous
  • inter- - international, interact, interrupt

Common Suffixes

  • -tion/-sion - action, extension, decision
  • -ment - development, environment, argument
  • -ness - happiness, darkness, awareness
  • -ful/-less - beautiful, hopeless, powerful
  • -able/-ible - comfortable, possible, terrible
Tip: When in doubt, write the word down. Often your eye will catch an error that your brain misses.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Wrong Right Reason
alot a lot Two words - 'a' and 'lot'
could of could have 'of' sounds like 'have' but is wrong
should of should have Same rule as above
seperate separate Remember: sep-a-rate
recieve receive i before e except after c
definately definitely Contains 'finite'

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Which is correct: "their book" or "there book"?

Q2: Spell the word meaning "to influence": affect or effect?

Q3: What is the correct spelling: neccessary or necessary?

Q4: Add the suffix -ing to the word "hop" - what do you get?

Q5: Complete this sentence: "You're going to lose / loose your keys."

✅ Answers

  1. "their book" - their shows possession (belonging to them)
  2. "affect" - affect is the verb meaning to influence
  3. necessary - one c, two s
  4. hopping - double the consonant after a short vowel
  5. lose - lose means to misplace; loose means not tight

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Proofread your work - leave 2-3 minutes at the end
  • Circle words you're unsure about and come back to them
  • Use alternative words if you can't spell something
  • Memorise the top 10 commonly misspelt words
  • Practise homophones - they catch many students out
  • Check subject-specific vocabulary in context

📝 Exam Technique

Spelling Exam Strategy: Spelling accuracy directly affects your AO6 mark (16 marks). Frequent errors can cap you at Level 1 or 2, regardless of how good your content is.
  • Before the exam: Memorise the top 10 commonly misspelt words (accommodation, separate, definitely, necessary, occurrence, beginning, disappear, environment, privilege, embarrass). Learn the mnemonic for each.
  • During writing: If you're unsure how to spell a word, use a simpler alternative you can spell correctly. "Huge" is better than a misspelt "collosal". Accuracy always beats ambition.
  • Proofreading (last 5 minutes): Read through your work specifically looking for spelling errors. Check homophones (their/there/they're, your/you're, its/it's) — these are the most common and most easily fixed errors.
  • Mark allocation: AO6 Level 4 (13-16 marks) requires "accurate" spelling. Level 2 (5-8 marks) describes "some errors." Even 3-4 spelling mistakes can drop you a whole level.
  • What examiners look for: Consistent accuracy, especially with high-frequency words and subject terminology. They don't expect perfection on every obscure word, but basic errors (their/there, seperate) are heavily penalised.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often write "could of" and "should of" instead of "could have" and "should have". Wrong: "I could of gone to the party if I had finished my work." Correct: "I could have gone to the party if I had finished my work." — "of" sounds like the contracted "'ve" but is grammatically incorrect.

Students often confuse homophones — words that sound the same but have different meanings and spellings. Wrong: "Their going to the park over they're to play with there dog." Correct: "They're going to the park over there to play with their dog." — they're = they are, there = place, their = belonging to them.

Students often misspell double-letter words by forgetting one of the pairs. Wrong: "accomodation", "necesary", "begining", "embarass" Correct: "accommodation" (double c, double m), "necessary" (one c, two s — one collar, two sleeves), "beginning" (double n), "embarrass" (double r, double s). Use mnemonics to remember the pattern.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

16 marks (AO6): Write a short formal paragraph about the environment, demonstrating consistently accurate spelling of high-frequency and subject-specific words.

The preservation of our environment is a matter of considerable urgency. Deforestation continues to accelerate at an unprecedented rate, with devastating consequences for biodiversity and climate stability. According to recent research, approximately 80% of the world's forests have already been destroyed or irreversibly degraded, and scientists emphasise that immediate intervention is necessary to prevent further deterioration. The disappearance of species, the pollution of oceans, and the occurrence of extreme weather events are all linked to humanity's persistent failure to accommodate environmental sustainability within economic planning. It is essential that governments worldwide commit to comprehensive strategies that address these interrelated challenges before the damage becomes truly irreversible.

Mark scheme: AO6 (14-16/16) — All high-frequency words spelt correctly: "environment", "deforestation", "accelerate", "unprecedented", "devastating", "biodiversity", "approximately", "disappearance", "occurrence", "accommodate", "irreversibly", "deterioration", "disappear", "necessary", "persistent". All homophones correct ("their" used correctly). Subject-specific vocabulary spelt accurately. Consistent accuracy throughout with zero spelling errors.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO6 — Technical Accuracy (16 marks): Spelling is a core component of AO6. At grade 5 (Level 3, 9-12 marks), spelling is "mostly accurate" — there are some errors, particularly with ambitious vocabulary and common traps (separate, definitely, necessary). These errors are noticeable but don't significantly impede meaning. At grade 9 (Level 4, 13-16 marks), spelling is "accurate" across the board — high-frequency words, ambitious vocabulary, and subject terminology are all consistently correct. The critical difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is consistency under pressure: a grade 9 writer maintains accuracy even when using ambitious vocabulary, while a grade 5 writer may spell common words correctly but stumble on more complex ones. The practical advice is simple: if you cannot spell a word confidently, use an alternative you can. "Huge" spelt correctly always beats "colossal" spelt incorrectly.

AO5 — Content and Organisation (24 marks): Spelling also indirectly affects AO5 because frequent spelling errors create a poor impression that can colour the examiner's reading of your content. More practically, if you avoid ambitious vocabulary because you fear misspelling it, your content may be less compelling. The solution is to learn the spelling of the ambitious words you most want to use, so you can deploy them with confidence.
