PE22: Skill Classification

Foundation Higher All Boards Paper 2

Understanding how skills are classified using different continua, including Knapp's continuum, the open/closed continuum, self-paced/externally paced, and gross/fine continua.

🎯 Skill vs Ability

Skill: A learned action with a predetermined result that can be reproduced consistently with minimum effort and time. Skills are developed through practice and experience.
Ability: An innate, inherited trait that underpins skilled performance. Abilities are the building blocks of skills and are largely genetically determined.

Key differences:

Skill Ability
Learned through practice Inherited and genetic
Can be improved significantly with training Relatively fixed and stable
Specific to a particular activity General and transferable
E.g. A tennis serve, a football pass E.g. Balance, coordination, reaction time
Relationship: Ability + Practice → Skill
You need underlying abilities to develop skills, but practice is essential to turn ability into skill. Without ability, a skill cannot be fully developed.

📊 Knapp's Continuum: Basic to Complex

Knapp's continuum classifies skills from basic to complex based on how much decision-making and coordination is required.

Basic Skills

  • Simple movements with few sub-routines
  • Require little concentration and decision-making
  • Learned quickly and performed consistently
  • Often fundamental movements

Complex Skills

  • Require high levels of concentration and coordination
  • Involve many sub-routines that must be performed in sequence
  • Take longer to learn and master
  • More difficult to reproduce consistently
Sporting Examples

Basic: A standing long jump, a chest pass in netball, a sprint start.

Complex: A gymnastics floor routine, a tennis serve, a javelin throw with full approach.

Exam Tip: Most skills fall somewhere between the two extremes. A netball chest pass is basic, but adding a defender and deciding when to pass makes it more complex. Context matters.

🔄 Open/Closed Continuum

This continuum classifies skills based on how much the environment influences the performance of the skill.

Open Skills

  • Performed in an unpredictable, changing environment
  • Require the performer to adapt their technique to external factors
  • Influenced by opponents, teammates, weather, and playing surface
  • Timing and technique must be adjusted constantly

Closed Skills

  • Performed in a predictable, stable environment
  • The performer controls when to start the skill
  • Technique follows a set pattern with little variation
  • Self-paced and internally regulated
Sporting Examples

Open: A football pass (opponents moving, teammates positioning), a rugby tackle (opponent's actions vary), returning a tennis shot (different spin, speed, placement).

Closed: A gymnastics vault (set apparatus, controlled run-up), a swimming start (still water, fixed blocks), a darts throw (consistent environment).

Feature Open Skills Closed Skills
Environment Unpredictable, changing Predictable, stable
Decision-making High - must adapt quickly Low - follows set pattern
External factors Opponents, teammates, conditions Minimal external influence
Pacing Often externally paced Usually self-paced

⏱️ Self-Paced vs Externally Paced Continuum

This continuum classifies skills based on who controls the timing and initiation of the movement.

Self-Paced Skills

  • The performer decides when to start the skill
  • No external pressure on timing
  • The performer controls the speed and rhythm
  • Usually performed in stable environments

Externally Paced Skills

  • The timing is determined by external factors
  • Opponents, a ball, or an event dictates when the skill must be performed
  • Requires quick reaction and adaptation
  • Usually performed in dynamic, changing environments
Sporting Examples

Self-paced: A golf drive (you choose when to swing), a free throw in basketball (you control the timing), a gymnastics routine (you set the pace).

Externally paced: Returning a badminton smash (opponent's shot dictates your response), a football tackle (opponent's movement triggers the action), a cricket batting shot (bowler's delivery determines timing).

💪 Gross/Fine Continuum

This continuum classifies skills based on the size of the muscle groups used and the precision of movement required.

Gross Skills

  • Involve large muscle groups and whole-body movements
  • Require strength, power and large-scale coordination
  • Less precision needed in the movement
  • Often fundamental, large-range actions

Fine Skills

  • Involve small muscle groups and precise, intricate movements
  • Require high levels of accuracy and coordination
  • Often involve hand-eye coordination
  • Subtle, controlled actions
Sporting Examples

Gross: A rugby tackle, a long jump, a rowing stroke, a press-up.

Fine: A snooker shot, archery release, darts throw, spinning a cricket ball (wrist action).

Continuum Gross End Fine End
Muscle groups Large (legs, back, chest) Small (fingers, wrist, hand)
Precision Low - powerful movements High - delicate movements
Energy cost High Low
Example sport Weightlifting, rowing Snooker, archery, darts

📊 Using Multiple Continua Together

Skills can be placed on multiple continua simultaneously. This is important for understanding how to coach and practise different skills effectively.

Example: A tennis serve
Knapp's continuum: Complex (many sub-routines)
Open/Closed: Mostly closed (performer controls the start, stable environment)
Self/Externally paced: Self-paced (player decides when to serve)
Gross/Fine: Mixed (large muscle groups for power, fine wrist action for spin)
Example: A football tackle
Knapp's continuum: Basic (relatively simple movement)
Open/Closed: Open (opponent's movement varies)
Self/Externally paced: Externally paced (must react to opponent)
Gross/Fine: Gross (large muscle groups, whole body)
Key Principle: The more open and externally paced a skill is, the more decision-making is required. Practice should therefore include game-like situations. Closed, self-paced skills can be practised in isolated, repetitive drills.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Define the terms 'skill' and 'ability' and explain the relationship between them.

Q2: Using a named sporting example, explain the difference between an open skill and a closed skill.

Q3: Classify a golf drive using all four skill classification continua.

Q4: Why is it important for a coach to understand skill classification when planning practice sessions?

Q5: Explain the difference between a self-paced skill and an externally paced skill, giving one example of each.

Q6: A gymnastics floor routine is classified as complex and closed. Explain why.

✅ Answers

  1. A skill is a learned action with a predetermined result that can be reproduced consistently with minimum effort. An ability is an innate, inherited trait that underpins skilled performance. The relationship is that abilities form the foundation for skills - without the necessary abilities, a skill cannot be fully developed, and practice turns ability into skill.
  2. An open skill is performed in an unpredictable environment and must be adapted to changing conditions. A closed skill is performed in a predictable, stable environment. Example: A football pass is open because the player must adapt to the position of opponents and teammates, the movement of the ball, and the playing surface. A swimming start is closed because the water is still, the blocks are fixed, and the swimmer controls when to dive.
  3. A golf drive: Knapp's continuum - complex (requires coordination of grip, stance, backswing, downswing, follow-through); Open/Closed - closed (stable environment, no opponents interfering); Self/Externally paced - self-paced (golfer decides when to swing); Gross/Fine - mixed (large muscle groups for power, fine wrist/hand control for club face accuracy).
  4. A coach needs to understand skill classification because it determines the type of practice required. Open and externally paced skills need game-like, variable practice with decision-making. Closed and self-paced skills benefit from fixed, repetitive drills. Complex skills may need to be broken down into sub-routines (part practice), while basic skills can be practised whole.
  5. A self-paced skill is one where the performer controls when to initiate the movement, e.g. a free throw in basketball where the player decides when to shoot. An externally paced skill is one where external factors determine the timing, e.g. returning a tennis serve where the opponent's delivery dictates when the return must be played.
  6. A gymnastics floor routine is complex because it involves many sub-routines (tumbling, jumps, spins, balances) that must be performed in a precise sequence, requiring high concentration and coordination. It is closed because the environment is predictable - the performer is alone on a set apparatus with no opponents to react to, and the routine follows a predetermined pattern.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always give sporting examples when classifying skills - marks depend on application
  • Remember that skills are on a continuum, not either/or - explain where on the continuum
  • Use the terminology precisely: "open/closed continuum" not "open or closed skill type"
  • When asked to classify a skill, use all four continua where possible
  • Link classification to practice type - open skills need variable practice, closed skills need fixed practice
  • Know the difference between skill and ability - this is a common 2-mark question

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often treat continua as categories rather than spectrums. Wrong: "A tennis serve is a closed skill." Correct: "A tennis serve is towards the closed end of the open/closed continuum because the server controls the initiation, though the serve must still consider the receiver's position."

Students often confuse ability with fitness. Wrong: "Ability is how fit you are." Correct: "Ability is an innate, inherited trait such as balance, coordination or reaction time - it is not the same as fitness."

📝 Exam Technique

PE Exam Tips:
Use classification continua (not just labels). For goal setting, always use SMART. Know the information processing model stages. For arousal questions, draw the inverted-U graph and explain optimal levels for different skill types.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think aggression is always bad in sport. Wrong: Aggression is always bad in sport Correct: Direct aggression (within rules, e.g. a hard but fair tackle) can be channelled positively. It is only hostile aggression (intent to harm outside rules) that is always negative.

Students often think extroverts are always better at sport than introverts. Wrong: Extroverts are always better at sport than introverts Correct: Introverts may excel in individual, precise sports (archery, golf, marathon running). Extroverts tend to prefer team and contact sports. Personality is a preference, not a determinant.

Students often think more arousal always means better performance. Wrong: More arousal always means better performance Correct: The inverted-U theory shows that performance improves with arousal only up to an optimal point. Beyond that, performance declines. Fine/closed skills need lower arousal; gross/open skills need higher.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

GCSE PE tests four AOs: AO1 (Knowledge, 30%) — recall facts about Skill Classification including definitions, classifications and specific examples; AO2 (Application, 30%) — apply knowledge to sporting contexts and training scenarios; AO3 (Analysis and Evaluation, 25%) — analyse data (graphs, tables), evaluate training methods or strategies, and justify recommendations; AO4 (Practical, 15%) — demonstrate relevant skills. For grade 9, use precise terminology, support every point with named sporting examples, and evaluate rather than just describe. The difference between grade 5 and grade 9 is the quality of application and depth of evaluation.
