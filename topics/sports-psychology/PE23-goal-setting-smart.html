PE23: Goal Setting and SMART Targets

Foundation Higher All Boards Paper 2

Understanding performance and outcome goals, SMART targets, and how effective goal setting can optimise sporting performance.

Fastmail — Private, reliable emailTry it free →

🎯 Why Set Goals?

Goal setting: The process of identifying something you want to achieve and establishing measurable objectives and timeframes to work towards it. Goal setting provides direction, motivation and focus for performers.

Benefits of goal setting in sport:

  • Provides clear direction and purpose for training
  • Increases motivation and commitment
  • Helps performers focus on improvement rather than just winning
  • Allows progress to be measured and tracked
  • Builds confidence when goals are achieved
  • Reduces anxiety by breaking large targets into manageable steps
  • Helps performers and coaches evaluate the effectiveness of training programmes

🏆 Performance Goals vs Outcome Goals

Goals can be categorised into two main types. Understanding the difference is crucial for effective goal setting.

Performance Goals

  • Focus on the process and technique of performance
  • Based on the performer's own standards and improvements
  • Within the performer's control
  • More effective for long-term motivation and development
  • Measured against personal previous performance

Outcome Goals

  • Focus on the end result or outcome of performance
  • Often involve comparison with others (winning, placing)
  • Not entirely within the performer's control (opponents matter)
  • Can increase anxiety and pressure if over-relied upon
  • Measured against external standards or other performers
Feature Performance Goal Outcome Goal
Focus Process and technique Result and comparison
Control Within performer's control Partly outside performer's control
Example "Improve my 100m time by 0.3s" "Win the 100m race at counties"
Failure impact Can adjust and keep working Can demotivate if not achieved
Effectiveness More effective for sustained motivation Can create unhealthy pressure
Sporting Examples

Performance goal: "I will improve my serve accuracy from 60% to 75% in the next 4 weeks." - This focuses on the technique and is within the tennis player's control.

Outcome goal: "I will win the regional tennis tournament." - This depends on the quality of opponents and is partly outside the player's control.

Exam Tip: Performance goals are generally more effective because they are within the performer's control. Even if you lose a match, you can still achieve a performance goal. This maintains motivation. Outcome goals are useful for long-term ambition but should be supported by performance goals.

📏 SMART Targets

For goals to be effective, they should follow the SMART principle. Each letter represents a characteristic that makes goals more achievable.

SMART Principle:
S = Specific - The goal should clearly state what needs to be achieved
M = Measurable - There should be a way to track progress and know when the goal is achieved
A = Accepted - The goal should be agreed upon by the performer and coach
R = Realistic - The goal should be achievable given the performer's current ability
T = Time-bound - There should be a clear deadline for achieving the goal

S - Specific

  • Vague goals like "get better at football" are not specific enough
  • A specific goal clearly identifies what will be improved
  • State the exact skill, fitness component or performance area
  • E.g. "Improve my free throw shooting percentage"

M - Measurable

  • There must be a way to measure progress objectively
  • Use times, distances, percentages, or scores
  • Without measurement, you cannot track improvement
  • E.g. "Increase my free throw percentage from 55% to 70%"

A - Accepted

  • The performer must agree with and commit to the goal
  • The coach and performer should discuss and set goals together
  • Goals imposed without agreement lack motivation
  • When the performer owns the goal, effort and commitment increase

R - Realistic

  • The goal must be achievable based on current ability and available time
  • Too easy = no motivation; too hard = demoralising
  • Consider the performer's starting level and resources available
  • E.g. Dropping 10 seconds off a 1500m time in one week is unrealistic

T - Time-bound

  • A clear timeframe creates urgency and prevents procrastination
  • Short-term goals (days/weeks) and long-term goals (months/years)
  • Without a deadline, goals can drift and motivation fades
  • E.g. "Achieve this improvement within 6 weeks"

✍️ SMART Goals in Practice

SMART Goal Example 1: Swimming

Not SMART: "I want to swim faster."

SMART: "I will reduce my 100m freestyle time from 1:15 to 1:10 within 8 weeks by attending three extra sprint sessions per week."

  • Specific: 100m freestyle time improvement
  • Measurable: From 1:15 to 1:10 (5-second target)
  • Accepted: Agreed with my coach and committed to
  • Realistic: 5 seconds over 8 weeks with extra training is achievable
  • Time-bound: Within 8 weeks
SMART Goal Example 2: Netball

Not SMART: "I want to be a better shooter."

SMART: "I will improve my goal shooting accuracy from 65% to 80% by the end of the season (12 weeks) by practising 50 shots after each training session."

  • Specific: Goal shooting accuracy
  • Measurable: From 65% to 80%
  • Accepted: Agreed with coach and team captain
  • Realistic: 15% improvement over a season with extra practice is achievable
  • Time-bound: By the end of the season (12 weeks)

📈 Evaluating Goal Effectiveness

It is important to regularly evaluate whether goals are being achieved and whether the goal-setting approach is working.

  • Review progress regularly - check if you are on track to meet your target
  • Adjust goals if necessary - if a goal is too easy or too hard, modify it
  • Record results - keep a training diary to track improvements over time
  • Seek feedback - discuss progress with coaches and use video analysis
  • Reset goals - once a goal is achieved, set a new, slightly harder goal
Goal Achievement Cycle: Set goal → Work towards it → Evaluate progress → Adjust if needed → Achieve goal → Set new goal. This cycle ensures continuous improvement and prevents complacency after achieving a goal.

🚀 Using Goals to Optimise Performance

Goal setting can be used strategically to optimise performance in different ways:

  • Short-term goals provide stepping stones towards long-term targets
  • Performance goals keep motivation high even when losing
  • Process goals focus on technique execution during performance
  • Goal specificity directs attention to the most important aspects of training
  • Goal difficulty should be challenging but achievable to maximise effort
Goal Setting for Optimisation:
Long-term outcome goal (e.g. "Make the county team")
→ Medium-term performance goal (e.g. "Run 1500m under 5:00 by March")
→ Short-term process goals (e.g. "Complete 3 interval sessions this week")
Breaking large goals into smaller steps makes them more manageable and maintains motivation.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between a performance goal and an outcome goal, using a sporting example.

Q2: What does each letter in SMART stand for? Explain why each element is important.

Q3: Rewrite this non-SMART goal as a SMART goal: "I want to be a better runner."

Q4: Why are performance goals generally considered more effective than outcome goals?

Q5: How can a performer evaluate the effectiveness of their goal-setting programme?

Q6: Explain why a goal that is too easy can be just as problematic as a goal that is too difficult.

✅ Answers

  1. A performance goal focuses on improving a specific aspect of the performer's own performance, while an outcome goal focuses on the result, often compared to others. Example: A performance goal for a swimmer is "reduce my 100m backstroke time by 3 seconds" because it focuses on personal improvement and is within their control. An outcome goal is "win the 100m backstroke at the regional championships" because it depends on the standard of other competitors.
  2. S = Specific (clearly states what needs to be achieved, giving direction); M = Measurable (allows progress to be tracked objectively); A = Accepted (performer agrees and commits, increasing motivation); R = Realistic (achievable given current ability, preventing demotivation); T = Time-bound (creates urgency and prevents drift).
  3. "I will reduce my 1500m time from 6:30 to 6:15 within 10 weeks by completing three training sessions per week including one interval session." (Specific: 1500m time; Measurable: from 6:30 to 6:15; Accepted: agreed with coach; Realistic: 15 seconds over 10 weeks; Time-bound: within 10 weeks.)
  4. Performance goals are more effective because they are within the performer's control. Even if a performer loses a match, they can still achieve performance goals (e.g. improved passing accuracy), which maintains motivation. Outcome goals depend partly on the quality of opponents, so failing to achieve them can demotivate even when the performer has improved.
  5. A performer can evaluate effectiveness by: reviewing progress against measurable targets at regular intervals, keeping a training diary to track improvements, discussing progress with their coach, using video analysis to assess technique, and checking whether goals have been achieved within the set timeframe. If goals are consistently not met, they may need to be more realistic.
  6. A goal that is too easy provides no challenge, so the performer may not put in maximum effort and motivation will be low. They may become complacent and not push themselves to improve. A goal that is too difficult can demoralise the performer when they fail to achieve it, leading to frustration, reduced confidence and potentially giving up. Both scenarios fail to optimise performance.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always define SMART with full explanations - don't just list the words
  • Use sporting examples for every concept - examiners want to see application
  • Performance goals = within your control; Outcome goals = depend on others
  • When writing SMART goals, include all five elements explicitly
  • Remember that goals should be reviewed and adjusted regularly
  • Link goal setting to motivation - this is the key benefit examiners look for

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse "Accepted" with "Achievable" in SMART. Wrong: "A = Achievable - the goal can be reached." Correct: "A = Accepted - the goal is agreed upon by the performer and coach, ensuring commitment." (Achievable overlaps with Realistic.)

Students often write goals that are not measurable. Wrong: "I will get better at passing." Correct: "I will improve my pass completion rate from 70% to 85%."

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think aggression is always bad in sport. Wrong: Aggression is always bad in sport Correct: Direct aggression (within rules, e.g. a hard but fair tackle) can be channelled positively. It is only hostile aggression (intent to harm outside rules) that is always negative.

Students often think extroverts are always better at sport than introverts. Wrong: Extroverts are always better at sport than introverts Correct: Introverts may excel in individual, precise sports (archery, golf, marathon running). Extroverts tend to prefer team and contact sports. Personality is a preference, not a determinant.

Students often think more arousal always means better performance. Wrong: More arousal always means better performance Correct: The inverted-U theory shows that performance improves with arousal only up to an optimal point. Beyond that, performance declines. Fine/closed skills need lower arousal; gross/open skills need higher.
