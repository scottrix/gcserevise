PE25: Guidance in Sport

Foundation Higher All Boards Paper 2

Understanding the four types of guidance in sport - visual, verbal, manual and mechanical - and when each is most effective for beginners and elite performers.

📝 What is Guidance?

Guidance: The help and direction given to a performer to assist them in learning and developing skills. Coaches use different types of guidance to support performers at different stages of learning and for different types of skill.

The four types of guidance are:

  • Visual guidance - showing the performer what to do through demonstration
  • Verbal guidance - explaining what to do through instructions and cues
  • Manual guidance - physically moving or supporting the performer
  • Mechanical guidance - using equipment or devices to support the performer
Choosing Guidance: The type of guidance used should match the performer's stage of learning and the nature of the skill. Beginners benefit more from visual and manual/mechanical guidance. Elite performers benefit more from verbal guidance for fine-tuning.

👁️ Visual Guidance

Visual Guidance: Showing the performer how to perform a skill through demonstrations, videos, or diagrams. The performer watches and copies the correct technique.

Methods of Visual Guidance

  • Live demonstration - coach or peer performs the skill correctly
  • Video analysis - watching recordings of correct technique or own performance
  • Diagrams and posters - visual aids showing technique or tactics
  • Mirrors - allow the performer to see their own technique in real time

Advantages

  • Gives a clear picture of what the skill should look like
  • Easiest way for beginners to understand a new skill
  • Performer can copy the movement pattern directly
  • Can highlight key body positions and timing

Disadvantages

  • May not capture the feel (kinaesthesia) of the movement
  • Poor demonstrations can lead to incorrect technique being copied
  • Does not explain why a technique works
  • Less useful for experienced performers who already know the technique
Sporting Examples

Beginner: A gymnastics coach demonstrates a cartwheel step by step, allowing the beginner to see the hand placement and body rotation before attempting it.

Elite: A sprinter watches slow-motion video of their start technique compared to an elite model, identifying subtle differences in block clearance angle.

🗣️ Verbal Guidance

Verbal Guidance: Explaining how to perform a skill using spoken instructions, coaching points, or cue words. The performer listens and interprets the information.

Methods of Verbal Guidance

  • Full explanation - detailed description of technique and tactics
  • Coaching points - key technical points to focus on
  • Cue words - short, memorable phrases (e.g. "eye on the ball", "follow through")
  • Questioning - asking the performer to think about their technique

Advantages

  • Quick and easy to deliver during practice or competition
  • Can explain the "why" behind a technique, not just the "what"
  • Cue words are effective for reminding performers of key points under pressure
  • Useful for experienced performers who need fine adjustments, not full demonstrations

Disadvantages

  • Can overload beginners with too much information
  • Without visual support, beginners may misunderstand instructions
  • Not effective for performers who are visual or kinaesthetic learners
  • Too much talking can slow down practice and reduce activity time
Sporting Examples

Beginner: A swimming coach says "reach, pull, push" to remind a beginner of the arm action phases in front crawl. Short, simple cues prevent information overload.

Elite: A golf coach says "smooth takeaway, hinge the wrists earlier" to give a tour player a specific technical adjustment. The elite player already has the basic technique and only needs verbal fine-tuning.

✋ Manual Guidance

Manual Guidance: The coach physically moves, supports or guides the performer's body to help them experience the correct movement. This provides kinaesthetic awareness of the skill.

Methods of Manual Guidance

  • Physical support - holding the performer to prevent falls or injury
  • Moving limbs - guiding the performer's arms or legs through the correct range of motion
  • Resistance - providing resistance to help the performer feel the correct muscle action
  • Partner work - a partner supports or guides the movement

Advantages

  • Helps the performer feel the correct movement (develops kinaesthetic awareness)
  • Reduces fear and builds confidence, especially for dangerous skills
  • Ensures safety for beginners attempting complex or risky movements
  • Allows the performer to experience the movement without needing to generate all the force

Disadvantages

  • Performer may become over-reliant on the support and struggle to perform independently
  • Can slow down the learning process if used for too long
  • Does not allow the performer to develop their own feel for the correct timing
  • Requires physical contact which some performers may find uncomfortable
Sporting Examples

Beginner: A gymnastics coach supports a beginner during a backflip by holding their hips and guiding the rotation, allowing them to experience the movement safely.

Beginner: A tennis coach stands behind a beginner and guides their arm through the correct forehand swing path, helping them feel the correct technique.

Important: Manual guidance should be gradually reduced (faded) as the performer gains confidence and ability. If the coach continues to provide physical support for too long, the performer becomes dependent and cannot perform the skill independently.

⚙️ Mechanical Guidance

Mechanical Guidance: Using equipment, devices or apparatus to support the performer and help them experience the correct movement. The equipment guides or constrains the movement.

Methods of Mechanical Guidance

  • Harnesses - support body weight or restrict unsafe movement (e.g. climbing harness)
  • Belts and ropes - swimming aids that support the body in the correct position
  • Aids and apparatus - training equipment that guides movement (e.g. bowling machine)
  • Safety equipment - crash mats, bungee cords, spotting rigs

Advantages

  • Provides consistent, repeatable support that a human cannot always provide
  • Greatly reduces the risk of injury for dangerous skills
  • Allows performers to attempt skills they could not safely try unsupported
  • Builds confidence as the performer knows they are protected

Disadvantages

  • Performer may become dependent on the equipment
  • The feel of the movement with equipment may differ from unassisted performance
  • Can be expensive or not always available
  • May create an artificial environment that doesn't transfer to competition
Sporting Examples

Beginner: A swimming teacher uses a float or pull buoy to support a beginner's legs, allowing them to focus on their arm technique without worrying about sinking.

Beginner: A trampolining coach uses a spotting rig with overhead harness to allow a beginner to practise somersaults safely without the risk of landing on their head.

All levels: A bowling machine in cricket delivers consistent balls, allowing batters to focus purely on their shot technique without variation in delivery.

📊 Comparing Types of Guidance

Type How it Works Best For Limitation
Visual Demonstration, video, diagrams Beginners learning new skills No feel of the movement
Verbal Instructions, cues, coaching points Experienced performers for fine-tuning Can overload beginners
Manual Physical support and guidance Beginners for dangerous/complex skills Risk of over-reliance
Mechanical Equipment and devices Beginners for safety and consistency Artificial feel; dependency risk

🔄 Guidance for Different Stages of Learning

Cognitive Stage (Beginner)

  • Visual guidance is most important - the performer needs to see what the skill looks like
  • Manual and mechanical guidance help with safety and experiencing the movement
  • Verbal guidance should be kept simple - short cues only
  • Demonstrations should be frequent and accurate

Associative Stage (Intermediate)

  • Visual guidance is still useful but less dominant
  • Verbal guidance becomes more important for refining technique
  • Manual/mechanical guidance should be gradually reduced (faded)
  • Performer starts to use intrinsic feedback more

Autonomous Stage (Elite)

  • Verbal guidance is most useful - small cues and tactical reminders
  • Visual guidance used mainly for video analysis of own performance
  • Manual and mechanical guidance rarely needed
  • Performer self-corrects using intrinsic feedback

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Describe the four types of guidance used in sport and give a sporting example of each.

Q2: Explain why visual guidance is particularly effective for beginners in the cognitive stage of learning.

Q3: What is the main disadvantage of manual guidance and how can a coach address this?

Q4: A swimming coach uses a pull buoy with a beginner. Name the type of guidance and explain why it is appropriate.

Q5: Explain why verbal guidance is more effective for an elite performer than for a complete beginner.

Q6: Compare manual and mechanical guidance, explaining one similarity and two differences.

✅ Answers

  1. Visual guidance: showing a skill through demonstration, e.g. a coach demonstrating a tennis serve. Verbal guidance: explaining a skill through instructions or cue words, e.g. "keep your eye on the ball" in cricket. Manual guidance: physically moving or supporting the performer, e.g. a coach guiding a beginner's arm through a javelin throw technique. Mechanical guidance: using equipment to support the performer, e.g. a trampolining harness for practising somersaults.
  2. Visual guidance is most effective for beginners because they have no prior experience of the skill and need to see what the correct technique looks like. A demonstration provides a clear mental image of the movement that the beginner can copy. Beginners in the cognitive stage learn best by watching and imitating, as they do not yet have the motor programmes stored in long-term memory to understand verbal instructions alone.
  3. The main disadvantage of manual guidance is that the performer may become over-reliant on the physical support and struggle to perform the skill independently. A coach can address this by gradually reducing (fading) the manual support as the performer gains confidence and ability, eventually removing it completely so the performer can execute the skill unassisted.
  4. This is mechanical guidance. The pull buoy is a piece of equipment that supports the swimmer's legs, keeping them buoyant. It is appropriate for a beginner because it allows them to focus on their arm pull technique without having to coordinate the leg kick simultaneously. It also provides safety by preventing the swimmer from sinking, building confidence in the water.
  5. Verbal guidance is more effective for elite performers because they already have well-established motor programmes and understand the basic technique. They only need small, specific verbal cues to fine-tune their performance (e.g. "hinge wrists earlier"). For a complete beginner, verbal guidance alone is often insufficient because they have no mental picture of the skill and may misunderstand or become overloaded by instructions without a visual reference.
  6. Similarity: Both manual and mechanical guidance provide physical support to help the performer experience the correct movement safely. Differences: (1) Manual guidance involves human physical contact (coach's hands), while mechanical guidance uses equipment/devices. (2) Manual guidance can be adjusted in real-time by the coach, whereas mechanical guidance provides consistent but less adaptable support.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always give a sporting example when describing each type of guidance
  • Link guidance type to the stage of learning - beginners vs elite
  • Remember to discuss both advantages and disadvantages for each type
  • Use the term "fading" when discussing how manual/mechanical guidance should be gradually removed
  • Verbal guidance for beginners = short cue words only; for elite = detailed technical adjustments
  • Manual and mechanical both address safety and kinaesthetic awareness but differ in delivery method

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse manual and mechanical guidance. Wrong: "Manual guidance is when you use a harness." Correct: "Manual guidance is when the coach physically moves or supports the performer's body with their hands. Mechanical guidance uses equipment or devices such as harnesses, floats or belts."

Students often say guidance should be used independently rather than combined. Wrong: "A coach should only use visual guidance for beginners." Correct: "Coaches often combine types of guidance - for example, demonstrating a skill (visual) while giving key coaching points (verbal) to reinforce learning."

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think aggression is always bad in sport. Wrong: Aggression is always bad in sport Correct: Direct aggression (within rules, e.g. a hard but fair tackle) can be channelled positively. It is only hostile aggression (intent to harm outside rules) that is always negative.

Students often think extroverts are always better at sport than introverts. Wrong: Extroverts are always better at sport than introverts Correct: Introverts may excel in individual, precise sports (archery, golf, marathon running). Extroverts tend to prefer team and contact sports. Personality is a preference, not a determinant.

Students often think more arousal always means better performance. Wrong: More arousal always means better performance Correct: The inverted-U theory shows that performance improves with arousal only up to an optimal point. Beyond that, performance declines. Fine/closed skills need lower arousal; gross/open skills need higher.
