PE28: Aggression, Personality and Motivation

Foundation Higher All Boards Paper 2

Understanding direct and indirect aggression, introvert and extrovert personality types, and intrinsic and extrinsic motivation in sport, with evaluation of each concept.

💢 Aggression in Sport

Aggression: Behaviour that is intended to harm another person or object. In sport, aggression must be distinguished from assertion - assertion is forceful but legitimate play within the rules, while aggression goes beyond the rules with intent to harm.
Key Distinction:
Assertion (channelled aggression): Forceful, legitimate play within the rules. E.g. A strong, fair shoulder charge in rugby.
Aggression: Behaviour with intent to harm, outside the rules. E.g. Punching an opponent in rugby.
Assertion is positive and legal. Aggression is negative and illegal.

🥊 Direct Aggression

Direct Aggression: Aggression that is aimed directly at another person, involving physical contact or confrontation with the intent to cause harm.
  • Involves direct physical contact with an opponent
  • Is clearly visible and easy to identify
  • Usually results in a penalty, sending-off or ban
  • More common in contact sports
Sporting Examples

Rugby: A player deliberately punching an opponent during a ruck.

Football: A dangerous two-footed tackle aimed at the player, not the ball.

Boxing: Hitting after the bell or a low blow (outside the agreed rules).

Ice hockey: A player deliberately slashing an opponent with their stick.

🗣️ Indirect Aggression

Indirect Aggression: Aggression that is not aimed directly at another person. It may involve verbal abuse, psychological intimidation, or aggression directed at objects rather than people.
  • Does not involve physical contact with the victim
  • Can be verbal, psychological or aimed at objects
  • Harder for officials to detect and penalise
  • Can be just as harmful as direct aggression
Sporting Examples

Football: A player verbally abusing an opponent with insulting language.

Tennis: Smashing a racket in frustration (aggression towards an object).

Cricket: A bowler deliberately bowling a beamer (head-high full toss) at a batter to intimidate.

Rugby: A player shouting abuse at the referee or an opponent from a distance.

Feature Direct Aggression Indirect Aggression
Target Directly at a person Verbal, psychological, or at objects
Physical contact Yes - physical harm intended No physical contact with victim
Detection Easily seen by officials Harder to detect
Example Punching an opponent Verbal abuse or intimidation
Consequence Usually penalised immediately May go unpunished

👤 Personality Types: Introvert vs Extrovert

Personality: The unique combination of characteristics, traits and behaviours that make an individual distinct. In sport, personality influences the type of activity a performer is drawn to and how they respond to different situations.

Introverts

  • Prefer individual sports and activities
  • Thrive in quiet, controlled environments
  • Self-motivated and internally focused
  • Comfortable with sustained concentration and precision
  • May find team interaction and crowd noise draining
  • Perform best with lower arousal levels

Extroverts

  • Prefer team sports and contact activities
  • Thrive in social, dynamic environments
  • Energised by interaction with others
  • Enjoy competition, excitement and risk-taking
  • May struggle with prolonged concentration on fine tasks
  • Perform best with higher arousal levels
Feature Introvert Extrovert
Social preference Solitary, individual Social, team-based
Suitable sports Archery, swimming, golf, athletics Rugby, football, netball, basketball
Arousal preference Lower arousal Higher arousal
Environment Quiet, controlled Dynamic, social
Concentration Long, sustained focus Quick, reactive focus
Motivation source Internal (self-satisfaction) External (social recognition)
Sporting Examples

Introvert: A long-distance runner who trains alone, focuses inwardly during races, and prefers the solitude of individual performance. The quiet, controlled environment of distance running suits their personality.

Extrovert: A rugby player who thrives on the physicality and social interaction of team training, is energised by the crowd, and enjoys the aggressive, dynamic nature of contact sport.

Evaluating Personality Theory: While personality type can suggest which sports a person may enjoy, it is not deterministic. Many introverts succeed in team sports and many extroverts excel in individual events. Personality is one factor among many that influences sports preference, alongside opportunity, coaching, and social influences.

🔥 Motivation in Sport

Motivation: The drive, desire and internal mechanism that stimulates a person to act or behave in a certain way. Motivation determines the direction, intensity and persistence of effort in sport.

❤️ Intrinsic Motivation

Intrinsic Motivation: Motivation that comes from within the performer. The drive to participate comes from personal enjoyment, satisfaction, interest, or the inherent pleasure of the activity itself.
  • Participating for fun, enjoyment and personal satisfaction
  • The activity itself is the reward
  • More sustainable and long-lasting
  • Associated with greater persistence and effort
  • Less likely to stop when external rewards are removed
Sporting Examples

A runner who trains every morning because they love the feeling of running and the sense of achievement after a hard session.

A footballer who plays because they enjoy the game, the camaraderie and the thrill of competition - not for the money or trophies.

A dancer who continues to practise because of the joy of movement and self-expression.

🏆 Extrinsic Motivation

Extrinsic Motivation: Motivation that comes from outside the performer. The drive to participate comes from external rewards such as trophies, medals, money, praise, or social recognition.
  • Participating for external rewards or to avoid punishment
  • The reward is separate from the activity itself
  • Can be effective in the short term
  • May decrease if rewards are removed
  • Types: tangible rewards (trophies, money, medals) and intangible rewards (praise, status, recognition)
Sporting Examples

A swimmer who trains hard primarily to win gold medals and gain sponsorship deals.

A young footballer who plays because their parents want them to and offer treats for good performance.

An athlete who competes for prize money and media recognition.

Feature Intrinsic Motivation Extrinsic Motivation
Source Internal - from within External - from outside
Drive Enjoyment, satisfaction, pride Rewards, trophies, praise, money
Sustainability Long-lasting and self-sustaining May fade if rewards are removed
Effort quality High quality, persistent effort May drop when reward achieved
Best for Long-term participation and development Initial engagement and short-term goals

⚖️ Evaluating Motivation Types

Key Evaluation Points: Most successful performers have a combination of both intrinsic and extrinsic motivation. Intrinsic motivation is generally more effective for long-term commitment because it is self-sustaining. However, extrinsic motivation can be useful to get performers started or to push through difficult periods. Over-reliance on extrinsic motivation is problematic because if rewards are removed, motivation may disappear.
  • Advantages of intrinsic motivation: Self-sustaining, leads to greater persistence, higher quality effort, less affected by setbacks, more enjoyable experience
  • Disadvantages of intrinsic motivation: May not be enough alone when facing hardship, some performers need initial external motivation to start
  • Advantages of extrinsic motivation: Effective for initial engagement, provides clear targets, can push performers through tough training, tangible rewards are motivating
  • Disadvantages of extrinsic motivation: Can undermine intrinsic motivation if overused, motivation disappears with the reward, may lead to cheating to win rewards, can create pressure and anxiety
Overjustification Effect: When excessive extrinsic rewards are given for an activity a person already enjoys intrinsically, their intrinsic motivation can actually decrease. The person starts to see the activity as something they do "for the reward" rather than "for enjoyment." Coaches should be careful not to over-reward intrinsically motivated performers.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Explain the difference between direct and indirect aggression, giving a sporting example of each.

Q2: Why are introverts more likely to choose individual sports while extroverts prefer team sports?

Q3: Describe the difference between intrinsic and extrinsic motivation. Which is more effective for long-term participation and why?

Q4: Explain the difference between assertion and aggression in sport. Why is this distinction important?

Q5: A parent offers their child money for every goal they score. Using psychological theory, evaluate the potential positive and negative effects of this approach.

Q6: How might an introvert's personality affect their choice of sport and their optimal arousal level? Give specific examples.

✅ Answers

  1. Direct aggression is aimed directly at another person with physical contact and intent to harm, e.g. a rugby player punching an opponent during a maul. Indirect aggression does not involve direct physical contact with the victim - it may be verbal abuse, psychological intimidation, or aggression towards objects, e.g. a tennis player smashing their racket or verbally abusing an opponent from across the net.
  2. Introverts prefer individual sports because they enjoy solitary, focused activities where they can concentrate in a quiet, controlled environment without social pressure. They are comfortable with sustained internal focus and lower arousal. Extroverts prefer team sports because they are energised by social interaction, enjoy the dynamic environment of team play, and thrive on higher arousal levels that come from competition and physical contact with others.
  3. Intrinsic motivation comes from within - the performer participates for enjoyment, satisfaction and the inherent pleasure of the activity. Extrinsic motivation comes from outside - the performer participates for external rewards such as trophies, medals, money or praise. Intrinsic motivation is more effective for long-term participation because it is self-sustaining; if a performer enjoys the activity itself, they will continue even when external rewards are absent. Extrinsic motivation may disappear when rewards are removed.
  4. Assertion is forceful but legitimate play within the rules of the sport, e.g. a strong, fair shoulder charge in rugby where the player is going for the ball. Aggression is behaviour with intent to harm that goes beyond the rules, e.g. deliberately elbowing an opponent off the ball. The distinction is important because assertion is a positive and legal part of sport, while aggression is harmful, illegal and should be penalised. Coaches and players must channel aggressive feelings into assertive, legal play.
  5. Positive effects: The money reward may increase the child's initial motivation to score goals, encourage them to work harder in training, and provide a clear, measurable target. Negative effects: The child may become solely focused on scoring (ignoring teamwork and passing), their intrinsic motivation for the game may decrease (overjustification effect), and if the money reward is removed, their motivation may collapse. The child may also cheat or behave selfishly to score. A better approach would be to praise effort and improvement, supporting intrinsic motivation.
  6. An introvert is likely to choose individual sports such as archery, golf, swimming or long-distance running, where they can focus inwardly without the pressure of social interaction. Their optimal arousal level would be lower - introverts perform best in calm, controlled environments where they can concentrate without the stimulation of a noisy crowd or team dynamic. For example, an introvert would perform best in archery with low arousal (steady hand, focused mind) rather than the high-arousal environment of a rugby match.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always distinguish between aggression and assertion - examiners test this distinction frequently
  • Give specific sporting examples for each type of aggression, personality and motivation
  • Remember: introvert ≠ shy; extrovert ≠ confident - focus on social energy and arousal preference
  • When evaluating motivation, always consider both intrinsic and extrinsic together
  • Know the overjustification effect - too much extrinsic reward can reduce intrinsic motivation
  • Most performers have a mix of both motivation types - don't present them as either/or

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often confuse assertion with aggression. Wrong: "A hard tackle in rugby is aggressive behaviour." Correct: "A hard but fair tackle within the rules is assertion. It only becomes aggression if the intent is to harm the opponent rather than win the ball."

Students often say extrinsic motivation is "bad." Wrong: "Extrinsic motivation is bad and should be avoided." Correct: "Extrinsic motivation can be effective for initial engagement and short-term goals, but over-reliance on it is problematic as motivation may disappear when rewards are removed. A combination of intrinsic and extrinsic motivation is ideal."

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often think aggression is always bad in sport. Wrong: Aggression is always bad in sport Correct: Direct aggression (within rules, e.g. a hard but fair tackle) can be channelled positively. It is only hostile aggression (intent to harm outside rules) that is always negative.

Students often think extroverts are always better at sport than introverts. Wrong: Extroverts are always better at sport than introverts Correct: Introverts may excel in individual, precise sports (archery, golf, marathon running). Extroverts tend to prefer team and contact sports. Personality is a preference, not a determinant.

Students often think more arousal always means better performance. Wrong: More arousal always means better performance Correct: The inverted-U theory shows that performance improves with arousal only up to an optimal point. Beyond that, performance declines. Fine/closed skills need lower arousal; gross/open skills need higher.
