Primary & Secondary Data

Understand the difference between primary and secondary data, methods of collecting each, and their advantages and disadvantages including experiments, surveys, observation and reference sources.

Key Fact: Primary data is collected firsthand by the investigator for a specific purpose.
Key Fact: Secondary data is data that already exists, collected by someone else for a different purpose.
Key Fact: Methods of collecting primary data include experiments, surveys/questionnaires and observation.
Key Fact: An experiment involves deliberately changing a variable and measuring the effect (e.g. clinical trials).
Key Fact: A survey or questionnaire collects information by asking people questions.
Key Fact: Observation involves watching and recording behaviour without interfering.
Key Fact: Sources of secondary data include the internet, government databases, books and previous studies.
Key Fact: A census collects data from every member of the population.
Key Fact: Advantages of primary data: it is up to date, specific to your investigation, and you know how it was collected.
Key Fact: Disadvantages of primary data: it can be time-consuming and expensive to collect.
Key Fact: Advantages of secondary data: it is quick and cheap to access, and large data sets may be available.
Key Fact: Disadvantages of secondary data: it may be outdated, may not exactly match your needs, and you cannot verify how it was collected.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Primary & Secondary Data, you must know:

  • Primary data: Data collected firsthand by the investigator for their own specific purpose.
  • Secondary data: Data that has already been collected by someone else, usually for a different purpose.
  • Census: A survey that collects data from every member of a population.
  • Experiment: A data collection method where the investigator deliberately changes a variable and measures the effect.
  • Observation: A data collection method where the investigator watches and records behaviour without interfering.
  • Survey: A method of collecting data by asking people questions, often via questionnaire or interview.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the difference between primary and secondary data?

Q: Name three sources of secondary data.

Q: What is a census?

Q: Give one advantage and one disadvantage of primary data.

Q: Why might secondary data be unreliable?

✅ Answers

  1. Primary data is collected firsthand by the investigator for a specific purpose; secondary data already exists and was collected by someone else, usually for a different purpose.
  2. Government databases (e.g. ONS), the internet, books, newspapers, previous studies, company records.
  3. A survey that collects data from every member of the population rather than a sample.
  4. Advantage: it is specific to your investigation and you know exactly how it was collected. Disadvantage: it can be time-consuming and expensive.
  5. It may be outdated, collected using different methods, biased, or not relevant to your specific hypothesis.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always justify whether primary or secondary data is more appropriate for a given investigation.
  • When evaluating secondary data, comment on its source, date and relevance.
  • A census is different from a sample survey — know when each is appropriate.
  • Observation means watching without interfering — if you change anything, it is an experiment.
  • If an exam question asks about data collection methods, link your choice to the hypothesis.

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Statistics Exam Tips:
Use precise statistical terminology in all answers. When performing calculations, always show working including the formula, substitution and final answer. For interpretation questions, always refer back to the context and use data to support your conclusions. For evaluation questions, consider reliability, validity, bias and alternative explanations. Edexcel 1ST0 and AQA 8382 reward answers that demonstrate understanding through correct use of statistical notation and evidence-based reasoning.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Saying secondary data is always less accurate than primary data ✓ Secondary data can be very accurate (e.g. government census data); the key issue is whether it is relevant and up to date for your investigation.

✗ Confusing observation and experiment ✓ Observation involves watching without interfering; an experiment involves deliberately changing a variable and measuring the effect.

✗ Thinking a census and a survey are the same ✓ A census collects data from every member of the population; a survey usually collects data from a sample.

✗ Claiming primary data is always better ✓ Primary data has advantages but may have a small sample or bias; secondary data can provide larger, more representative data sets quickly and cheaply.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

A student wants to compare the average rainfall in London and Manchester over the last 10 years. Should they use primary or secondary data? Justify your answer.

The student should use secondary data. Rainfall data for the last 10 years already exists and is freely available from the Met Office or other meteorological sources. Advantages: It is quick and free to access; the data has been collected professionally using standardised instruments over a long period, making it reliable. Collecting primary data would be impractical because the student would need rain gauges in both cities running for 10 years, which is far beyond the time and cost available for a school investigation. The student should check that the secondary data comes from a reputable source and that the recording methods are consistent between the two cities.
