Statistical Enquiry Cycle

Learn the stages of the Statistical Enquiry Cycle: Plan, Collect, Process, Present, Interpret and Evaluate, and how they connect in a real-world investigation.

Key Fact: The Statistical Enquiry Cycle (SEC) is a structured approach to carrying out a statistical investigation.
Key Fact: The five/six stages are: Plan, Collect, Process, Present, Interpret and Evaluate.
Key Fact: Some specifications list five stages (combining Interpret and Evaluate); others list six.
Key Fact: Plan: Define the hypothesis, identify variables, decide on methods and sample.
Key Fact: Collect: Gather data using your chosen method — survey, experiment, observation or secondary sources.
Key Fact: Process: Clean, organise and summarise the data — calculate statistics, group data, deal with anomalies.
Key Fact: Present: Display data using appropriate charts, graphs and diagrams (bar charts, histograms, scatter graphs, etc.).
Key Fact: Interpret: Draw conclusions from the data and relate them back to the original hypothesis.
Key Fact: Evaluate: Assess the investigation — were the methods appropriate? Were there bias or limitations?
Key Fact: The cycle is iterative — evaluation may lead you back to planning a better investigation.
Key Fact: Both Edexcel and AQA require you to understand and apply the full cycle.
Key Fact: Exam questions often ask you to identify which stage a particular action belongs to.

📋 Key Vocabulary and Concepts

For Statistical Enquiry Cycle, you must know:

  • Statistical Enquiry Cycle: A structured cycle of stages used to carry out a statistical investigation: Plan, Collect, Process, Present, Interpret, Evaluate.
  • Plan: The stage where you define the hypothesis, identify variables and decide on data collection methods.
  • Process: The stage where you clean, organise and summarise raw data.
  • Present: The stage where you display data using appropriate statistical diagrams and charts.
  • Interpret: The stage where you draw conclusions from the data and relate them to the hypothesis.
  • Evaluate: The stage where you assess the strengths and weaknesses of the investigation and suggest improvements.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: List the stages of the Statistical Enquiry Cycle in order.

Q: At which stage would you calculate the mean and range of a data set?

Q: At which stage would you draw a scatter graph to show the relationship between two variables?

Q: Why is the Evaluate stage important?

Q: How might the Evaluate stage lead back to the Plan stage?

✅ Answers

  1. Plan, Collect, Process, Present, Interpret, Evaluate.
  2. Process.
  3. Present.
  4. It allows you to assess the reliability and validity of your conclusions and to suggest improvements for future investigations.
  5. If evaluation reveals flaws in the method or sampling, you may need to plan a revised investigation to obtain more reliable results.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Learn the stages in order — exam questions often ask you to name or sequence them.
  • When asked which stage an action belongs to, think about what the action achieves.
  • Calculating statistics is 'Process'; drawing a chart is 'Present'; writing a conclusion is 'Interpret'.
  • Evaluation questions are common — always comment on bias, sample size and method.
  • Remember the cycle can be iterative — evaluation leads to improvement and re-planning.

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Statistics Exam Tips — Statistical Enquiry Cycle:
1. For Statistical Enquiry Cycle questions, show every step of your working clearly — method marks count even if the final answer is wrong
2. Check your answer makes sense in context (estimation, units, reasonableness)
3. Use correct mathematical notation and state formulae before substituting values
4. If a Statistical Enquiry Cycle question asks you to 'prove' or 'show', write a logical chain of reasoning with a conclusion line
5. For problem-solving, identify the topic first, then recall the relevant method

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ Confusing the Process and Present stages ✓ Process involves calculating summary statistics and cleaning data; Present involves drawing charts and diagrams.

✗ Thinking the cycle is strictly one-way ✓ The cycle is iterative — evaluation often leads back to planning improvements.

✗ Putting 'drawing conclusions' in the Present stage ✓ Drawing conclusions is part of the Interpret stage; Present is for creating visual displays only.

✗ Forgetting to link conclusions back to the hypothesis in the Interpret stage ✓ Always relate your findings explicitly to the original hypothesis, stating whether the data supports it.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

A student investigates whether taller people have larger shoe sizes. They collect heights and shoe sizes from 30 classmates, calculate the mean for each group, draw a scatter graph and conclude the hypothesis is supported. Identify the stage for each action.

1. 'They collect heights and shoe sizes from 30 classmates' — Collect stage: gathering raw data. 2. 'Calculate the mean for each group' — Process stage: summarising the data using a statistic. 3. 'Draw a scatter graph' — Present stage: displaying the data visually. 4. 'Conclude the hypothesis is supported' — Interpret stage: drawing a conclusion and relating it to the hypothesis. The student should also carry out the Evaluate stage, assessing whether 30 classmates is a representative sample, whether there may be bias (e.g. all same age), and whether the method could be improved.
