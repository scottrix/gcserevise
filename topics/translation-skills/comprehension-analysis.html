Comprehension & Analysis

Strategies for answering comprehension questions on Latin passages, including identifying key information, understanding nuance, and analysing language and style.

Key Fact: Comprehension questions test your understanding of a Latin passage: they ask what is happening, why, and how the author presents it.
Key Fact: Always read the question carefully and answer exactly what is asked — not what you think is asked or what you wish was asked.
Key Fact: For comprehension answers, refer to specific Latin words or phrases as evidence — answers without textual reference score poorly.
Key Fact: Analysis questions ask HOW the author achieves an effect: identify the technique, quote the Latin, and explain the effect.
Key Fact: When asked 'how does the author show X?', focus on language and style, not just on summarising what happens.
Key Fact: Common comprehension question types: what is happening, who is involved, where does this fit in the wider narrative, what does a specific word mean in context.
Key Fact: Common analysis question types: how does the author create tension, how is a character presented, how does the writer convey emotion, what is the effect of a particular word or phrase.
Key Fact: For 'reference to the Latin' questions, quote the specific Latin word(s) or phrase — do not just translate into English and quote your translation.
Key Fact: When asked about a character's feelings or motivation, base your answer on what the Latin actually says, not on what you imagine they might feel.
Key Fact: If a question asks you to explain a word 'in this context', give the specific meaning the word has in this particular passage, not its general dictionary meaning.

❓ Practice Questions

Q: What is the difference between a comprehension question and an analysis question?

Q: Why must you quote Latin rather than English in your answers?

Q: How should you answer a question asking 'How does the author create tension?'

Q: What does 'in this context' mean in a question?

Q: What should you do if a question asks about a character's motivation?

Q: How many points should you make in a comprehension answer?

Q: What is the most common mistake in comprehension answers?

Q: How do you 'refer to the Latin' in an answer?

✅ Answers

  1. Comprehension asks WHAT is happening (content, events, characters). Analysis asks HOW the author presents it (language, style, techniques and their effects).
  2. Because the question asks you to refer to the Latin text, and marks are awarded for engaging with the original language. Quoting only your English translation does not demonstrate understanding of the Latin.
  3. Identify specific techniques (short sentences, alliteration, word order, vocabulary choice), quote the Latin for each, and explain the effect on the reader. Do not just summarise the events.
  4. It asks for the specific meaning the word has in this particular passage, not the general dictionary definition. The same Latin word can mean different things in different contexts.
  5. Base your answer on what the Latin text actually says — what the character says, does, or what the narrator tells us. Do not invent motivations not supported by the text.
  6. Match the number to the marks available: roughly one point per mark. A 4-mark question needs approximately four distinct points with textual evidence.
  7. Answering a different question from what was asked — e.g. summarising events when asked about style, or giving general context when asked about a specific passage.
  8. Quote the specific Latin word(s) or short phrase that supports your point. For example: 'The author shows the soldiers' fear using the word perterriti (line 4), meaning terrified.'

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Read the question twice and underline key words before writing anything.
  • Every point must be supported by a reference to the Latin text — get into this habit for every answer.
  • For 'how' questions, focus on language and style techniques, not content summary.
  • If a question is worth 4 marks, aim for 4 distinct points with Latin evidence.

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Latin Exam Tips:
Always use specific Latin quotations to support your answers. For literature questions, apply the PEA method (Point, Evidence, Analysis). Use precise grammatical terminology when discussing language. For civilisation questions, reference named ancient sources. When translating, identify the main verb first and then work out each noun's case before producing idiomatic English.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ You can answer comprehension questions in general terms without quoting the Latin. ✓ Answers without specific reference to the Latin text score poorly. Always quote the relevant Latin word or phrase to support each point.

✗ Analysis means describing what happens in the passage. ✓ Analysis means explaining HOW the author uses language and style to achieve an effect. Describing content (what happens) is summary, not analysis.

✗ You should quote long passages of Latin in your answer. ✓ Quote only the specific word or short phrase that supports your point. Long quotations without focused analysis waste time and earn no extra marks.

✗ If you understand the passage in English, you will do well on comprehension. ✓ Understanding the passage is necessary but not sufficient. You must also be able to identify specific Latin words, explain their meaning and effect, and support every point with textual evidence.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

How should you approach a question asking 'How does the author convey the soldiers' fear in lines 3-6? Refer to the Latin.' (4 marks)

For a 4-mark question, I would make four distinct points, each supported by specific Latin from lines 3-6. First, I would identify a vocabulary choice: 'The author uses the word perterriti (line 3), meaning terrified, directly stating the soldiers' fear.' Second, I would note a stylistic device: 'The alliteration of tremunt terrentur (line 4) repeats the harsh 't' sound, imitating the trembling and creating an onomatopoeic effect of fear.' Third, I would comment on word order: 'The author places the enemy name hostes first in the sentence (line 5), foregrounding the threat and making it the soldiers' first thought.' Fourth, I would identify a further technique: 'The short, abrupt sentence in line 6 (fugerunt omnes) mirrors the sudden, panicky flight — the brief sentence structure reflects the soldiers' desperate urgency.' Each point names a technique (vocabulary, alliteration, word order, sentence structure), quotes the Latin, and explains how it conveys fear. This approach ensures I address 'how' rather than merely describing what happens, and every point is grounded in the specific Latin text.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 (Knowledge & Understanding): Demonstrate knowledge and understanding of comprehension & analysis, including Latin language forms, literary techniques, and Roman cultural context.

AO2 (Analysis & Evaluation): Analyse and evaluate comprehension & analysis, identifying how language and style create meaning, and assessing the significance of themes, characterisation, and historical context.

AO3 (Application): Apply knowledge and understanding of comprehension & analysis to translate Latin texts accurately, analyse set text passages, and construct well-supported arguments using Latin evidence.
