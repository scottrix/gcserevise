English to Latin Translation

Strategies for translating English sentences into Latin, using only the Restricted Vocabulary List and Restricted Grammar List as specified by each exam board.

Key Fact: The English-to-Latin section uses only the Restricted Vocabulary List (RVL) and Restricted Grammar List (RGL) — you must NOT use vocabulary or constructions outside these lists.
Key Fact: Step 1: Identify the subject, verb, and object in the English sentence. Determine the Latin verb's tense, voice, person, and number.
Key Fact: Step 2: Choose the correct Latin nominative for the subject and the correct accusative for the object, checking declension and gender.
Key Fact: Step 3: Select the correct verb form from the principal parts, using the right conjugation, tense, voice, and person.
Key Fact: Step 4: Add any prepositions, adjectives (agreeing in GNC), adverbs, and subordinate clauses required by the English.
Key Fact: Common constructions in the RGL: present, imperfect, and perfect tenses; active and passive voices; accusative + infinitive; ut/ne + subjunctive.
Key Fact: Adjective agreement: always check gender, number, and case. 'The good soldier' = miles bonus (masc. nom. sg.), but 'of the good soldier' = militis boni (masc. gen. sg.).
Key Fact: Word order in Latin: SOV is the basic pattern, but the verb often comes at the end. Adjectives can follow or precede their noun; prepositions precede their noun.
Key Fact: Check your work: verify every ending — a single wrong letter can change the case, tense, or person.
Key Fact: The English-to-Latin section is worth fewer marks than Latin-to-English, but every mark counts. Aim for accuracy over ambition.

For English to Latin Translation, you must know:

✅ Answers

  1. The Restricted Vocabulary List and Restricted Grammar List define the exact words and grammatical constructions you may use in the English-to-Latin section. Using anything outside these lists will be marked incorrect.
  2. Identify the tense (present/imperfect/perfect), voice (active/passive), person (1st/2nd/3rd), and number (singular/plural) from the English sentence. Then select the correct form from the verb's principal parts.
  3. Gender, Number, Case: an adjective must match its noun in all three. 'The good soldier' = miles bonus, but 'the good soldiers' = milites boni, and 'of the good soldier' = militis boni.
  4. Subject-Object-Verb (SOV). The verb typically comes at the end. However, Latin word order is flexible and emphasis can alter the standard order.
  5. Milites fortiter pugnabant. Milites (subject, nom. pl.), fortiter (adverb, 'bravely'), pugnabant (imperfect active 3rd plural of pugnare).
  6. Verify every ending: is each noun in the correct case? Does each adjective agree in GNC? Is the verb in the right tense, voice, person, and number? Are prepositions followed by the correct case?
  7. Present, imperfect, and perfect indicative, active and passive. Some boards also include the pluperfect and future. Check your specific board's RGL.
  8. No. Accuracy over ambition: use only constructions from the RGL, and keep your Latin simple and correct. An ambitious but wrong sentence scores less than a simple correct one.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Only use RVL vocabulary and RGL grammar — anything outside these lists will be marked wrong.
  • Double-check every ending before moving on: one wrong letter can cost the mark for the whole sentence.
  • Keep it simple: a correct simple sentence scores higher than an ambitious but incorrect complex one.
  • Learn the RVL thoroughly — you should be able to produce any word on the list from English to Latin.

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Latin Exam Tips:
Always use specific Latin quotations to support your answers. For literature questions, apply the PEA method (Point, Evidence, Analysis). Use precise grammatical terminology when discussing language. For civilisation questions, reference named ancient sources. When translating, identify the main verb first and then work out each noun's case before producing idiomatic English.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ You can use any Latin word you know in the English-to-Latin section. ✓ You must only use words from the Restricted Vocabulary List. Using a correct Latin word that is not on the RVL will be marked as incorrect.

✗ Word order doesn't matter in Latin, so you can put words in any order. ✓ While Latin word order is flexible, there are conventions (SOV, adjective position, preposition position). More importantly, wrong word order can create ambiguity or change meaning. Follow standard patterns.

✗ Adjectives always come before the noun in Latin. ✓ Latin adjectives can come before or after the noun. While both positions are grammatically correct, the position can affect emphasis. In the English-to-Latin section, either position is acceptable.

✗ The English-to-Latin section requires complex, literary Latin. ✓ The exam expects straightforward, grammatically correct Latin using only the RVL and RGL. Simplicity and accuracy are rewarded, not literary ambition.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Explain the method you would use to translate the English sentence 'The brave soldiers gave the gifts to the queen' into Latin, using only the RVL and RGL. (6 marks)

First, I would identify the subject ('the brave soldiers'), verb ('gave'), direct object ('the gifts'), and indirect object ('to the queen'). For the subject, 'soldiers' = milites (3rd declension, masculine, nominative plural). The adjective 'brave' must agree in GNC: fortes (3rd declension, masculine, nominative plural). So 'the brave soldiers' = milites fortiter — but wait, that's an adverb. The adjective form is milites fortes. For the verb, 'gave' is perfect tense, active voice, third person plural: dedere or dederunt (from do, dare, dedi, datus). For the direct object, 'the gifts' = dona (2nd declension neuter, nominative/accusative plural — but as the object, it is accusative: dona). For the indirect object, 'to the queen' requires the dative case: reginae (1st declension, feminine, dative singular of regina). I would check each word against the RVL to confirm it is permitted. Assembling the sentence in SOV order: milites fortes reginae dona dederunt (subject — indirect object — direct object — verb). Finally, I would verify every ending: milites (nom. pl.), fortes (masc. nom. pl., agreeing with milites), reginae (dat. sg.), dona (acc. pl. neuter), dederunt (perfect active 3rd pl.). This systematic approach ensures every element is in the correct case, tense, and agreement.
