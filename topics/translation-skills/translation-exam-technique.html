Translation Exam Technique

Practical strategies for the translation exam, including time management, handling difficult passages, and checking your work effectively.

Key Fact: Time management: allocate roughly 1 minute per mark. For a 90-minute Language paper worth 100 marks, spend about 50 minutes on the translation section and 40 on comprehension/analysis.
Key Fact: Attempt every sentence — partial credit is available for correct elements even if the full translation is wrong.
Key Fact: If you are stuck on a sentence, move on and return to it later. Understanding later sentences often clarifies earlier ones.
Key Fact: For the English-to-Latin section, keep your sentences simple and within the RVL and RGL. Accuracy scores more than ambition.
Key Fact: When checking your work, verify every ending: each noun's case, each verb's tense and person, each adjective's GNC agreement.
Key Fact: Read your translation back in English — if it doesn't make sense, something is wrong with your understanding of the Latin.
Key Fact: For the unseen passage, use the introductory heading or context (e.g. 'from Caesar's account of the Gallic War') to anticipate vocabulary and themes.
Key Fact: Common translation errors: confusing nominative and accusative, missing the subjunctive in subordinate clauses, and misreading verb tenses.
Key Fact: In the exam, use the glossary provided for words outside the DVL — do not guess. If a word is glossed, use the glossed meaning.
Key Fact: Practise with past papers under timed conditions to build speed and confidence before the exam.

✅ Answers

  1. Allocate roughly 1 minute per mark. Spend about 50 minutes on the translation sections and 40 on comprehension and analysis. Do not over-run on early questions.
  2. Move on and return to it later. Later sentences often provide context that clarifies earlier difficulties. Never leave the passage — attempt every sentence for partial credit.
  3. Verify every noun's case, every verb's tense/person/voice, every adjective's GNC agreement, and that prepositions take the correct case. Then read your English back to see if it makes sense.
  4. The heading (e.g. 'from Caesar's De Bello Gallico') tells you the author, period, and topic, helping you anticipate vocabulary, themes, and style before you start translating.
  5. The glossary gives the specific meaning intended by the examiner. Guessing based on a derivative or similar word may give the wrong meaning in context.
  6. Spend approximately 1 minute per mark available. A 10-mark question deserves about 10 minutes. This prevents running out of time on lower-value questions.
  7. Confusing nominative and accusative (especially with similar endings), missing subjunctive mood in subordinate clauses, misreading imperfect as perfect, and failing to recognise ablative uses.
  8. It builds speed, familiarises you with the exam format, reveals common vocabulary gaps, and develops confidence in handling unseen passages under timed conditions.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Attempt every sentence — even a partially correct translation earns marks.
  • Keep English-to-Latin simple: accuracy within the RVL/RGL scores more than ambition.
  • Use the passage heading to anticipate content and vocabulary.
  • Practise under timed conditions: speed and confidence come from practice, not just knowledge.

📝 Exam Technique

GCSE Latin Exam Tips:
Always use specific Latin quotations to support your answers. For literature questions, apply the PEA method (Point, Evidence, Analysis). Use precise grammatical terminology when discussing language. For civilisation questions, reference named ancient sources. When translating, identify the main verb first and then work out each noun's case before producing idiomatic English.

⚠️ Common Errors

✗ You should translate the hardest sentences first while you are fresh. ✓ Start with the sentences you can translate confidently to build momentum and earn marks. Return to difficult sentences later when context from the rest of the passage may help clarify them.

✗ There is no point attempting a sentence if you can't translate all of it. ✓ Partial credit is awarded for correct elements. Even if you get only the subject and verb right, you earn marks for those. Never leave a sentence blank.

✗ Checking your work is a waste of time in the exam. ✓ Checking catches simple errors (wrong case ending, wrong tense, wrong agreement) that cost marks. A quick review of every noun's case and every verb's tense can recover several marks.

✗ The English-to-Latin sentences require the same level of vocabulary as the Latin-to-English section. ✓ The English-to-Latin section uses only the Restricted Vocabulary List (a subset of the DVL) and the Restricted Grammar List. You need fewer words but must know them more precisely.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

Describe your strategy for approaching the Language paper, from opening the paper to final checking. (6 marks)

On opening the paper, I would first read the heading of each passage to understand the context (author, topic, period) — this helps anticipate vocabulary and themes. I would then check the mark allocations to plan my time using the 1-minute-per-mark rule. I would start with the Latin-to-English translation, reading the whole passage for general sense before translating sentence by sentence. For each sentence, I identify the main verb first, then work out each noun's case and function. If I am stuck on a sentence, I move on and return later — later context often clarifies earlier difficulties. I attempt every sentence because partial credit is available. For the English-to-Latin section, I keep my sentences simple and strictly within the RVL and RGL, checking each word against the lists. For comprehension and analysis questions, I read each question twice, underline key words, and support every point with specific Latin quotations. In the final 5-10 minutes, I check my work systematically: every noun's case, every verb's tense and person, every adjective's GNC agreement, and whether each preposition takes the correct case. I also read my English translations back to check they make sense. This organised approach ensures I attempt every question, manage my time, and catch the simple errors that cost marks.
