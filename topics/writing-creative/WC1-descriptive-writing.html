WC1: Descriptive Writing

Foundation Higher All Boards AO5 AO6

Writing descriptively to create vivid images and atmosphere - painting pictures with words.

📋 What is Descriptive Writing?

Definition: Descriptive writing creates a vivid picture in the reader's mind using sensory details, figurative language, and careful word choices. It focuses on capturing a moment, place, or person rather than telling a story.

Descriptive writing aims to:

  • Create vivid mental images for the reader
  • Establish atmosphere and mood
  • Engage all five senses
  • Transport the reader to a specific time and place
  • Show rather than tell
Key Difference:
Narrative writing tells a story with a plot.
Descriptive writing paints a picture of a moment or place.

👁️ Sensory Imagery

The Five Senses

Great descriptive writing engages all five senses to create an immersive experience.

Golden Rule: Don't just describe what you see. Include sounds, smells, textures, and even tastes to bring your writing to life.

Visual (Sight)

  • Colours, shapes, sizes, movements
  • Light and shadow
  • Facial expressions and body language
Example - Visual

The sunset bled crimson and gold across the horizon, casting long shadows that stretched like fingers across the abandoned playground.

Auditory (Sound)

  • Volume, pitch, rhythm
  • Natural and artificial sounds
  • Silence and its quality
Example - Auditory

Wind whistled through cracked windows, while somewhere distant, a dog barked its lonely warning into the night.

Olfactory (Smell)

  • Often the most evocative sense
  • Can trigger memories and emotions
  • Use specific, recognisable scents
Example - Olfactory

The air hung heavy with the smell of damp earth and decaying leaves, underlaid with something metallic - like old pennies.

Tactile (Touch)

  • Textures, temperatures, physical sensations
  • The feel of surfaces and materials
  • Atmospheric conditions
Example - Tactile

The brickwork was rough beneath my fingertips, cold and slightly damp, as if the building itself was sweating.

Gustatory (Taste)

  • Often overlooked but powerful
  • Can be metaphorical
  • Links to smell and memory
Example - Gustatory

Fear rose in my throat, bitter and metallic, coating my tongue with the taste of copper pennies.

🎭 Show, Don't Tell

Core Principle: Instead of telling the reader what to feel or think, show them through specific details that allow them to experience it themselves.
Telling (Weak) Showing (Strong)
It was scary. My heart hammered against my ribs. Sweat prickled on my palms.
She was nervous. She twisted the ring on her finger, her gaze flicking constantly to the door.
The room was messy. Clothes spilled from the wardrobe like a fabric avalanche. Books lay open, spines cracked, pages scattered.
He was angry. His jaw tightened. A vein pulsed at his temple. His knuckles whitened around the pen.
The weather was bad. Rain lashed the windows. Thunder growled in the distance. The sky bruised purple with approaching storm.

🔮 Figurative Language

Metaphor

A direct comparison stating one thing IS another.

Examples

The city was a beast, never sleeping, always hungry.

Her smile was a lifeline in the storm of his grief.

Simile

A comparison using "like" or "as".

Examples

The fog curled through the streets like a wandering spirit.

Her voice was as sharp as broken glass.

Personification

Giving human qualities to non-human things.

Examples

The wind screamed through the empty house.

Time crept slowly, dragging its heels.

Sensory Language

Words that appeal directly to the senses.

Examples

Visual: gleaming, towering, speckled

Sound: whispering, crashing, humming

Touch: velvety, scorching, rough

Smell: acrid, fragrant, musty

Taste: bitter, savoury, metallic

📍 Spatial Organisation

Zoom Technique

Move from wide view to close-up, or vice versa.

Structure Options:
  • Wide to narrow: Establish setting, then zoom to specific details
  • Narrow to wide: Start with detail, then pull back to reveal context
  • Pan across: Move through space like a camera panning
Wide to Narrow Example

The city sprawled beneath me, a patchwork of lights and shadows. Below, the market square bustled with late-night traders. Near the fountain, a young couple argued, their voices sharp against the ambient hum. The girl's necklace caught the lamplight - a silver bird, wings outstretched, frozen mid-flight.

Micro and Macro Description

  • Macro: The big picture - settings, weather, time, atmosphere
  • Micro: Small details - a single object, texture, expression
Balance is Key: Mix macro and micro description. Macro establishes context; micro creates intimacy and authenticity.

🌙 Atmosphere and Mood

Creating Atmosphere

Atmosphere is the emotional climate of your writing - what the reader feels.

  • Word choice: Select words with appropriate connotations
  • Sensory details: Evoke feelings through the senses
  • Sentence structure: Short sentences create tension; long, flowing sentences create calm
  • Figurative language: Reinforce mood through imagery
Tense Atmosphere

Shadows pooled in corners. The floorboards groaned. Something scraped in the darkness above - slow, deliberate. She held her breath.

Peaceful Atmosphere

Sunlight dappled the grass through swaying branches. A bee droned lazily among the wildflowers. The stream murmured its endless, gentle song.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Telling instead of showing Reader can't experience it Use sensory details and actions
Listing adjectives Feels flat and lazy Weave description through action
Overusing figurative language Becomes clichéd and cluttered Use sparingly for maximum effect
Forgetting senses beyond sight Missed opportunities for depth Consciously include sound, smell, touch, taste
No clear focus or viewpoint Reader feels lost Establish a clear perspective and spatial logic
Narrative instead of description Wrong task - this is descriptive writing Focus on capturing a moment, not telling events

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AO5 - Content and Organisation (24 marks)

  • Level 6 (21-24): Convincing, compelling description with extensive vocabulary
  • Level 5 (17-20): Compelling description with varied vocabulary
  • Level 4 (13-16): Effective description with some variety
  • Level 3 (9-12): Some successful description
  • Level 2 (5-8): Simple description with limited variety
  • Level 1 (1-4): Basic description, little variety

AO6 - Technical Accuracy (16 marks)

  • Level 4 (13-16): Wide vocabulary, varied sentences, accurate SPaG
  • Level 3 (9-12): Reasonable vocabulary, some variety, mostly accurate
  • Level 2 (5-8): Limited vocabulary, simple sentences, some errors
  • Level 1 (1-4): Basic vocabulary, repeated sentence structure, frequent errors
Examiner's Tip: The best descriptive writing makes the examiner FEEL something. Focus on creating atmosphere rather than describing everything you can think of.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What is the difference between descriptive writing and narrative writing?

Q2: Name three techniques for creating "show, don't tell" writing.

Q3: Why is it important to include senses beyond just sight?

Q4: What is the "zoom technique" and how does it help structure description?

Q5: Rewrite this telling sentence to show instead: "The old house was creepy."

✅ Answers

  1. Descriptive writing paints a picture of a moment or place, focusing on sensory details and atmosphere. Narrative writing tells a story with a plot, characters, and events unfolding over time.
  2. Use sensory details, describe physical actions and reactions, include specific observations, use figurative language, focus on concrete details rather than abstract labels.
  3. Sound, smell, touch, and taste create a more immersive experience. They trigger stronger emotional responses and memories in readers, making the description more vivid and memorable.
  4. The zoom technique moves from wide view (macro) to close-up detail (micro), or vice versa. It gives structure and logical flow to description, helping readers visualise the scene progressively.
  5. Example: "Windows stared like hollow eyes from the crumbling facade. Ivy clawed up the rotting walls. Inside, floorboards groaned beneath invisible feet, and the air tasted of mould and forgotten memories."

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Read the image or prompt carefully - use it as inspiration, not just description
  • Plan your structure: viewpoint, spatial movement, key moments
  • Use a clear viewpoint - either first person "I" or third person observer
  • Open with impact - grab attention from the first line
  • Balance sensory details - don't just describe visuals
  • Vary sentence length for rhythm and effect
  • End strongly - a final image or impression that lingers
  • Leave time to check spelling and punctuation

📝 Exam Technique

Descriptive Writing Exam Strategy: You have 45 minutes for this question (40 marks total: 24 AO5 + 16 AO6). Spend 5 minutes planning, 35 minutes writing, and 5 minutes proofreading.
  • First 5 minutes: Study the image carefully. Identify 3-4 key details to zoom in on. Decide on your viewpoint (first or third person) and the atmosphere you want to create.
  • Writing time: Aim for 2-3 well-developed paragraphs. Use the zoom technique: start wide, then move to specific, vivid details.
  • Mark allocation: AO5 (24 marks) rewards your content and organisation — vivid imagery, sustained atmosphere, and structured description. AO6 (16 marks) rewards technical accuracy — varied sentences, ambitious vocabulary, and correct SPaG.
  • What examiners look for: A piece that makes them feel something. Sensory details beyond sight, precise word choices, and a clear structural journey through the scene.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often write a story instead of a description. Wrong: Telling events in order — "I walked into the room, then I saw a ghost, then I ran away." Correct: Capturing a single moment in vivid detail — "The room held its breath. Dust motes hung suspended in the thin light. In the corner, something shifted."

Students often list adjectives to describe things. Wrong: "The old, dark, scary, creepy, abandoned house stood on the hill." Correct: Weave description through action and senses — "The house clung to the hillside, its windows staring hollow, the smell of rot seeping from its cracked walls."

Students often forget senses beyond sight. Wrong: Describing only what can be seen — "The room was dark with black walls and a grey floor." Correct: Engaging multiple senses — "The walls pressed in, slick with damp. Something metallic lingered on the tongue. Floorboards groaned beneath the thinnest footfall."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

40 marks: Describe a busy market. You could write about: the stalls and their goods; the people and their voices; the smells and sounds.

The market sprawled beneath a canopy of ripped tarpaulin and rusted scaffolding, a cathedral of commerce where every sense was ambushed. First came the smell — a thick, cloying collision of fried onions, overripe mangoes, and the raw iron tang of butchered meat. It caught in the throat, insistent and primal. Then the sound: a wall of noise that had no beginning and no end, just the relentless hum of a hundred competing voices, each one fighting for attention like birds in a dawn chorus. "Fresh! Fresh! Get your fresh!" A woman's voice cut through, sharp as a cleaver, and somewhere a child wailed, lost and shrieking above the tide of haggling.

Stalls jostled for space like teeth in a crowded mouth. Fabric cascaded in bolts of shocking magenta and electric blue, competing with pyramids of spices — turmeric gold, paprika fire, cumin the colour of desert sand. A butcher's block wore its history in dark rings, and the old man behind it moved his knife with the precision of a surgeon, each stroke deliberate, each cut clean. His hands were a geography of scars. Beside him, a boy no older than ten counted change into a palm, fingers moving faster than thought.

And then, between one breath and the next, the rain came. It fell like a rumour at first — a few fat drops that the stallholders swore at, scrambling to cover their wares with plastic sheeting. Then it poured, turning the cobbles into mirrors, and the market became something else entirely: a place of reflections, of doubled images, of people moving through water as though walking through memory itself.

Mark scheme: AO5 (21-24/24) — Compelling, vivid description with extensive vocabulary; sustained atmosphere; excellent zoom structure from sensory overview to specific human details to dramatic shift. AO6 (14-16/16) — Wide vocabulary ("cloying", "insistent", "geography of scars"); varied sentences including minor sentences for impact ("Then it poured"); accurate SPaG throughout; sophisticated punctuation with colons and dashes used for effect.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO5 — Content and Organisation (24 marks): For descriptive writing, this AO rewards your ability to create a vivid, sustained picture. At grade 5 (Level 3, 9-12 marks), students produce "some successful description" with occasional sensory detail but lack consistency. To reach grade 9 (Level 6, 21-24 marks), your description must be "convincing and compelling" — every detail should earn its place, the atmosphere should be sustained throughout, and the reader should feel transported. The key difference is control: grade 5 writers describe what they see; grade 9 writers curate what the reader experiences, choosing each image for maximum impact.

AO6 — Technical Accuracy (16 marks): This is where many students lose marks unnecessarily. At grade 5 (Level 3, 9-12 marks), vocabulary is "reasonable" and sentences show "some variety." At grade 9 (Level 4, 13-16 marks), you need a "wide vocabulary" used precisely, "varied sentence structures" used for deliberate effect, and consistently accurate spelling, punctuation, and grammar. The critical leap from grade 5 to grade 9 is purposeful variety — not randomly mixing sentence types, but choosing a short sentence because it creates tension, or a complex sentence because it builds atmosphere. Examiners can tell the difference between random variety and intentional craft.
