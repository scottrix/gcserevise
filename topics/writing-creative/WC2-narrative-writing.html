WC2: Narrative Writing

Foundation Higher All Boards AO5 AO6

Writing engaging stories with plot, character and setting - crafting narratives that captivate readers.

📋 What is Narrative Writing?

Definition: Narrative writing tells a story with a beginning, middle, and end. It features characters, a plot with events, a setting, and often explores themes through conflict and resolution.

Narrative writing includes:

  • A clear plot structure with events that unfold
  • Characters who develop or change
  • A setting that grounds the story
  • Conflict or tension that drives the narrative
  • A resolution that brings closure
Key Difference:
Narrative writing tells a STORY with events and plot.
Descriptive writing paints a PICTURE of a moment or place.

📐 Plot Structure

Classic Story Arc

Most effective stories follow a recognisable structure.

The Five-Part Structure:
  1. Exposition: Introduce characters, setting, situation
  2. Rising Action: Build tension through complications
  3. Climax: The turning point or peak of tension
  4. Falling Action: Consequences of the climax
  5. Resolution: Loose ends tied up, new equilibrium

For Exam Stories

You don't have time for a full novel structure. Focus on:

Compact Story Structure
  • Opening: Hook the reader, establish situation and character
  • Development: One key incident or moment of change
  • Turning Point: Realisation, decision, or revelation
  • Ending: Resolution or open-ended reflection

In Medias Res

Starting in the middle of the action - a powerful technique for exam stories.

Example - In Medias Res Opening

The door crashed open. I didn't wait to see who it was - I grabbed my bag and ran, the sound of heavy footsteps pounding behind me.

👥 Character Development

Creating Believable Characters

Characters drive your story. Make them feel real.

  • Give them a want: What does your character desire?
  • Give them a flaw: Perfect characters are boring
  • Show personality: Through dialogue, actions, and reactions
  • Use telling details: A single trait can define a character
Show Character Through Action: Instead of telling us someone is brave, show them facing fear. Instead of telling us someone is kind, show them helping another character.

Characterisation Techniques

Physical Description

Her hands were always busy - braiding, folding, straightening things that didn't need straightening. A nervous energy radiated from every gesture.

Dialogue

"I'm fine," she said, staring at the floor. "Absolutely fine. Nothing to worry about. Why would there be anything to worry about?"

Action and Reaction

When the teacher asked for volunteers, Jake shrank back. But when Sarah stumbled, he was the first to help her up, avoiding eye contact the whole time.

🏞️ Setting Creation

Bringing Settings to Life

Setting isn't just backdrop - it affects mood, character, and plot.

  • Time: Era, season, time of day - all create atmosphere
  • Place: Physical location with specific details
  • Weather: A powerful mood-setter
  • Sensory details: What can be seen, heard, smelled?
Setting with Atmosphere

The coastal town huddled against the cliffs, its cottages stacked like afterthoughts. Seagulls screamed overhead, and the salt wind carried the smell of drying fish nets. In winter, the tourists left, and the town turned in on itself, shuttered windows reflecting the grey sea.

Setting and Mood

Mood Setting Elements
Tense Shadows, silence, confined spaces, cold
Peaceful Open spaces, natural light, gentle sounds, warmth
Mysterious Fog, twilight, hidden areas, unfamiliar places
Nostalgic Old objects, familiar sounds, seasonal markers

💬 Dialogue

Writing Effective Dialogue

Dialogue should reveal character and advance the plot.

Dialogue Rules:
  • Each character should have a distinct voice
  • Keep it realistic - people interrupt, trail off, use incomplete sentences
  • Avoid info-dumps - don't use dialogue to explain the plot
  • Use action beats instead of endless "he said, she said"
Weak Dialogue

"Hello, Sarah. I am very angry with you because you lied to me yesterday about where you were going," said John angrily.

Strong Dialogue

"You lied to me." John's jaw tightened. "Yesterday. About where you were going."

Sarah didn't look up from her phone. "I didn't lie."

"You said you were at the library."

"I was. Before."

"Before what, Sarah?"

She finally met his eyes. "Before."

🎭 Narrative Voice

First Person (I)

  • Immediate and personal
  • Limited to what the narrator knows and sees
  • Creates intimacy with the reader
First Person Example

I knew something was wrong the moment I walked through the door. The house was too quiet. Too still. My mother's keys sat on the hall table, but she wasn't in the kitchen where she always was at five o'clock.

Third Person (He/She/They)

  • More flexibility and distance
  • Can show multiple perspectives
  • Can be limited (one character's view) or omniscient (all-knowing)
Third Person Example

Marcus had never been good at waiting. He paced the small room, his fingers drumming against his thigh. Outside, the city hummed its endless song, oblivious to the decision being made behind the closed door. Somewhere in that building, his future was being decided.

⚡ Tension and Suspense

Building Tension

Tension keeps readers engaged. Without it, stories feel flat.

  • Withhold information: Make readers want to know more
  • Create time pressure: A ticking clock raises stakes
  • Use dramatic irony: Reader knows something the character doesn't
  • Foreshadow: Hint at what's to come
  • Use sentence structure: Short sentences speed up; long sentences slow down
Building Tension

She counted the steps. One. Two. Three. The floorboard creaked behind her. She stopped. Held her breath. Listened. Nothing. She took another step. There it was again - the faintest creak. Someone was following her.

Pacing

  • Slow: Long sentences, detailed description, reflection - for building atmosphere
  • Fast: Short sentences, action verbs, dialogue - for action and tension

🎯 Openings and Endings

Strong Openings

Grab the reader from the first line.

Opening Techniques
  • In medias res: "The knife was already in my hand when I realised what I was about to do."
  • Shocking statement: "Nobody expected me to be the one who found the body."
  • Question: "Have you ever wondered what your last words will be?"
  • Atmospheric: "The storm had been building for three days."
  • Character voice: "Look, I didn't ask for this. Any of it."

Effective Endings

Leave a lasting impression.

Ending Techniques
  • Reflection: Character looks back on events with new understanding
  • Circular: Returns to the opening image or idea
  • Open-ended: Leaves questions unanswered, inviting reader thought
  • Twist: Reveals something that reframes the story
  • Resonant image: A final powerful image that lingers
Exam Tip: Don't try to write a complete novel. Focus on ONE key moment or incident, and explore it fully. Quality over quantity.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Too much backstory Slows down the narrative Weave background into action
Flat characters Reader doesn't care what happens Give characters wants and flaws
Weak opening Examiner loses interest quickly Start with action, conflict, or intrigue
Rushed ending Feels unsatisfying Plan your ending, leave time to write it
Telling instead of showing Removes reader from experience Show emotions through actions and details
Too many characters Confuses reader, dilutes focus Focus on 1-2 main characters maximum
Descriptive instead of narrative Wrong task - narrative needs plot Include events that happen and change

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AO5 - Content and Organisation (24 marks)

  • Level 6 (21-24): Convincing, compelling narrative with extensive vocabulary
  • Level 5 (17-20): Compelling narrative with varied vocabulary
  • Level 4 (13-16): Effective narrative with some variety
  • Level 3 (9-12): Some successful narrative elements
  • Level 2 (5-8): Simple narrative with limited variety
  • Level 1 (1-4): Basic narrative, little variety

AO6 - Technical Accuracy (16 marks)

  • Level 4 (13-16): Wide vocabulary, varied sentences, accurate SPaG
  • Level 3 (9-12): Reasonable vocabulary, some variety, mostly accurate
  • Level 2 (5-8): Limited vocabulary, simple sentences, some errors
  • Level 1 (1-4): Basic vocabulary, repeated sentence structure, frequent errors
Examiner's Tip: The best narratives focus on a single key moment and explore it in depth. Don't try to cover years of story - find the drama in one significant incident.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What are the five parts of a classic story arc?

Q2: What does "in medias res" mean and why is it effective?

Q3: Name two ways to reveal character without directly stating traits.

Q4: What is the difference between first person and third person narrative voice?

Q5: Rewrite this weak opening to be more engaging: "One day I went to the shops and something happened."

✅ Answers

  1. Exposition (introduction), Rising Action (complications), Climax (turning point), Falling Action (consequences), Resolution (conclusion).
  2. "In medias res" means starting in the middle of the action. It's effective because it immediately grabs the reader's attention and creates intrigue from the first line.
  3. Through dialogue (how they speak), through actions (what they do), through reactions (how they respond to events), through interactions with others, through telling details of appearance or behaviour.
  4. First person uses "I" and is limited to the narrator's perspective, creating intimacy. Third person uses "he/she/they" and can be more flexible, showing multiple perspectives or maintaining distance.
  5. Example: "The shops were the last place I wanted to be on a Tuesday. But that was before I saw the man in the grey coat watching me from across the street."

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Plan before you write - know your beginning, middle, and end
  • Focus on ONE key incident - don't try to cover too much
  • Start with impact - hook the reader immediately
  • Show character through action and dialogue, not description
  • Use short sentences to build tension in key moments
  • Include a turning point - something must change
  • End with resonance - leave a final impression
  • Leave time to check spelling and punctuation
