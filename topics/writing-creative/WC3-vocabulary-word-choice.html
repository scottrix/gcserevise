WC3: Vocabulary and Word Choice

Foundation Higher All Boards AO6

Selecting and using ambitious vocabulary effectively - choosing the right words for maximum impact.

📋 Why Vocabulary Matters

Key Point: Your vocabulary choices directly affect your AO6 mark (up to 16 marks). A wide, ambitious vocabulary demonstrates control and precision - but only when used appropriately.

Good vocabulary use:

  • Creates precise, vivid images
  • Avoids repetition that makes writing feel flat
  • Sets appropriate tone and register
  • Shows sophistication without pretension
  • Matches the mood and purpose of your writing
Quality Over Quantity:
Using a few well-chosen, precise words beats stuffing your writing with obscure vocabulary. Every word should earn its place.

🎯 Precise Adjectives

Beyond "Good" and "Bad"

Replace vague adjectives with specific, evocative alternatives.

Vague Precise Alternatives
Good exceptional, outstanding, admirable, commendable, valuable
Bad atrocious, dreadful, abysmal, reprehensible, inadequate
Big colossal, immense, vast, cavernous, monumental
Small minuscule, microscopic, compact, meagre, insignificant
Scary terrifying, petrifying, ominous, menacing, sinister
Happy ecstatic, elated, jubilant, content, radiant
Sad devastated, heartbroken, despondent, melancholy, forlorn
Nice pleasant, agreeable, delightful, charming, considerate
Connotation Matters: "Cheap" and "inexpensive" have similar meanings but different connotations. "Cheap" suggests poor quality; "inexpensive" suggests good value. Choose the word with the right emotional shade.
Vague vs Precise

Vague: The big house looked scary in the dark.

Precise: The cavernous mansion loomed ominous and menacing in the shadows.

💪 Powerful Verbs

Verbs Drive Your Writing

Strong verbs create movement and energy. Avoid weak verb + adverb combinations.

Weak (Verb + Adverb) Strong (Single Verb)
ran quickly sprinted, dashed, raced, bolted
walked slowly strolled, ambled, trudged, meandered
said quietly whispered, murmured, mumbled, breathed
looked angrily glared, scowled, sneered, fumed
hit hard slammed, smashed, battered, pounded
said loudly shouted, bellowed, roared, exclaimed
moved slowly crept, edged, inched, crawled
ate quickly devoured, gobbled, gulped, wolfed
Weak Verbs

She walked quickly across the room and looked angrily at him. "You shouldn't have done that," she said quietly.

Powerful Verbs

She stormed across the room and glared at him. "You shouldn't have done that," she hissed.

Action Verbs for Movement

  • Fast movement: hurtled, surged, erupted, careered
  • Slow movement: drifted, lingered, meandered, crept
  • Aggressive movement: lunged, thrust, seized, barged
  • Gentle movement: glided, floated, eased, slipped

🔄 Avoiding Repetition

The Problem with Repetition

Using the same words repeatedly makes writing feel lazy and flat.

Common Repeat Offenders: said, went, looked, got, nice, good, thing, something, very, really

Alternatives to "Said"

Neutral Angry Happy Sad
stated snapped beamed sobbed
replied growled chirped whimpered
answered hissed laughed choked
added retorted grinned mourned

Alternatives to "Went"

Context Alternatives
Movement on foot wandered, strode, trudged, marched, limped
Movement by vehicle drove, rode, sped, cruised, rattled
Departure departed, left, exited, vanished, retreated
Destination reached, arrived, entered, approached
Repetitive Writing

She went to the door. She went outside. It was nice. She went to the shop. The shop was nice too. She got some things.

Varied Vocabulary

She approached the door and stepped outside. The morning air was crisp and welcoming. She strolled to the corner shop, its faded sign creaking in the breeze. Inside, she gathered her usual supplies.

🎓 Ambitious Synonyms

Tier 2 and Tier 3 Vocabulary

Ambitious vocabulary shows sophistication - but use it naturally.

Warning: Don't use words you don't fully understand. Misused ambitious vocabulary is worse than correctly used simple vocabulary.

Abstract Nouns for Sophistication

Simple Ambitious
happiness euphoria, bliss, contentment, elation
sadness despair, melancholy, grief, desolation
anger fury, rage, indignation, resentment
fear trepidation, dread, terror, apprehension
confusion bewilderment, disorientation, perplexity
hope optimism, aspiration, anticipation

Adverbs to Avoid (and Alternatives)

Many adverbs weaken writing. Replace them with stronger verbs.

Adverb to Avoid Stronger Alternative
very happy ecstatic, overjoyed, delighted
very sad devastated, heartbroken, crushed
very angry furious, livid, incensed
very tired exhausted, drained, fatigued
really big enormous, gigantic, colossal
really small minuscule, microscopic, tiny

🎭 Appropriate Register

Matching Tone to Purpose

Register means the level of formality in your writing. Match it to your audience and purpose.

Register When to Use Features
Formal Articles, letters to officials, speeches Standard English, sophisticated vocabulary, full sentences
Informal Personal stories, dialogue, informal letters Colloquialisms, contractions, conversational tone
Neutral Most creative writing, descriptive pieces Accessible language, varied vocabulary, balanced tone
Creative Writing Register: For narrative and descriptive writing, aim for accessible but varied vocabulary. Too formal feels stiff; too informal feels unpolished.
Too Formal (for creative writing)

The individual proceeded to traverse the thoroughfare in a leisurely manner whilst contemplating the vicissitudes of existence.

Too Informal (for exam writing)

He walked down the street proper chilled, just thinking bout stuff, yeah? Like, deep thoughts and that.

Appropriate Register

He wandered down the street, lost in thought. The afternoon sun warmed his back as he considered, perhaps for the first time, what he truly wanted.

📍 Vocabulary for Effect

Creating Atmosphere Through Word Choice

Build mood through consistent vocabulary choices.

Tense Atmosphere Vocabulary

Verbs: crept, lurched, loomed, thudded, scraped

Adjectives: ominous, sinister, oppressive, suffocating, jagged

Nouns: shadows, silence, darkness, dread, foreboding

Peaceful Atmosphere Vocabulary

Verbs: drifted, glowed, murmured, swayed, settled

Adjectives: gentle, warm, serene, tranquil, soft

Nouns: light, breeze, warmth, calm, stillness

Semantic Fields

A semantic field is a group of words related to a particular theme.

Semantic Field: Water

The room flooded with light. Her voice rippled through the air. Memories surged back. She drowned in thought, waves of emotion washing over her.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Using words you don't understand Leads to incorrect usage, loses marks Only use words you know confidently
Overusing "very" and "really" Weakens writing, shows limited vocabulary Use single stronger words instead
Repeating the same words Makes writing feel flat and lazy Keep a mental list of synonyms
Thesaurus stuffing Words feel forced and unnatural Use ambitious vocabulary naturally
Wrong register for task Inappropriate for audience/purpose Consider who you're writing for
Ignoring connotations Wrong emotional tone for context Think about word associations

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AO6 - Technical Accuracy (16 marks)

  • Level 4 (13-16): Wide vocabulary, varied sentences, accurate SPaG
  • Level 3 (9-12): Reasonable vocabulary, some variety, mostly accurate
  • Level 2 (5-8): Limited vocabulary, simple sentences, some errors
  • Level 1 (1-4): Basic vocabulary, repeated sentence structure, frequent errors
Examiner's Tip: Vocabulary is judged on range AND appropriateness. A wide vocabulary used incorrectly scores lower than a narrower vocabulary used effectively. Quality and precision matter more than quantity.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Replace "very happy" with a single, more precise word.

Q2: What is the difference in connotation between "cheap" and "inexpensive"?

Q3: Rewrite "She walked slowly across the room" using a powerful verb instead of walked + slowly.

Q4: Why should you avoid using words you don't fully understand?

Q5: Give three alternatives to "said" for angry dialogue.

✅ Answers

  1. Ecstatic, overjoyed, elated, jubilant, delighted (any of these or similar).
  2. "Cheap" has negative connotations of poor quality. "Inexpensive" has neutral/positive connotations of good value. Same basic meaning, different emotional impact.
  3. She crept/ambled/trudged/meandered/strolled across the room (any precise verb that shows slow movement).
  4. Misused ambitious vocabulary is obvious to examiners and loses marks. It shows lack of control. Better to use simpler vocabulary correctly than ambitious vocabulary incorrectly.
  5. Snapped, growled, hissed, retorted, barked, snarled (any three of these or similar).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Build vocabulary gradually - learn a few new words each week
  • Practice using new words in sentences before the exam
  • Read widely to absorb vocabulary naturally
  • Check you understand a word's meaning AND connotations
  • Don't force ambitious vocabulary - use it where it fits
  • Proofread for repeated words and find alternatives
  • Consider register - match formality to task and audience
  • Focus on verbs - they drive your writing
