WC5: Punctuation

Foundation Higher All Boards AO6

Using punctuation accurately and for effect - mastering the marks that give your writing structure and style.

📋 Why Punctuation Matters

Key Point: Punctuation is essential for AO6 (Technical Accuracy). Errors cost marks, but correct and sophisticated use can lift your writing to higher levels. Punctuation isn't just rules - it's about communication and effect.

Punctuation:

  • Clarifies meaning - prevents confusion
  • Controls pace - slows down or speeds up reading
  • Creates effects - emphasis, suspense, drama
  • Shows technical control to examiners
  • Makes writing easier to read and understand
Two Aspects of Punctuation:
1. Accuracy: Using punctuation correctly
2. Effect: Using punctuation deliberately for impact

⬤ Full Stops (.)

Basic Rule

Use a full stop at the end of a sentence that is a statement or command.

Correct Use

The rain stopped. She looked outside. The streets glistened.

Please close the door.

Full Stops for Effect

Full stops create finality and emphasis. Use them deliberately.

Effect: Short sentences ending with full stops create a sense of finality, certainty, or drama. Each full stop is a pause, a breath, a moment of emphasis.
Full Stops Creating Tension

He checked the lock. Checked it again. It was secure. He was sure. Then he heard the handle turn.

Common Errors

  • Comma splice: The door opened, she walked in. (Should be two sentences or joined properly)
  • Missing full stop: The door opened she walked in

⁂ Commas (,)

Basic Uses

1. Lists

Separate items in a list. Use Oxford comma (comma before "and") for clarity.

List Commas

She packed her bag with books, a torch, some food, and a map.

2. Compound Sentences

Before coordinating conjunctions joining two independent clauses.

Compound Sentence Comma

The storm passed, and the sun emerged from behind the clouds.

3. After Fronted Subordinate Clauses

When a subordinate clause starts a sentence, follow it with a comma.

Fronted Subordinate Clause

Although she was tired, she kept walking.

When the bell rang, everyone froze.

Because it was raining, they cancelled the match.

4. Embedded Clauses

Use commas around a clause embedded within a sentence.

Embedded Clause

The old house, which had stood empty for years, finally collapsed.

My neighbour, a retired teacher, often waves from her garden.

5. Direct Address

Separate the name of the person being addressed.

Direct Address

"Sarah, can you help me?"

"I don't know, Tom, what do you think?"

Commas for Effect

Commas slow the reader down, creating pauses.

Commas Creating Rhythm

The forest stretched before her, dark and deep, mysterious and ancient, waiting, always waiting.

Comma Splice Error: Never join two complete sentences with just a comma. This is one of the most common punctuation errors in exams.

Wrong: The door opened, she walked in.

Right: The door opened. She walked in.

Right: The door opened, and she walked in.

⋗ Colons (:)

Basic Uses

1. Introducing a List

Use a colon when the list follows a complete sentence.

Colon for Lists

She packed three things: a torch, a map, and her lucky charm.

2. Introducing an Explanation or Example

Use a colon to introduce something that explains or expands on what came before.

Colon for Explanation

There was only one explanation: someone had betrayed them.

He had one goal: to survive.

3. Between Two Related Sentences

Use a colon when the second sentence explains or illustrates the first.

Colon Between Sentences

The answer was obvious: he had been there all along.

She remembered why she'd come: the letter, hidden in the drawer.

Colons for Effect

Colons create anticipation - the reader knows something important follows.

Colon Creating Anticipation

He opened the envelope and read the single word inside: goodbye.

⋙ Semi-Colons (;)

Basic Uses

1. Linking Related Sentences

Use a semi-colon to join two complete sentences that are closely related.

Semi-Colon Linking Sentences

The storm raged outside; inside, the fire crackled warmly.

She had always been afraid of the dark; tonight, she faced her fear.

2. Separating Items in Complex Lists

When list items already contain commas, use semi-colons to separate them.

Semi-Colon in Complex Lists

She visited three cities: Paris, France; Rome, Italy; and Berlin, Germany.

The winners were: Sarah, from Manchester; James, from Leeds; and Priya, from London.

Semi-Colons for Effect

Semi-colons create balance and connection between ideas.

Semi-Colon vs Full Stop: A semi-colon suggests a closer connection than a full stop. Use it when you want to show that two sentences are thematically linked.
Semi-Colon Creating Balance

He wanted to speak; she wanted to listen. Neither moved.

— Dashes (—)

Basic Uses

1. Adding Extra Information

Use dashes to add information - more dramatic than commas.

Dash for Extra Information

The old house - the one nobody entered - stood at the end of the lane.

Her secret - if it could be called that - was safe with him.

2. Showing a Break in Thought

Use a dash to show interruption or a sudden change.

Dash Showing Break

I thought I was alone until -

"Don't move."

3. Emphasising the End of a Sentence

A dash before the final word or phrase creates emphasis.

Dash for Emphasis

There was only one thing left to do - run.

She opened her eyes and saw - nothing.

Dashes for Effect

Dashes are more dramatic than other punctuation. Use them for impact.

Em Dash: In creative writing, use the em dash (—) without spaces, or with spaces on either side. Be consistent throughout your work.

" " Speech Marks

Basic Rules

Speech Punctuation Rules:
  1. Open speech marks before the first word spoken
  2. Punctuation goes inside the closing speech marks
  3. Start a new line for each new speaker
  4. Use a comma, full stop, question mark, or exclamation mark inside the quotes
  5. Use a comma after speech tags like "she said" or "he asked"

Speech Before the Tag

Speech First

"I don't understand," she said.

"Where are we going?" he asked.

"Stop!" she shouted.

Speech After the Tag

Tag First

She said, "I don't understand."

He asked, "Where are we going?"

Split Speech

Speech Split by Tag

"I don't understand," she said, looking confused, "why you're doing this."

"If you go," he warned, "don't come back."

Multiple Speakers

Dialogue Format

"Are you coming?" Sarah asked.

James nodded. "Just give me a minute."

"We don't have a minute."

"Then I guess we'll have to make time."

' Apostrophes (')

Basic Uses

1. Contractions

Show where letters have been omitted.

Contractions

don't = do not

can't = cannot

it's = it is

they're = they are

we've = we have

wouldn't = would not

2. Possession

Show that something belongs to someone or something.

Singular Possession

the girl's book (the book belonging to the girl)

Tom's car

the dog's tail

Plural Possession

the girls' books (the books belonging to the girls)

the dogs' tails

the children's toys (irregular plural - add 's)

Its vs It's: This is the most common apostrophe error.

"It's" = "It is" or "It has" (contraction)

"Its" = belonging to it (possession, NO apostrophe)

Example: The cat licked its paw. It's hungry now.

Common Apostrophe Errors

Error Correction
The dog wagged it's tail. The dog wagged its tail.
Her parent's are here. Her parents are here. (No possession - no apostrophe)
I went to get some pizza's. I went to get some pizzas. (Plural - no apostrophe)
The teams captain. The team's captain.

… Ellipsis (…)

Basic Uses

1. Showing Unfinished Thought

Use ellipsis to show speech trailing off or interruption.

Trailing Off

"I thought you said..." She paused. "Never mind."

"But what if—"

"No what-ifs. Not now."

2. Creating Suspense

Ellipsis builds anticipation and tension.

Creating Suspense

She opened the door slowly... and screamed.

The footsteps grew closer... louder... until they stopped right behind her.

3. Showing Time Passing

Use ellipsis to indicate a passage of time.

Time Passing

She waited... and waited... until finally, the phone rang.

Ellipsis in Exams: Three dots (...) is the standard ellipsis. Don't overuse it - ellipsis is powerful when used sparingly.

❓ Question Marks (?) and Exclamation Marks (!)

Question Marks

Use at the end of a direct question.

Question Marks

"Where are you going?" she asked.

What had she been thinking?

Why would anyone do such a thing?

Exclamation Marks

Use for strong emotion, urgency, or emphasis. Don't overuse.

Exclamation Marks

"Stop!" she screamed.

What a disaster!

He couldn't believe it!

Restraint with Exclamation Marks: In formal writing, use exclamation marks rarely. In creative writing, use them for genuine emphasis - too many weaken their effect.
Overused (Weak) Sparingly Used (Strong)
She ran! She couldn't believe it! The door opened! There he was! She ran. She couldn't believe it. The door opened. There he was.

⚠️ Common Punctuation Errors

Error Type Example Correction
Comma splice The door opened, she walked in. The door opened. She walked in. / The door opened, and she walked in.
Missing comma after fronted clause When the bell rang everyone froze. When the bell rang, everyone froze.
Its/it's confusion The dog licked it's paw. The dog licked its paw.
Wrong speech punctuation "Hello". She said. "Hello," she said.
Missing apostrophe The dogs tail wagged. The dog's tail wagged.
Apostrophe for plurals I bought two apple's. I bought two apples.
Semi-colon misuse The door opened; she walked in. Acceptable if showing close relationship between the actions.
Proofreading Tip: Read your work aloud, punctuation and all. Full stop = pause. Comma = shorter pause. This helps catch missing or misplaced punctuation.

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AO6 - Technical Accuracy (16 marks)

  • Level 4 (13-16): Wide vocabulary, varied sentences, accurate SPaG
  • Level 3 (9-12): Reasonable vocabulary, some variety, mostly accurate
  • Level 2 (5-8): Limited vocabulary, simple sentences, some errors
  • Level 1 (1-4): Basic vocabulary, repeated sentence structure, frequent errors
Examiner's Tip: Spelling, punctuation, and grammar errors can limit you to Level 2 or below, regardless of content quality. Always leave time to proofread your work.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Add the missing punctuation: "the door opened she walked in slowly and looked around"

Q2: What's the difference between these two sentences? "The students' books" vs "The student's books"

Q3: When would you use a colon instead of a full stop?

Q4: Correct the speech punctuation: "Hello she said how are you"

Q5: Explain when to use a semi-colon instead of a comma.

✅ Answers

  1. "The door opened. She walked in slowly and looked around." OR "The door opened. She walked in, slowly, and looked around." (Several acceptable versions depending on intended pace and emphasis.)
  2. "The students' books" = the books belonging to multiple students (plural possession). "The student's books" = the books belonging to one student (singular possession).
  3. Use a colon when the second part explains, illustrates, or expands on the first part. A full stop separates unrelated sentences. Colons create anticipation.
  4. "Hello," she said. "How are you?" OR "Hello," she said, "how are you?"
  5. Use a semi-colon to join two complete, related sentences. Use a comma to separate clauses within a sentence or items in a list. Never use a comma to join two complete sentences (comma splice).

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Leave 5 minutes at the end to proofread your work
  • Read aloud in your head - hear where pauses should be
  • Check every speech mark pair - open and close
  • Double-check its/it's, your/you're, their/they're
  • Use sophisticated punctuation (colons, semi-colons) for higher marks
  • But only use punctuation you're confident with - errors cost marks
  • Check apostrophes in every possessive noun
  • Verify commas after fronted subordinate clauses
