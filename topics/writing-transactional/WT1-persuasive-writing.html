WT1: Persuasive Writing

Foundation Higher All Boards AO5 AO6

Writing to persuade and convince an audience - using rhetorical devices and persuasive techniques effectively.

📋 What is Persuasive Writing?

Definition: Persuasive writing aims to convince the reader to agree with your viewpoint or take a specific action. It uses a range of techniques to appeal to logic, emotion, and credibility.

Persuasive writing requires you to:

  • Establish a clear viewpoint or argument
  • Use rhetorical devices to strengthen your case
  • Address potential counter-arguments
  • Appeal to your audience's emotions and logic
  • End with a clear call to action
Key Formula:
Clear viewpoint + Rhetorical devices + Evidence + Counter-arguments + Call to action = Effective persuasion

🎯 Key Persuasive Techniques

1. Rhetorical Questions

Questions that don't require an answer but make the reader think.

Example

"Do we really want our children growing up in a world without nature?"

"Is this the best we can do?"

2. The Rule of Three (Triples)

Grouping three ideas together for impact and memorability.

Example

"Education, opportunity, and hope for all."

"Fast, reliable, and affordable."

3. Emotive Language

Words that evoke strong feelings in the reader.

Example

"Innocent animals suffer cruel, unnecessary experiments."

"Devastated families left heartbroken by cuts to services."

4. Direct Address

Speaking directly to the reader using pronouns like "you" and "we".

Example

"You have the power to change this."

"Together, we can make a difference."

5. Statistics and Facts

Using data to support your argument with authority.

Example

"Over 80% of students support this change."

"Studies show that regular exercise reduces stress by 40%."

6. Expert Opinion

Quoting experts to add credibility to your argument.

Example

"As Dr. Smith from Cambridge University states, 'This approach has proven results.'"

📝 Structuring Your Argument

The Classic Structure

Introduction:

  • Hook the reader with a striking opening
  • State your viewpoint clearly
  • Outline what you'll discuss

Main Body:

  • Present your strongest argument first
  • Support each point with evidence
  • Use a new paragraph for each point
  • Address counter-arguments

Conclusion:

  • Summarise your main points
  • Reinforce your viewpoint
  • End with a powerful call to action

⚔️ Addressing Counter-Arguments

Strong persuasive writing acknowledges opposing views and refutes them.

Example Approach

Some may argue that banning homework would reduce learning. However, research shows that quality matters more than quantity, and shorter, focused tasks are more effective than hours of repetitive exercises.

Useful phrases for counter-arguments:
  • "Some may argue that... However..."
  • "Critics claim... But the evidence shows..."
  • "While it's true that..., this doesn't mean..."
  • "Opponents suggest... Nevertheless..."

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Being too aggressive Alienates your audience Use respectful, reasonable tone
Not supporting claims Arguments lack credibility Use facts, statistics, examples
Ignoring the audience Writing feels disconnected Use direct address and appropriate tone
No counter-argument Shows one-sided thinking Acknowledge and refute opposing views
Weak conclusion Ending lacks impact End with a clear call to action

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AO5: Content and Organisation (24 marks)

  • Level 6 (21-24): Convincing, compelling ideas; extensive vocabulary
  • Level 5 (17-20): Effective, varied ideas; wide vocabulary
  • Level 4 (13-16): Sound ideas; clear vocabulary
  • Level 3 (9-12): Some relevant ideas; appropriate vocabulary

AO6: Technical Accuracy (16 marks)

  • Level 6 (13-16): Wide range of punctuation; sophisticated spelling
  • Level 5 (9-12): Range of punctuation; mostly accurate spelling
  • Level 4 (5-8): Some variation in punctuation; some errors
  • Level 3 (1-4): Basic punctuation; frequent errors
Examiner's Tip: Plan before you write. Spend 5 minutes planning your arguments, evidence, and structure. This ensures a coherent, well-organised response.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Name three persuasive techniques and explain how each works.

Q2: Why is it important to address counter-arguments in persuasive writing?

Q3: Write an opening paragraph persuading your school to introduce a healthy eating policy.

Q4: What is the purpose of a "call to action" in persuasive writing?

✅ Answers

  1. Rhetorical questions (make reader think), emotive language (evoke feelings), statistics (add credibility) - plus many other valid techniques.
  2. It shows you've considered different viewpoints and strengthens your argument by preemptively refuting opposition.
  3. Your paragraph should include: clear viewpoint, rhetorical question or hook, and persuasive techniques.
  4. A call to action tells the reader exactly what you want them to do after reading, making your writing purposeful and memorable.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Always establish your viewpoint in the first paragraph
  • Use a range of techniques - don't repeat the same one
  • Support every point with evidence or examples
  • Address the counter-argument to show balanced thinking
  • End with a powerful, memorable call to action
  • Use paragraphs to structure your argument clearly
  • Check your spelling and punctuation carefully

📝 Exam Technique

Persuasive Writing Exam Strategy: You have 45 minutes for this question (40 marks total: 24 AO5 + 16 AO6). Spend 5 minutes planning, 35 minutes writing, and 5 minutes proofreading.
  • First 5 minutes: Identify your audience and purpose from the question. Circle both. Then plan: your viewpoint, 3-4 key arguments, evidence for each, a counter-argument to address, and your call to action.
  • Writing time: Establish your viewpoint in paragraph 1. Use DAFOREST techniques throughout. Address the counter-argument. End with a powerful call to action.
  • Mark allocation: AO5 (24 marks) rewards your arguments, evidence, and structure. AO6 (16 marks) rewards technical accuracy — formal register, varied sentences, correct SPaG.
  • What examiners look for: A clear viewpoint sustained throughout. A range of persuasive techniques used naturally (not just listed). Evidence supporting each point. A balanced argument that acknowledges and refutes opposition.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often list techniques without integrating them naturally. Wrong: "I will now use a rhetorical question. Why should we accept this? Now I will use a statistic. 80% of people agree." Correct: Weave techniques seamlessly into your argument — "Why should we accept a system that fails 80% of the people it claims to serve?"

Students often state opinions without supporting evidence. Wrong: "Homework is bad and should be banned because students don't like it." Correct: Support every claim — "Homework should be reformed, not banned. Research from the University of London found that excessive homework — over two hours per night — correlates with increased stress and decreased academic performance."

Students often forget to address the counter-argument. Wrong: Only presenting one side — "Uniforms are terrible and everyone hates them." Correct: Acknowledge and refute — "Some argue that uniform promotes equality. However, when families spend an average of £300 per year on branded uniform items, it is clear that uniform creates financial inequality rather than reducing it."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

40 marks: Write a letter to your local council arguing that more should be done to protect green spaces in your area.

Dear Councillor Reeves,

I am writing to urge the council to take immediate action to protect the remaining green spaces in our borough — spaces that are disappearing at an alarming rate, and with them, the wellbeing of our community.

Consider this: our borough has lost 40% of its public green space in the last decade alone. Fields where children once played have become car parks. The ancient oak on Mill Lane — a tree that has stood for over two hundred years — was felled in a single morning to make way for a distribution centre. When did concrete become more valuable than community?

Some may argue that development is necessary for economic growth, and I accept that housing is needed. However, this is not an either-or choice. Research from the University of Exeter demonstrates that access to green spaces improves mental health by 30%, reduces crime rates, and increases local property values. Protecting green spaces is not an obstacle to prosperity — it is a foundation of it.

Furthermore, the Climate Change Committee has identified urban green spaces as critical infrastructure for flood prevention and air quality. With our borough experiencing its worst flooding in living memory last autumn, removing the very land that absorbs excess water is not just short-sighted — it is dangerous.

I therefore urge the council to: implement an immediate moratorium on development of designated green spaces; invest in the creation of new community gardens; and establish a Green Spaces Protection Policy with legal weight. Our parks, our playing fields, and our wild places are not empty spaces waiting to be filled — they are living assets that sustain us. Please act before there is nothing left to protect.

Yours sincerely,

A concerned resident

Mark scheme: AO5 (21-24/24) — Convincing, compelling argument with clear viewpoint sustained throughout; well-structured with logical progression; counter-argument addressed and refuted; powerful call to action with specific demands. AO6 (14-16/16) — Formal register maintained; wide vocabulary ("moratorium", "infrastructure", "short-sighted"); varied sentences including rhetorical questions, colon for list, and dash for emphasis; accurate SPaG; correct letter format.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO5 — Content and Organisation (24 marks): For persuasive writing, this AO rewards the strength of your argument and how well you organise it. At grade 5 (Level 3, 9-12 marks), students present "some relevant ideas" but may lack evidence, structure, or a clear viewpoint. At grade 9 (Level 6, 21-24 marks), the argument is "convincing and compelling" — every point is supported with evidence, the counter-argument is acknowledged and skilfully refuted, and the structure builds to a powerful conclusion. The key leap from grade 5 to 9 is substance: grade 9 writers don't just state opinions, they build a case, using evidence, reasoning, and rhetoric to make their argument genuinely persuasive rather than merely opinionated.

AO6 — Technical Accuracy (16 marks): Persuasive writing requires a formal register, which means no contractions, no slang, and sophisticated vocabulary. At grade 5 (Level 3, 9-12 marks), the tone may slip into informality, and sentences may lack variety. At grade 9 (Level 4, 13-16 marks), the register is consistently formal, vocabulary is wide and precise, and sentence structures are varied for rhetorical effect — rhetorical questions, balanced clauses, and emphatic short sentences all serve the persuasive purpose.
