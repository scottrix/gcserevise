WT4: Articles

Foundation Higher All Boards AO5 AO6

Writing newspaper and magazine articles - mastering headlines, structure, and journalistic style.

📋 What Makes a Good Article?

Definition: An article is written for publication in a newspaper, magazine, or online platform. It informs, persuades, or entertains a specific audience while following journalistic conventions.

Key elements of effective articles:

  • Eye-catching headline that draws the reader in
  • Engaging opening that hooks the reader
  • Clear structure with subheadings if needed
  • Appropriate tone for the publication and audience
  • Strong, memorable conclusion
Article Formula:
Catchy headline + Engaging opening + Clear structure + Relevant content + Strong conclusion = Effective article

📰 Article Structure

1. The Headline

Your first opportunity to grab attention - make it count.

Headline Techniques:
  • Use alliteration: "Baking Ban Begins"
  • Ask a question: "Is This the End of Free Speech?"
  • Make a bold statement: "Our Schools Are Failing"
  • Use a pun or wordplay: "Storm Brewing Over Tea Tax"
  • Create intrigue: "The Hidden Danger in Your Home"
Example Headlines

Persuasive article: "Why Every School Needs a Garden"

News article: "Council U-Turn on Parking Charges"

Feature article: "The Teenagers Changing Their Communities"

2. The Opening Paragraph

Hook the reader with your first few lines.

Opening Techniques:
  • Start with a dramatic statement
  • Use a rhetorical question
  • Begin with an interesting fact or statistic
  • Open with a brief anecdote or scenario
Example Opening

"Every year, 8 million tonnes of plastic end up in our oceans. That's equivalent to dumping a garbage truck full of plastic into the sea every minute. The question is: what are we going to do about it?"

3. The Main Body

  • Present your points in a logical order
  • Use topic sentences to introduce each paragraph
  • Include evidence, facts, and expert opinions
  • Use subheadings to break up longer articles
  • Maintain reader interest throughout

4. The Conclusion

  • Summarise your main argument
  • Leave the reader with something to think about
  • End with a powerful final statement
  • Include a call to action if appropriate

📝 Types of Articles

News Articles

Report factual information objectively.

  • Answer: Who? What? Where? When? Why? How?
  • Use inverted pyramid structure (most important info first)
  • Include quotes from relevant sources
  • Maintain objective tone

Opinion/Comment Articles

Express and argue for a viewpoint.

  • Clear opinion from the start
  • Use persuasive techniques
  • Include evidence to support your view
  • Address counter-arguments

Feature Articles

Explore topics in depth, often with a human interest angle.

  • More creative, engaging style
  • Can include personal stories and interviews
  • Longer, more detailed exploration
  • Strong narrative element
Article Opening Examples

News: "Local councillor Jane Smith has announced her resignation following allegations of misconduct."

Opinion: "It's time we faced the truth: homework is doing more harm than good."

Feature: "When 15-year-old Aisha Patel started her recycling campaign, she never imagined it would reach national headlines."

✍️ Journalistic Style

Feature Description Example
Short paragraphs Easy to read, visually appealing 1-3 sentences per paragraph
Direct quotes Add credibility and interest "This is unacceptable," said resident Tom Green.
Factual language Builds trust and authority Specific statistics, dates, names
Active voice More engaging and direct "The council decided" not "It was decided"
Varied sentence length Creates rhythm and maintains interest Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones
Top Tip: Articles are meant to be read quickly. Use short paragraphs, clear topic sentences, and subheadings to guide the reader through your argument.

🎯 Audience and Publication

Adapt your writing style to suit your target publication and audience.

Publication Examples

School newsletter: Informal but respectful, focused on school community

Local newspaper: Semi-formal, community-focused, accessible language

Broadsheet newspaper: Formal, analytical, sophisticated vocabulary

Teen magazine: Informal, engaging, relatable, contemporary references

Key Questions to Ask

  • Who will read this article?
  • What publication am I writing for?
  • What tone is appropriate?
  • What level of vocabulary should I use?

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Boring headline Won't attract readers Use techniques like alliteration, questions, or bold statements
Weak opening Readers won't continue Start with a hook - fact, question, or dramatic statement
Long, dense paragraphs Difficult to read Keep paragraphs short - 2-3 sentences
No clear structure Reader loses track Use subheadings and clear topic sentences
Wrong tone for audience Feels inappropriate Match style to publication and readers

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AO5: Content and Organisation (24 marks)

  • Engaging headline and opening
  • Clear structure appropriate for article format
  • Consistent tone matched to audience
  • Effective use of journalistic conventions

AO6: Technical Accuracy (16 marks)

  • Varied sentence structures
  • Appropriate vocabulary for publication
  • Accurate spelling and punctuation
  • Correct use of quotes if included
Examiner's Tip: Spend time crafting your headline - it's your first impression. A good headline can boost your mark. Make it catchy, relevant, and appropriate for your target publication.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: Write three different headlines for an article about school uniform using different techniques.

Q2: What is the "inverted pyramid" structure and when would you use it?

Q3: Write an opening paragraph for an opinion article about social media.

Q4: How should article style differ between a teen magazine and a broadsheet newspaper?

✅ Answers

  1. Examples: "Uniform Uproar" (alliteration), "Should We Ditch School Uniform?" (question), "The Hidden Cost of School Uniform" (intrigue), "Uniform: Unfair and Unnecessary" (bold statement).
  2. The inverted pyramid puts the most important information first, then supporting details in decreasing order of importance. Used for news articles to ensure key facts aren't cut if space is limited.
  3. Your opening should hook the reader with a dramatic statement, surprising fact, or rhetorical question about social media.
  4. Teen magazine: informal, relatable, contemporary references, conversational tone. Broadsheet: formal, analytical, sophisticated vocabulary, serious tone.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Craft a catchy, relevant headline
  • Hook the reader in your first sentence
  • Use short paragraphs - they're easier to read
  • Include facts, statistics, or quotes for credibility
  • Match your tone to the target publication
  • Use subheadings for longer articles
  • End with a powerful, memorable statement
