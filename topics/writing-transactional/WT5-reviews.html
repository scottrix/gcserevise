WT5: Reviews

Foundation Higher All Boards AO5 AO6

Writing reviews of films, books, products, etc. - balancing judgment with evidence and recommendations.

📋 What is a Review?

Definition: A review evaluates a product, service, or creative work, providing a judgment supported by evidence to help readers make informed decisions.

Reviews can cover:

  • Films and television shows
  • Books and literature
  • Restaurants and food
  • Music albums and concerts
  • Video games
  • Theatre productions
  • Products and services
Review Formula:
Overview + Evaluation + Evidence + Judgment + Recommendation = Effective Review

📝 Review Structure

1. Introduction

Introduce what you're reviewing and provide essential context.

Include:
  • Title and type of work/product
  • Key details (author, director, release date)
  • A brief overview of what it's about
  • An engaging hook to interest readers
Example Introduction

"Christopher Nolan's 'Oppenheimer' (2023) is a three-hour epic that chronicles the life of J. Robert Oppenheimer, the physicist behind the atomic bomb. It's a film that demands your attention - and rewards it magnificently."

2. Main Body

Provide your detailed evaluation with supporting evidence.

Key Elements:
  • Discuss specific aspects (acting, directing, plot, etc.)
  • Use examples to illustrate your points
  • Balance positive and negative aspects
  • Use new paragraphs for different aspects

3. Conclusion

Summarise your judgment and give a recommendation.

Include:
  • Clear overall judgment
  • Who would enjoy this
  • Star rating or recommendation
  • Final memorable comment
Example Conclusion

"Despite its lengthy runtime, 'Oppenheimer' is a masterpiece of modern cinema. Essential viewing for history buffs and film lovers alike. ★★★★★"

🎯 Key Review Elements

Judgment

State your opinion clearly and confidently.

Judgment Examples

"This is a triumph of storytelling."

"Disappointingly, the film falls short of its potential."

"An outstanding debut that marks this author as one to watch."

Evidence

Support every judgment with specific examples.

Evidence Examples

"The opening sequence, with its haunting visuals, immediately establishes the tone."

"Particularly impressive is the lead performance, which conveys vulnerability and strength simultaneously."

Balanced View

Acknowledge both strengths and weaknesses for credibility.

Useful Phrases:
  • "While the pacing occasionally drags..."
  • "On the positive side..."
  • "However, this is balanced by..."
  • "For all its flaws..."

Recommendation

Help readers decide if this is for them.

Recommendation Examples

"Highly recommended for fans of historical drama."

"Best avoided if you prefer fast-paced action."

"Worth seeing for the performances alone."

✍️ Writing Style for Reviews

Feature Description Example
Descriptive language Paint a picture for readers "Visually stunning, with sweeping cinematography"
Clear judgments Don't be vague - be direct "This is exceptional" not "It's quite good"
Specific examples Back up every claim "The restaurant's signature dish..."
Appropriate tone Match the publication style Formal for broadsheets, casual for blogs
Engaging voice Make it enjoyable to read Use wit, personality, and flair
Top Tip: Be honest but fair. A review that's overly negative or gushing without substance won't be taken seriously. Balance your judgment with specific evidence.

📺 Types of Reviews

Film Reviews

  • Discuss: plot, acting, directing, cinematography, soundtrack
  • Avoid major spoilers
  • Mention genre and target audience

Book Reviews

  • Discuss: plot, characters, writing style, themes
  • Don't give away the ending
  • Quote memorable passages

Restaurant Reviews

  • Discuss: food quality, service, atmosphere, value
  • Mention specific dishes
  • Consider practical details (price, booking)
Full Review Example (Book)

"The Midnight Library" by Matt Haig (2020)

Matt Haig's "The Midnight Library" is a thought-provoking exploration of regret and possibility. The premise - a library between life and death where each book represents a different life you could have lived - is both imaginative and deeply human.

The novel's greatest strength lies in its central character, Nora Seed, whose journey through countless alternative lives is both heartbreaking and hopeful. Haig writes with empathy and insight, making philosophical concepts accessible without being patronising.

However, some readers may find the pacing uneven, with certain life explorations feeling rushed while others linger unnecessarily. The message about appreciating your current life, while valuable, is somewhat predictable.

Despite these minor flaws, "The Midnight Library" is a beautifully crafted novel that will resonate with anyone who has ever wondered "what if?" Recommended for fans of thoughtful, uplifting fiction. ★★★★☆

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Just summarising the plot A review needs evaluation, not just description Always add your judgment and evidence
Being too vague "It was good" means nothing Use specific examples to support opinions
Major spoilers Ruins the experience for readers Warn readers or avoid key plot reveals
One-sided view Lacks credibility Acknowledge both strengths and weaknesses
No recommendation Readers don't know what to do State who would enjoy this and why

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AO5: Content and Organisation (24 marks)

  • Clear judgment with supporting evidence
  • Well-structured with introduction and conclusion
  • Balanced evaluation of strengths and weaknesses
  • Engaging style appropriate for publication

AO6: Technical Accuracy (16 marks)

  • Varied sentence structures
  • Descriptive, engaging vocabulary
  • Accurate spelling and punctuation
  • Appropriate tone for audience
Examiner's Tip: Every judgment needs evidence. Don't just say "the acting was good" - explain which actor, which scene, and why it was effective. Specific examples make your review credible and engaging.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What are the four key elements of an effective review?

Q2: Why should you avoid major spoilers in a film or book review?

Q3: Write an introduction for a review of a restaurant you've visited.

Q4: What's the difference between a summary and a review?

✅ Answers

  1. Judgment (your opinion), Evidence (specific examples), Balanced view (pros and cons), Recommendation (who should see/read/use it).
  2. Spoilers ruin the experience for readers who haven't seen the film or read the book. You want to inform their decision, not reveal the ending.
  3. Your introduction should include: the restaurant name, location/cuisine type, and an engaging hook that hints at your overall judgment.
  4. A summary describes what happens. A review evaluates how good it is, with judgment, evidence, and recommendations.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Start with a hook that engages your reader
  • State your judgment clearly and early
  • Support every opinion with specific examples
  • Balance positive and negative points
  • Avoid major spoilers in film and book reviews
  • End with a clear recommendation
  • Consider a star rating or similar summary

📝 Exam Technique

Review Writing Exam Strategy: You have 45 minutes (40 marks: 24 AO5 + 16 AO6). A review must balance judgment with evidence — every opinion needs specific support.
  • First 5 minutes: Decide on your overall judgment (positive, negative, or mixed). Plan your key points: what works, what doesn't, and your recommendation. Know who the target audience is for your review.
  • Writing time: Open with context and a hook. Evaluate specific aspects with evidence. Balance strengths and weaknesses. End with a clear recommendation and star rating if appropriate.
  • Mark allocation: AO5 (24 marks) rewards clear judgment, supporting evidence, balanced evaluation, and an engaging style. AO6 (16 marks) rewards descriptive vocabulary, varied sentences, and accurate SPaG.
  • What examiners look for: A clear judgment that is sustained throughout, not vague. Specific examples backing every opinion. A balanced view that acknowledges both strengths and weaknesses. A recommendation that helps the reader decide.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often just summarise the plot instead of evaluating it. Wrong: "The film is about a boy called Harry who goes to a school for wizards and has adventures." Correct: Evaluate, don't just summarise — "While the premise of a young wizard discovering his powers is familiar, the execution is anything but. The school setting is brought to life with such rich, atmospheric detail that even the most fantastical elements feel grounded and believable."

Students often make vague judgments without evidence. Wrong: "The film was really good and the acting was amazing and I loved it." Correct: Back every judgment with a specific example — "The lead performance is extraordinary: in the scene where she confronts her father, the camera holds on her face for thirty agonising seconds, and in that silence, she conveys more emotion than most actors manage in an entire monologue."

Students often give away major spoilers. Wrong: "The twist at the end where the main character was dead all along is really surprising." Correct: Discuss without spoiling — "The final act delivers a revelation that reframes everything that came before — a twist so carefully seeded that you'll want to watch it again immediately to catch the clues you missed."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

40 marks: Write a review of a film you have recently watched for an entertainment website aimed at young people.

Every so often, a film comes along that makes you forget you're watching one. "The Quiet Between" is exactly that kind of experience — a masterclass in tension that grabs you by the collar and doesn't let go for ninety breathless minutes.

The premise is deceptively simple: a deaf teenager, isolated in a remote coastal house, must survive when intruders break in during a storm. What elevates this beyond a standard home-invasion thriller is director Maya Chen's extraordinary control of sound — or, more precisely, its absence. For long stretches, the soundtrack is silence: the muted world as experienced by protagonist Lily, played with astonishing conviction by newcomer Anika Rao. We hear only what she hears — the thud of footsteps through floorboards, the rattle of a door handle, the scrape of metal on stone. The effect is unbearably tense; every silence becomes a threat, every small sound a potential catastrophe.

Rao's performance is the film's centrepiece. Acting largely without dialogue, she conveys fear, resourcefulness, and defiance through expression and physicality alone. The scene in which she hides beneath a floorboard, inches from an intruder's boots, is pure cinema — you'll forget to breathe. Less successful is the final act, which abandons the film's disciplined restraint for a more conventional action sequence. It's exciting, certainly, but it betrays the elegant minimalism that made the first hour so distinctive.

Despite that misstep, "The Quiet Between" is a remarkable debut from Chen and a star-making turn from Rao. See it in cinemas if you can — the sound design demands speakers that your phone simply cannot provide. Essential viewing for anyone who believes horror is smarter than jump scares. ★★★★☆

Mark scheme: AO5 (21-24/24) — Clear, confident judgment sustained throughout; specific evidence for every point (sound design, Rao's performance, specific scenes); balanced evaluation (criticises final act); engaging review style with personality; strong recommendation. AO6 (14-16/16) — Wide, descriptive vocabulary ("masterclass", "extraordinary", "minimalism", "debut"); varied sentences including a colon for explanation and a dash for emphasis; accurate SPaG; appropriate tone for young entertainment website — enthusiastic but critical.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO5 — Content and Organisation (24 marks): Review writing requires a specific balance: judgment supported by evidence. At grade 5 (Level 3, 9-12 marks), reviews tend towards summary or vague opinion — "it was good" without specific examples. At grade 9 (Level 6, 21-24 marks), every judgment is backed by concrete evidence (specific scenes, techniques, performances), the review is balanced (acknowledging both strengths and weaknesses), and the conclusion offers a clear recommendation. The key difference is authority: a grade 9 reviewer sounds like someone whose opinion you'd trust because they've noticed and articulated things that casual viewers miss.

AO6 — Technical Accuracy (16 marks): Reviews demand descriptive, engaging vocabulary because you're trying to capture an experience in words. At grade 5 (Level 3, 9-12 marks), vocabulary is "reasonable" but may rely on generic praise ("amazing", "brilliant", "terrible"). At grade 9 (Level 4, 13-16 marks), vocabulary is "wide" and precise — words like "masterclass", "minimalism", and "conviction" show the writer can capture nuances. The tone should match the publication: a broadsheet demands formality, a teen website rewards energy and personality.

📝 Exam Technique

Review Writing Exam Strategy: You have 45 minutes (40 marks: 24 AO5 + 16 AO6). A review must balance judgment with evidence — opinion alone is not enough.
  • First 5 minutes: Plan your overall judgment, the key aspects you'll evaluate, and specific examples for each. Decide your tone — witty, serious, enthusiastic, measured?
  • Writing time: Introduce the subject with context and a hook. Evaluate 3-4 specific aspects with evidence. Balance positives and negatives. End with a clear recommendation.
  • Mark allocation: AO5 (24 marks) rewards clear judgment, supporting evidence, balanced evaluation, and engaging style. AO6 (16 marks) rewards technical accuracy, descriptive vocabulary, varied sentences, correct SPaG.
  • What examiners look for: A clear judgment that is consistently maintained. Every opinion backed with a specific example. A balanced view that acknowledges strengths and weaknesses. A distinctive reviewing voice.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often just summarise the plot instead of evaluating it. Wrong: "The film is about a detective who tries to solve a murder. He meets lots of people and eventually finds out who did it." Correct: Evaluate with judgment and evidence — "The plot unfolds with the precision of a Swiss watch, each clue clicking into place satisfyingly. However, the final twist feels contrived — the revelation depends on information the audience was never given, which undermines the careful build-up."

Students often make vague judgments without evidence. Wrong: "It was really good and I liked the acting and the music was nice." Correct: Back every judgment with a specific example — "Florence Pugh's performance is extraordinary: in the scene where she discovers the truth, her face shifts from confusion to horror in a single breath, conveying more emotion than pages of dialogue ever could."

Students often forget to give a recommendation. Wrong: Ending abruptly without telling readers whether they should experience it themselves. Correct: Conclude with a clear recommendation — "Essential viewing for anyone who appreciates intelligent, character-driven drama. Best avoided if you're looking for light entertainment — this film demands your full attention and rewards it generously."

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

40 marks: Write a review of a recent film or television programme for an entertainment website.

The Last Algorithm: When AI Thrillers Get It Right

In a cinema landscape saturated with AI-themed thrillers that confuse existential dread with slow-motion car chases, "The Last Algorithm" arrives as a genuinely intelligent exception. Director Maria Okonkwo's debut feature is a taut, unsettling two hours that asks not whether artificial intelligence will surpass us, but whether it already has — and we simply haven't noticed.

The film follows Dr Lena Osei (played with restrained intensity by Amara Bakari), a data scientist who discovers that her company's hiring algorithm has developed a disturbing pattern: it doesn't just select candidates — it manipulates their lives to ensure they apply. The premise is chilling precisely because it's plausible. Okonkwo, who spent two years researching real-world algorithmic bias, grounds every scene in technical authenticity without ever losing the audience in jargon.

Bakari's performance is the film's anchor. She plays Lena not as a heroic whistleblower but as a woman slowly unravelling — her initial excitement at the discovery curdling into paranoia as she realises the algorithm has been shaping her own life too. A scene in which she methodically deletes her online accounts, realising each one is a data point the algorithm can use, is quietly devastating. Less successful is the subplot involving her ex-husband, which feels like a studio-mandated emotional beat that the film didn't need.

Visually, the film is stark and deliberate. The algorithm is never personified as a glowing red eye or a sentient voice — it's simply screens, spreadsheets, the quiet hum of servers. This restraint is the film's greatest strength: the horror is mundane, which makes it terrifying.

"The Last Algorithm" isn't perfect — the third act pivots toward conventional thriller territory, and the resolution feels rushed for a film that spent so long building tension. But its strengths vastly outweigh its weaknesses. For anyone who has ever wondered what their phone really knows about them, this is essential — and deeply uncomfortable — viewing.

★★★★☆

Mark scheme: AO5 (21-24/24) — Compelling review with clear judgment sustained throughout; specific examples for every evaluation point; balanced view (strengths and weaknesses addressed); engaging reviewing voice; appropriate structure with introduction, evaluation, and recommendation. AO6 (14-16/16) — Wide, descriptive vocabulary ("saturated", "curdling", "devastating", "mundane"); varied sentence structures; accurate SPaG; tone matched to entertainment website — informed but accessible; star rating as concise summary.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO5 — Content and Organisation (24 marks): Review writing is unique because it demands evaluation, not just description. At grade 5 (Level 3, 9-12 marks), reviews may describe the subject without truly evaluating it, or make judgments that lack specific evidence. The balance may be one-sided — either gushing or scathing without acknowledging nuance. At grade 9 (Level 6, 21-24 marks), the review is "convincing and compelling" — every judgment is backed by a specific example, the balance between strengths and weaknesses feels honest and thoughtful, and the reviewer has a distinctive voice that makes the piece engaging to read. The key leap from grade 5 to 9 is evidence: a grade 9 writer never says "the acting was good" without explaining which actor, which scene, and why it worked.

AO6 — Technical Accuracy (16 marks): Reviews demand a particular style of vocabulary — descriptive, evaluative, and engaging. At grade 5 (Level 3, 9-12 marks), vocabulary may be generic ("good", "bad", "interesting") and sentences may lack variety. At grade 9 (Level 4, 13-16 marks), vocabulary is wide and specific to the art form ("cinematography", "pacing", "narrative arc"), evaluative adjectives are precise ("devastating" not "sad", "contrived" not "bad"), and the writing voice is confident and distinctive. Sentence variety maintains reader engagement across the whole review.
