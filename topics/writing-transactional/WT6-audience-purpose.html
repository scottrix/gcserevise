WT6: Audience and Purpose

Foundation Higher All Boards AO5

Adapting writing for different audiences and purposes - understanding tone, register, and style choices.

📋 Why Audience and Purpose Matter

Golden Rule: Every piece of writing should be shaped by who will read it (audience) and what you want to achieve (purpose). These two factors determine your tone, vocabulary, structure, and style.

In the exam, you must:

  • Identify who your audience is
  • Understand your purpose for writing
  • Adapt your language and tone accordingly
  • Show awareness of these choices in your writing
Key Relationship:
Audience + Purpose = Tone + Style + Vocabulary + Structure

🎯 Understanding Audience

Types of Audience

Audience Type Characteristics Example
General public Broad, varied readership Newspaper readers
Specific age group Children, teenagers, adults, elderly Teen magazine readers
Professionals Expert knowledge, formal Doctors, teachers, lawyers
Personal/known Friends, family, colleagues Informal letter recipient
Authority figures MPs, headteachers, officials Council, school governors

Questions to Ask About Your Audience

  • What age group are they?
  • What do they already know about the topic?
  • What is their likely attitude toward the subject?
  • What tone will engage them?
  • What vocabulary level is appropriate?

📝 Understanding Purpose

Main Purposes for Writing

Purpose Goal Key Features
Persuade Convince reader to agree or act Rhetorical devices, arguments, call to action
Inform Provide facts and information Clear, factual, objective language
Entertain Amuse and engage reader Humour, creativity, vivid descriptions
Advise Give guidance and recommendations Helpful tone, clear instructions, examples
Argue Present a case for a viewpoint Logical structure, evidence, counter-arguments
Describe Create vivid images Sensory details, figurative language
Same Topic, Different Purposes

Topic: School uniform

Persuade: "School uniform should be abolished. It suppresses individuality and costs families a fortune..."

Inform: "School uniform policies vary across the UK. Approximately 90% of secondary schools require uniform..."

Advise: "When choosing a school uniform, look for durable fabrics, adjustable waistbands, and easy-care materials..."

✍️ Adapting Your Writing

Formal vs Informal Register

Feature Formal Informal
Audience Strangers, professionals, officials Friends, family, peers
Vocabulary Sophisticated, precise, subject-specific Everyday, colloquial, slang acceptable
Contractions Cannot, do not, it is Can't, don't, it's
Sentences Complex, varied, sophisticated Simpler, conversational, varied
Tone Objective, respectful, serious Personal, emotional, relaxed
First person Limited, avoided in some contexts Common, personal experiences
Top Tip: The exam usually requires a formal or semi-formal tone. However, you must still adapt your writing to the specific audience - a speech to teenagers will be different from a letter to an MP, even if both are formal.

🎭 Tone Choices

Different Tones for Different Contexts

Tone Examples

Serious: "Climate change poses an existential threat to our planet."

Light-hearted: "Let's face it - we've all been tempted to hit the snooze button just one more time..."

Passionate: "We cannot stand by while our oceans are polluted, our forests destroyed!"

Objective: "Research indicates that 73% of students prefer later start times."

Conversational: "You know that feeling when you walk into a room and forget why you're there?"

Matching Tone to Purpose and Audience

  • Speech to students: Passionate, direct address, relatable
  • Letter to MP: Respectful, formal, evidence-based
  • Article for teen magazine: Conversational, engaging, contemporary
  • Newspaper editorial: Persuasive, authoritative, serious
  • Blog post: Personal, informal, chatty

🔍 Vocabulary Choices

Selecting the Right Words

Consider:
  • Age appropriateness - younger audiences need simpler vocabulary
  • Subject knowledge - experts understand technical terms
  • Cultural awareness - references must be accessible
  • Tone requirements - emotive words for persuasion, neutral for information
Same Idea, Different Audiences

For primary school children: "Too much sugar is bad for your teeth and can make you unhealthy."

For teenagers: "Excessive sugar consumption leads to tooth decay and health problems like diabetes."

For health professionals: "High sugar intake is correlated with dental caries, obesity, and type 2 diabetes mellitus."

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake Why It's Wrong How to Fix It
Wrong tone for audience Appears inappropriate or disrespectful Match tone to who will read it
Ignoring the purpose Writing doesn't achieve its goal Keep purpose in mind throughout
Too informal for formal task Loses credibility and marks Use formal language for most exam tasks
Vocabulary mismatch Too complex or too simple Adapt vocabulary to audience knowledge
Inconsistent tone Writing feels disjointed Maintain consistent register throughout

📊 Mark Scheme Guidance

AO5: Content and Organisation (24 marks)

  • Register matched to audience and purpose
  • Tone consistent and appropriate
  • Vocabulary choices show audience awareness
  • Structure suits the form and purpose
Examiner's Tip: The exam question will tell you your audience and purpose. Read them carefully! A speech "to your school assembly" needs a different tone than "to a local council meeting." Circle the audience and purpose, then plan your tone and style before writing.

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: How would your writing differ if addressing a headteacher versus addressing your peers?

Q2: Explain the difference between writing to persuade and writing to inform.

Q3: A speech about healthy eating - how would tone differ for primary school children versus adults?

Q4: Why is it important to avoid contractions in formal writing?

✅ Answers

  1. Headteacher: formal register, respectful tone, evidence-based arguments, no contractions. Peers: informal register, relatable tone, personal examples, contractions acceptable.
  2. Persuade aims to convince the reader to agree or take action, using rhetorical devices and emotional appeal. Inform aims to provide facts objectively, with neutral language and clear explanations.
  3. Primary children: simple vocabulary, fun analogies, engaging tone, direct address like "your body needs..." Adults: more sophisticated vocabulary, statistics and evidence, respectful tone, addressing them as decision-makers.
  4. Contractions (can't, don't) are associated with speech and informal writing. Writing them in full (cannot, do not) creates a more formal, professional tone suitable for letters to officials, articles, and most exam tasks.

🎯 Exam Tips

  • Circle or highlight the audience and purpose in the question
  • Plan your tone and style before writing
  • Match your vocabulary to your audience's knowledge level
  • Keep your purpose in mind throughout - every paragraph should serve it
  • Maintain consistent register - don't switch between formal and informal
  • Use direct address for speeches and articles to connect with audience
  • Check that your tone matches what the question asks for
