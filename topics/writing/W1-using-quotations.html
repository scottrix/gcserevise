W1: Using Quotations

Foundation Higher All Boards AO1 AO2

Embedding and analysing quotations effectively - the key to strong literary essays.

📋 Why Quotations Matter

The Role of Quotations

Quotations are the EVIDENCE for your arguments. Without quotations, your essay is just opinion.

Remember: Every point you make needs evidence. "Show, don't just tell." A quotation shows exactly what the writer did.

What Quotations Do

  • Prove you've read the text closely
  • Support your interpretation
  • Allow detailed analysis
  • Give your essay authority

🔍 Selecting Quotations

Good Quotations Have:

Feature Why It Matters
Rich language Words to analyse (metaphors, imagery)
Significance Reveals character, theme, or context
Conciseness Short enough to analyse word by word
Flexibility Can be used for multiple points

What to Avoid

Poor Quotation Choices
  • Too long: "He went to the door and opened it slowly, looking around to see if anyone was watching before stepping outside into the cold night air"
  • Too vague: "He felt sad"
  • Just description: "The room was large and cold"

Good Quotation Examples

Strong Quotations from "Macbeth"
  • "Stars, hide your fires" - Imagery, imperative, symbolism
  • "dagger of the mind" - Metaphor, oxymoron, psychological insight
  • "fair is foul" - Paradox, theme establishment
  • "vaulting ambition" - Metaphor, revealing Macbeth's flaw

📝 Embedding Quotations

What is Embedding?

Making quotations part of your sentence structure, rather than dropping them in separately.

Bad vs Good Embedding

Bad (Dropped) Good (Embedded)
Macbeth is ambitious. "I have no spur to prick the sides of my intent, but vaulting ambition." Macbeth acknowledges that he has "no spur" to drive him except his "vaulting ambition."
Lady Macbeth is cruel. "Unsex me here." Lady Macbeth asks the spirits to "unsex me here," rejecting femininity.
The witches are evil. "Fair is foul and foul is fair." The witches' paradox that "fair is foul" establishes moral inversion.
Technique: Introduce the quotation with your own words, then quote only the key words. The quotation becomes part of YOUR sentence.

Embedding Methods

  1. Introduce with context: "When Macbeth says..."
  2. Use only key words: The adjective "vaulting" suggests...
  3. Split the quotation: Macbeth claims he has "no spur" and identifies "vaulting ambition" as his only motivation.

🔬 Analysing Quotations

The Quotation Analysis Method

PEEL for Quotations:
Point - Make your argument
Evidence - Quote specific words
Explain - Analyse language and effect
Link - Connect to theme/context

Zooming In

Don't just identify a technique - zoom in on specific words:

Zooming In

Quotation: "Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood / Clean from my hand?"

Analysis:

  • "Neptune's ocean" - Hyperbole, Roman god, classical reference elevating the tragedy
  • "wash" - Verb of cleansing, but impossible here
  • "blood" - Metonymy for guilt, visual stain
  • "Clean" - Ironic, nothing can make Macbeth clean again

Word-Level Analysis

What to Analyse Questions to Ask
Word class Is it a powerful verb? Abstract noun? Emotive adjective?
Connotations What associations does this word carry?
Sound Harsh or soft sounds? Alliteration? Sibilance?
Contrast Does it contrast with other words or ideas?

📚 Memorising Quotations

Since Exams Are Closed Book

You need to memorise key quotations. Here's how:

Strategies

Strategy How It Works
Flashcards Quotation on one side, analysis on the other
Grouping by theme Organise quotations by theme (ambition, guilt, etc.)
Key quotations Memorise 5-8 per text that can be used for multiple points
Writing practice Write paragraphs using quotations from memory
Saying aloud Recite quotations - auditory memory helps

How Many Quotations?

Recommended: 5-8 key quotations per major text. Choose quotations that can be used for multiple themes or characters. Quality over quantity.

✍️ Quotation Analysis Examples

Example 1: "Macbeth"

"Stars, hide your fires"

Macbeth's imperative command to the stars to "hide your fires" reveals his desire for darkness to conceal his "black and deep desires." The imperative verb "hide" shows his active rejection of moral scrutiny, while "fires" symbolises truth and exposure. By asking for darkness, Macbeth acknowledges that his ambitions are wrong - he knows they cannot survive in light. This connects to the play's theme of appearance vs reality, as Macbeth seeks to hide his true intentions behind a facade of loyalty.

Example 2: "An Inspector Calls"

"We are members of one body"

The Inspector's declaration that "we are members of one body" uses an organic metaphor to express social interconnectedness. The collective pronoun "we" includes everyone, challenging the Birlings' individualistic worldview, while "body" suggests that society functions as a living organism where each part affects the others. This reflects Priestley's socialist message that social responsibility is not optional - we cannot ignore others' suffering without harming ourselves.

🔗 Linking Quotations

Connecting to Themes

After analysing language, link to themes:

  • "This reflects the theme of..."
  • "Through this, the writer explores..."
  • "This connects to the wider idea of..."

Connecting to Context

Link to context where relevant:

  • "For Shakespeare's Jacobean audience, this would have..."
  • "Written in 1945, this reflects..."
  • "This challenges Victorian ideas about..."

⚠️ Common Mistakes

Mistake Fix
Quoting without analysis Always explain what the quotation shows
Quoting too much Select key words, not whole speeches
Dropping quotations Embed them in your sentence structure
Describing what it means Analyse HOW language creates effect
Using the same quotation repeatedly Learn a range of quotations

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What makes a good quotation for analysis?

Q2: What's the difference between dropping and embedding a quotation?

Q3: How many quotations should you memorise per text?

Q4: After analysing language, what should you link to?

✅ Answers

  1. Good quotations are: rich in language techniques (metaphor, imagery), significant to character/theme, concise (short enough to analyse word by word), and flexible (can be used for multiple points).
  2. Dropping: quotation stands alone as separate sentence. Embedding: quotation is part of your sentence structure. Embedded quotations flow naturally and show stronger integration.
  3. Recommended 5-8 key quotations per text. Choose quotations that work for multiple themes/characters. Quality over quantity - better to know a few well than many poorly.
  4. After analysing language, link to: themes (what idea is explored), character development, and context (when/why written, audience response).

📝 Exam Technique

Top Tip: In closed-book exams, quotation recall under time pressure is critical. Spend the first 5 minutes of planning time jotting down the 3-4 quotations you will use. This prevents you forgetting key quotes mid-essay and ensures every paragraph has evidence ready before you start writing.

Time Management for Quotation-Based Answers

Stage Time What to Do
Planning 5 mins Note your 3-4 key quotations and which point each supports
Writing 30 mins Embed a short quotation in every paragraph — aim for 3-5 embedded quotes total
Checking 5 mins Verify each quotation is accurate, embedded, and analysed

What Examiners Look For

  • Quotations embedded into sentences, not dropped in separately
  • Short, precise quotations (2-5 words) rather than long extracts
  • Word-level analysis after each quotation — zoom in on connotations
  • Quotations that genuinely support the point being made
  • Accurate quotation recall — misquoting loses credibility

Mark Allocation

AO1 (interpretation) and AO2 (analysis) both depend on quotation use. Without evidence, AO1 arguments are unsupported and AO2 analysis has nothing to work on. A paragraph without a quotation typically cannot score above Level 2 on either AO.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often drop quotations into essays as stand-alone sentences. Wrong: Macbeth is ambitious. "I have no spur to prick the sides of my intent but vaulting ambition." Correct: Macbeth acknowledges he has "no spur" except his "vaulting ambition," embedding the quotation into his own analytical statement.

Students often quote large chunks of text without analysing the key words. Wrong: Shakespeare shows Macbeth's guilt when he says "Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood clean from my hand?" Correct: Macbeth's hyperbolic reference to "Neptune's ocean" emphasises the impossibility of cleansing his guilt — even a god's ocean cannot wash away his crime.

Students often misremember quotations, changing key words. Wrong: "Stars hide your fire" — missing the plural "fires" and the imperative structure. Correct: "Stars, hide your fires" — the plural "fires" and comma create an imperative command to multiple sources of light, strengthening Macbeth's desire for concealment.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

25 marks: How does Shakespeare present Macbeth's struggle with his conscience in Act 1, Scene 7?

Shakespeare presents Macbeth's conscience as a powerful but ultimately insufficient restraint against ambition. In his soliloquy, Macbeth acknowledges he has "no spur to prick the sides of my intent, but only vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself." The equestrian metaphor is devastatingly precise: "spur" implies motivation, yet Macbeth concedes he has "no spur" — his only driver is "vaulting ambition," where the adjective "vaulting" suggests something that leaps beyond its natural limits. The verb "o'erleaps" conveys reckless overextension, while the reflexive "itself" reveals that ambition's destructive energy will ultimately turn inward. Shakespeare reinforces Macbeth's moral awareness through his recognition that Duncan's "virtues will plead like angels," where the simile elevates the king's goodness to a divine standard against which Macbeth's "bloody instructions" are measured. The juxtaposition of "virtues" and "bloody" underscores the moral chasm Macbeth must cross. For a Jacobean audience versed in the Great Chain of Being, Macbeth's awareness of this moral order makes his subsequent choice all the more damnable — he is not ignorant of right and wrong but chooses to silence his conscience.

Mark scheme: AO1 (10/10) — sustained, confident interpretation with an informed personal response; AO2 (10/10) — detailed analysis of language with precise word-level zoom; AO3 (5/5) — integrated Jacobean context linked to the Great Chain of Being.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 (Interpretation): At grade 5, students use quotations to support basic points ("Macbeth is ambitious — 'vaulting ambition'"). At grade 9, quotations are woven into sustained, exploratory arguments where each quotation extends the interpretation rather than merely backing it up. The quotation becomes a springboard, not a stopping point.

AO2 (Analysis): At grade 5, students identify techniques within quotations ("This is a metaphor"). At grade 9, students zoom into individual words, explore connotations, and connect micro-analysis to macro-effects on meaning. The shift from "the writer uses a metaphor" to "the equestrian metaphor of 'o'erleaps' conveys reckless overextension" marks the boundary between Level 4 and Level 6.

From Grade 5 to Grade 9: The critical difference is precision. Grade 5 quotes and names; grade 9 quotes, embeds, zooms, and connects. Every quotation in a grade 9 answer is short (2-5 words), embedded syntactically, analysed at word level, and linked to theme or context. There is never a quotation without analysis, and never analysis without a quotation.
