W2: Essay Structure

Foundation Higher All Boards AO1 AO4

Organising literature essays effectively - introductions, paragraphs, and conclusions.

📋 Why Structure Matters

Good Structure:

  • Shows you can organise ideas logically
  • Helps examiner follow your argument
  • Ensures you cover all parts of the question
  • Keeps you focused, prevents rambling
AO4 (5-10%) covers spelling, punctuation, and structure. A well-organised essay scores higher.

📝 The Basic Structure

Three Parts

td>Set up your argument
Part Length Purpose
Introduction 2-3 sentences
Body Paragraphs 3-5 paragraphs Develop your argument with evidence
Conclusion 2-3 sentences Sum up and evaluate
Essay Formula:
Introduction → Point 1 → Point 2 → Point 3 → Conclusion

🎯 Writing Introductions

What an Introduction Does

  • Identifies the text and writer
  • Responds to the question
  • States your main argument

Introduction Formula

  1. Name - text and writer
  2. Context - if relevant
  3. Thesis - your main argument
Introduction Example

Question: How does Shakespeare present Macbeth's ambition?

Introduction: In "Macbeth," Shakespeare presents ambition as a destructive force that leads to moral corruption and eventual downfall. Through Macbeth's soliloquies and interactions with Lady Macbeth, we see how ambition transforms a noble warrior into a tyrannical murderer.

What to Avoid

  • Don't retell the plot
  • Don't give a biography of the writer
  • Don't start with "In this essay I will..."

📊 PEEL Paragraphs

The PEEL Structure

Each body paragraph should follow PEEL:

Letter What What You Do
P Point Make a clear argument
E Evidence Quote from the text
E Explain Analyse the quotation
L Link Connect to question/theme/context

Example PEEL Paragraph

PEEL in Action

P: Shakespeare presents Macbeth's ambition as immediately destructive to his moral character.

E: Macbeth acknowledges his "vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself / And falls on th'other."

E: The metaphor of horse-riding suggests ambition is reckless - it leaps without caution. "O'erleaps itself" implies ambition exceeds its own bounds, while "falls" foreshadows Macbeth's eventual downfall.

L: This establishes the tragic pattern of the play: ambition leads to overreaching, which leads to destruction.

🔗 Linking Paragraphs

Why Link?

Linking creates a COHESIVE essay - one that flows rather than jumping between points.

Linking Words

Type Examples
Adding Furthermore, Additionally, Moreover
Contrasting However, Conversely, In contrast
Developing Building on this, This deepens when
Concluding Ultimately, In conclusion, Therefore
Linking in Practice

End of Paragraph 1: ...shows Macbeth's initial hesitation.

Start of Paragraph 2: However, this hesitation disappears after the murder, and Macbeth becomes increasingly ruthless.

📝 Writing Conclusions

What a Conclusion Does

  • Summarises your main points
  • Evaluates the writer's methods
  • Offers a final insight

Conclusion Formula

  1. Summarise: Restate main argument
  2. Evaluate: Comment on writer's effectiveness
  3. Context: Link to broader significance
Conclusion Example

Shakespeare presents ambition as a corrupting force that destroys Macbeth's humanity. Through soliloquies, imagery, and contrast with other characters, Shakespeare shows how unchecked ambition leads to isolation, tyranny, and death. For a Jacobean audience, this reinforced the dangers of overreaching and the importance of accepting one's place in the divine order.

What to Avoid

  • Don't introduce new points
  • Don't just repeat the introduction
  • Don't say "In conclusion" - just conclude

📐 Different Question Types

Character Questions

Structure:

  • Introduction: overall presentation
  • Paragraph 1: first impression
  • Paragraph 2: development/turning point
  • Paragraph 3: final impression
  • Conclusion: overall significance

Theme Questions

Structure:

  • Introduction: define theme, state argument
  • Paragraph 1: how theme introduced
  • Paragraph 2: how theme developed
  • Paragraph 3: how theme resolved
  • Conclusion: overall message about theme

Extract Questions

Structure:

  • Introduction: identify extract's place in text
  • Paragraphs 1-3: analyse extract (language, structure, form)
  • Paragraph 4: link to whole text
  • Conclusion: significance of this moment

Planning Tips

  • Identify 3-4 main points before writing
  • Know your quotations for each point
  • Sketch the structure: Intro → P1 → P2 → P3 → Conclusion

Mistake Fix

No clear argument Have a thesis in your introduction

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What are the three parts of an essay?

Q2: What does PEEL stand for?

Q3: What should an introduction include?

Q4: What should you avoid in a conclusion?

✅ Answers

  1. Introduction, Body Paragraphs, Conclusion. Introduction sets up argument, body develops with evidence, conclusion summarises and evaluates.
  2. Point - Make your argument. Evidence - Quote the text. Explain - Analyse the quotation. Link - Connect to question/theme/context.
  3. Introduction should: identify text and writer, respond to the question, state your main argument (thesis). Keep it 2-3 sentences.
  4. Don't introduce new points. Don't just repeat the introduction. Don't say "In conclusion." The conclusion should summarise and evaluate, not add new ideas.

📝 Exam Technique

Top Tip: Always spend 5 minutes planning before writing. A student with a strong plan and 3 well-structured paragraphs will outperform a student with 5 rambling paragraphs every time. Examiners reward coherent argument over quantity — structure is your roadmap to every mark.

Time Management by Mark Allocation

Task Time Why
Read and annotate question 2 mins Identify key words in the question — every paragraph must address them
Plan structure 3 mins Decide thesis, 3 main points, and quotations for each
Write introduction 2 mins State text, writer, and your thesis — keep it under 3 sentences
Write body paragraphs 28 mins Roughly 9 mins per paragraph — point, evidence, analysis, link
Write conclusion 3 mins Summarise argument and evaluate writer's overall effectiveness
Check and refine 2 mins Verify SPaG, check paragraphs link, ensure no new points in conclusion

What Examiners Look For

  • A clear thesis stated in the introduction and sustained throughout
  • Paragraphs that develop the argument sequentially, not randomly
  • Effective linking between paragraphs showing progression of thought
  • A conclusion that evaluates rather than repeats
  • AO4: accurate SPaG and controlled, varied sentence structures

Mark Allocation

AO1 (interpretation) depends on clear, structured argument. AO4 (5-10% of total marks) rewards coherent organisation and accurate SPaG. An essay with brilliant ideas but no structure loses marks on both AO1 and AO4 — structure is not optional, it is a marking criterion.

⚠️ Common Errors

Watch Out!

Students often retell the plot instead of structuring an argument. Wrong: First Macbeth meets the witches, then he writes to Lady Macbeth, then he kills Duncan. Correct: Shakespeare presents Macbeth's ambition as increasingly destructive: first through the witches' temptation, then through Lady Macbeth's manipulation, and finally through Macbeth's own moral collapse.

Students often write paragraphs that don't connect to each other. Wrong: Paragraph 1 about ambition. Paragraph 2 about guilt. Paragraph 3 about the supernatural. No links between them. Correct: Each paragraph builds on the last — "Having established Macbeth's ambition, Shakespeare then shows how guilt consumes him, suggesting that unchecked ambition inevitably leads to psychological torment."

Students often start conclusions with "In conclusion" or introduce entirely new points. Wrong: In conclusion, I think Macbeth is a play about power. Also, the witches represent fate. Correct: Shakespeare ultimately presents ambition as a self-destructive force — the play's tragic resolution confirms that moral corruption, once begun, cannot be reversed.

✍️ Model Answer

Full-Mark Response

25 marks: How does Priestley present the theme of social responsibility in "An Inspector Calls"?

Priestley presents social responsibility as both a moral imperative and a practical necessity, structuring the play to expose the consequences of its absence. The Inspector's declaration that "we are members of one body" employs an organic metaphor that positions society as an interdependent organism — the collective pronoun "we" deliberately includes the Birlings, dismantling their belief that responsibility is individual rather than communal. Structurally, Priestley arranges each confession chronologically, building dramatic intensity as Eva's descent becomes more desperate with each Birling's actions. Sheila's realisation after the Inspector's questioning — "But these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people" — marks the play's turning point, as the exclamatory shift from abstraction to humanity signals her moral awakening. Written in 1945 but set in 1912, the play's dramatic irony allows Priestley to condemn pre-war individualism from a post-war perspective where collective responsibility had proven essential for national survival. The cyclical structure — ending with a second inspector's call — reinforces Priestley's message that social responsibility is not a lesson learned once but a perpetual obligation.

Mark scheme: AO1 (10/10) — sustained, exploratory argument with clear thesis and progression; AO2 (5/5) — analysis of structural choices (chronological confessions, cyclical ending); AO3 (5/5) — integrated 1945/1912 context; AO4 (5/5) — coherent organisation, varied sentences, accurate SPaG.

📊 AO Deep Dive

Assessment Objective Analysis

AO1 (Interpretation): At grade 5, students organise essays chronologically — following the text's plot order. At grade 9, students organise essays thematically or argumentatively, with each paragraph building a cumulative case. The thesis introduced in paragraph 1 is developed, challenged, and resolved across the whole essay.

AO4 (SPaG and Structure): At grade 5, students use basic paragraphing with some linking words. At grade 9, students deploy sophisticated cohesion — paragraphs connect through contrast, development, and consequence. Sentence structures vary deliberately for emphasis and clarity, and SPaG is virtually error-free.

From Grade 5 to Grade 9: The shift is from paragraphs that stand alone to paragraphs that work together. Grade 5 writes three separate points; grade 9 writes one sustained argument across three stages. The introduction sets a thesis, each paragraph advances it, and the conclusion evaluates it. Linking is not decorative ("Furthermore...") but logical ("This destruction extends beyond the individual when...").
