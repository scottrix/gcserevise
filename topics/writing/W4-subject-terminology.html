W4: Subject Terminology

Foundation Higher All Boards AO2

Using literary terminology accurately - language, form, structure, and dramatic terms.

📋 Why Terminology Matters

AO2 Requirements

AO2 asks you to analyse language, form, and structure using "relevant subject terminology where appropriate."

Important: Terminology helps you discuss WHAT writers do. But don't just name techniques - always explain their EFFECT.

The Right Approach

Wrong Right
"The writer uses a metaphor." "The metaphor of 'x' suggests..."
"There's alliteration here." "The alliteration of 's' sounds creates a sinister effect..."
"The writer uses language." "The writer's use of emotive language evokes..."

📝 Language Terms

Figurative Language

Term Definition Example
Metaphor One thing IS another "Life's but a walking shadow"
Simile Comparison using "like" or "as" "solitary as an oyster"
Personification Human qualities given to non-human "Sleep that knits up the raveled sleave"
Symbolism Object representing larger idea Blood = guilt in Macbeth
Oxymoron Two contradictory terms "Fair is foul"
Hyperbole Exaggeration "Neptune's ocean"
Irony Saying opposite of meaning Birling: "unsinkable"

Sound Devices

Term Definition Effect
Alliteration Repeated consonant sounds Creates rhythm, links words
Sibilance Repeated 's' sounds Soft or sinister effect
Assonance Repeated vowel sounds Creates internal music
Onomatopoeia Words sounding like their meaning Vivid, immediate

Word-Level Terms

Term Definition Use
Connotations Associations a word carries "Serpent" = evil, temptation
Semantic field Group of related words Darkness: night, shadow, black
Emotive language Words creating emotion "Butcher", "fiend"
Imperative Command verb "Stars, hide your fires"
Rhetorical question Question not expecting answer "Is this a dagger?"

📝 Using Terminology in Essays

The Right Balance

Don't overuse terminology: A strong essay doesn't name every technique. Use terminology when it helps you explain HOW a writer creates effect.

How to Use

Good Terminology Use

Shakespeare's use of soliloquy allows the audience access to Macbeth's inner thoughts. In his "dagger" soliloquy, the metaphor of "dagger of the mind" reveals his psychological torment. The oxymoron suggests the conflict between physical reality and mental vision. The enjambment throughout the speech creates a sense of ongoing, troubled thought, reflecting his indecision.

Terms to Use Sparingly

These are fine but don't add much analysis:

  • "The writer uses language..."
  • "The writer uses imagery..."
  • "There are techniques..."

Terms Worth Using

These show precise understanding:

  • Specific techniques: metaphor, soliloquy, dramatic irony
  • Structural terms: enjambment, caesura, cyclical
  • Form terms: sonnet, ballad, dramatic monologue

⚠️ Common Mistakes

Mistake Fix
Spelling terms wrong Practise spelling: metaphor, personification, oxymoron, soliloquy
Using terms inaccurately Check definitions before using
Naming without explaining Always explain effect after naming
Overusing terminology Use when it helps analysis, not for its own sake
"The writer uses a technique" Name the specific technique

❓ Practice Questions

Q1: What's the difference between a metaphor and a simile?

Q2: What is a soliloquy and why is it useful for playwrights?

Q3: What does dramatic irony mean?

Q4: Name three structural terms and their effects.

✅ Answers

  1. Metaphor says something IS something else ("life's a walking shadow"). Simile compares using "like" or "as" ("solitary as an oyster"). Metaphors create stronger, more direct connections.
  2. A soliloquy is a speech where a character alone on stage speaks their thoughts aloud. It reveals inner thoughts directly to the audience, creating intimacy and showing psychological state.
  3. Dramatic irony is when the audience knows more than the characters. It creates tension, makes audience complicit, and highlights characters' mistakes or blindness.
  4. Any three from: enjambment (creates flow), caesura (creates pause), cyclical structure (suggests repetition), foreshadowing (creates anticipation), climax (height of tension).
